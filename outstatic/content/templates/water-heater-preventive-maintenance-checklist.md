# Standard Operating Procedure: Water Heater Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocols for residential and light-commercial water heater units. Regular maintenance is essential to extend the equipment's lifespan, improve energy efficiency, maintain water quality, and mitigate the risk of catastrophic tank failure or leaks. Technicians and facility managers are required to adhere to these procedures annually to ensure compliance with safety standards and manufacturer warranty requirements.

## Section 1: Safety & Preparation
*   **Power Disconnection:** For electric units, turn off the dedicated circuit breaker. For gas units, rotate the gas control valve to the "Pilot" or "Off" position.
*   **Water Supply:** Shut off the cold water inlet valve to the heater.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Utilize heat-resistant gloves and safety goggles, particularly when handling drain valves or hot water lines.
*   **Drain Access:** Attach a garden hose to the drain valve located at the base of the tank, ensuring the other end terminates in a floor drain or exterior safe-discharge area.

## Section 2: Sediment Flushing & Tank Cleaning
*   **Pressure Relief:** Open a hot water faucet at a nearby sink to prevent a vacuum lock while draining.
*   **Tank Drain:** Open the drain valve and allow the tank to discharge approximately 1–2 gallons of water. Observe the water for sediment content (scale/sand).
*   **Full Flush:** If sediment is significant, repeat the flushing process until the water runs clear.
*   **Valve Inspection:** Once flushing is complete, close the drain valve, remove the hose, and ensure the valve is fully seated and free of leaks.

## Section 3: Anode Rod Inspection & Replacement
*   **Accessing the Rod:** Locate the anode rod hex head on top of the tank. Use a 1 1/16" socket to loosen and remove the rod.
*   **Condition Assessment:** Inspect the rod for calcium buildup and exposed steel core. If more than 6 inches of the steel core are visible, or if the rod is heavily coated in mineral deposits, it must be replaced.
*   **Reinstallation:** Apply Teflon tape to the threads of the new anode rod and tighten securely to ensure a proper seal.

## Section 4: Testing & System Re-pressurization
*   **System Refill:** Open the cold water supply valve. Keep the hot water faucet open until all air is purged from the lines and a steady stream of water flows.
*   **T&P Valve Test:** Manually lift the lever on the Temperature and Pressure (T&P) relief valve to ensure a quick discharge of water. If it does not discharge or fails to close completely, replace the valve immediately.
*   **Power Restoration:** Only after the tank is verified as completely full, turn the gas/electric power back on.
*   **Leak Check:** Monitor the drain valve, T&P valve, and inlet/outlet connections for 15 minutes to confirm no seepage is occurring.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** If the drain valve is plastic, be extremely gentle. These valves are notorious for breaking; consider upgrading to a full-port brass ball valve during your next major service.
*   **Pro Tip:** If your area has "hard water," increase the frequency of flushing to every six months to prevent heating element burnout.
*   **Pitfall (Dry Firing):** Never turn on the power or light the pilot if the tank is not 100% full. Powering an empty electric water heater will burn out the heating elements in seconds.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring Corrosion):** If you see rust around the base of the tank, this is usually an indicator of an internal leak. Maintenance cannot fix this; replacement is required.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should I perform this preventive maintenance?**
Ideally, once every 12 months. If you live in an area with particularly hard water, perform the flush every 6 months.

**2. Why does my hot water smell like rotten eggs?**
This is often caused by sulfate-reducing bacteria reacting with the anode rod. You may need to swap your current magnesium anode rod for an aluminum or zinc-alloy replacement.

**3. Is it normal to hear popping or rumbling sounds?**
No. These sounds are typically caused by steam bubbles trapped underneath layers of sediment at the bottom of the tank. A thorough flush usually resolves the issue, but if it persists, it may indicate excessive scale buildup on the heating elements.
