# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Preventative Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory maintenance requirements for industrial diesel generator sets to ensure operational reliability, longevity, and compliance with safety standards. Adherence to this schedule is critical to preventing engine failure during power outages and ensuring that all standby power systems are ready for immediate load activation. All maintenance activities must be logged in the equipment service record.

## Pre-Maintenance Safety Protocol
*   Ensure the generator is in "OFF" mode or locked out/tagged out (LOTO) to prevent accidental startup.
*   Allow the engine to cool for at least 30 minutes before touching hot surfaces or draining fluids.
*   Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, chemical-resistant gloves, and steel-toed boots.

## H2: Daily / Pre-Start Inspection
*   **Fuel System:** Check fuel levels and inspect for leaks at fittings, lines, and filters.
*   **Lubrication System:** Check oil levels using the dipstick; ensure the level is between the "Add" and "Full" marks.
*   **Cooling System:** Inspect coolant levels in the radiator or expansion tank; check for signs of hose leaks or fraying.
*   **Air Intake:** Check the air cleaner indicator for restricted airflow or clogging.
*   **Battery:** Inspect battery terminal connections for tightness and corrosion.

## H2: Monthly / 50-Hour Operational Maintenance
*   **Battery Maintenance:** Check electrolyte levels (if not maintenance-free) and measure battery voltage; clean terminals with a wire brush if oxidation is present.
*   **Engine Run Test:** Operate the generator under load for at least 30 minutes to allow the engine to reach operating temperature and purge moisture from the exhaust system.
*   **Vibration Check:** Monitor for abnormal vibrations or metallic noises during operation.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust manifold and piping for leaks or signs of soot buildup.

## H2: Semi-Annual / 250-Hour Maintenance
*   **Fluid Changes:** Drain and replace engine oil and replace oil filters.
*   **Fuel Filtration:** Replace fuel-water separators and primary/secondary fuel filters.
*   **Air Filtration:** Replace engine air filters to prevent airflow restriction.
*   **Cooling System:** Inspect coolant inhibitor levels; flush and replace coolant if the manufacturer's chemical concentration threshold is not met.
*   **Drive Belts:** Inspect alternator and fan belts for cracks, glazing, or improper tension.

## H2: Annual / 500-Hour Maintenance
*   **Cooling System Flush:** Perform a full drain and flush of the radiator cooling system; replace hoses if rubber degradation is detected.
*   **Valve Clearance:** Check and adjust valve lash/clearance according to the engine manufacturer’s specifications.
*   **Electrical Inspection:** Inspect the generator control panel, circuit breakers, and automatic transfer switch (ATS) for loose connections or charred insulation.
*   **Load Bank Testing:** Conduct a simulated load test (if possible) to ensure the engine can maintain frequency and voltage under full rated capacity.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Wet Stacking" Trap:** Never run a diesel generator for long periods without a substantial load. Running a generator at light loads causes unburnt fuel to accumulate in the exhaust, known as "wet stacking," which can cause permanent engine damage.
*   **Pitfall: Fuel Degradation:** Diesel fuel has a limited shelf life. If the generator is rarely used, implement a fuel polishing schedule or use high-quality fuel stabilizers to prevent sludge formation in the tank.
*   **Pro Tip: Oil Analysis:** Periodically send a sample of the used engine oil to a lab. This can detect internal engine wear (metal particles) long before a catastrophic failure occurs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I change the coolant?**
A: Typically every 2 years or 500-1,000 hours, depending on the type of coolant used. Always consult your specific engine manufacturer's manual, as some long-life coolants have longer service intervals.

**Q: Why does my generator produce black smoke during a startup?**
A: Black smoke usually indicates incomplete combustion, often caused by clogged air filters, faulty injectors, or the engine operating at a load that is too low.

**Q: What is the most common cause of generator failure?**
A: The leading cause of failure is battery neglect. Because diesel generators are often idle, the starting batteries fail to hold a charge or lose their ability to deliver the cold cranking amps required to turn over a heavy engine. Always keep a smart charger connected.
