# Standard Operating Procedure: Rental Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting move-in, routine, and move-out property inspections. Maintaining a rigorous inspection protocol is critical for mitigating risk, documenting asset condition, ensuring tenant compliance with lease agreements, and justifying security deposit deductions. All property managers must execute these inspections with objectivity, thoroughness, and photographic evidence to maintain the integrity of the property portfolio.

## Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Review the original move-in inspection report and photos.
*   Ensure all keys, remote controls, and access codes are available.
*   Verify the tenant has been provided with the required 24–48 hour notice (if occupied).
*   Equip yourself with a high-resolution camera or mobile device, a notepad, a flashlight, and a moisture meter if necessary.

## Exterior Property Assessment
*   **Roof and Gutters:** Inspect for loose shingles, debris, or sagging gutters.
*   **Siding and Paint:** Check for peeling paint, rotting wood, or damaged masonry.
*   **Landscaping:** Confirm grass is mowed, hedges are trimmed, and no overgrown branches are damaging the structure.
*   **Hardscape:** Evaluate driveways, sidewalks, and patios for cracks or trip hazards.
*   **Exterior Lighting:** Test all porch and motion-sensor lights.
*   **Fencing and Gates:** Ensure gates latch properly and fencing is structurally sound.

## Interior: Living Areas and Bedrooms
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Check for water stains, cracks, or holes (including nail holes).
*   **Flooring:** Inspect carpet for stains/tears; check hardwood/tile for scratches or lifting.
*   **Doors and Windows:** Test all locks, ensure windows open/close smoothly, and check screens for tears.
*   **Electrical:** Test every outlet (using an outlet tester) and flip every light switch.
*   **HVAC:** Check air filters and ensure the thermostat is functional.

## Interior: Kitchen and Bathrooms
*   **Plumbing:** Run water in all sinks/tubs to check drainage; inspect under cabinets for active leaks or mold.
*   **Appliances:** Run a short cycle on the dishwasher, check the oven temperature, and ensure the refrigerator is cooling.
*   **Fixtures:** Tighten loose towel racks or faucet handles.
*   **Ventilation:** Confirm exhaust fans in the bathroom and range hood are operational.
*   **Caulking:** Inspect grout and silicone seals in showers/tubs to prevent water intrusion.

## Safety and Compliance
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test every unit; ensure they are mounted in accordance with local fire codes.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Check expiration dates and ensure they are mounted in accessible locations.
*   **Trip Hazards:** Identify and photograph any uneven transition strips or loose carpet.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Video Documentation:** Walk through the property with a video camera while narrating your observations. This provides a "continuous" record that photos alone cannot replicate.
*   **Pro Tip: Date-Stamps:** Always ensure your camera settings include a date and time stamp on the photo files.
*   **Pitfall: Rushing the Inspection:** Never perform an inspection in the dark or while a tenant is pressuring you. If you miss a hidden leak or a damaged floorboard, it becomes the owner’s expense rather than the tenant’s.
*   **Pitfall: Subjectivity:** Avoid using vague terms like "dirty" or "bad condition." Use objective descriptors: "3-inch scratch on kitchen floor" or "accumulation of grease on range hood."

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I conduct routine inspections?**
A: Industry standards suggest conducting a routine inspection every 6 months to ensure the tenant is maintaining the property and to identify minor maintenance issues before they become expensive repairs.

**Q: What do I do if a tenant refuses entry for an inspection?**
A: Refer to your specific state landlord-tenant laws and the language in your lease agreement. Typically, you must serve a formal written notice of intent to enter. If they continue to deny access, consult legal counsel regarding a notice to cure or quit.

**Q: Should I share the inspection report with the tenant?**
A: Yes. Providing a copy of the report fosters transparency and builds a professional relationship. If the inspection was a move-in or move-out report, having the tenant sign off on the findings is mandatory to avoid security deposit disputes.
