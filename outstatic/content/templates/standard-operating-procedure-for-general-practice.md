# Standard Operating Procedure: General Practice Operations

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as the foundational framework for maintaining high-quality clinical care, operational efficiency, and regulatory compliance within our general practice. By standardizing administrative and clinical workflows, we ensure consistent patient outcomes, mitigate risk, and maintain a seamless experience for both staff and patients. Adherence to these protocols is mandatory for all personnel to ensure safe, ethical, and efficient healthcare delivery.

## 1. Patient Intake and Administrative Workflow
*   Verify patient identity using at least two unique identifiers (Full Name and Date of Birth).
*   Review and update patient demographic and insurance information in the Electronic Health Record (EHR).
*   Conduct a brief pre-screening for urgent symptoms to determine if the patient requires immediate triage.
*   Confirm the purpose of the visit and ensure necessary documentation (referrals, test results) is attached to the chart.
*   Collect required co-pays and obtain necessary consent signatures for treatment.

## 2. Clinical Rooming and Vitals
*   Escort the patient to the exam room promptly.
*   Obtain and record accurate vital signs (BP, heart rate, temperature, O2 saturation, weight/height).
*   Document the patient’s chief complaint and reconcile current medication list.
*   Update allergy status and check for drug-drug interactions in the EHR.
*   Sanitize exam equipment and surfaces according to infection control protocols before the physician enters.

## 3. Provider Consultation and Documentation
*   Provider reviews the patient’s history, current symptoms, and relevant diagnostic data.
*   Perform physical examination with a chaperone present when required by clinic policy.
*   Discuss clinical findings, diagnosis, and treatment options clearly with the patient.
*   Document the encounter in the EHR in real-time, ensuring all ICD-10 and CPT coding requirements are met.
*   Finalize clinical notes, prescriptions, and any necessary lab or imaging orders before the patient leaves the room.

## 4. Discharge and Follow-up Planning
*   Provide the patient with a detailed written summary, including medication instructions and specific follow-up actions.
*   Confirm that the patient understands the care plan and verify that all questions have been addressed.
*   Schedule follow-up appointments or specialist referrals before the patient departs.
*   Process clinical orders (labs, pharmacy requests) immediately following the discharge.
*   Prepare the exam room for the next patient (dispose of sharps, change linens, disinfect surfaces).

## 5. Pro Tips & Pitfalls

**Pro Tips:**
*   **The "Huddle":** Conduct a 5-minute morning huddle with the entire team to identify high-acuity patients or potential scheduling bottlenecks.
*   **Template Utilization:** Build standardized EHR templates for common ailments (e.g., URIs, Hypertension) to save documentation time and ensure compliance.
*   **Patient Portal:** Actively encourage patients to use the secure portal for non-urgent requests to reduce telephone volume.

**Pitfalls to Avoid:**
*   **Documentation Lag:** Delaying charting until the end of the day leads to errors and incomplete medical records. Chart as you go.
*   **Skipping Verification:** Never assume identity based on face recognition; failing to check two identifiers is the most common cause of medical error.
*   **Communication Gaps:** Assuming a patient understands "follow-up as needed." Always provide specific timelines and clear instructions.

## 6. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if a patient’s identity information does not match our records?**
A: Do not proceed with clinical care until the discrepancy is resolved. Contact the patient's insurance provider or request official identification to update the record properly to prevent medical record fraud or incorrect treatment.

**Q: How do we handle walk-in patients when the schedule is full?**
A: All walk-ins must be triaged by a nurse or clinician immediately. If they are not in acute distress, offer the earliest available opening or provide information on the nearest Urgent Care facility. 

**Q: How often must clinical equipment be calibrated or inspected?**
A: All diagnostic and clinical equipment must undergo maintenance and calibration according to the manufacturer’s schedule, or at least annually. Logs must be maintained in the clinic’s central operations binder for audit purposes.
