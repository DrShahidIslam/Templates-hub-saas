# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection protocols for diesel generator sets to ensure operational readiness, longevity, and safety. Regular inspections are critical for identifying potential failures before they occur, particularly for standby systems that must engage during emergency power outages. All inspections should be conducted by qualified personnel using appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, hearing protection, and oil-resistant gloves.

## 1. General Safety & Environmental Check
*   Ensure the generator control panel is set to "AUTO" or "OFF" mode based on maintenance requirements to prevent accidental startup.
*   Verify the engine is cool enough to handle before opening any caps (fuel, oil, or coolant).
*   Check the immediate area for signs of debris, nesting materials, or water ingress.
*   Ensure proper ventilation is operational if the generator room is enclosed.

## 2. Fluid Level Inspection
*   **Engine Oil:** Check the dipstick. Oil should be at the "FULL" mark. If low, add the manufacturer-recommended grade.
*   **Coolant System:** Inspect the coolant level in the radiator or expansion tank. Check hoses for cracks, bulges, or signs of seepage at the clamps.
*   **Fuel System:** Check the fuel gauge and, if equipped, the primary fuel tank level. Verify the fuel supply valve is in the "OPEN" position.
*   **Leaks:** Visually scan the floor under the unit for any drops of oil, coolant, or fuel.

## 3. Battery & Electrical System
*   **Battery Voltage:** Use a multimeter to verify the battery bank voltage meets manufacturer specifications.
*   **Connections:** Inspect terminals for corrosion. Ensure cables are tight and free of fraying.
*   **Battery Charger:** Verify the "Float" indicator light is on and that the charger is receiving AC power.
*   **Control Panel:** Check for any "Alarm" or "Fault" codes on the display. Ensure the emergency stop button is not engaged.

## 4. Air Intake & Exhaust System
*   **Air Filters:** Check the air cleaner service indicator. If red, replace the filter element immediately.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the muffler and piping for rust, loose bolts, or carbon deposits.
*   **Clearance:** Ensure there are no flammable materials stored near the exhaust manifold or piping.

## 5. Final Operational Verification
*   **Lubrication:** Inspect all external linkages and pivots for signs of binding.
*   **Drive Belts:** Check fan and alternator belt tension. Ensure there are no cracks or signs of glazing on the belt surface.
*   **Record Keeping:** Log all findings in the generator maintenance logbook, including engine hours, fluid levels, and any parts replaced.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always perform a "Load Bank Test" annually. Running a generator without load for extended periods causes "wet stacking," where unburnt fuel accumulates in the exhaust system.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a 3-month fuel rotation policy or use high-quality fuel stabilizers to prevent algae growth and fuel degradation.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore minor leaks. A small coolant seep often leads to a major pump failure under the high-pressure conditions of an emergency start.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not overfill oil. Overfilling can lead to aeration, high pressure, and potential seal damage.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should the diesel generator be inspected?**
A: For standby generators, a visual inspection is recommended monthly. A more comprehensive technical maintenance check should be performed by a certified technician at least semi-annually or according to the manufacturer’s run-hour intervals.

**Q: What is "Wet Stacking" and why is it dangerous?**
A: Wet stacking occurs when a diesel engine runs at light loads or is left idling. The engine doesn't reach the required operating temperature, causing unburnt fuel and carbon to build up in the exhaust. This can eventually lead to permanent engine damage or fire hazards.

**Q: What should I do if the control panel displays a "Low Battery" fault?**
A: First, check the AC breaker feeding the battery charger to ensure it hasn't tripped. If the charger is functional, have the battery load-tested, as deep-cycle lead-acid batteries typically require replacement every 2 to 3 years regardless of superficial appearance.
