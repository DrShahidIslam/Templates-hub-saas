# Standard Operating Procedure: Formal Tender Process

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for managing end-to-end tender operations. The objective is to ensure transparency, compliance, cost-efficiency, and the selection of the most qualified vendors. By following this standardized workflow, the organization mitigates procurement risk, ensures competitive bidding, and maintains a clear audit trail for all contractual engagements.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Requirements Gathering

*   **Define Scope of Work (SOW):** Draft a comprehensive document outlining deliverables, technical specifications, timelines, and service level agreements (SLAs).
*   **Establish Evaluation Criteria:** Define the weighting system (e.g., 60% price, 40% technical capability) and identify mandatory compliance requirements.
*   **Budget Approval:** Secure formal sign-off from the Finance Department to ensure the project budget is allocated and approved.
*   **Identify Bidders:** Compile a list of potential vendors through internal databases, market research, or public outreach.

## Phase 2: Solicitation and Tendering

*   **Draft RFP/RFQ Documentation:** Create the Request for Proposal (RFP) or Request for Quotation (RFQ), including instructions for submission, legal terms, and conditions.
*   **Approval of Tender Documents:** Legal and Procurement management must review and sign off on the solicitation documents.
*   **Publication/Distribution:** Issue the tender via the organization’s portal, email, or trade publications.
*   **Pre-Bid Meeting:** Host a mandatory or optional Q&A session for prospective bidders to clarify scope and requirements.
*   **Addendum Management:** If changes are required, issue formal addenda to all registered bidders simultaneously to ensure a level playing field.

## Phase 3: Evaluation and Selection

*   **Receipt and Logging:** Log all submissions in a secure, time-stamped register; ensure all bids are kept confidential until the deadline.
*   **Compliance Check:** Perform an initial "pass/fail" review of submissions to ensure they met the mandatory documentation and deadline requirements.
*   **Technical Evaluation:** Subject matter experts review technical proposals against the pre-defined scoring matrix.
*   **Commercial Evaluation:** The Finance team reviews the pricing models, ensuring they are competitive and within budget parameters.
*   **Stakeholder Recommendation:** Compile a summary report detailing the scoring of all vendors and a formal recommendation for the winning bidder.

## Phase 4: Contract Award and Closure

*   **Notification:** Send formal "Award" letters to the winning bidder and "Regret" letters to unsuccessful participants.
*   **Contract Negotiation:** Finalize terms, clarify specific line items, and ensure all insurance and indemnity clauses are secured.
*   **Contract Signing:** Execute the contract with authorized signatories from both parties.
*   **Debriefing:** Provide feedback to unsuccessful bidders upon request (maintaining confidentiality of competitors' pricing).
*   **Document Archiving:** Securely archive the tender file, including scorecards, communication logs, and the signed contract for future audit purposes.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Always maintain a "Communication Log." Any conversation with a bidder must be documented. If you answer a question for one bidder, the answer must be shared with all.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "Weighted Scoring Matrix" to remove personal bias during the selection process.
*   **Pitfall:** Scope Creep. If the SOW is vague, you will receive incomparable bids. Spend 80% of your effort in the preparation phase.
*   **Pitfall:** Failure to perform due diligence. Never skip a financial health check or background reference check on a new vendor.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What is the difference between an RFP and an RFQ?**
An RFQ (Request for Quotation) is typically used for standardized goods where price is the primary driver. An RFP (Request for Proposal) is used for complex projects where technical approach, innovation, and expertise are as important as the price.

**2. What should I do if I receive a bid after the deadline?**
Strictly reject it. Accepting a late bid compromises the integrity of the process and invites legal challenges or accusations of favoritism.

**3. Is it mandatory to choose the lowest bidder?**
No. Most procurement policies state that the "Best Value" should be chosen. This allows you to select a bidder who may be slightly more expensive but offers superior technical quality, reliability, or risk mitigation.
