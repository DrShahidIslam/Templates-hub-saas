# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Audit for Real Estate Operations

This document establishes the standardized framework for conducting comprehensive internal audits within a real estate organization. The primary objective of this audit is to ensure fiscal responsibility, regulatory compliance, operational efficiency, and the mitigation of risks associated with property management, brokerage activities, and corporate governance. Adherence to this SOP ensures that all internal controls are robust, transparent, and aligned with industry standards such as IFRS/GAAP and local real estate regulatory requirements.

## 1. Financial Controls and Revenue Assurance
*   **Bank Reconciliations:** Verify that all property-specific bank accounts are reconciled monthly against property management software (e.g., Yardi, AppFolio).
*   **Accounts Receivable (AR) Aging:** Review all tenant delinquency reports; confirm that late fees are assessed per lease agreements and collection efforts are documented.
*   **Security Deposit Management:** Audit the escrow/trust accounts to ensure security deposits are segregated from operating funds and held in accordance with local landlord-tenant laws.
*   **Vendor Disbursements:** Cross-reference invoices against Purchase Orders (POs) and service contracts to prevent duplicate payments or unauthorized expenditures.
*   **Revenue Recognition:** Validate that base rent, common area maintenance (CAM) charges, and ancillary fees are accurately calculated and billed per the lease abstraction.

## 2. Lease Administration and Compliance
*   **Lease Abstract Accuracy:** Compare system-stored lease terms (rent steps, expiration dates, renewal options) against the executed legal documents.
*   **Insurance Verification:** Ensure all tenants have active Certificates of Insurance (COI) that meet the minimum liability coverage stipulated in their lease.
*   **Compliance with Local Statutes:** Confirm that all rental disclosures and mandatory local filings (e.g., rent control registrations) are up-to-date.
*   **Renewal Processes:** Verify that renewal notifications were sent within the required timeframe to minimize vacancy risk.

## 3. Property Operations and Maintenance
*   **Maintenance Logs:** Review work order completion timestamps to ensure service levels are meeting property standards.
*   **Capital Expenditure (CapEx) Tracking:** Verify that major repairs or renovations are backed by competitive bidding processes and are being capitalized correctly rather than expensed.
*   **Procurement Audit:** Confirm that vendor contracts follow the company’s signature authority matrix.
*   **Safety Inspections:** Document that annual fire, elevator, and building safety inspections have been completed and certified by relevant authorities.

## 4. Regulatory and Legal Documentation
*   **Licensing:** Audit all broker and salesperson licenses to ensure they are current and compliant with the state Real Estate Commission.
*   **Anti-Money Laundering (AML):** Ensure that proper KYC (Know Your Customer) protocols are followed for large-scale transactions or investor capital inflows.
*   **Fair Housing Compliance:** Review marketing materials and internal tenant selection criteria to ensure they adhere to Fair Housing Act regulations.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - The "Silo" Trap:** Avoid auditing property management independently of finance. Discrepancies often occur where operational data meets accounting ledger entries.
*   **Pro Tip - Utilize Data Analytics:** Instead of manual sampling, use data visualization tools to flag outliers in maintenance costs or unexpected dips in occupancy rates.
*   **Pro Tip - Surprise Audits:** Conduct unannounced "spot checks" on petty cash or on-site inventory to ensure better adherence to day-to-day policies.
*   **Pitfall - Lack of Documentation:** If an action isn't documented, assume it didn't happen for the purpose of the audit. Ensure all verbal approvals are followed by a "paper trail" email.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How frequently should these internal audits occur?**
A: High-risk areas (like cash handling and trust accounts) should be audited monthly. Broader operational audits should occur quarterly, with a full-scale comprehensive audit performed annually.

**Q: What is the biggest red flag to watch for during a real estate audit?**
A: Inconsistent security deposit accounting is the most common red flag. If deposits are being moved into operating accounts or are missing, it indicates a significant breakdown in financial controls that could lead to legal exposure.

**Q: Who should be responsible for executing this internal audit?**
A: Ideally, a dedicated Internal Audit department or an external auditor. If the company is too small for a dedicated team, the audit should be performed by a member of the finance department who is not involved in the day-to-day processing of property transactions to maintain segregation of duties.
