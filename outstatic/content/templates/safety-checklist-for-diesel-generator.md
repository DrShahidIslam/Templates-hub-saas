# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Safety Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory safety protocols and inspection requirements for diesel generator units. Ensuring the integrity of these systems is critical for preventing mechanical failure, minimizing fire hazards, and ensuring reliable power output during outages. This checklist must be completed by qualified personnel prior to manual startups or as part of a scheduled preventive maintenance routine. Failure to adhere to these safety steps may result in equipment damage, environmental contamination, or severe personal injury.

## Pre-Start Safety & Site Preparation

*   **PPE Verification:** Ensure all personnel are wearing appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including hearing protection, safety glasses, and gloves.
*   **Ventilation Check:** Confirm that the generator enclosure or room ventilation system is unobstructed and functional to prevent carbon monoxide accumulation.
*   **Clearance Zone:** Ensure the area surrounding the generator is free of flammable materials, debris, or unauthorized equipment.
*   **Grounding Inspection:** Verify that the generator’s grounding connection is secure and free from corrosion.
*   **Exhaust Path:** Inspect the exhaust stack to ensure it is clear of obstructions and directed safely away from building air intakes.

## Fluid & Mechanical System Inspection

*   **Fuel Level:** Check fuel levels and ensure the supply tank valves are in the open position. Inspect fuel lines for signs of weeping or cracks.
*   **Lube Oil Check:** Verify engine oil levels using the dipstick. Oil should be at the "Full" mark; check for signs of coolant contamination (milky appearance).
*   **Coolant System:** Inspect coolant levels in the radiator or expansion tank. Check hoses for bulges, leaks, or loose clamps.
*   **Battery Status:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion and ensure connections are tight. Check the battery charger for a "Float" status.
*   **Belt Tension:** Visually inspect fan and alternator belts for fraying, cracking, or improper tension.

## Electrical & Control System Verification

*   **Control Panel Display:** Check the digital interface for any active "Fault" or "Warning" codes. Clear all non-critical warnings before starting.
*   **Breaker Status:** Ensure the main circuit breaker is in the "Open" (OFF) position before starting to prevent an unexpected load transfer.
*   **Emergency Stop Button:** Physically inspect the Emergency Stop (E-Stop) mushroom button to ensure it is not depressed or damaged.
*   **Transfer Switch:** Confirm that the Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) is in the correct mode (Auto/Manual) as required by the operational plan.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip (The "Warm-Up" Rule):** Always allow the engine to reach operating temperature under a low load before switching the main breaker to "ON." This prevents thermal shock to internal engine components.
*   **Pro Tip (Logbook Logging):** Maintain a physical or digital logbook for every inspection. Tracking "Run Hours" vs. "Oil Pressure" trends is the best way to predict a failure before it happens.
*   **Pitfall (Cold Starting):** Avoid starting the generator with the main breaker closed. Sudden high-load spikes on a cold engine can lead to stalling or damage to the generator windings.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring Seepage):** Never ignore "small" leaks. Diesel fuel leaks can degrade rubber engine mounts and hoses over time, leading to catastrophic system failure.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a diesel generator be inspected if it is on standby?**
For most commercial and industrial applications, a visual safety inspection and a "no-load" run-test should be conducted weekly, with a comprehensive maintenance service performed every 6–12 months.

**2. What should I do if I smell raw diesel fuel near the unit?**
Immediately cease all inspection activities, ensure the room is ventilated, and initiate the emergency shutdown procedure. Do not attempt to start the engine. Isolate the fuel supply and contact a certified technician to locate and repair the leak.

**3. Why is it important to check the battery even if the generator has a charger?**
Even with a trickle charger, batteries have a finite lifespan. Vibration and heat can cause internal plate degradation. Regular physical inspection of the terminals and voltage testing ensures the unit will actually crank when the utility power fails.
