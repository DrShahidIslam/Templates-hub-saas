# Standard Operating Procedure: Monthly Preventive Maintenance (PM)

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory monthly preventive maintenance protocols designed to ensure operational continuity, extend asset longevity, and maintain safety compliance across all facility equipment. By adhering to this structured monthly regimen, the operations team will preemptively identify mechanical wear, software vulnerabilities, and structural degradation, thereby minimizing costly unplanned downtime and ensuring peak performance efficiency.

## 1. Pre-Maintenance Preparation
*   **Schedule Coordination:** Confirm maintenance windows with department heads to minimize operational disruption.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Ensure all required spare parts, lubricants, and specialized diagnostic tools are inventoried and ready at the staging area.
*   **Safety Review:** Conduct a brief safety huddle to review Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) requirements and personal protective equipment (PPE) specific to the tasks at hand.
*   **Documentation:** Retrieve the physical or digital maintenance logs for the previous month to identify recurring issues or "watch items."

## 2. Infrastructure & Utility Inspections
*   **HVAC Systems:** Inspect air filters for debris buildup; check thermostat calibration and refrigerant levels.
*   **Electrical Panels:** Utilize thermal imaging to identify "hot spots" indicating loose connections; verify that all emergency shut-off labels are legible and unobstructed.
*   **Plumbing & Drainage:** Test backup sump pumps, inspect visible pipe joints for corrosion or condensation, and clear floor drain debris.
*   **Lighting & Life Safety:** Perform a manual test of all emergency egress lighting and smoke/CO detectors.

## 3. Machinery & Equipment Servicing
*   **Lubrication:** Apply industry-standard lubricants to all identified pivot points, bearings, and drive chains according to the manufacturer’s technical manual.
*   **Calibration Check:** Compare equipment output metrics against baseline standards to ensure precision; recalibrate sensors as needed.
*   **Filter & Seal Inspection:** Replace clogged filters and examine seals or gaskets for signs of dry rot, cracking, or fluid leakage.
*   **Belt & Drive Tension:** Check drive belts for fraying and verify appropriate tension levels to prevent slippage and motor strain.

## 4. Software & Digital Systems
*   **System Backups:** Verify the success of the most recent full-system data backup and test the restoration process on a sample file.
*   **Security Patches:** Audit installed software for pending security updates; schedule patch installation for off-peak hours.
*   **Data Log Review:** Analyze equipment performance logs for anomalous spikes in power consumption or cycle times that indicate underlying mechanical strain.

## 5. Post-Maintenance Verification & Reporting
*   **System Testing:** Run all serviced equipment through a full operational cycle to ensure stability.
*   **Asset Tagging:** Ensure every piece of equipment carries a visible, up-to-date maintenance sticker indicating the date of service and technician initials.
*   **Log Completion:** Update the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) with notes on findings, parts used, and hours logged.
*   **Area Cleanup:** Restore the work zone to 5S standards, ensuring all discarded materials, rags, and tools are removed.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always take "before and after" photos of high-wear components; this visual evidence is invaluable for warranty claims and long-term capital expenditure planning.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement a "Red Tag" system for equipment that fails inspection during the PM; do not return it to service until the deficiency is fully resolved.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-lubrication is a common error; excessive grease can attract dust and overheat bearings. Always follow exact manufacturer volume specifications.
*   **Pitfall:** Skipping the documentation phase renders the PM "non-existent" in the eyes of auditors. Treat the log entry with the same importance as the repair itself.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if a required spare part is out of stock during a PM check?**
A: Document the deficiency in the CMMS immediately, order the part, and mark the equipment with a "Pending Maintenance" tag. Notify the facility manager so the repair can be prioritized for the next operational window.

**Q: Can we perform PM tasks while equipment is running?**
A: Only if specified by the manufacturer and if safety protocols (such as guards or light curtains) are fully active. As a general rule, all high-risk maintenance must be performed during a controlled shutdown.

**Q: How do we determine if an asset requires more frequent maintenance than monthly?**
A: If an asset experiences frequent failures, shows consistent premature wear during monthly inspections, or is operated beyond its designed duty cycle, escalate to a bi-weekly maintenance schedule and perform a root-cause analysis (RCA).
