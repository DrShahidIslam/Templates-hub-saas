# Standard Operating Procedure: Administrative Assistant Onboarding

The goal of this onboarding SOP is to ensure that all new Administrative Assistants are fully integrated into the team, equipped with the necessary tools, and aligned with company culture within their first two weeks. A structured onboarding process minimizes administrative friction, sets clear performance expectations, and empowers the new hire to provide immediate, high-value support to the leadership team and the organization at large.

## Phase 1: Pre-Arrival & Systems Provisioning
*Ensure these tasks are completed at least 48 hours before the start date.*

* [ ] **Workspace Setup:** Prepare desk, ergonomic chair, and office supplies.
* [ ] **Hardware Provisioning:** Procure and configure laptop, monitor, keyboard, mouse, and phone.
* [ ] **Account Creation:** Provision email, Slack/Teams, project management software (Asana/Jira), and internal file storage (Google Drive/SharePoint).
* [ ] **Security Access:** Set up Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) and provide secure password manager credentials.
* [ ] **Welcome Email:** Send a detailed email containing the start time, arrival instructions, parking details, and a high-level schedule for Day 1.

## Phase 2: Day One Orientation
*Focus on company culture, logistics, and initial tool navigation.*

* [ ] **HR Documentation:** Complete tax forms, benefits enrollment, and sign the Employee Handbook.
* [ ] **Company Overview:** Conduct a briefing on the company mission, values, and organizational structure.
* [ ] **IT/Security Briefing:** Provide a walkthrough of cybersecurity protocols and internal data handling policies.
* [ ] **Office Tour:** Introduce the new hire to key stakeholders, kitchen facilities, and supply storage areas.
* [ ] **Lunch Integration:** Arrange a team lunch to facilitate relationship building.

## Phase 3: Role-Specific Training
*Focus on the core technical competencies required for administrative support.*

* [ ] **Calendar Management:** Review calendar access, executive preferences, and scheduling prioritization logic.
* [ ] **Communication Protocols:** Establish guidelines for phone etiquette, email draft expectations, and internal messaging tone.
* [ ] **File Management:** Review the filing structure, naming conventions, and document retention policies.
* [ ] **Software Deep-Dive:** Provide training on travel booking tools, expense reporting software (e.g., Concur/Expensify), and meeting management tools (Zoom/Teams).
* [ ] **Delegation Framework:** Clarify the specific tasks to be handled independently vs. tasks requiring managerial approval.

## Phase 4: Week One Review & Goal Setting
*Focus on alignment and feedback.*

* [ ] **Initial Check-in:** Discuss their experience during the first week and identify any blockers.
* [ ] **Performance Metrics:** Establish clear KPIs (e.g., response time, accuracy of meeting minutes, successful travel coordination).
* [ ] **Feedback Loop:** Create a recurring 1-on-1 meeting cadence to discuss ongoing administrative requirements.
* [ ] **Actionable Roadmap:** Define specific projects or administrative audits the assistant will own within the first 30 days.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

* **Pro Tip: The "Shadowing" Session.** Have the new assistant shadow an experienced team member during a complex task (like organizing a large offsite) to observe real-world decision-making.
* **Pro Tip: Documentation Audit.** Ask the new hire to document their process as they learn. It often highlights gaps in existing SOPs that veteran employees overlook.
* **Pitfall: Information Overload.** Avoid dumping all documentation and access credentials on Day 1. Pace the technical training to prevent "cognitive fatigue."
* **Pitfall: Assuming Cultural Alignment.** Don't assume the new hire understands the office "unwritten rules" (e.g., meeting punctuality, desk cleanliness, break etiquette). Be explicit.

## FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much autonomy should I give an Administrative Assistant in the first week?**
A: Start with high-supervision/low-autonomy tasks. Gradually increase autonomy once you have vetted their attention to detail and ability to follow your specific communication preferences.

**Q: Should I provide a list of VIP contacts immediately?**
A: Yes. Providing a "Who’s Who" directory—including internal executives and key external vendors—is critical to helping the assistant prioritize incoming requests effectively.

**Q: What is the most common reason for onboarding failure?**
A: Lack of clear communication regarding *expectations*. If an assistant does not know exactly how you like your meetings scheduled or your emails managed, they will rely on guesswork, which often leads to unnecessary friction.
