# Standard Operating Procedure: Commercial Roof Preventative Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for conducting semi-annual preventative maintenance inspections on commercial roofing systems. The objective of this procedure is to identify minor deficiencies—such as membrane punctures, sealant degradation, or drainage obstructions—before they escalate into catastrophic water intrusion events. Consistent adherence to this SOP will extend the service life of the roofing assembly, maintain manufacturer warranty compliance, and minimize long-term capital expenditure.

## Phase 1: Safety & Access Protocols
*   Ensure all personnel have completed Fall Protection training (OSHA 1926.501).
*   Verify all ladders, harness anchor points, and roof hatches are structurally sound before use.
*   Document weather conditions; do not perform inspections during active precipitation or high winds.
*   Establish a perimeter cordon if the roof edge is within 6 feet of the working area.

## Phase 2: Roof Perimeter & Vertical Surfaces
*   Inspect metal copings and edge flashings for loose fasteners, open seams, or structural shifting.
*   Check parapet walls for cracked masonry, loose mortar, or compromised sealants.
*   Examine counter-flashings for separation from the wall substrate.
*   Ensure all termination bars are secured and that the sealant covering the top edge is intact.

## Phase 3: Field Membrane & Surface Integrity
*   Walk the entire field of the roof to identify punctures, tears, or blisters in the membrane (TPO, EPDM, or PVC).
*   Check for "ponding" water areas; note locations where water remains stagnant 48 hours after rain.
*   Inspect seams for signs of "fishmouthing" or edge curling.
*   Examine areas under HVAC units for potential damage from service technician foot traffic.
*   Identify areas of excessive debris accumulation that may trap moisture or accelerate material degradation.

## Phase 4: Penetrations & Roof Accessories
*   Inspect all pipe boots and pitch pans for UV degradation or cracking in the sealant.
*   Verify that all HVAC curbs are properly flashed and that the mechanical units are not causing vibrations that could compromise seal integrity.
*   Check solar panel racking mounts (if applicable) for signs of loose bolts or membrane thinning.
*   Examine lightning protection systems to ensure cable clips are secure and not abrading the roof surface.

## Phase 5: Drainage & Scuppers
*   Clear all roof drains of leaves, sediment, and biological growth.
*   Ensure drain strainers (domes) are firmly attached and functioning correctly.
*   Inspect scupper openings for blockages and ensure the metal sleeve is securely sealed to the membrane.
*   Verify that interior downspouts or leader pipes are free-flowing by visually confirming discharge at the base.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **The "48-Hour Rule":** Always schedule your inspection 48 hours after the last significant rainfall. This allows for the identification of ponding areas and makes active leaks easier to spot through moisture staining on the underside of the deck or insulation.
*   **Document with Photos:** Always take "Before" and "After" photos of every repair. This creates an audit trail that is invaluable for insurance claims and manufacturer warranty disputes.
*   **Avoid the "Over-Seal" Pitfall:** Do not use cheap hardware store silicone over professional roofing membranes. Use only manufacturer-approved sealants compatible with your specific roofing chemistry (e.g., EPDM vs. TPO), or you risk permanent chemical damage to the membrane.
*   **Check the Log:** Always review the previous maintenance report before starting. Ensure that previously identified "watch items" have not progressed into active failures.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform this maintenance?**
A: We mandate a minimum of two inspections per year (typically spring and fall). Additionally, an inspection should be performed immediately following any major weather event involving hail or wind speeds exceeding 50 mph.

**Q: Can my internal janitorial staff perform these inspections?**
A: No. While janitorial staff can assist in debris removal, the inspection of flashings, seams, and penetrations requires the technical eye of a trained roofing professional or a certified facilities manager to prevent voiding the manufacturer's warranty.

**Q: What is the most common cause of commercial roof failure?**
A: Neglected drainage. A single clogged drain can lead to structural overload from ponding water, which quickly exploits minor seam defects that would otherwise remain dormant for years. Keep the drains clear.
