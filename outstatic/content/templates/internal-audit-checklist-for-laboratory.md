# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Laboratory Audit

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the requirements for conducting a comprehensive internal audit of laboratory operations. The objective of this audit is to verify compliance with ISO/IEC 17025 standards, internal quality management systems (QMS), and regulatory safety protocols. By systematically evaluating technical competence, documentation accuracy, and facility maintenance, the laboratory ensures the integrity of test results, minimizes operational risks, and maintains a state of continuous improvement.

## 1. Documentation and Quality Management System
*   **Document Control:** Verify that all active SOPs, test methods, and manuals are the current versions and are signed/dated appropriately.
*   **Records Integrity:** Ensure analytical records (raw data) are legible, unaltered, and protected against unauthorized access.
*   **Corrective Actions:** Review the log of non-conformities; verify that root cause analyses were performed and corrective actions were effectively implemented.
*   **Personnel Competency:** Audit training records, certificates of qualification, and annual competency assessments for all laboratory staff.

## 2. Equipment and Calibration
*   **Calibration Schedule:** Check that all critical instruments (balances, pipettes, centrifuges, etc.) have current calibration certificates traceable to national standards.
*   **Maintenance Logs:** Verify that preventive maintenance schedules are strictly adhered to and that maintenance logs are up to date.
*   **Verification Protocols:** Ensure that daily or periodic performance checks (e.g., balance check weights, temperature monitoring) are recorded without gaps.
*   **Out-of-Service Protocol:** Confirm that defective or out-of-calibration equipment is clearly tagged and quarantined to prevent accidental use.

## 3. Sample Management and Chain of Custody
*   **Receipt and Logging:** Verify that all incoming samples are assigned unique identifiers and documented in the Laboratory Information Management System (LIMS).
*   **Storage Conditions:** Audit storage areas to ensure samples are kept at specified environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, light exposure).
*   **Disposal:** Check that sample disposal procedures comply with environmental regulations and internal retention policies.
*   **Traceability:** Confirm the chain of custody from sample receipt through analysis to final report issuance.

## 4. Laboratory Environment and Safety
*   **Environmental Controls:** Review charts for ambient temperature, humidity, and pressure monitoring in the laboratory, ensuring they remain within defined tolerance limits.
*   **Housekeeping:** Inspect workbenches and storage areas for cleanliness and prevention of cross-contamination.
*   **Safety Equipment:** Verify the inspection status of eyewash stations, safety showers, fire extinguishers, and spill kits.
*   **Hazardous Materials:** Ensure all chemicals are correctly labeled (GHS compliance) and that Safety Data Sheets (SDS) are easily accessible.

## 5. Method Validation and Analytical Quality
*   **Method Adherence:** Observe analysts to ensure they are following approved methods exactly as written in the SOPs.
*   **Reagent Integrity:** Check that reagents are within their expiration date, properly labeled (date opened/expiry), and stored correctly.
*   **Quality Control (QC):** Review control charts for reference standards and blanks to ensure the laboratory is maintaining statistical process control.
*   **Proficiency Testing:** Audit participation records for inter-laboratory comparisons and proficiency testing programs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Perform "vertical audits" where you select one sample or report at random and trace it from receipt, through analysis and equipment logs, to the final report. This is the most effective way to identify process gaps.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an unannounced "spot check" approach for safety and housekeeping to get a realistic view of daily compliance versus "prepared" audit behavior.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid the "check-the-box" mentality. An audit should be an investigation, not just a verification of signatures. If a record looks perfect but doesn't make sense, investigate further.
*   **Pitfall:** Don't ignore the "soft" aspects of the lab culture, such as staff hesitation to report errors. A lack of non-conformity reports (NCRs) is often a sign of a toxic culture, not a perfect lab.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should an internal audit be conducted?**
A: According to ISO/IEC 17025 standards, internal audits must be conducted on a planned schedule, typically annually, or whenever there are significant changes to the laboratory operations, staffing, or QMS.

**Q: What should I do if I find a major non-conformity?**
A: A major non-conformity must be documented immediately in an NCR. The lab manager should implement an immediate containment action, perform a formal root cause analysis, and establish a timeline for corrective action before the audit report is finalized.

**Q: Should the laboratory staff be notified of the audit dates?**
A: It is recommended to announce audit dates in advance to ensure key personnel are available for interviews. However, specific areas of focus or specific samples selected for "traceability checks" should remain confidential until the moment of the audit to ensure the results reflect standard day-to-day operations.
