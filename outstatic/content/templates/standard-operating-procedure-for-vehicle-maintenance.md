# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Fleet Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for maintaining organizational vehicles to ensure operational safety, regulatory compliance, and the extension of asset lifecycle. By adhering to this structured maintenance regimen, the operations team mitigates the risk of mechanical failure, minimizes unplanned downtime, and reduces long-term capital expenditure through proactive care. All personnel responsible for vehicle operations or fleet oversight are required to follow these procedures strictly.

## Phase 1: Pre-Trip and Daily Inspections
Before a vehicle enters service for the day, the assigned operator must perform a visual assessment.
*   **Fluid Check:** Verify engine oil, coolant, and windshield washer fluid levels are within manufacturer-specified ranges.
*   **Tire Condition:** Check tire pressure (including spare) and inspect tread depth for signs of uneven wear or debris.
*   **Lighting Systems:** Test headlights, high beams, turn signals, brake lights, and hazard lights.
*   **Dashboard Warnings:** Confirm no "Check Engine" or alert icons remain illuminated upon ignition.
*   **Damage Assessment:** Walk around the vehicle to document any new scratches, dents, or broken glass that were not present previously.

## Phase 2: Scheduled Preventative Maintenance (PM)
Preventative maintenance must be scheduled based on manufacturer mileage intervals or specific calendar dates, whichever occurs first.
*   **Oil and Filter Service:** Drain and replace engine oil and oil filter; ensure the use of the correct viscosity grade.
*   **Fluid Flushes:** Perform scheduled brake fluid, transmission fluid, and coolant flushes per the owner's manual specifications.
*   **Braking System:** Inspect brake pad thickness, rotors/drums for scoring, and check brake lines for leaks or corrosion.
*   **Filter Replacements:** Replace air filters and cabin air filters to maintain engine efficiency and air quality.
*   **Battery Health:** Test battery voltage and clean terminal connections to prevent sulfation and power loss.

## Phase 3: Documentation and Record Keeping
Rigorous documentation is required for audit purposes and to maintain the resale value of the fleet.
*   **Service Logs:** Record the date, mileage, service performed, parts used, and the technician’s identification in the fleet management database.
*   **Receipt Archiving:** Attach digital scans of all third-party invoices to the specific vehicle profile in the management system.
*   **Maintenance Alerts:** Set automated reminders for upcoming services based on forecasted usage data.
*   **Driver Reports:** Maintain a digital file of all daily inspection sheets submitted by operators for trend analysis.

## Phase 4: Emergency Maintenance Protocol
In the event of an unplanned breakdown, the following steps must be taken to ensure safety and continuity.
*   **Safety Protocol:** If the vehicle is immobile, the driver must move to a safe location, engage hazard lights, and deploy emergency triangles.
*   **Emergency Contact:** Notify the Operations Manager immediately with the vehicle ID, exact location, and a brief description of the symptoms (e.g., strange noises, loss of power).
*   **Towing Coordination:** Use only approved, insured, and verified towing vendors to transport the vehicle to the designated service center.
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** Post-repair, the maintenance lead must document the failure point to determine if the issue was preventable.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "color-coded" scheduling system (e.g., Red for urgent repairs, Yellow for upcoming service, Green for fully operational) to visualize fleet readiness at a glance.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always use OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) parts whenever possible; they carry longer warranties and ensure peak performance compared to generic aftermarket alternatives.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "minor" noises. A squealing brake pad or a light engine rattle is often a precursor to a catastrophic and expensive failure. Address these immediately.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-reliance on "Wait until it breaks" (reactive maintenance). This increases total cost of ownership by 30-50% compared to proactive preventative scheduling.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should we update our fleet maintenance schedule?**
A: You should review and adjust your maintenance schedules annually or whenever the manufacturer issues updated service bulletins or technical service updates.

**Q: Who is authorized to sign off on vehicle repairs?**
A: Only the designated Fleet Manager or a certified technician/third-party service center manager may sign off on repairs, confirming the vehicle is safe to return to the road.

**Q: What should I do if a driver reports a safety issue, but the dashboard shows no warning lights?**
A: Treat the driver's report as a high-priority alert. Human observation often detects mechanical issues (vibration, smells, handling changes) before electronic sensors trigger a dashboard alert. Remove the vehicle from service for a manual diagnostic test.
