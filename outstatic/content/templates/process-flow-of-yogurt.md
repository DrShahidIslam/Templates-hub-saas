# Standard Operating Procedure: Industrial Yogurt Production

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized industrial process for yogurt manufacturing, ensuring consistent quality, food safety, and compliance with regulatory hygiene standards. The process flow encompasses milk standardization, thermal treatment, fermentation, and packaging. Adherence to these protocols is critical to maintaining product texture, acidity, and shelf-life stability.

## 1. Preparation and Standardization
*   **Milk Reception:** Verify the temperature of raw milk upon arrival (must be ≤ 4°C). Conduct antibiotic residue and organoleptic testing.
*   **Clarification/Separation:** Pass milk through a centrifugal separator to remove impurities and standardize the fat content to the specific product formulation.
*   **Dry Ingredient Incorporation:** Add stabilizers, sweeteners, or milk solids non-fat (MSNF) in a high-shear mixer to ensure complete dissolution and prevent graininess.

## 2. Thermal Treatment and Homogenization
*   **Pre-heating:** Heat the standardized milk mixture to 60–65°C to facilitate the efficiency of homogenization.
*   **Homogenization:** Subject the mixture to 2000–2500 psi to break down fat globules, preventing cream separation and improving the viscosity of the final curd.
*   **Pasteurization (Heat Treatment):** Heat to 90–95°C and hold for 5–10 minutes. This denatures whey proteins (specifically lactoglobulin), which is essential for creating a firm gel structure.

## 3. Fermentation and Incubation
*   **Cooling:** Rapidly cool the milk base to the inoculation temperature (40–45°C) to provide an optimal environment for bacterial cultures.
*   **Inoculation:** Add the specific starter culture (typically *Lactobacillus bulgaricus* and *Streptococcus thermophilus*). Use a sterile dosing pump to ensure uniform distribution.
*   **Incubation:** Hold the mixture at 42–44°C in a temperature-controlled vessel. Monitor pH levels closely; the process is complete when the target pH (usually 4.5–4.6) is reached.

## 4. Cooling and Final Processing
*   **Breaking the Curd:** For stirred-style yogurt, gently agitate the coagulated mass to achieve the desired viscosity.
*   **Cooling:** Cool the product to 15–20°C immediately upon reaching the target pH to halt bacterial fermentation and prevent excessive acidity.
*   **Fruit/Flavor Injection:** Inject fruit preparations or flavorings using an aseptic dosing system into the cooled yogurt base.

## 5. Packaging and Storage
*   **Filling:** Ensure all filling equipment is sanitized via Clean-in-Place (CIP) systems prior to use. Monitor fill weights to ensure consistency.
*   **Sealing and Labeling:** Apply induction seals to cups and verify that lot codes, expiration dates, and ingredient labels are legible and accurate.
*   **Cold Chain Storage:** Move final product to a storage facility maintained at 2–4°C to ensure product stability.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a strict culture rotation schedule to prevent bacteriophage attacks, which can inhibit fermentation and lead to batch failure.
*   **Pitfall - Over-incubation:** Allowing the pH to drop below 4.4 will lead to syneresis (wheying off), resulting in a thin, overly tart product with poor consumer appeal.
*   **Pitfall - Temperature Fluctuations:** Inconsistent incubation temperatures will result in uneven texture and potential mold/yeast contamination if the target acidity is not achieved quickly enough.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How do I prevent the yogurt from becoming too watery?**
A: Ensure your milk solids content is sufficient (typically 12-14% total solids) and verify that the pasteurization temperature reaches at least 90°C to properly denature whey proteins, which helps bind water in the gel matrix.

**Q: Why is my yogurt batch testing positive for high acidity?**
A: This is often caused by leaving the yogurt in the incubation phase for too long or failing to cool the product rapidly enough once the target pH of 4.6 is reached.

**Q: Can I reuse the starter culture from the previous batch?**
A: It is strongly recommended against in a professional environment. Reusing cultures ("back-slopping") increases the risk of contamination and genetic drift, leading to inconsistent viscosity and flavor profiles. Always use high-quality commercial starter kits.
