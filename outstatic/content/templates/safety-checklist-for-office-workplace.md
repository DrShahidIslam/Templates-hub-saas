# Standard Operating Procedure: Office Workplace Safety Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the requirements for conducting comprehensive safety inspections within the office environment. The objective of this protocol is to identify potential hazards, ensure compliance with Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) regulations, and maintain a secure, ergonomic workspace for all employees. Regular inspections are mandatory to mitigate liability, prevent workplace injuries, and ensure that safety equipment remains fully functional and accessible.

## Section 1: Physical Environment and Walkways
*   **Trip Hazards:** Ensure all corridors and walkways are free of loose cables, boxes, stationery, or personal items.
*   **Floor Integrity:** Inspect carpets for fraying, curling edges, or loose tiles that could cause a tripping accident.
*   **Lighting:** Verify that all overhead lights are functional. Replace flickering bulbs or faulty ballasts immediately to prevent eye strain.
*   **Obstructions:** Confirm that all exits and paths to emergency routes remain completely unobstructed by furniture or equipment.

## Section 2: Electrical and Fire Safety
*   **Power Strips:** Check that power strips are not daisy-chained. Ensure they are plugged directly into wall outlets and are not overloaded.
*   **Cables:** Examine power cords for signs of fraying, cracking, or exposed wiring. Replace damaged equipment immediately.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Confirm fire extinguishers are mounted correctly, the pressure gauge is in the "green" zone, and the inspection tag is current.
*   **Smoke Detectors:** Perform monthly testing on detectors to ensure the alarm signal is audible and battery levels are sufficient.

## Section 3: Ergonomics and Workstation Setup
*   **Chair Stability:** Inspect office chairs for structural integrity, including wheel locks and height adjustment mechanisms.
*   **Monitor Height:** Ensure monitors are positioned at eye level to prevent neck strain; verify that screen glare is minimized.
*   **Keyboard/Mouse Placement:** Confirm that workstations allow for a neutral wrist position to reduce the risk of repetitive strain injuries (RSI).
*   **Desk Load:** Ensure heavy items are stored in bottom drawers to prevent the cabinet from tipping over when opened.

## Section 4: Emergency Preparedness
*   **First Aid Kit:** Audit the contents of all first aid kits to ensure all supplies are within their expiration dates and fully stocked.
*   **Evacuation Map:** Verify that evacuation floor plans are posted in prominent, visible locations and clearly indicate the nearest emergency exit.
*   **Communication:** Confirm that all staff are aware of the designated emergency assembly point outside the building.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Delegate the inspection process to different department leads on a rotating basis. This promotes a "safety culture" and ensures that different areas of the office receive a fresh pair of eyes every month.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a mobile-based form (like Google Forms or a dedicated safety app) to digitize these checklists. This allows you to track recurring issues and generate trend reports over time.
*   **Pitfall - The "Check-the-Box" Mentality:** Do not simply mark items as "passed" without physically inspecting them. Ensure staff understand that this is a risk-mitigation tool, not just administrative paperwork.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring "Minor" Hazards:** A loose carpet corner or a slightly frayed cable may seem trivial, but these are the most common sources of injury. Address small issues immediately rather than waiting for a scheduled quarterly review.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**How often should these safety inspections be performed?**
For a standard administrative office environment, a comprehensive physical inspection should be conducted monthly. High-traffic areas (like kitchens or mailrooms) should ideally be checked weekly.

**What should I do if I identify a major hazard that cannot be fixed immediately?**
Isolate the area. If a hazard (e.g., a broken chair or exposed wiring) poses an immediate risk, place a "Do Not Use" sign on the object, notify the relevant department, and cordon off the area until repairs are completed.

**Who is responsible for the safety of the office?**
While the Facilities or Operations Manager owns the process, office safety is a shared responsibility. Every employee is expected to maintain their own workstation and report hazards to management as soon as they are identified.
