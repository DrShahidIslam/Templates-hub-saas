# Standard Operating Procedure: Rental Move-In/Move-Out Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for tenants to conduct a thorough property inspection. The primary objective is to document the precise condition of the rental unit at the start of a lease, protecting the tenant’s security deposit and ensuring accountability for any pre-existing damages. This document should be completed alongside the landlord or property manager whenever possible, and results must be submitted in writing within the timeframe specified by your lease agreement (typically 48–72 hours).

## Room-by-Room Inspection Checklist

### General Interior & Entry
*   **Doors:** Test all locks, deadbolts, and handles. Check for proper latching and alignment.
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for stains, tears, scratches, or loose tiles/boards.
*   **Walls/Ceilings:** Note any holes, gouges, water stains, or peeling paint.
*   **Windows:** Ensure all open, close, and lock securely. Check screens for tears.
*   **Electrical:** Test every outlet (using a phone charger or outlet tester) and toggle all light switches.

### Kitchen & Appliances
*   **Appliances:** Run the dishwasher through a short cycle; test all stove burners and the oven temperature; check that the refrigerator/freezer are cooling and seals are intact.
*   **Plumbing:** Check under the sink for active leaks or signs of water damage; test hot and cold water pressure.
*   **Cabinetry:** Open and close all drawers and cabinets to ensure hinges and tracks function smoothly.
*   **Surfaces:** Inspect countertops for chips, burns, or deep scratches.

### Bathroom(s)
*   **Toilet:** Flush to ensure full power and check for leaks at the base.
*   **Shower/Tub:** Test water pressure and drainage speed. Look for mold or damaged caulking.
*   **Ventilation:** Turn on exhaust fans to ensure they are functional and pulling air.
*   **Fixtures:** Inspect faucets and showerheads for corrosion or mineral buildup.

### Safety Systems
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test every unit by pressing the "test" button. Note the expiration date on the back of each unit.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Ensure the gauge is in the "green" zone and the safety pin is intact.
*   **Emergency Exits:** Verify that all secondary egress windows or doors are unobstructed.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **The Power of Media:** Take high-resolution photos and videos of everything, especially pre-existing damage. Use a timestamp app to ensure the files are dated.
*   **The "Slow-Motion" Walkthrough:** Do not rush. If the landlord is present, ask them to wait while you inspect each item. If they try to hurry you, remind them that this protects both parties.
*   **Email the Results:** Always submit your completed inspection checklist via email. This creates a permanent, time-stamped digital paper trail that is difficult to dispute later.

### Common Pitfalls
*   **Ignoring Small Defects:** Tenants often ignore small scratches or carpet stains, assuming they are "too small to matter." Landlords may charge for these at the end of the lease; document them now to avoid liability.
*   **Verbal Agreements:** Avoid relying on verbal promises such as "I’ll have that fixed next week." Always get maintenance requests or repairs in writing.
*   **Skipping Hidden Areas:** Don’t forget to look inside the oven, behind the refrigerator, and inside the HVAC filter compartment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What happens if I find an issue that wasn't on the move-in form?**
If you discover a defect after moving in, notify your landlord immediately via written communication. Do not wait for a maintenance request; state clearly that this is an addendum to your move-in inspection.

**2. Should I fix minor damages myself?**
Generally, no. Unless your lease specifically permits tenant repairs, you risk damaging the property further or voiding the landlord's insurance/warranty on appliances. Always report issues to the property manager.

**3. Does this inspection protect me from normal wear and tear?**
The inspection document is used to differentiate between "wear and tear" (which is expected) and "damage" (which is actionable). Documenting the condition prevents the landlord from labeling legitimate wear and tear as new damage caused by your residency.
