# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Property Inspection

Effective property evaluation requires a systematic approach to move beyond superficial aesthetics and identify structural, mechanical, and functional realities. This SOP outlines the professional standard for conducting a house viewing to ensure data-driven decision-making, mitigate financial risk, and prevent "buyer’s remorse." Use this procedure to document findings consistently across multiple viewings.

## Phase 1: Exterior and Structural Integrity
*   **Roofline:** Check for sagging, missing shingles, or misaligned gutters. Look for signs of water pooling or improper drainage.
*   **Foundation:** Scan for horizontal or stair-step cracks in the brickwork or concrete. Note any settling that causes doors to stick or floors to slope.
*   **Windows & Frames:** Inspect for rot, seal failure (fogged glass), and functional locking mechanisms.
*   **Exterior Siding/Masonry:** Look for cracks, signs of moisture intrusion, or bubbling paint which may indicate underlying wood rot.
*   **Landscaping & Drainage:** Verify that the ground slopes *away* from the house foundation to prevent basement water ingress.

## Phase 2: Interior Systems and Utilities
*   **Electrical Panel:** Open the panel (safely) to check for a modern breaker system rather than outdated fuses. Note if the wiring is copper or aluminum.
*   **HVAC:** Check the age of the furnace and AC condenser unit. Listen for unusual rattling or humming.
*   **Plumbing:** Flush all toilets and run taps simultaneously in the kitchen and bathrooms to check for consistent water pressure and drainage speed.
*   **Water Heater:** Note the manufacture date. If it is older than 10 years, anticipate a replacement cost.
*   **Attic & Crawlspace:** Check for insulation depth, signs of past leaks (water staining on rafters), and evidence of pests (droppings or chewed wiring).

## Phase 3: Interior Finishes and Functional Flow
*   **Moisture/Mildew:** Check corners of ceilings and closets for dampness or a musty odor. This is a critical indicator of mold or poor ventilation.
*   **Floor Condition:** Identify soft spots in subflooring, particularly near bathrooms and kitchens.
*   **Ceilings:** Look for hairline cracks or yellowing, which often indicate recurring roof leaks or pipe sweating.
*   **Storage Capacity:** Evaluate if the current closets and pantry space meet your lifestyle requirements.
*   **Natural Light:** Assess window orientation to determine how much light the home receives at different times of the day.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "Sensory Check":** Turn off your phone and music. Stand still for a full minute. Listen for HVAC noise, traffic vibrations, or thin walls. Smell for dampness or masking agents like heavy air fresheners.
*   **Don't Ignore the "Staging":** Do not let high-end furniture or fresh paint distract you. Look *past* the decor. Professional staging is designed to appeal to emotions; your job is to appeal to your logic.
*   **The Neighborhood Test:** Visit the property twice—once during the day and once in the evening (ideally on a Friday or Saturday night). A quiet street at 10 AM can be a hub of noise at 10 PM.
*   **Pitfall - The "Quick Fix" Trap:** Never assume a cosmetic issue (like a stained ceiling) is just a "quick fix." Assume it is the symptom of a larger, expensive problem until proven otherwise by a home inspector.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I bring a flashlight if the house has lights?**
A: Yes. Use a high-lumen flashlight to inspect dark corners, the back of the furnace, and the underside of cabinets. Natural light is often insufficient to catch structural defects.

**Q: Is it acceptable to take photos or videos?**
A: Absolutely. With the owner's or agent's permission, document everything. You will likely visit multiple properties; video walkthroughs are essential for refreshing your memory later in the day.

**Q: How do I identify if the neighborhood is a "good" investment?**
A: Look for signs of pride of ownership in surrounding properties—manicured lawns, well-maintained exteriors, and a lack of abandoned vehicles. These are high-level indicators of a stable or appreciating neighborhood.
