# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC Preventative Maintenance

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for performing comprehensive preventative maintenance on HVAC systems. The objective of this procedure is to ensure system longevity, optimize energy efficiency, maintain indoor air quality (IAQ), and mitigate the risk of catastrophic mechanical failure. All technicians are required to follow these steps systematically, document findings, and submit a digital report upon completion. Adherence to this SOP ensures compliance with manufacturer specifications and industry standards for climate control reliability.

## HVAC Preventative Maintenance Checklist

## Phase 1: Electrical and Safety Inspection
*   **Disconnect Verification:** Confirm all power disconnects are locked out and tagged out (LOTO) before accessing internal components.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Inspect all terminal blocks, contactors, and relays. Tighten loose wiring and replace components showing signs of heat discoloration or pitting.
*   **Voltage/Amperage Testing:** Measure and record voltage and amperage for the compressor and fan motors; ensure readings remain within the manufacturer’s specified range (±10%).
*   **Capacitor Testing:** Check microfarad ratings on all run/start capacitors; replace any unit that tests below 10% of its rated value.

## Phase 2: Refrigeration and Cooling Cycle
*   **Refrigerant Levels:** Monitor system pressures (suction and liquid lines) to ensure proper subcooling and superheat levels.
*   **Leak Detection:** Conduct a visual inspection of all flare nuts, service valves, and coil return bends for oil residue, which indicates a refrigerant leak.
*   **Coil Cleaning:** Inspect the evaporator and condenser coils. Clean with an approved non-acidic coil cleaner to remove debris, oxidation, and biological growth.
*   **Drain System:** Flush the condensate drain line with algaecide or high-pressure air/water to prevent blockages and water damage.

## Phase 3: Airflow and Mechanical Components
*   **Filter Management:** Replace all air filters; note the MERV rating and verify the flow direction arrow matches the system airflow.
*   **Blower Assembly:** Clean the blower wheel to prevent imbalance and check for bearing noise or excessive play in the motor shaft.
*   **Belt Maintenance:** If belt-driven, check tension and alignment. Replace belts showing signs of cracking, glazing, or fraying.
*   **Ductwork Inspection:** Perform a visual inspection of visible transitions and plenums to identify air leaks or disconnected insulation.

## Phase 4: System Calibration and Testing
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Verify the thermostat accuracy and check for correct staging of heating/cooling cycles.
*   **Safety Controls:** Test high-pressure, low-pressure, and float switches to ensure the unit safely shuts down during abnormal operating conditions.
*   **Operational Run Test:** Operate the system through its full sequence of operation (Heating, Cooling, Fan only) to confirm stable performance.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always take "Before" and "After" photos of the coils and filters. This provides tangible proof of service to the client and validates billing.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an infrared thermometer to verify temperature split (Delta T) across the evaporator coil; it is the fastest way to confirm system health.
*   **Pitfall - Over-tightening:** Avoid overtightening electrical terminals, as this can crack plastic housings or strip threads, leading to intermittent power failures.
*   **Pitfall - Chemical Damage:** Never use high-pressure power washers on delicate aluminum fin coils, as this will flatten the fins and permanently restrict airflow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should preventative maintenance be performed?**
A: Industry standards dictate a minimum of twice per year—once before the cooling season and once before the heating season—to ensure the system is optimized for peak load.

**Q: Why does the system fail even after maintenance?**
A: Maintenance identifies existing mechanical health; however, it cannot predict the exact moment a motor or contactor will fail due to unforeseen power surges or manufacturing defects.

**Q: What should I do if the system is low on refrigerant?**
A: Do not simply "top off" the refrigerant. Refrigerant is a sealed loop; low levels indicate a leak. You must find and repair the leak before recharging the system to comply with environmental regulations (EPA Section 608).
