# Standard Operating Procedure: Insurance Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements for conducting a property inspection for insurance purposes. The objective is to document the current condition of the asset, verify risk factors, and ensure compliance with underwriting guidelines. Accurate reporting mitigates liability, prevents coverage disputes, and ensures the valuation remains aligned with the physical state of the property.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   **Documentation Review:** Review the policy application or existing file to identify specific areas of concern noted by the underwriter.
*   **Safety Gear:** Ensure possession of personal protective equipment (PPE) including high-visibility vest, safety glasses, and sturdy, non-slip footwear.
*   **Tools:** Verify that all equipment is functional: digital camera/smartphone, flashlight, moisture meter, ladder, and a clipboard or tablet with the inspection template.
*   **Communication:** Confirm the appointment time with the policyholder or property manager at least 24 hours in advance.

## Phase 2: Exterior Inspection
*   **Roof Assessment:** Inspect roof material for missing shingles, moss, oxidation, or structural sagging. Note the approximate age of the roof.
*   **Siding and Trim:** Check for cracks, rot, moisture intrusion, or damage caused by vegetation or pests.
*   **Foundation:** Document visible cracks (horizontal vs. vertical), uneven settling, or proximity to tree roots.
*   **Site Hazards:** Identify potential liability risks such as unfenced pools, uneven walkways, debris, or dangerous trees.
*   **Utilities:** Verify condition of external electrical panels, HVAC units, and secondary structures (sheds, detached garages).

## Phase 3: Interior Inspection
*   **Electrical System:** Check for outdated wiring (knob-and-tube or aluminum), overloaded panels, or improper grounding.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect under sinks and behind appliances for signs of active leaks, water stains, or mold growth.
*   **HVAC:** Note the age and condition of the furnace, water heater, and central AC unit.
*   **Safety Features:** Verify the presence and functionality of smoke detectors, carbon monoxide detectors, and fire extinguishers.
*   **General Housekeeping:** Document excessive clutter, hazardous materials storage, or signs of pest infestation.

## Phase 4: Documentation and Reporting
*   **Photography:** Capture high-resolution photos of all four elevations of the structure, specific damages, and high-value internal systems.
*   **Standardized Notes:** Input all findings into the insurance portal immediately while data is fresh.
*   **Verification:** Ensure all digital files are backed up and geotagged according to company data retention policies.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always carry a business card and identification. Property owners are more cooperative when they understand your role is to verify their coverage, not to find reasons to cancel it.
*   **Pro Tip:** If you encounter a restricted area (locked room/basement), document the refusal clearly and notify your supervisor immediately; do not force access.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not provide verbal opinions on whether a defect will cause a claim denial. Use neutral language such as "noted for underwriting review."
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "blind spots." If you cannot see an area (e.g., a steep roof), use a drone or note it as "inaccessible" rather than guessing the condition.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should I do if the policyholder refuses access to a specific room?**
Document the specific area that was inaccessible, the reason provided by the policyholder, and the time of the refusal. Report this to the underwriting department immediately for further instruction.

**2. Is a photo of a water stain sufficient evidence of a leak?**
A photo is the starting point, but you must also use a moisture meter to determine if the stain is active. Always record the moisture percentage and the location relative to known plumbing paths.

**3. What is the priority if I find an immediate life-safety hazard?**
Safety takes precedence over documentation. If you discover a severe risk (e.g., exposed high-voltage wiring, gas leak), notify the owner immediately, document the incident, and follow your company’s "Immediate Risk" protocol for escalating the issue to the insurer.
