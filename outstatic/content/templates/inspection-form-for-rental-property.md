# Standard Operating Procedure: Rental Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements for conducting comprehensive rental property inspections. The objective is to maintain property asset value, ensure tenant compliance with lease agreements, and document the physical state of the premises for liability protection. This protocol applies to move-in, routine periodic, and move-out inspections. All inspections must be documented with time-stamped, high-resolution photography and, where applicable, video evidence.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Review the original move-in inspection report and previous periodic inspection notes.
*   Notify the tenant in writing at least 24–48 hours in advance (or per local jurisdiction law).
*   Ensure the inspection kit is ready: mobile device/tablet with property management software, backup battery, flashlight, and PPE (gloves/shoe covers).
*   Verify all keys and access codes are functional.

## Phase 2: Interior Inspection Checklist

## Exterior & Entry
*   Verify condition of entry doors, locks, and deadbolts.
*   Inspect windows for cracks, damaged screens, or broken latches.
*   Check siding, paint, and masonry for signs of weather damage or water intrusion.
*   Examine gutters, downspouts, and perimeter drainage for blockages.
*   Note condition of walkways, driveways, and landscaping (ensure no unauthorized modifications).

## Kitchen & Utility Areas
*   Test all appliances (stove burners, oven heat, refrigerator cooling, dishwasher cycles).
*   Run faucets and inspect under-sink cabinetry for plumbing leaks or signs of water damage (mold/mildew).
*   Check countertops and backsplash for heat damage or structural cracks.
*   Confirm operation of garbage disposal and ventilation fan.

## Living & Sleeping Quarters
*   Inspect flooring (carpets, hardwoods, tile) for stains, tears, or excessive wear.
*   Test all light switches, ceiling fans, and wall outlets.
*   Examine walls and ceilings for hairline cracks, water stains, or unauthorized paint colors.
*   Check interior doors for sticking, broken handles, or damage to trim/molding.

## HVAC & Safety Systems
*   Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors (replace batteries if necessary).
*   Verify HVAC unit is operating and note the condition of air intake filters.
*   Check water heater for signs of rust or leaking at the base.
*   Ensure fire extinguisher is present, accessible, and the gauge is in the green zone.

## Phase 3: Post-Inspection Documentation
*   Upload all high-resolution media to the centralized management database.
*   Flag any items requiring maintenance (Minor = Schedule vendor; Major = Immediate repair).
*   Generate the official inspection report and share a copy with the tenant if required by lease or local regulation.
*   Reconcile the report against the "Security Deposit Deduction" criteria if performing a move-out inspection.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip (The Ceiling Scan):** Always look up at the ceiling while walking through a room. Water stains are often missed because they are located in corners or behind light fixtures.
*   **Pro Tip (The Photo Strategy):** Take "macro" photos of damage (close-ups) and "micro" photos of the surrounding area (context). A photo of a broken wall without context is harder to document as evidence.
*   **Pitfall (Assumption of Functionality):** Never assume an appliance works because it looks clean. Always turn it on and listen/feel for proper operation.
*   **Pitfall (Hasty Inspections):** Failing to document wear-and-tear vs. tenant damage. Ensure you clearly distinguish between the two to avoid legal disputes during security deposit returns.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I distinguish between "normal wear and tear" and "tenant damage"?**
A: Normal wear and tear is the expected, gradual deterioration of a property over time (e.g., slight fading of paint). Tenant damage is destruction beyond normal use (e.g., holes in drywall, pet stains, or broken window glass).

**Q: Am I legally allowed to take photos of a tenant's personal belongings?**
A: You should avoid photographing a tenant’s private, personal items unless they are obstructing your view of the property structure. Focus exclusively on the condition of the floors, walls, ceilings, and fixtures.

**Q: What is the most critical step if I discover a major maintenance issue during an inspection?**
A: Document the damage with clear photography, notify the tenant immediately of the repair timeline if it affects habitability, and issue a work order to your maintenance team within 24 hours.
