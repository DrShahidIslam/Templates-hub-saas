# Standard Operating Procedure: Safety Footwear Compliance and Maintenance

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements, selection criteria, and maintenance protocols for safety footwear within our facility. The primary objective of this policy is to mitigate risks associated with crush injuries, punctures, slips, trips, falls, and electrical hazards. Adherence to these guidelines is a condition of employment for all personnel operating within designated hazardous zones. Failure to comply with these standards compromises site safety and will result in disciplinary action.

## 1. Selection and Procurement
*   **Identify Hazard Exposure:** Conduct a formal risk assessment of the work area to determine the required protection rating (e.g., impact resistance, puncture resistance, electrical hazard (EH) rating, or chemical resistance).
*   **Verification of Standards:** Ensure all footwear carries a visible stamp confirming compliance with local regulatory standards (e.g., ASTM F2413 in the US or EN ISO 20345 in Europe).
*   **Proper Sizing:** Conduct fittings at the end of a shift when feet are at their largest to ensure adequate room for toe movement and to prevent blisters.
*   **Ergonomic Suitability:** Evaluate weight, arch support, and flexibility to ensure the shoe is suitable for the duration of the shift and the nature of the terrain.

## 2. Daily Inspection Protocol
*   **Visual Check:** Inspect the exterior leather or synthetic material for deep cuts, tears, or separation from the sole.
*   **Sole Integrity:** Examine the tread depth; if the tread is worn smooth or there is evidence of embedded debris (glass, metal shards, or oils), the shoe must be cleaned or replaced.
*   **Internal Inspection:** Reach inside the shoe to ensure the lining is intact and that no nails, staples, or rigid interior components are protruding toward the foot.
*   **Fastening Systems:** Verify that laces, eyelets, or straps are fully functional and do not pose a tripping hazard (ensure laces are tucked away or trimmed to appropriate lengths).

## 3. Maintenance and Hygiene
*   **Cleaning Routine:** Remove mud, chemicals, or debris daily using a stiff brush and water; avoid using harsh solvents that may degrade the integrity of the material.
*   **Drying Procedures:** Air-dry boots at room temperature. Never use direct heat sources (radiators, industrial blowers) as this causes leather to crack and weakens adhesive bonds.
*   **Conditioning:** Apply leather conditioner periodically to maintain suppleness and prevent cracking.
*   **Liner Replacement:** Replace insoles every 3–6 months or when support has visibly flattened to maintain ergonomic health and prevent plantar fasciitis.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Rotate two pairs of safety boots if you work in damp conditions. Giving a pair 24 hours to dry thoroughly prevents fungal growth and extends the life of the footwear.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use anti-fatigue insoles if your role involves standing on concrete for more than 4 hours continuously.
*   **Pitfall:** Never wear "retired" safety shoes. Once a boot has sustained a heavy impact (e.g., a steel toe absorbing a drop), the internal structure is compromised and the shoe must be discarded, even if it looks undamaged.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "DIY" repairs. Do not use duct tape or epoxy to patch holes in protective footwear, as this destroys the material's fire, chemical, or impact-resistant properties.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I replace my safety shoes?**
A: Under standard daily wear, safety shoes should be replaced every 6 to 12 months. However, if the protective toe cap is dented or the sole is compromised by chemicals or heavy wear, they must be replaced immediately, regardless of age.

**Q: Can I use aftermarket insoles in my safety-rated footwear?**
A: Yes, provided the insole does not alter the fit of the boot in a way that causes pressure points. Ensure that any replacement insole does not negatively impact the electrical hazard (EH) or static-dissipative (SD) rating of the boot.

**Q: What should I do if my safety shoes become saturated with a chemical?**
A: Immediately remove the footwear and decontaminate per the facility’s chemical safety guidelines. If the shoe material has absorbed the chemical, it must be disposed of as hazardous waste, as chemical degradation can render the protective layers ineffective.
