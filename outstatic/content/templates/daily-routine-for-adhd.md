# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Executive Function Management (ADHD)

This SOP is designed to provide a structured, low-friction framework for managing daily tasks for individuals with ADHD. By externalizing cognitive load into a repeatable system, this routine minimizes decision fatigue, reduces the impact of executive dysfunction, and creates consistent "anchor points" throughout the day. Adherence to this protocol is intended to prioritize momentum over perfection, focusing on system maintenance rather than task mastery.

## Section 1: The Morning Launch Protocol
*Objective: Transition from sleep to task-readiness with minimal executive decision-making.*

* [ ] **Physical Reset:** Immediately consume 16oz of water and engage in 2 minutes of light movement to initiate circadian regulation.
* [ ] **Medication Compliance:** Take prescribed medication immediately upon waking, ideally alongside a protein-rich snack to optimize efficacy.
* [ ] **Visual Anchor:** Review the "Daily Top Three"—the three non-negotiable tasks identified the previous evening—on a physical whiteboard or notepad.
* [ ] **Environmental Clearing:** Perform a 5-minute "reset" of the workspace. Remove all items not related to the first task of the day.

## Section 2: The Core Work/Task Blocks
*Objective: Utilize time-boxing to sustain focus and prevent hyper-fixation or task-paralysis.*

* [ ] **The "Five-Minute" Rule:** If a task is blocked, commit to working on it for only five minutes. This lowers the barrier to entry.
* [ ] **Pomodoro Integration:** Utilize a physical kitchen timer (not a phone) to set 25-minute work sprints followed by 5-minute mandatory "brain breaks."
* [ ] **Sensory Regulation:** Engage noise-canceling headphones or "Brown Noise" soundtracks to anchor focus and filter out environmental stimuli.
* [ ] **Context Switching:** If switching tasks, write down the status of the current task ("where I left off") before pivoting to avoid cognitive leakage.

## Section 3: The Evening Shutdown Routine
*Objective: Close mental "open loops" to prevent anxiety and facilitate sleep onset.*

* [ ] **The Brain Dump:** Empty all lingering thoughts, worries, or new task ideas onto a "Parking Lot" notepad. Do not process them yet.
* [ ] **Digital Hygiene:** Close all browser tabs that are not required for tomorrow’s first task.
* [ ] **The "Next Day" Setup:** Identify exactly what task you will start with tomorrow morning. Place the physical materials for that task on your desk tonight.
* [ ] **Environment Reset:** Ensure common areas are returned to a baseline state of order to prevent visual overwhelm upon waking.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

* **Pro Tip: The "Don't Break the Chain" Fallacy:** Do not view the routine as a pass/fail system. If you miss a step, skip it and resume at the next scheduled checkpoint. The goal is frequency, not perfection.
* **Pro Tip: Externalize Everything:** If it is not written down, it does not exist. Use a "command center" (a single physical calendar or whiteboard) for all dates and deadlines.
* **Pitfall: The "Planning Trap":** Avoid spending more time organizing your tasks than actually performing them. If you feel yourself over-optimizing your to-do list, stop and execute the smallest task immediately.
* **Pitfall: Emotional Regulation:** Recognize that ADHD-related fatigue often manifests as irritation. If you feel "stuck," acknowledge it as a physiological state, not a moral failing, and take a 10-minute sensory break.

## FAQ

**Q: What should I do if I have a "zero-productivity" day?**
A: Acknowledge the day as a "maintenance day." Focus only on the bare essentials (medication, hydration, and the evening shutdown). Do not attempt to "catch up" by overworking the following day, as this leads to burnout.

**Q: How do I handle tasks that feel physically painful to start?**
A: Use "Body Doubling." Work alongside someone else (in person or via a virtual co-working site). The presence of another person often creates enough social pressure to bypass executive paralysis.

**Q: Is it better to use digital or analog tools?**
A: For ADHD, analog tools (paper, whiteboards, physical timers) are generally superior. Digital tools offer infinite potential for distraction; physical tools provide tactile feedback and visual permanence that helps the brain "see" the passage of time.
