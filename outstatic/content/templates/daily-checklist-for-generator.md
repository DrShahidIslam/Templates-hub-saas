# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Generator Inspection and Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory daily inspection requirements for industrial backup power generators. Regular monitoring is essential to ensure operational readiness, prevent premature mechanical failure, and guarantee seamless load transfer during power outages. This checklist must be completed by the designated facility technician or operator to maintain site compliance and emergency preparedness.

## Level 1: Visual and Fluid Inspections

*   **Fuel Levels:** Verify that fuel levels are at or above the designated operational threshold (typically >75% for diesel units). Check for any signs of external leakage around the tank and fuel lines.
*   **Coolant Levels:** Inspect the radiator/coolant reservoir. Ensure fluid is at the "Full" cold mark. Check for signs of coolant discoloration or oily residue.
*   **Lubrication Oil:** Check the engine oil dipstick. Oil should be at the "Full" mark. Ensure the oil does not appear milky (indicates coolant contamination) or excessively metallic.
*   **Battery Condition:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion or loose connections. Verify that the battery charger indicator is showing a "Float/Trickle" status.
*   **Hose/Belt Integrity:** Check all radiator hoses and fan belts for cracking, fraying, or signs of dry rot. Ensure tension is within manufacturer specifications.

## Level 2: Control Panel and Environment

*   **Control Panel Status:** Verify the generator control unit is in "AUTO" mode. Ensure there are no active fault codes, alarms, or "Maintenance Required" warnings displayed.
*   **Engine Run Time:** Record the current total engine hours to ensure maintenance intervals (e.g., oil changes) are triggered accurately.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust piping and silencer. Ensure there are no blockages, carbon buildup, or visible leaks at the joints.
*   **Room Environment:** Confirm the generator room is free of debris, storage boxes, or flammable materials. Ensure intake and exhaust louvers are unobstructed.
*   **Ambient Temperature:** Verify that the room temperature remains within the specified range for the engine block heater to function efficiently.

## Level 3: Final Verification and Documentation

*   **Log Entry:** Sign and date the physical or digital logbook immediately following the inspection.
*   **Immediate Escalation:** If any abnormal readings are discovered (e.g., low oil, active alarms), notify the Maintenance Manager immediately and flag the unit as "Non-Operational" until resolved.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **The "Cleanliness" Metric:** A clean engine bay is the best diagnostic tool. Leaks are significantly easier to spot on a clean engine. If you notice a "clean spot" on a dusty surface, a slow leak is likely present.
*   **Avoid Over-Tightening:** When checking belts or hoses, do not overtighten during adjustments. Over-tensioning can lead to premature bearing failure in the water pump or alternator.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Small Drips:** Never categorize a drip as "minor." In a pressurized fuel or oil system, small drips often become high-pressure spray leaks during the vibration of a full-load startup.
*   **The Battery Trap:** Even if the generator starts, a battery nearing the end of its life can fail during a cold start or under high load. Always test voltage; don't rely solely on the "green light" on the charger.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How often should a load bank test be performed if the daily checks are perfect?**
Daily checks are for static monitoring; a full load bank test should be performed at least annually (or per manufacturer recommendations) to ensure the generator can handle its rated load without overheating or stalling.

**2. What should I do if the control panel shows an alarm I don't recognize?**
Do not attempt to bypass or clear an unknown alarm. Immediately refer to the generator’s O&M manual. If the error persists, contact a certified technician; ignoring an alarm can lead to engine "lockout" in some modern control systems.

**3. Is it necessary to run the generator daily?**
No. Most standby generators are programmed for a weekly "Exercise Run." Running the unit unnecessarily increases engine wear and fuel consumption. Follow the site’s established exercise schedule rather than manual daily firing.
