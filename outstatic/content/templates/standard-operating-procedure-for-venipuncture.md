# Standard Operating Procedure: Venipuncture

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements and clinical steps for performing a venipuncture. As an operations manager, I emphasize that this procedure must be conducted with strict adherence to universal precautions, patient identification protocols, and aseptic techniques to ensure patient safety, specimen integrity, and the reduction of pre-analytical errors.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Patient Identification

*   **Review Requisition:** Verify the physician’s order, identifying the specific tests required and checking for any special handling instructions (e.g., chilled, protected from light).
*   **Assemble Supplies:** Gather all necessary equipment:
    *   Appropriate vacutainer tubes (color-coded).
    *   Double-ended needle and tube holder or butterfly needle assembly.
    *   Tourniquet (single-use recommended).
    *   70% isopropyl alcohol wipes.
    *   Sterile gauze and adhesive bandage/tape.
    *   Biohazard sharps container.
    *   Gloves (non-latex if required).
*   **Patient Identification:** Use two patient identifiers (e.g., full name and date of birth). Match these strictly against the requisition form and the patient’s ID wristband.
*   **Patient Consent and Assessment:** Explain the procedure clearly to the patient. Assess the patient for any history of fainting (syncope) or allergies to latex or adhesive.

## Phase 2: Site Selection and Preparation

*   **Positioning:** Ensure the patient’s arm is supported and extended in a downward position.
*   **Tourniquet Application:** Apply the tourniquet 3–4 inches above the intended puncture site. Do not leave the tourniquet on for longer than 60 seconds to avoid hemoconcentration.
*   **Vein Selection:** Palpate the antecubital fossa. Prioritize the median cubital vein, followed by the cephalic or basilic veins. Avoid sites with hematomas, edema, or IV lines.
*   **Cleansing:** Cleanse the site using a 70% isopropyl alcohol wipe in a circular motion, moving from the center outward. Allow the site to air-dry completely (this prevents stinging and hemolysis).

## Phase 3: The Venipuncture Procedure

*   **Anchor the Vein:** Use the thumb of your non-dominant hand to pull the skin taut below the puncture site.
*   **Insertion:** Insert the needle at a 15–30 degree angle, bevel facing upward, in a smooth, continuous motion.
*   **Collection:** Push the collection tube onto the needle within the holder. Follow the correct Order of Draw (e.g., Blood Culture → Citrate → Serum → Heparin → EDTA).
*   **Mix Specimens:** Immediately invert each tube containing additives 5–10 times (do not shake).
*   **Removal:** Release the tourniquet once blood flow is established. Once the final tube is filled, remove the tube first, then withdraw the needle.
*   **Hemostasis:** Apply immediate, firm pressure to the puncture site with sterile gauze. Elevate the arm if necessary. Apply a bandage only after bleeding has ceased.

## Phase 4: Post-Procedure and Documentation

*   **Disposal:** Immediately engage the needle safety mechanism and discard the needle into an approved sharps container.
*   **Labeling:** Label all tubes at the bedside in the presence of the patient, ensuring patient name, ID number, date, time, and collector initials are recorded.
*   **Documentation:** Record the procedure in the patient’s electronic medical record (EMR), noting the site of collection and any complications encountered.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip (The "Anchor"):** Always anchor the vein firmly. A "rolling vein" is usually the result of inadequate skin tension rather than a bad vein.
*   **Pitfall (Hemolysis):** Avoid pulling back on a syringe plunger too quickly, and never "milk" the site. Hemolysis renders samples invalid for many tests, such as potassium and LDH.
*   **Pro Tip (Order of Draw):** Always memorize the standard Order of Draw to prevent cross-contamination of additives (e.g., EDTA contamination in a calcium test).
*   **Pitfall (Tourniquet Time):** Leaving a tourniquet on too long leads to hemoconcentration, which can falsely elevate protein, calcium, and iron levels.

## FAQ

**Q: What should I do if I miss the vein?**
A: Do not "fish" or probe with the needle. If the needle does not yield blood, withdraw slightly and redirect or terminate the attempt. Never exceed two attempts; request assistance if unsuccessful.

**Q: Can I draw blood from a patient with an active IV infusion?**
A: Avoid drawing from an arm with an IV line. If unavoidable, the blood must be drawn from a site *below* the IV infusion site, and the IV must be turned off for at least 2 minutes prior to collection.

**Q: How do I handle a patient who begins to faint?**
A: Immediately withdraw the needle and activate the safety feature. Assist the patient to a supine position, elevate their legs, apply a cold compress to the neck or forehead, and monitor vital signs until the patient is conscious and alert.
