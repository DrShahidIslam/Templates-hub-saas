# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Audit for Oil and Gas Operations

This Internal Audit SOP provides a standardized framework for evaluating the operational, safety, and financial integrity of oil and gas assets. Given the high-risk nature of the industry, this audit is designed to ensure strict adherence to regulatory standards (such as OSHA, EPA, and API), verify equipment reliability, and confirm the accuracy of production and inventory reporting. Adherence to this procedure is mandatory for all internal auditors to ensure consistent documentation and risk mitigation across all field and corporate operations.

## Phase 1: Operational and Safety Compliance
*   **Permit to Work (PTW) Verification:** Inspect active PTW logs to ensure all high-risk tasks (hot work, confined space entry, working at heights) are signed off and risk assessments are current.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) Audit:** Verify that site personnel possess site-specific PPE and that all safety equipment (e.g., respirators, gas detectors) is calibrated and within inspection intervals.
*   **Emergency Response Protocols:** Verify the accessibility and status of fire suppression systems, emergency shutdown (ESD) systems, and eyewash/safety shower stations.
*   **Hazardous Material Storage:** Ensure all chemicals (including drilling muds and solvents) have up-to-date Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and secondary containment protocols in place.

## Phase 2: Asset Integrity and Maintenance
*   **Preventative Maintenance (PM) Compliance:** Review CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) data to confirm that critical equipment (pumps, compressors, valves) has undergone scheduled maintenance.
*   **Calibration Records:** Audit flow meters, pressure gauges, and temperature sensors to ensure they are calibrated to NIST or equivalent industry standards.
*   **Piping and Vessel Inspection:** Review mechanical integrity reports, specifically checking for signs of corrosion, leaks, or wall-thickness degradation in pipelines and pressure vessels.
*   **Integrity Management Programs:** Verify that cathodic protection levels are being monitored and recorded at the required intervals.

## Phase 3: Financial and Production Reporting
*   **Production Meter Accuracy:** Reconcile wellhead flow readings against sales meter readings to identify potential discrepancies or unauthorized losses.
*   **Inventory Reconciliation:** Compare physical storage levels (tank levels, stockpile volume) against ledger balances to ensure accurate reporting of hydrocarbons.
*   **Waste and Environmental Reporting:** Audit disposal manifests for hazardous waste and produced water to ensure compliance with environmental regulations.
*   **Contractor Expenditure:** Review service contracts against actual invoices to prevent overbilling or unauthorized vendor service additions.

## Phase 4: Regulatory and Governance
*   **Reporting Timelines:** Confirm that all mandatory reports (spill logs, emissions data) have been submitted to relevant regulatory bodies within the legal deadlines.
*   **Training Records:** Verify that all employees and contractors have completed required safety and technical competency certifications.
*   **Incident Reporting:** Review the "near-miss" registry to ensure all incidents were documented, investigated, and addressed with corrective actions.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Start with the "Ghost" Walk:** Before reviewing paperwork, walk the floor/site. Discrepancies between how things are *done* and how they are *documented* are your best lead for finding deeper issues.
*   **Pro Tip: Focus on Variance:** When auditing production numbers, pay closest attention to periods of high equipment downtime or significant swings in throughput; these are the highest risk areas for reporting errors.
*   **Pitfall - The "Paper-Pushing" Trap:** Do not rely solely on digital logs. Auditors often fall into the trap of accepting a completed form as proof of work. Cross-verify digital records with physical evidence (e.g., physical oil levels in a tank).
*   **Pitfall - Scope Creep:** Oil and gas facilities are complex. Define a strict audit scope before arrival to avoid getting distracted by non-critical operational anomalies that fall outside the current audit mandate.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should an internal audit be conducted for high-risk operations?**
For high-risk upstream and midstream assets, a comprehensive internal audit should be conducted at least annually, with quarterly "spot checks" focusing on safety-critical systems and production reconciliation.

**2. What should be the primary focus if an audit detects a significant regulatory non-compliance?**
The immediate priority is to assess the risk to life and the environment. If the non-compliance poses an immediate threat, the auditor must have the authority to trigger a "Stop Work Order" before proceeding with the remainder of the audit process.

**3. How do we handle discrepancies between site-level reports and corporate data?**
Document the discrepancy, perform a "root cause analysis" to determine if the error is human, mechanical, or procedural, and require the site manager to submit a formal remediation plan within 14 days of the audit closing.
