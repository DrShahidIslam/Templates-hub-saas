# Standard Operating Procedure: Comprehensive Automotive Inspection

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocol for conducting a professional automotive inspection. This process ensures technician accountability, maintains high safety standards, and facilitates transparent communication with clients. This procedure applies to all vehicles entering the service bay for routine maintenance, diagnostics, or pre-purchase evaluations. Adherence to this SOP minimizes liability, improves diagnostic accuracy, and increases customer trust.

---

## Inspection Checklist

## 1. Exterior and Body Integrity
*   Check for body panel alignment, dings, dents, or scratches.
*   Inspect all exterior lighting: headlights (high/low beam), turn signals, brake lights, and hazard lights.
*   Examine windshield and side windows for cracks, chips, or wiper-induced scratching.
*   Verify wiper blade condition and functionality.
*   Inspect tires for tread depth (measure in 32nds), uneven wear patterns, and sidewall dry rot.
*   Check tire pressure and adjust to manufacturer specifications.

## 2. Under-Hood Diagnostics
*   Inspect all fluid levels: Engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer reservoir.
*   Check for signs of active leaks (oil, coolant, or transmission fluid).
*   Examine battery terminals for corrosion and verify secure mounting.
*   Inspect air filter condition and cabin air filter debris levels.
*   Check accessory drive belts for fraying, glazing, or improper tension.
*   Listen for unusual engine noises at cold start and idle.

## 3. Under-Carriage and Suspension
*   Inspect exhaust system for rust, holes, or loose hangers.
*   Check CV axle boots for rips or grease leakage.
*   Examine shock absorbers and struts for oil weeping.
*   Check suspension bushings for play or dry cracking.
*   Inspect brake rotors and pads; measure remaining pad thickness and check for heat-related discoloration.
*   Confirm integrity of the under-body shielding and splash guards.

## 4. Interior and Electronics
*   Verify dashboard warning lights (Check Engine, ABS, SRS, TPMS).
*   Test climate control functionality (AC cooling and heater core performance).
*   Check interior lighting and instrument cluster clarity.
*   Test power window and lock switches.
*   Check parking brake engagement and release.
*   Scan for any electronic fault codes using the OBD-II diagnostic tool.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **The "Double-Check" Rule:** If you find a component near the end of its life, take a photograph. Visual evidence is the most effective tool for selling necessary repairs to the client.
*   **Sequential Movement:** Start at the front left tire and work clockwise around the vehicle. This prevents skipping components and ensures a repeatable workflow.
*   **Standardized Documentation:** Use a standardized digital form to ensure no line items are missed during high-volume service days.

### Pitfalls
*   **Assuming "New" Means "Good":** Never assume a part is functional just because it looks new; verify actual performance.
*   **The "Quick Peek" Trap:** Never inspect a brake pad solely through the wheel spokes. Always remove the wheel to accurately measure both the inner and outer pads.
*   **Ignoring Service History:** Always cross-reference the inspection findings with the vehicle’s service history to avoid suggesting redundant maintenance.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should the inspection sheet be updated?**
A: The checklist should be reviewed every quarter to ensure it aligns with current vehicle technology and common failure points for the models serviced in your shop.

**Q: What should I do if a safety-critical item is failed during an inspection?**
A: Immediately document the defect, place a "Do Not Drive" warning on the vehicle, and escalate the issue to the Service Manager to inform the customer of the liability and risks.

**Q: Should the inspection be performed while the engine is hot or cold?**
A: Ideally, perform initial walkarounds and exterior checks while the vehicle is cool, but perform engine-running diagnostics (like cooling system pressure tests) once the engine has reached operating temperature.
