# Standard Operating Procedure: Laboratory Equipment Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the systematic requirements for the inspection, calibration, and preventive maintenance of laboratory equipment. Proper maintenance is critical to ensuring experimental data integrity, operational safety, and compliance with institutional and regulatory standards. All laboratory personnel are responsible for adhering to these procedures to minimize equipment downtime and prevent hazardous conditions resulting from mechanical or electrical failure.

## 1. Preventive Maintenance (PM) Scheduling
*   Establish a centralized master equipment log containing model numbers, serial numbers, and warranty expiration dates.
*   Consult manufacturer-recommended service intervals (e.g., monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, annually).
*   Flag equipment that requires external calibration services (e.g., balances, pipettes, centrifuges) at least 30 days prior to due dates.
*   Assign specific maintenance ownership to designated lab leads or safety officers.

## 2. Daily & Weekly Routine Maintenance
*   **Visual Inspection:** Check power cords for fraying and ensure connections are secure.
*   **Sanitization:** Clean surfaces using manufacturer-approved agents. Do not use corrosive solvents on touchscreens or sensitive optical sensors.
*   **Functionality Checks:** Perform basic start-up checks (e.g., verifying freezer temperature logs, checking centrifuge balance indicators).
*   **Log Entries:** Record all routine checks in the physical or digital equipment maintenance logbook immediately after completion.

## 3. Corrective Maintenance & Repair
*   **Out-of-Service Tagging:** If equipment malfunctions, affix a "DO NOT USE" tag prominently to the unit and unplug it if necessary.
*   **Reporting:** Submit a detailed incident report to the Lab Manager documenting the error code, observed behavior, and last known successful operation.
*   **Authorized Repairs:** Only trained or certified personnel may perform internal repairs. If the equipment is under warranty, contact the manufacturer's technical support immediately.
*   **Validation:** Post-repair, the equipment must undergo a performance validation test before being returned to active service.

## 4. Calibration & Quality Assurance
*   Use only NIST-traceable standards for calibration where applicable.
*   Keep original calibration certificates in a secure, accessible file folder.
*   If a device fails calibration, perform an "Impact Analysis" to determine if data generated since the last successful calibration is compromised.
*   Affix updated calibration stickers to equipment, clearly displaying the date of calibration and the next due date.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Create a QR code for each piece of equipment that links directly to its digital manual and maintenance log. This drastically reduces onboarding time and prevents logbook loss.
*   **Pro Tip:** Schedule preventive maintenance during low-activity periods (e.g., between academic semesters or end-of-year shutdown) to minimize research disruption.
*   **Pitfall - Deferred Maintenance:** Ignoring minor equipment "quirks" often leads to catastrophic failure. Address strange noises or inconsistent readings immediately rather than waiting for scheduled maintenance.
*   **Pitfall - Unauthorized Modifications:** Adding "DIY" modifications to equipment can void warranties and create significant safety hazards. Always seek engineering approval for custom setups.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if the equipment fails its calibration test?**
A: Immediately remove the equipment from use, document the failure in the logbook, and notify your supervisor. All data collected using that equipment since its last successful calibration must be reviewed for potential inaccuracies.

**Q: Can I perform maintenance on equipment if I am not the designated lead?**
A: You may perform basic cleaning and routine visual checks as outlined in the SOP. However, internal hardware or software maintenance should only be performed by personnel explicitly trained and authorized for that specific piece of equipment.

**Q: How long must I retain equipment maintenance logs?**
A: Per standard regulatory compliance, maintenance records should be retained for the entire lifespan of the equipment, plus a minimum of five years after decommissioning, unless specified otherwise by your institution's data retention policy.
