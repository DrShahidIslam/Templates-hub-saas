# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Property Viewing Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements for conducting a thorough property inspection during a house viewing. Whether you are a prospective buyer or an investor, objective evaluation is critical to mitigating long-term financial risk. The following framework is designed to move beyond aesthetic appeal and focus on structural integrity, mechanical systems, and neighborhood viability to ensure you make an informed acquisition decision.

## Phase 1: External & Structural Assessment
Before entering the home, evaluate the "envelope" of the property to identify potential high-cost issues.

*   **Roofline:** Inspect for sagging, missing shingles, or misaligned gutters that could indicate water runoff issues.
*   **Foundation:** Scan for visible cracks (specifically "stair-step" cracks in brickwork) or signs of settling/heaving.
*   **Exterior Walls:** Check the condition of siding, stucco, or masonry for rot, peeling paint, or moisture intrusion.
*   **Drainage:** Observe the slope of the land. Does it grade toward the house (a significant flood risk) or away from it?
*   **Windows & Doors:** Check for proper sealing, broken seals (fogged glass), and functional frames.

## Phase 2: Interior & Mechanical Systems
Once inside, shift focus to the infrastructure that sustains the living environment.

*   **Electrical:** Locate the breaker box. Is it modern (circuit breakers) or outdated (fuses)? Look for frayed wiring or scorched outlets.
*   **Plumbing:** Test water pressure in all sinks and showers. Check underneath cabinets for signs of current or past leaks (discoloration or dampness).
*   **HVAC:** Check the age and condition of the furnace and water heater. Listen for abnormal noises during operation.
*   **Walls/Ceilings:** Look for water stains or fresh paint that might be masking underlying ceiling leaks.
*   **Flooring:** Walk the perimeter of every room to check for spongy areas or unlevel floorboards, which may indicate subfloor damage.

## Phase 3: Neighborhood & Environmental Factors
A house is inseparable from its surroundings; evaluate the externalities that impact resale value and quality of life.

*   **Noise Pollution:** Identify proximity to highways, flight paths, trains, or industrial zones.
*   **Traffic Flow:** Observe local traffic patterns during your arrival and departure. 
*   **Curb Appeal of Neighbors:** Are adjacent properties maintained? Neighborhood upkeep is a primary driver of long-term property appreciation.
*   **Natural Light:** Note the orientation of the house. Southern exposure generally provides better natural light, whereas northern exposure may be darker.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Bring a marble or small ball to test for level floors; bring a flashlight to inspect dark corners and crawl spaces.
*   **Pro Tip:** Do not be afraid to take photos and videos. It is easy to forget details once you leave the premises.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "staging bias." Furniture and decor are designed to distract you. Focus on the bones of the building, not the interior design.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "smell test." Musty odors are the leading indicator of black mold or significant dampness. If the house smells heavily of plug-in air fresheners, investigate further.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I bring a professional inspector to the first viewing?**
A: Typically, no. A professional inspection is a contingency to be performed after your offer is accepted. However, if you are inexperienced, bringing a contractor or a knowledgeable friend to the second viewing is highly recommended.

**Q: How do I identify if the plumbing is polybutylene or lead?**
A: Look for grey, plastic-looking pipes (polybutylene) or dull grey metal pipes that scratch easily (lead). If in doubt, consult a professional, as these materials can be difficult or expensive to insure and replace.

**Q: Is it acceptable to visit the property at different times of the day?**
A: Absolutely. It is best practice to visit once during the day to see natural light and general condition, and once during the evening to assess neighborhood safety and noise levels.
