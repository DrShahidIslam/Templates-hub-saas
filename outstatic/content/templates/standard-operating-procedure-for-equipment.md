# Standard Operating Procedure: Equipment Lifecycle & Operational Management

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the procurement, daily operation, maintenance, and decommissioning of operational equipment. Adherence to these guidelines ensures peak equipment performance, minimizes unplanned downtime, maintains compliance with safety regulations, and protects the organization’s capital investment. All personnel operating or managing equipment are responsible for following this procedure to ensure a safe, efficient, and standardized working environment.

## Phase 1: Pre-Operational Inspection
Before initiating any equipment, the operator must verify that the environment and hardware are in a ready state.
*   **Visual Inspection:** Check for signs of physical damage, loose connections, or fluid leaks.
*   **Safety Guards:** Confirm all safety shields, emergency stop buttons, and interlocks are functional and unencumbered.
*   **Fluid & Power Levels:** Verify that power sources, fuel levels, or pressure gauges meet the manufacturer’s minimum operational requirements.
*   **Calibration Check:** Ensure the device is within its active calibration period by verifying the inspection sticker.

## Phase 2: Operational Execution
Standardized operation is critical to the longevity of the equipment and the quality of output.
*   **Warm-up Cycle:** Run the equipment through the manufacturer-specified warm-up cycle before applying load.
*   **Loading Parameters:** Do not exceed the maximum load capacity or duty cycle specified in the technical manual.
*   **Real-time Monitoring:** Observe gauges, displays, and auditory feedback for anomalies (e.g., unusual vibration, burning smells, or erratic readings).
*   **Emergency Protocol:** In the event of an anomaly, immediately trigger the Emergency Stop (E-Stop) and notify the shift supervisor.

## Phase 3: Post-Operational Maintenance
Proper shutdown and hygiene prevent premature wear and prepare the machine for the next user.
*   **Controlled Shutdown:** Follow the exact power-down sequence to prevent electrical surges or mechanical damage.
*   **Debris Removal:** Clean all debris, residue, or chemical buildup from surfaces and internal components.
*   **Lubrication:** Perform required daily lubrication as dictated by the maintenance schedule.
*   **Logbook Documentation:** Record the duration of use, total output count, and any observations in the equipment logbook.

## Phase 4: Preventative Maintenance & Reporting
Maintenance is a proactive, not reactive, responsibility.
*   **Scheduled Servicing:** Follow the calendar-based maintenance schedule provided in the asset’s technical file.
*   **Consumable Tracking:** Monitor the usage of consumables (filters, belts, lubricants) and request replenishments 48 hours before stock depletion.
*   **Defect Reporting:** If a malfunction occurs, immediately tag the equipment as "Out of Service" with a physical "Lock-Out/Tag-Out" (LOTO) device to prevent unauthorized use.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Treat the equipment manual as the "Source of Truth." If the SOP conflicts with the manual, contact the engineering lead immediately.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain an "Equipment Birth Certificate" folder for every asset, containing the invoice, warranty, and every maintenance record for easy auditing.
*   **Pitfall (The "Quick Fix"):** Never attempt makeshift repairs or bypass safety sensors to "save time." This leads to catastrophic equipment failure and severe safety liabilities.
*   **Pitfall (Neglect):** Ignoring minor sounds or vibrations because "it still works" is the fastest path to a massive repair bill. Report subtle deviations early.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should I do if the equipment logbook is missing?**
Do not operate the equipment. Notify your supervisor immediately. A missing logbook prevents us from tracking history and is a major compliance risk.

**2. How do I decide if an issue requires a technician or if I can fix it myself?**
If a fix requires opening the equipment chassis or removing safety-rated fasteners, it must be performed by a certified technician. Routine cleaning and filter changes are the only authorized user-level maintenance.

**3. What is the process if I discover a critical failure during a shift?**
Initiate the LOTO (Lock-Out/Tag-Out) process immediately, inform your department head, and file an Incident Report form before leaving your shift.
