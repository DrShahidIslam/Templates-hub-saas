# Standard Operating Procedure: Fire Hydrant System Preventive Maintenance

This document outlines the mandatory procedures for the routine inspection, testing, and maintenance of the facility’s fire hydrant system. The primary objective is to ensure that the system remains in a constant state of operational readiness to suppress fire emergencies. Adherence to this SOP is critical for life safety, property protection, and compliance with NFPA 25 (Standard for the Inspection, Testing, and Maintenance of Water-Based Fire Protection Systems) and local fire codes. All maintenance must be documented in the Fire Safety Logbook for audit purposes.

## Monthly Inspection Checklist

*   **Exterior Visual Inspection:**
    *   Verify that hydrants are free from external damage, cracks, or corrosion.
    *   Ensure hydrants are clearly visible and not obstructed by vehicles, foliage, or construction materials.
    *   Check for signs of external leakage at the hydrant head, nozzle caps, and flange.
    *   Confirm that operating nuts and nozzle caps are not seized or painted shut.
*   **Signage and Access:**
    *   Ensure directional signage is present and legible.
    *   Verify that the "No Parking" zone (where applicable) is clearly marked.
    *   Check for adequate clearance (minimum 360-degree access) for fire department connections.

## Quarterly Operational Testing

*   **Hydrant Lubrication:**
    *   Apply approved lubricant to all nozzle threads and operating stems.
    *   Remove and inspect nozzle caps; check gaskets for brittleness or degradation and replace if necessary.
*   **Flush Test:**
    *   Briefly open the hydrant to flush out stagnant water and sediment.
    *   Observe the flow rate and water clarity; ensure the hydrant drains properly after closing to prevent freezing.
*   **Valve Functionality:**
    *   Operate the hydrant valve fully open and then fully closed to ensure smooth operation.
    *   Verify that no "water hammer" occurs during operation.

## Annual Comprehensive Maintenance

*   **Pressure Testing:**
    *   Measure static and residual pressure at the hydrant outlet using a calibrated pitot gauge.
    *   Compare results against the original commissioning data or the last annual test to identify potential pipe scaling or blockages.
*   **Hydrostatic Testing:**
    *   Perform a hydrostatic pressure test on the hydrant barrel to identify hidden leaks.
*   **Painting and Preservation:**
    *   Scrape and repaint the hydrant using fire-code-compliant reflective paint if fading or rust is detected.
    *   Ensure the hydrant identification color code (based on flow capacity) is accurate.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Dry Barrel" Check:** After flushing, listen closely to the drain hole. If you hear suction or if the water level does not drop, the drain is blocked. This is a critical failure in cold climates, as water remaining in the barrel will freeze and burst the casting.
*   **Pro Tip: Record Keeping:** Always document the flow pressure (PSI) and GPM (Gallons Per Minute). If you notice a year-over-year decrease, it is a leading indicator of internal pipe corrosion or valve failure.
*   **Pitfall: Over-Tightening:** Do not over-tighten nozzle caps. Use a standard hydrant wrench and apply hand pressure only. Over-tightening can damage gaskets and make the caps impossible to remove during an actual emergency.
*   **Pitfall: Unauthorized Use:** Never use fire hydrants as a water source for irrigation, construction, or vehicle washing. This introduces debris into the system and causes premature wear of the valve seat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should the hydrant system be professionally serviced by a certified fire protection contractor?**
A: While internal staff can perform monthly visual inspections, a licensed fire protection contractor should conduct the annual flow and pressure testing to ensure compliance with insurance and local fire marshal requirements.

**Q: What should I do if I find a hydrant leaking during a routine inspection?**
A: Do not attempt to repair the internal hydrant valve yourself unless you are a trained fire system technician. Isolate the hydrant using the underground shut-off valve (if accessible), tag it "OUT OF SERVICE," and contact your facilities maintenance team immediately.

**Q: Why is it important to check the nozzle threads?**
A: Fire departments rely on standardized thread patterns. If the threads are cross-threaded or damaged due to neglect, the fire brigade will be unable to attach their hoses, effectively rendering the hydrant useless during a fire.
