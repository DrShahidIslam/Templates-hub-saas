# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for Diesel Generators

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocols for industrial diesel generator sets. Consistent maintenance is critical to ensuring power reliability, extending the operational lifespan of the engine, and mitigating the risk of critical failure during power outages. This procedure must be performed by qualified personnel according to the manufacturer’s specific service intervals or a minimum of every 250 operational hours (or quarterly, whichever comes first). Always adhere to site-specific Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures before beginning any mechanical intervention.

## Preventive Maintenance Checklist

## Section 1: Engine Lubrication and Cooling Systems
*   **Oil Level Check:** Verify oil level is at the "Full" mark on the dipstick.
*   **Oil Condition:** Inspect for signs of contamination (milky appearance indicating water ingress or metallic flakes).
*   **Oil and Filter Change:** Drain oil while warm and replace filters per manufacturer specifications.
*   **Coolant Level:** Ensure the radiator is filled to the appropriate level; check for leaks in hoses and clamps.
*   **Coolant Quality:** Test coolant for freeze point, boil point, and SCA (Supplemental Coolant Additive) concentration levels.
*   **Drive Belts:** Inspect alternator and fan belts for fraying, cracks, or improper tension (replace if deflection exceeds manufacturer limits).

## Section 2: Fuel and Air Intake Systems
*   **Fuel Level:** Ensure the main tank is filled to at least 90% capacity to prevent condensation buildup.
*   **Fuel Filters:** Replace primary and secondary fuel/water separators. Drain any accumulated sediment from the water separator bowl.
*   **Fuel Lines:** Inspect all fuel hoses for brittle conditions, cracks, or weeping at the fittings.
*   **Air Intake:** Check the air filter restriction indicator; replace element if restricted or visibly clogged.
*   **Air Intake Piping:** Verify all clamps are tight and there are no tears in the intake boots, which could lead to unfiltered air entering the engine.

## Section 3: Electrical and Starting Systems
*   **Battery Voltage:** Check battery voltage and ensure terminal connections are tight and free of oxidation.
*   **Electrolyte Level:** If using lead-acid batteries, check cell levels and top off with distilled water if required.
*   **Battery Charger:** Verify the float charger is operational and maintains the battery at the correct voltage.
*   **Control Panel:** Inspect the digital controller for active fault codes or pending alarms.
*   **Starter Motor:** Listen for sluggish starts during the test run; inspect connections for heat damage.

## Section 4: Exhaust and Operational Testing
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust manifold, piping, and muffler for leaks, soot buildup, or excessive rust.
*   **Test Run:** Run the generator under load (if an Automatic Transfer Switch and load bank are available) for a minimum of 30 minutes.
*   **Gauges:** Monitor oil pressure, coolant temperature, and frequency/voltage stability throughout the test run.
*   **Structural Integrity:** Listen for abnormal vibrations or metallic noises during operation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always record the "Hours Run" meter reading before and after maintenance. Keeping a chronological log is essential for identifying patterns of premature component failure.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an oil analysis kit annually. It acts as a blood test for your engine, revealing internal wear (bearings/rings) long before a mechanical breakdown occurs.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening filters. Always follow the manufacturer’s torque specifications (usually hand-tight plus a quarter turn) to avoid damaging the filter housing gasket.
*   **Pitfall:** "Wet Stacking." Running a diesel generator at less than 30% load for long periods leads to unburned fuel buildup in the exhaust. Always ensure the generator reaches full operating temperature during testing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I run the generator if it is for emergency standby only?**
A: Even if no power outage occurs, a standby generator should be run under load for at least 30 minutes every month to lubricate seals, cycle the battery, and verify system readiness.

**Q: Can I use automotive-grade oil in my generator?**
A: No. Diesel generators require heavy-duty diesel engine oil (typically CI-4 or CJ-4 rated) specifically formulated for high-compression, high-heat environments. Using the wrong oil will drastically reduce engine life.

**Q: What do I do if I find water in the fuel separator?**
A: Drain the separator immediately using the drain valve. If water continues to appear after subsequent checks, the main fuel tank likely has condensation or contamination and requires professional fuel polishing.
