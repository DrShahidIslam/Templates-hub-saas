# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for Diesel Generators

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance (PM) schedule required to ensure the operational readiness, longevity, and reliability of diesel generator sets. Proper adherence to these protocols minimizes the risk of catastrophic engine failure, ensures compliance with safety regulations, and guarantees power stability during emergency grid outages. This procedure applies to all facility engineering and maintenance personnel responsible for standby and prime-power generator assets.

## Phase 1: Daily/Weekly Visual Inspections
*   **Visual Fluid Check:** Inspect for leaks under the unit (oil, coolant, or fuel).
*   **Fuel Level:** Verify the fuel tank level is at least 75% capacity to prevent condensation buildup and ensure adequate runtime.
*   **Battery Voltage:** Confirm the battery charger is functioning and the float voltage is within the manufacturer's specified range.
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check engine oil dipstick and coolant expansion tank levels.
*   **Control Panel:** Ensure the controller is in "Auto" mode with no active fault codes or warnings present.
*   **Exhaust System:** Visually inspect the exhaust stack and silencer for signs of excessive soot, rust, or blockages.

## Phase 2: Monthly/Quarterly Performance Checks
*   **Air Intake:** Inspect air filter intake pipes and connections for cracks or loose clamps. Replace air filter elements if the air restriction indicator is triggered.
*   **Coolant System:** Test coolant concentration (glycol/water ratio) and pH levels to prevent corrosion and freezing.
*   **Battery Maintenance:** Clean battery terminals, verify cable tightness, and perform a load test if the battery is over 18 months old.
*   **Engine Run Test:** Perform a "No-Load" run for 15–30 minutes to allow the engine to reach operating temperature and circulate fluids.
*   **Transfer Switch Inspection:** Briefly inspect the Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) cabinet for dust, moisture, or signs of overheating at the lugs.

## Phase 3: Annual/Bi-Annual Major Maintenance
*   **Fluid Service:** Drain and replace engine oil and replace all oil filters.
*   **Fuel System:** Replace fuel-water separators and primary/secondary fuel filters. Inspect fuel lines for brittleness or weeping.
*   **Cooling System:** Flush and replace engine coolant; inspect hoses for degradation and replace if showing signs of cracking or swelling.
*   **Lubrication:** Lubricate engine-driven accessories, including cooling fan bearings and belt tensioners.
*   **Electrical Inspection:** Use an infrared thermal imaging camera to check electrical connections at the alternator and circuit breakers for "hot spots."
*   **Load Bank Testing:** Conduct a simulated load test (at 80–100% capacity) for 2–4 hours to verify system performance under stress and prevent "wet stacking."

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Wet Stacking" Trap:** Never run your diesel generator exclusively at no-load for extended periods. This leads to unburned fuel accumulation in the exhaust system (wet stacking). Always schedule periodic load bank testing to burn off carbon deposits.
*   **Pro Tip: Fuel Degradation:** Diesel fuel has a shelf life. Use a fuel stabilizer if the generator does not run frequently, and consider a fuel polishing service every 2–3 years to remove microbial growth and sludge.
*   **Pitfall: Over-tightening:** Avoid overtightening hose clamps and drain plugs, which can strip threads or damage rubber components. Always use a torque wrench to manufacturer specifications.
*   **Pitfall: Neglecting the ATS:** Maintenance teams often focus on the engine but ignore the Automatic Transfer Switch. A functional generator is useless if the switch fails to transfer the load during an outage.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should I replace the diesel fuel filters?**
Typically, fuel filters should be replaced every 250–500 operating hours or at least once a year, whichever comes first. If the environment is dusty or the fuel quality is inconsistent, increase the frequency of inspection.

**2. What is "Wet Stacking" and how do I prevent it?**
Wet stacking occurs when an engine operates under a light load or no load, preventing it from reaching proper internal temperatures. This causes fuel to remain unburned, leading to carbon buildup and smoke. Prevent this by running a load bank test annually to simulate full-capacity operation.

**3. Is it necessary to replace the coolant even if it looks clean?**
Yes. Coolant contains additives and corrosion inhibitors that chemically break down over time, even if the liquid remains clear. Failure to replace coolant on schedule can lead to internal engine block corrosion and premature failure of the water pump.
