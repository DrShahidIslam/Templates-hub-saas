# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance (PM) Management

This Standard Operating Procedure establishes a standardized framework for the planning, execution, and documentation of preventive maintenance (PM) activities. The objective of this procedure is to maximize equipment uptime, extend asset lifecycle, reduce emergency repair costs, and ensure compliance with safety standards. Adherence to this protocol is mandatory for all maintenance personnel to ensure consistent operational reliability across the facility.

## 1. Pre-Maintenance Preparation and Scheduling
*   **Audit Asset Register:** Review the Master Asset List to ensure all equipment requiring maintenance is current and categorized by criticality.
*   **Generate Work Orders:** Create digital or physical work orders for all scheduled tasks at least 72 hours prior to the maintenance window.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Confirm availability of required spare parts, specialized tools, and consumables (lubricants, filters, seals).
*   **Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Review:** Verify that LOTO procedures are updated and accessible for each asset scheduled for service.
*   **Stakeholder Notification:** Coordinate with department heads to schedule downtime during off-peak hours to minimize production impact.

## 2. Execution and Inspection Checklist
*   **Safety Verification:** Apply LOTO devices and verify zero-energy states before any internal inspections or component handling.
*   **Cleaning and Degreasing:** Remove debris, dust, and grime from exterior housings and internal components to facilitate accurate inspections.
*   **Visual Integrity Check:** Inspect structural components for cracks, corrosion, leaks, or signs of fatigue.
*   **Mechanical Performance:** Test drive motors, inspect belts for tension/fraying, lubricate bearings, and ensure moving parts operate within manufacturer tolerance.
*   **Electrical Inspection:** Use thermography or multimeters to check for loose connections, overheated wiring, or signs of electrical arcing.
*   **Software and Calibration:** Validate firmware updates and verify that sensors and PLC readings align with calibrated physical gauges.
*   **Functional Testing:** Run the equipment through a test cycle under low load before returning to full production capacity.

## 3. Documentation and Closure
*   **Data Recording:** Document all findings, including pre-maintenance metrics and post-maintenance performance readings, in the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS).
*   **Exception Reporting:** Log any issues that were identified but could not be resolved during the PM window for a follow-up corrective work order.
*   **Parts Inventory Update:** Deduct consumed parts from the inventory ledger to ensure accurate reorder triggers.
*   **Sign-off:** Obtain secondary verification sign-off from a supervisor or lead technician to ensure the asset is safe and operational.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Utilize "Condition-Based Maintenance" (CBM) alongside fixed-schedule PMs. If a sensor indicates a part is still performing at 95% efficiency, consider extending the service interval to save resources.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "Library of Failures." Tag historical PM records with specific failure modes to help technicians identify root causes faster in the future.
*   **Pitfall - "PM Stacking":** Avoid overloading a single week with maintenance tasks. It leads to rushed inspections and high labor burnout. Balance the schedule across the fiscal quarter.
*   **Pitfall - Pencil Whipping:** Never allow technicians to sign off on tasks they did not actually perform. This creates a false sense of security and leads to catastrophic equipment failure.

## FAQ
**Q: What is the ideal frequency for preventive maintenance?**
A: Frequency should be based on a combination of manufacturer recommendations, historical failure data, and operational environment severity. Start with the OEM manual and adjust intervals based on actual wear patterns.

**Q: Should I perform PMs on equipment that appears to be working perfectly?**
A: Yes. The purpose of preventive maintenance is to address "hidden" failures (e.g., internal seal degradation or slow accumulation of debris) before they manifest as critical downtime.

**Q: How do I handle emergency repairs that occur right before a scheduled PM?**
A: If an emergency repair addresses components intended for the upcoming PM, "bundle" the remaining PM tasks into the emergency repair work order to maximize the machine's downtime efficiency.
