# Standard Operating Procedure: Billing and Collection Audit

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for auditing the billing and collection lifecycle. The primary objective of this audit is to ensure financial accuracy, verify compliance with contractual terms, identify revenue leakage, and maintain the integrity of accounts receivable (AR). By performing these audits consistently, the organization mitigates risk, improves cash flow forecasting, and ensures that all billing documentation remains audit-ready for both internal and external financial reviews.

---

## Audit Checklist: Billing and Collection

## Phase 1: Data Integrity and Billing Accuracy
*   **Verify Master Data:** Confirm that customer billing profiles match current signed contracts (billing rates, payment terms, and tax status).
*   **Cross-Reference Invoices:** Perform a 3-way match audit between the Purchase Order (PO), proof of delivery/service rendered, and the generated invoice.
*   **Tax Compliance:** Ensure sales tax or VAT is calculated according to the specific jurisdiction of the service delivery.
*   **Discount & Rebate Validation:** Audit all applied discounts to ensure they are supported by documented authorization or pre-approved promotional codes.
*   **Currency and FX:** For international accounts, verify that currency conversions adhere to the corporate policy exchange rates.

## Phase 2: Revenue Recognition and Posting
*   **System Reconciliation:** Audit the sub-ledger against the General Ledger (GL) to ensure all billed amounts have been posted to the correct revenue accounts.
*   **Adjustments Audit:** Review all credit memos and invoice write-offs; ensure each has a valid business reason code and secondary management approval.
*   **Duplicate Detection:** Run an automated report to identify duplicate invoice numbers, dates, or amounts for the same client.
*   **Timing Accuracy:** Validate that revenue is recognized in the period the service was provided, not necessarily when payment was received (Accrual Basis).

## Phase 3: Collection and Accounts Receivable (AR)
*   **Aging Analysis:** Review the aging report for all accounts exceeding 60+ days; verify that collection efforts (calls, emails, dunning notices) are documented.
*   **Payment Allocation:** Audit cash receipts to ensure payments are correctly applied to the specific invoices identified by the customer.
*   **Unapplied Cash Review:** Investigate any payments sitting in "suspense" or "unapplied" accounts for more than 30 days.
*   **Collection Escalation:** Verify that accounts moved to third-party collection agencies follow the established internal legal escalation policy.

## Phase 4: Compliance and Documentation
*   **Audit Trail:** Confirm that the system retains a digital log of who created, edited, or deleted any invoice.
*   **Evidence Retention:** Verify that all supporting documentation (signed contracts, service logs) is archived according to the organizational data retention policy.
*   **Segregation of Duties:** Ensure the person responsible for billing is not the same person authorizing credit memos or handling cash receipts.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Automate Exception Reports:** Set up dashboard alerts for any invoice that deviates from standard pricing by more than 5%.
*   **Regular Sampling:** Instead of auditing 100% of invoices, conduct a deep-dive "statistically significant" sample audit monthly to maintain constant oversight.
*   **Vendor/Client Feedback Loop:** Include a "Billing Accuracy" survey in your quarterly check-ins with top-tier clients to catch billing errors early.

### Pitfalls
*   **The "Silent" Write-off:** Failing to monitor small balance write-offs can lead to massive aggregate revenue loss over a fiscal year.
*   **Outdated Master Data:** Relying on verbal updates instead of signed contract addendums is the #1 cause of invoice disputes.
*   **Lack of Segregation:** Allowing the AR clerk to handle bank reconciliations creates a significant internal control vulnerability prone to fraud.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should a comprehensive billing audit be performed?**
A: While automated checks should run daily, a full manual review of high-risk or high-value accounts should be conducted monthly, with a comprehensive audit of all processes occurring at least quarterly.

**Q: What is the most common reason for collection delays?**
A: Disputes stemming from missing or incorrect purchase order numbers on invoices. Ensuring the client’s PO requirements are captured in the master billing profile is essential.

**Q: If an error is found, what is the required corrective action?**
A: Errors must be logged in a "Corrective Action Register." Depending on the severity, this may require issuing a credit memo, re-invoicing, or adjusting the master data, followed by a formal communication to the client to maintain transparency.
