# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Inspection Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory steps for conducting a comprehensive vehicle inspection. Whether preparing a vehicle for sale, performing routine fleet maintenance, or assessing a rental return, this systematic approach ensures that no safety, performance, or aesthetic detail is overlooked. Adhering to this protocol mitigates liability, maximizes vehicle longevity, and maintains high operational standards.

## 1. Exterior and Bodywork
*   **Paint and Body:** Check for scratches, dents, or signs of mismatched paint (suggesting prior collision repair).
*   **Glass and Mirrors:** Inspect all windows, windshields, and side mirrors for cracks, chips, or pitting that could obscure vision.
*   **Lights:** Test high/low beams, turn signals (front/rear), brake lights, hazard lights, and reverse lights.
*   **Tires:** Measure tread depth (minimum 4/32” recommended). Check for dry rot, sidewall bulges, or uneven wear patterns. Ensure matching tire brands and specs on the same axle.
*   **Body Panels:** Verify panel gaps are uniform; check doors, hood, and trunk for smooth opening/closing and secure latching.

## 2. Under the Hood (Mechanical Systems)
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer reservoir. Look for leaks or dark/sludgy fluid.
*   **Belts and Hoses:** Inspect serpentine belt for fraying or cracking. Check cooling hoses for bulges, leaks, or hardened rubber.
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion. Ensure the battery is securely mounted and the casing is not bloated.
*   **Engine Bay:** Look for fresh oil drips, coolant residue, or any signs of "wet" spots that indicate active leaks.

## 3. Interior and Electronics
*   **Upholstery:** Inspect seats for tears, stains, or worn springs. Check seat adjustment mechanisms.
*   **Dashboard and Controls:** Test A/C and heating systems at all fan speeds. Verify operation of radio, infotainment screen, and navigation.
*   **Warning Lights:** Ensure no "Check Engine," "ABS," "Airbag," or other malfunction indicators remain illuminated after engine start.
*   **Safety Features:** Ensure all seatbelts retract and lock properly. Check for functional door locks and window switches.

## 4. Operational/Road Test
*   **Braking:** Test for squealing, grinding, or steering wheel vibrations during moderate to firm braking.
*   **Steering:** Ensure the vehicle tracks straight on level ground without pulling to one side.
*   **Transmission:** Check for smooth gear shifts without hesitation, jerking, or slipping.
*   **Suspension:** Listen for clunking or rattling noises when traveling over speed bumps or uneven pavement.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Perform inspections in daylight or under high-intensity LED lighting. Hidden scratches and fluid leaks are often missed in dimly lit garages.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always check the spare tire or tire inflation kit. This is the most frequently overlooked item and a common failure point during roadside emergencies.
*   **Pitfall (Cold Engine):** Never skip the cold start. Start the engine after it has been sitting for several hours to listen for valve clatter or exhaust smoke (blue smoke indicates oil burning; white smoke may indicate head gasket issues).
*   **Pitfall (Cleaning Bias):** Do not let a clean, waxed exterior distract you from mechanical red flags. A "detailed" car can sometimes be used to mask long-term neglect.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should a comprehensive inspection be performed?**
A: For commercial or daily-use fleet vehicles, a formal inspection should occur every 5,000 miles or at every oil change, whichever comes first.

**Q: What is the most critical fluid to check?**
A: While all are important, engine oil is vital for longevity. If the oil is milky, it indicates internal coolant leakage, which is a major engine failure indicator.

**Q: Should I worry about small engine oil seepage?**
A: "Seepage" (light moisture) is often common in older vehicles; however, "leaks" (dripping onto the driveway or garage floor) should always be addressed immediately to prevent component failure.
