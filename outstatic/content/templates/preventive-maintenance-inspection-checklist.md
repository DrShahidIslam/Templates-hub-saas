# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance Inspection (PMI)

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for performing comprehensive Preventive Maintenance Inspections (PMI). The primary objective of this procedure is to proactively identify potential equipment failures, ensure operational longevity, verify safety compliance, and minimize unplanned downtime. All personnel executing these inspections must adhere strictly to these guidelines to ensure consistency, accuracy, and data integrity across all maintenance logs.

## 1. Preparation and Safety Protocols
*   **Documentation Review:** Review the specific equipment maintenance manual and previous inspection logs to identify recurring issues or specific manufacturer requirements.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Don appropriate PPE as dictated by the equipment’s hazard assessment (e.g., safety glasses, gloves, steel-toed boots, high-visibility vest).
*   **Energy Isolation:** Execute Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures before any internal component inspection to prevent accidental machine startup.
*   **Workplace Clearance:** Ensure the immediate area is free of debris, slip hazards, and unauthorized personnel.

## 2. External Visual and Structural Inspection
*   **Integrity Check:** Inspect the outer housing for cracks, dents, corrosion, or signs of structural deformation.
*   **Fasteners and Mounts:** Check all accessible bolts, screws, and mounting hardware for tightness; replace or tighten as required.
*   **Fluid Leakage:** Inspect the floor and equipment base for signs of oil, hydraulic fluid, or coolant leakage.
*   **Safety Guards/Labels:** Verify that all safety guarding is in place and that mandatory warning labels are legible and intact.

## 3. Operational and Functional Testing
*   **Power Supply:** Inspect electrical cables for fraying, exposed wiring, or heat damage. Ensure connections are secure.
*   **Startup/Shutdown Cycle:** Observe the equipment during startup to detect abnormal noise, vibration, or grinding.
*   **Control Interface:** Test all emergency stop buttons, indicator lights, and digital control panels for responsiveness.
*   **Calibration:** Verify that gauges, sensors, and meters are providing readings within the manufacturer’s specified tolerances.

## 4. Internal Component and Lubrication
*   **Lubrication Points:** Verify grease levels and apply lubricants according to the lubrication schedule; clean excess lubricant to prevent debris buildup.
*   **Filter Inspection:** Remove and inspect air, oil, and fuel filters. Replace if clogged or if the service interval has been reached.
*   **Drive Systems:** Inspect belts for tension and wear; inspect chains for slack and proper lubrication.
*   **Ventilation:** Clean intake vents and cooling fans to ensure adequate airflow and prevent overheating.

## 5. Reporting and Data Logging
*   **Maintenance Log:** Record all findings in the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS).
*   **Photo Evidence:** Attach high-resolution photos of any identified wear-and-tear or part replacements.
*   **Sign-off:** Finalize the inspection report with the date, technician name, and supervisor validation signature.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always practice "Clean-as-you-go." A clean machine is easier to inspect and reveals leaks or cracks much faster than a neglected one.
*   **Pro Tip:** Listen to your equipment. An experienced operator can often diagnose a failing bearing or loose tensioner by the change in acoustic signature before it appears in a sensor reading.
*   **Pitfall - The "Check-the-box" Mentality:** Rushing through an inspection without critical observation leads to "phantom maintenance," where failures occur shortly after an "all-clear" report.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Small Deviations:** Never dismiss a minor vibration or a slight temperature increase as "normal." These are almost always lead indicators of a larger, impending failure.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a Preventive Maintenance Inspection be performed?**
A: Frequency is determined by the manufacturer’s specifications and usage intensity. Generally, high-cycle equipment requires weekly or monthly inspections, while static or low-use equipment may only require quarterly or bi-annual checks.

**Q: What should I do if I find a defect that is not on the checklist?**
A: Add the finding as an "exception" in the remarks section of the digital log. If the defect poses an immediate safety risk, cease operation immediately and notify your supervisor.

**Q: Is it necessary to replace parts that are still functional during an inspection?**
A: Follow the manufacturer's "Replace by Time/Hour" intervals. Even if a part appears functional, internal wear may cause a sudden failure. Proactive replacement is significantly cheaper than the cost of emergency downtime.
