# Standard Operating Procedure: Ultrasound Preventive Maintenance (PM)

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance (PM) protocols for diagnostic ultrasound systems. Regular maintenance is essential to ensure patient safety, diagnostic image accuracy, electrical integrity, and the longevity of high-value imaging transducers. This procedure applies to all biomedical engineering staff and clinical engineering technicians responsible for fleet maintenance.

---

## Preventive Maintenance Checklist

### Section 1: Visual and Physical Inspection
*   **Chassis and Exterior:** Inspect the main console, monitor housing, and base for cracks, loose panels, or signs of physical impact.
*   **Casters and Locks:** Test all wheels for smooth rotation and verify that the brake/locking mechanism engages and disengages securely.
*   **Cables and Strain Reliefs:** Inspect all external power cords and peripheral cables for fraying, exposed wiring, or kinks.
*   **Control Panel/Keyboard:** Ensure all physical keys, trackballs, and touchscreens are responsive and free of fluid residue.
*   **Air Filters:** Remove and clean or replace intake/exhaust air filters to prevent internal overheating.

### Section 2: Electrical and Safety Testing
*   **Grounding Integrity:** Perform a chassis leakage current test and ground resistance test per IEC 60601-1 standards.
*   **Power Supply:** Verify the power-up sequence is stable; monitor for any abnormal fan noises or error codes during the boot-up phase.
*   **Battery Backup (If applicable):** Test the internal UPS/battery health to ensure it can support the system for the manufacturer-specified duration during a power loss.
*   **Peripheral Connectivity:** Verify that printers, DVD drives, and footswitches are properly recognized by the system.

### Section 3: Transducer and Image Quality Assessment
*   **Transducer Cable Integrity:** Inspect the entire length of each probe cable for "knots," crushed areas, or sheath damage.
*   **Connector Pins:** Inspect the transducer connector pins for bent, pushed-in, or corroded pins.
*   **Acoustic Lens Inspection:** Examine the probe face for bubbles, delamination, cuts, or "patchy" wear patterns.
*   **Image Artifact Check:** Using a standard tissue-mimicking phantom, assess for dead elements, shadowing, or dropouts.
*   **Calibration:** Verify that the system software reports accurate depth, measurement, and penetration settings.

### Section 4: Software and Documentation
*   **System Logs:** Review the system error log for recurring hardware alerts or software crashes.
*   **Software Updates:** Confirm that the firmware and application software versions meet current security and compliance standards.
*   **Data Integrity:** Ensure that patient data is being correctly pushed to the PACS/DICOM server.
*   **PM Tagging:** Apply the latest PM inspection sticker, record the completion date, and sign off in the CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **The "Water Sink" Test:** When inspecting transducer lenses, use a light coating of coupling gel; bubbles or uneven distribution on the lens often indicate microscopic delamination not visible to the naked eye.
*   **Scheduled Downtime:** Always coordinate PMs with clinical supervisors at least 48 hours in advance to prevent operational disruptions.
*   **Proactive Replacement:** If a transducer cable shows signs of "twisting" or "curling," advise the clinical team to stop using it immediately, as internal coaxial breakage is imminent.

### Pitfalls
*   **Over-Cleaning:** Never use alcohol or harsh chemicals on transducer faces unless explicitly approved by the manufacturer; this can damage the acoustic lens material.
*   **Compressed Air Misuse:** Be careful when using compressed air on internal components; ensure the system is powered off and that you do not force debris into the hard drive or sensitive optical components.
*   **Ignoring Fan Noise:** A loud fan is the most common early warning sign of impending power supply or motherboard failure due to thermal stress.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should ultrasound preventive maintenance be performed?**
Most manufacturers recommend bi-annual (every 6 months) PMs. However, systems in high-volume environments (e.g., Emergency Departments) may require quarterly inspections.

**2. What do I do if I find a "dead element" on a probe?**
A dead element manifests as a vertical black line in the image. If present, the transducer should be removed from clinical use and sent to a specialized probe repair facility or returned to the OEM for exchange.

**3. Is it necessary to perform electrical safety tests on a battery-operated portable unit?**
Yes. Even if the unit is running on battery power, the charger/docking station must be tested for ground integrity and leakage current whenever it is connected to mains power.
