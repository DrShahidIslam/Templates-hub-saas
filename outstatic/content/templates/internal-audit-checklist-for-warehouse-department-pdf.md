# Standard Operating Procedure: Warehouse Internal Audit

This document outlines the standard procedure for conducting a comprehensive internal audit of warehouse operations. The objective of this audit is to ensure inventory accuracy, optimize spatial utilization, verify adherence to health and safety protocols, and maintain operational compliance. By performing these systematic checks, management can mitigate shrinkage, prevent workplace accidents, and improve overall supply chain efficiency. All findings must be documented, and corrective action plans (CAPs) must be implemented for any identified non-conformities.

## 1. Inventory Management & Control
*   **Cycle Count Verification:** Compare system records (WMS/ERP) against physical stock counts for a random sample of SKUs.
*   **Stock Rotation Check:** Verify adherence to FEFO (First-Expired, First-Out) or FIFO protocols.
*   **Damaged/Obsolete Goods:** Ensure all damaged, expired, or unsellable stock is segregated in a designated "Quarantine Area" and correctly logged in the system.
*   **Unit of Measure (UOM) Accuracy:** Confirm that the physical labeling (eaches, cases, pallets) matches the system definition.
*   **Variance Analysis:** Audit the process for investigating and resolving stock discrepancies found during cycle counts.

## 2. Warehouse Safety & Regulatory Compliance
*   **PPE Adherence:** Confirm that all warehouse staff are wearing mandated personal protective equipment (safety shoes, high-visibility vests, etc.).
*   **Equipment Inspection:** Verify that daily pre-start inspection logs for forklifts, reach trucks, and pallet jacks are completed and filed.
*   **Clearance & Egress:** Inspect aisles for obstructions; ensure fire exits, extinguishers, and eye-wash stations are accessible and unblocked.
*   **Hazardous Materials:** Validate that SDS (Safety Data Sheets) are available and that hazardous materials are stored according to compatibility charts.
*   **Training Records:** Ensure all operators possess valid, up-to-date certifications for the machinery they are operating.

## 3. Facility Maintenance & Security
*   **Access Control:** Check that facility entry points (docks, pedestrian doors) are secure and that visitor logs are maintained.
*   **Storage Integrity:** Inspect pallet racking for structural damage, missing safety clips, or overloading.
*   **Lighting and Signage:** Verify that all warehouse areas are sufficiently lit and that safety/evacuation signage is visible and intact.
*   **Housekeeping:** Evaluate the cleanliness of floor spaces, charging stations, and waste disposal areas to prevent pest infestation or tripping hazards.

## 4. Shipping & Receiving Processes
*   **Documentation Audit:** Review a sample of Bill of Lading (BOL) and Packing Slip records to ensure they match outbound shipments.
*   **Receiving Workflow:** Observe the incoming goods inspection process to ensure quality control checks are performed before put-away.
*   **Staging Efficiency:** Check that staging lanes are clearly demarcated and that outbound orders are staged to prevent mixing.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Surprise Audit" Factor:** Conduct unannounced audits quarterly to capture the "true state" of the warehouse floor, rather than a polished version prepared specifically for a scheduled visit.
*   **Pro Tip: Leverage Technology:** Use handheld scanners to perform real-time verification during the audit to minimize manual data entry errors.
*   **Pitfall - The "Shelf-Blindness" Bias:** Auditors often become so familiar with their environment that they stop noticing safety hazards or organizational clutter. Consider rotating the auditor role between different department heads.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Root Causes:** Never settle for simply identifying a stock error. If a variance exists, trace the transaction history to identify *why* the error occurred (e.g., mislabeling at receiving) to prevent recurrence.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a full warehouse internal audit be conducted?**
While cycle counts should occur daily or weekly, a full operational audit covering safety, compliance, and processes should be performed at least quarterly to maintain ISO standards and operational integrity.

**2. What is the most common reason for inventory discrepancies?**
The most frequent cause is human error during the put-away or picking process, specifically failing to scan items into the correct location, leading to "ghost inventory."

**3. What should be done if a safety violation is discovered during the audit?**
Immediate action must be taken to mitigate the risk (e.g., stopping a damaged forklift). Document the incident, notify the safety officer, and ensure a corrective action is recorded and signed off by the warehouse manager within 24–48 hours.
