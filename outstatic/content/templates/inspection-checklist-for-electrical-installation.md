# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Installation Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for inspecting electrical installations to ensure compliance with national electrical codes, safety standards, and project specifications. The objective of this inspection is to verify that all components—ranging from grounding systems to final termination points—are installed correctly, securely, and in a manner that mitigates the risk of fire, arc flash, or equipment failure. Adherence to this checklist is mandatory for all site supervisors and electrical inspectors prior to system energization.

## Pre-Inspection Requirements
*   Verify that the installation corresponds to the latest approved electrical blueprints.
*   Ensure all installers have proper Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures in place if the system is partially energized.
*   Confirm that all required test equipment (Multimeter, Megohmmeter, Ground Resistance Tester) is calibrated and current.

## Phase 1: Grounding and Bonding
*   Verify that the Main Grounding Electrode Conductor (GEC) is securely clamped to the grounding electrode system.
*   Inspect all metallic enclosures and raceways for continuous bonding.
*   Check that bonding jumpers are installed across non-conductive gaps in conduit systems.
*   Ensure that all equipment grounding conductors are properly sized according to circuit amperage and NEC/local code requirements.

## Phase 2: Wiring and Raceway Systems
*   Verify that conduit runs are properly supported and strapped at required intervals.
*   Check that all conductors are of the correct gauge (AWG) and color-coded according to the installation schedule.
*   Confirm that no conductors exceed the allowable fill capacity for the specific raceway type.
*   Inspect wire terminations to ensure they are torqued to manufacturer specifications (use a calibrated torque wrench).
*   Verify that all wire connections are clean, free of oxidation, and properly protected by anti-oxidant compound where required.

## Phase 3: Panelboards and Overcurrent Protection
*   Check that the panelboard is mounted plumb and level.
*   Confirm that the panel directory is clearly labeled and matches the actual circuit configuration.
*   Verify that breakers are correctly sized and that the Interrupting Rating (AIC) meets the fault current requirements.
*   Ensure that there is adequate working clearance (typically 36 inches depth) in front of the panelboard.
*   Check for the presence of proper bushing/connectors where wires enter the panel cabinet to prevent insulation abrasion.

## Phase 4: Final Testing and Verification
*   **Continuity Test:** Ensure all circuits are continuous and free of unintended shorts to ground.
*   **Insulation Resistance Test (Megger):** Perform a megger test on feeder conductors to verify insulation integrity.
*   **Voltage Measurement:** Confirm phase-to-phase and phase-to-neutral voltages are within the specified tolerances (e.g., +/- 5%).
*   **Polarity Check:** Verify correct polarity for all receptacle outlets and confirm GFCI/AFCI functionality using an approved tester.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a torque screwdriver for terminal connections. "Hand tight" is rarely accurate and leads to high-resistance connections and potential fires.
*   **Pro Tip:** Document the installation with photos before closing wall cavities or ceiling grids; this simplifies future troubleshooting.
*   **Pitfall:** Overlooking "nuisance tripping." Ensure that shared neutrals are not being used across multi-wire branch circuits, which is a common cause of breaker instability.
*   **Pitfall:** Failure to tighten knockouts. Loose or missing locknuts on conduit entries result in poor grounding continuity, which can be catastrophic during a fault event.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I perform a visual inspection only?**
A: No. While visual inspection is critical, electrical installations must be verified through physical testing (continuity, insulation resistance, and voltage) to confirm internal integrity that cannot be seen.

**Q: What is the most common cause of inspection failure?**
A: Improper or undocumented grounding and bonding remains the most frequent point of failure. If the path to ground is high-impedance or discontinuous, the entire installation is considered non-compliant.

**Q: How often should I calibrate my testing equipment?**
A: Calibration should be performed annually or as specified by the manufacturer. Always check the calibration sticker on your tools before beginning the inspection process.
