# Standard Operating Procedure: Motorcycle Preventative Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory maintenance requirements to ensure the optimal performance, longevity, and safety of the motorcycle. Regular preventative maintenance is critical to mitigate mechanical failure, ensure rider safety, and maintain the vehicle's resale value. This checklist should be performed at the manufacturer-recommended intervals (typically every 3,000–5,000 miles) or before any long-distance expedition. Always consult your specific service manual for model-specific torque specifications and fluid capacities.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Check Preparation
*   Ensure the motorcycle is on a level surface and the engine is cool (unless checking oil levels as specified by the manual).
*   Place the bike on a center stand or paddock stand for stability.
*   Verify you have the correct tools, including a torque wrench, metric/standard socket set, and appropriate cleaning agents.
*   Clean the bike before starting to prevent debris from entering sensitive components.

## 2. Drivetrain and Chassis
*   **Drive Chain/Belt:** Inspect for proper tension (slack). Clean and lubricate the chain using a dedicated O-ring safe lubricant. Check sprockets for "hooked" or missing teeth.
*   **Tires:** Inspect tire pressure (cold) against the manufacturer’s sidewall specification. Check for uneven wear, dry rot, or embedded debris.
*   **Brakes:** Check brake pad thickness; replace if material is less than 2mm. Inspect rotors for scoring or warping. 
*   **Suspension:** Inspect fork seals for "weeping" or oil leaks. Check rear shock for signs of bottoming out or fluid leakage.

## 3. Fluids and Engine Performance
*   **Engine Oil:** Check oil levels via sight glass or dipstick. Inspect the oil color; if it appears milky (coolant contamination) or excessively metallic, seek professional diagnostic assistance.
*   **Coolant (Liquid-cooled only):** Check the coolant reservoir level. Never open a hot radiator cap. Ensure hoses are free of cracks or swelling.
*   **Air Filter:** Remove the airbox cover and inspect the filter element. If visible dust/debris is present, clean or replace it to ensure engine breathability.
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion. Ensure connections are tight. If the battery is non-sealed, check distilled water levels.

## 4. Electrical and Controls
*   **Lighting:** Test high/low beam, turn signals (front and rear), brake light (actuated by both front lever and rear pedal), and instrument cluster backlighting.
*   **Cables/Linkages:** Check throttle and clutch cables for fraying or binding. Lubricate pivot points on the kickstand, brake pedal, and clutch lever.
*   **Fasteners:** Conduct a "bolt check." Ensure critical fasteners—specifically axle nuts, caliper bolts, and handlebar clamps—are tightened to factory torque specifications.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **The "Gold Standard" Torque:** Never overtighten bolts by "feel." Always use a calibrated torque wrench. Stripping a thread in an aluminum engine case is a costly and preventable error.
*   **Avoid "Over-Lubing":** When cleaning your chain, avoid using degreasers that degrade rubber O-rings. When lubricating, apply a thin coat; excess grease acts as a magnet for road grit, which accelerates sprocket wear.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Brake Fluid:** Many riders neglect brake fluid, which is hygroscopic (absorbs water). Old fluid causes a "spongy" lever and can corrode the internal brake system over time. Replace fluid every two years.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a digital service log. Note the mileage, date, and parts replaced. This is invaluable for troubleshooting and essential for preserving resale value.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I check my tire pressure?**
A: You should check your tire pressure at least once every two weeks or before every long ride. Tires lose pressure naturally over time, and incorrect pressure significantly impacts handling and fuel economy.

**Q: Can I use automotive motor oil in my motorcycle?**
A: Generally, no. Many motorcycles use a "wet clutch" that shares oil with the engine. Automotive oils often contain "friction modifiers" that can cause your clutch to slip. Always use oil specifically labeled for motorcycles (JASO MA/MA2 rating).

**Q: My brake lever feels "mushy." Is that normal?**
A: No. A mushy or soft lever usually indicates air in the hydraulic lines or degraded brake fluid. This is a critical safety issue; you must bleed the brake system or have it serviced by a professional before riding.
