# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance Schedule (PMS) Management

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for developing, implementing, and monitoring a Preventive Maintenance Schedule (PMS) chart. The objective of this procedure is to minimize unplanned equipment downtime, extend asset lifecycle, ensure regulatory compliance, and optimize maintenance labor costs. By maintaining a living, data-driven schedule, the organization transitions from a reactive “break-fix” culture to a proactive maintenance strategy, ensuring operational continuity and safety.

## Phase 1: Asset Inventory and Criticality Assessment
*   **Compile Master Asset List:** Extract all equipment data from the CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) or inventory logs, including make, model, serial number, and location.
*   **Determine Criticality Scores:** Assign each asset a criticality rating (1 to 5) based on production impact, safety risks, and replacement costs. 
*   **Define Maintenance Requirements:** Consult Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) manuals to extract specific service intervals, lubrication schedules, and component replacement timelines.
*   **Standardize Data Entry:** Ensure all assets are categorized by type (e.g., HVAC, Conveyor, Robotics) to facilitate bulk scheduling.

## Phase 2: Building the Maintenance Schedule Chart
*   **Establish Intervals:** Categorize tasks into daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, semi-annual, and annual buckets.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Map specific technician skill sets to task requirements to ensure the right personnel are assigned to the right assets.
*   **Sequence the Work:** Organize tasks to group geographically related equipment to minimize technician travel time within the facility.
*   **Define Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):** Set targets for Schedule Compliance, Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF), and Maintenance Backlog levels within the chart framework.

## Phase 3: Execution and Monitoring
*   **Dissemination:** Distribute the PMS chart to the maintenance team via the digital dashboard or printed shop-floor master board.
*   **Execution Tracking:** Require technicians to log start/stop times and parts consumed upon task completion.
*   **Verification:** Conduct supervisor spot-checks on completed work orders to ensure quality standards meet SOP guidelines.
*   **Reporting:** Review the PMS chart weekly during operations meetings to identify overdue tasks and address resource constraints immediately.

## Phase 4: Continuous Improvement and Audit
*   **Feedback Loop:** Collect technician input on manual procedures; if an OEM interval is found to be excessive or insufficient, trigger a formal review.
*   **Seasonal Adjustments:** Update the PMS chart quarterly to account for environmental factors (e.g., summer humidity or winter temperature fluctuations).
*   **Annual Audit:** Conduct an end-of-year review of the PMS chart against total asset downtime to identify trends and justify future capital expenditure or retirement of obsolete equipment.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Color Coding:** Use a high-visibility color-coding system (e.g., Red for daily/critical, Blue for monthly, Green for annual) to provide visual cues for immediate status awareness.
*   **Mobile Integration:** Use QR codes on assets that link directly to the PMS digital chart, allowing technicians to pull up instructions on handheld tablets.
*   **Buffer Time:** Build a 10-15% buffer into the schedule for emergency "walk-in" repairs so the core preventive schedule remains protected.

### Pitfalls to Avoid
*   **The "Paper Tiger" Trap:** Avoid creating a complex spreadsheet that is never updated. If a task isn't logged, it didn't happen.
*   **Ignoring OEM Data:** Never adjust intervals based on "gut feeling" without a documented trial period; always defer to OEM requirements for warranty compliance.
*   **Over-Maintenance:** Avoid performing service too frequently. "Tinkering" with equipment that is running perfectly can introduce human error and accelerate wear.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What is the difference between Preventive Maintenance and Predictive Maintenance?**
Preventive maintenance is time or usage-based (e.g., changing oil every 500 hours). Predictive maintenance uses sensor data (e.g., vibration analysis or thermography) to perform maintenance only when conditions indicate a failure is imminent.

**2. How should I handle a task that is consistently overdue?**
If a task is consistently missed, re-evaluate the resource allocation or the complexity of the task. It may be that the task requires a specialist contractor rather than in-house staff, or the interval is unrealistic for current operational demand.

**3. Should every piece of equipment be on a preventive schedule?**
No. Low-criticality assets that are inexpensive to replace and have zero impact on production or safety should be run to failure. Focus your PMS resources on high-value, high-criticality assets to maximize ROI.
