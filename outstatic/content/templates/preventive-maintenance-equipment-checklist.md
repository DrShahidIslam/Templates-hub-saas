# Standard Operating Procedure: Equipment Preventive Maintenance (PM)

Preventive maintenance (PM) is a systematic approach to equipment care designed to extend asset life, ensure operational safety, and minimize costly downtime. This SOP establishes a standardized framework for inspecting, cleaning, and servicing machinery. Adherence to these procedures ensures that all critical equipment remains in optimal working condition, reduces the frequency of emergency repairs, and maintains compliance with safety and regulatory standards.

## 1. Preparation and Safety Protocols
*   **Documentation Review:** Consult the specific Equipment Maintenance Manual (EMM) or digital CMMS profile for the asset before beginning.
*   **Lockout/Tagout (LOTO):** Strictly adhere to LOTO procedures. Ensure all energy sources (electrical, pneumatic, hydraulic) are isolated and secured before touching internal components.
*   **PPE Verification:** Don appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) as dictated by the site safety plan (e.g., safety glasses, cut-resistant gloves, steel-toed boots).
*   **Workspace Staging:** Clear the area around the equipment to allow for full access to maintenance panels and underside components.

## 2. Visual and Functional Inspection
*   **External Integrity:** Inspect housing, frames, and protective guards for cracks, deformation, or missing fasteners.
*   **Power Supply Check:** Examine power cords for fraying, exposed wiring, or scorched insulation. Ensure plugs are secure and contacts are free of corrosion.
*   **Operational Sound Check:** Listen for abnormal vibrations, grinding, or high-pitched whining during start-up.
*   **Indicator Verification:** Test all emergency stops, warning lights, and digital readouts for accuracy and responsiveness.

## 3. Cleaning and Lubrication
*   **Debris Removal:** Use compressed air or appropriate vacuums to remove dust, metal shavings, or accumulated residue from cooling fans and air vents.
*   **Component Sanitization:** Wipe down touchscreens and control interfaces with manufacturer-approved cleaning agents.
*   **Lubrication Application:** Apply specified lubricants to gears, drive chains, and sliding tracks. Wipe away excess grease to prevent dust accumulation.
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check hydraulic fluid, oil, or coolant levels; top off using only the manufacturer-recommended specifications.

## 4. Calibration and Fastening
*   **Tightening Sequence:** Perform a "torque check" on all critical mounting bolts and housing screws using a calibrated torque wrench.
*   **Alignment Verification:** Ensure drive belts, chains, and shafts are properly aligned and tensioned according to the EMM.
*   **System Calibration:** Run the machine through its diagnostic cycle to ensure sensors, limits, and throughput speeds match baseline calibration settings.

## 5. Documentation and Sign-off
*   **Log Completion:** Update the physical maintenance log or digital CMMS with the date, technician name, and specific actions taken.
*   **Parts Tracking:** Record any parts replaced (filters, belts, fuses) to maintain accurate inventory levels.
*   **Deficiency Reporting:** If a repair is required beyond the scope of PM, immediately flag the equipment as "Out of Service" and initiate a formal work order.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Clean-As-You-Go" Rule:** Always maintain a clean workspace throughout the PM process. Grease and loose debris are the primary catalysts for equipment failure and technician injury.
*   **Pro Tip: Use Predictive Data:** If the equipment has sensors, check the last 30 days of data logs *before* starting the physical inspection to identify specific areas of instability.
*   **Pitfall: Over-Lubrication:** A common error is applying too much grease, which can overheat bearings and attract contaminants. Always follow the exact volume specified in the manual.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Anomalies:** Do not dismiss a minor "rattle" or a flickering display light. These are often the precursors to major mechanical failures.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should we update our PM checklist?**
A: Review and update your checklists annually or whenever a manufacturer issues a service bulletin, equipment is upgraded, or your facility safety standards change.

**Q: What should I do if I find a part is worn but not yet broken?**
A: Record the wear in the "Notes" section of your maintenance log. If the failure is imminent, schedule a repair during the next scheduled downtime to avoid emergency interruptions.

**Q: Can I use generic lubricants to save on costs?**
A: Never use generic lubricants unless explicitly stated as equivalent in the manual. Using incorrect viscosity or chemical compositions can void warranties and cause premature component failure.
