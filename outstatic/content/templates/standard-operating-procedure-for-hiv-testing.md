# Standard Operating Procedure: HIV Diagnostic Testing

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for performing HIV diagnostic testing in a clinical or community-based setting. The objective of this procedure is to ensure the highest standards of patient safety, diagnostic accuracy, data privacy, and ethical counseling. All personnel involved in the testing process must adhere strictly to Universal Precautions and maintain patient confidentiality in accordance with local health regulations and HIPAA/GDPR standards.

## 1. Pre-Test Preparation and Counseling
*   **Verification of Identity:** Confirm the patient’s identity using two forms of identification.
*   **Informed Consent:** Clearly explain the nature of the test, the limitations of the window period, and the voluntary nature of the procedure. Obtain written or electronic informed consent.
*   **Risk Assessment:** Conduct a brief, non-judgmental interview to assess recent exposure history and provide risk-reduction education.
*   **Environment Setup:** Ensure the testing area is clean, private, and equipped with a biohazard disposal container and appropriate PPE (gloves, lab coats).
*   **Documentation:** Prepare the patient file, ensuring all demographic information is recorded accurately.

## 2. Specimen Collection and Processing
*   **Hand Hygiene:** Perform thorough hand washing and don non-sterile gloves.
*   **Sample Acquisition:** Following the specific manufacturer’s instructions for the assay kit (e.g., finger-prick capillary blood, oral fluid, or venous draw), collect the specimen.
*   **Labeling:** Immediately label the sample with the unique patient identifier and time of collection to prevent misidentification.
*   **Assay Execution:** Apply the specimen to the test device immediately. Do not use test kits beyond their expiration date or those stored outside of the required temperature range.
*   **Timing:** Use a reliable timer to monitor the development time indicated by the manufacturer. Do not read results before or after the designated window.

## 3. Results Interpretation and Reporting
*   **Reading Results:** Observe the control line (C) first. If the control line is absent, the test is invalid, and a new test must be performed.
*   **Documentation:** Record the result (Non-Reactive, Reactive, or Invalid) in the patient’s confidential record immediately.
*   **Post-Test Counseling:**
    *   **Non-Reactive:** Explain the "window period" and advise on future preventive behaviors.
    *   **Reactive:** Maintain a calm, supportive demeanor. Explain that a reactive result is a preliminary finding and requires a secondary, laboratory-based confirmatory test (e.g., Western Blot or Nucleic Acid Test).
*   **Referral:** Provide the patient with clear, actionable information regarding confirmatory testing sites and immediate linkage to HIV care providers.

## 4. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - The Window Period:** Never tell a patient they are "definitely" HIV-negative if they have had a high-risk exposure within the last 3 months. Advise re-testing after the window period has closed.
*   **Pro Tip - Control Lines:** Always inspect the integrity of the test cassette before use. A missing control line is the most common cause of diagnostic error.
*   **Pitfall - Confidentiality Breaches:** Never discuss patient results in common areas or within earshot of other patients. Always use secure, encrypted digital filing systems.
*   **Pro Tip - Empathy:** The moment a patient receives a "Reactive" result is high-stress. Use clear, simple language and avoid medical jargon. Have a "Resources Packet" ready to hand them immediately.

## 5. FAQ
**Q: What should I do if the test result is "Invalid"?**
A: An invalid result means the test kit failed to function correctly. Do not attempt to interpret the T-line. Dispose of the invalid kit as biohazardous waste, document the error, and perform a new test using a fresh kit and a new specimen.

**Q: Can a patient be tested if they are on PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis)?**
A: Yes, but it is important to note that PrEP use does not interfere with the detection of HIV antibodies in a rapid test, though it may occasionally suppress viral load markers in confirmatory tests. Always document current medication use during pre-test counseling.

**Q: How should I handle a patient who becomes visibly distressed after a reactive result?**
A: Prioritize the patient’s immediate mental health. Remain in the room, offer privacy, and provide contact information for local mental health crisis resources or specialized counselors. Do not rush the patient out of the clinic.
