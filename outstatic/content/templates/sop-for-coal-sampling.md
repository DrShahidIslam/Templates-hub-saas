# Standard Operating Procedure: Coal Sampling and Quality Assurance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the representative sampling of coal stockpiles, rail cars, and conveyor belts. The objective of this process is to ensure the collection of unbiased, statistically significant samples that accurately reflect the chemical and physical properties (calorific value, moisture, ash, and sulfur content) of the total coal batch. Adherence to this SOP is critical for contractual compliance, quality control, and operational safety.

## 1. Pre-Sampling Preparation and Safety
*   Verify all personnel possess valid PPE: Hard hat, high-visibility vest, steel-toed boots, safety glasses, and hearing protection.
*   Ensure a calibrated sampling probe, shovel, or mechanical cross-belt sampler is operational and clean.
*   Prepare clean, sealable, moisture-proof containers (e.g., heavy-duty plastic bags or airtight drums) labeled with a unique Batch ID.
*   Confirm atmospheric conditions are safe for fieldwork; avoid sampling during heavy precipitation unless using sheltered automated systems.

## 2. Sampling Execution (Manual Method)
*   **Incremental Selection:** Ensure samples are taken at regular intervals across the entire shipment or pile to avoid bias toward top-layer particles.
*   **Grid Pattern:** For stockpiles, utilize a systematic grid sampling pattern. Remove the top 6 inches of weathered coal before extracting the sample to ensure core representation.
*   **Depth Penetration:** Insert the sampling probe or shovel to a depth of at least 18–24 inches to retrieve a representative cross-section of the coal size distribution.
*   **Mass Requirements:** Collect sufficient total mass as per the relevant ASTM or ISO standard (typically 5–10kg minimum, depending on top size).
*   **Sample Reduction:** If the gross sample exceeds lab requirements, utilize a mechanical riffle splitter or the "cone and quarter" method to reduce the sample size to a manageable volume without introducing bias.

## 3. Sample Preservation and Chain of Custody
*   **Moisture Retention:** Seal containers immediately after collection to prevent loss of inherent moisture, which significantly impacts analytical results.
*   **Labeling:** Affix tamper-evident labels detailing the Sample ID, Date/Time, Location, Operator Name, and Batch/Reference Number.
*   **Chain of Custody:** Log the sample in the digital tracking system and sign the physical Chain of Custody (CoC) form.
*   **Transport:** Store samples in a climate-controlled environment, away from direct sunlight or extreme heat, until they are transported to the laboratory.

## 4. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always clean your equipment thoroughly between samples. Cross-contamination from high-ash or high-sulfur coal residues can invalidate the analytical results of the next batch.
*   **Pitfall - The "Segregation" Trap:** Avoid grabbing only "fines" or only "lumps." Coal naturally segregates during handling; aim for a mix that represents the visual distribution of the entire pile.
*   **Pro Tip:** Conduct a duplicate sampling run every 20 samples to verify the repeatability of your collection method.
*   **Pitfall - Surface Bias:** Never sample exclusively from the surface. Rain and oxidation alter the chemical composition of surface coal compared to the bulk interior.

## 5. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I calibrate my sampling equipment?**
A: Mechanical sampling systems should be calibrated quarterly. Manual tools (probes) should be inspected for structural integrity and dimension accuracy before every shift.

**Q: What is the biggest risk factor for inaccurate coal analysis?**
A: Moisture loss. If the sample is not sealed properly immediately upon collection, the moisture content—a key metric in determining the coal's net calorific value—will be incorrectly reported.

**Q: Can I use a regular garden shovel for manual sampling?**
A: No. You must use a standard, calibrated scoop or a dedicated sampling probe that complies with ASTM D2234 or ISO 18283 standards to ensure the correct volume-to-particle-size ratio.
