# Standard Operating Procedure: Quality Control Inspection (Japan Market)

This document outlines the professional standards for conducting a comprehensive inspection of goods manufactured or sourced for the Japanese market. Japan is renowned for having the world's most stringent quality control (QC) expectations, where even minor cosmetic imperfections are often classified as critical defects. This SOP ensures that all products meet the "Omotenashi" standard of perfection and comply with local regulatory requirements, including Consumer Product Safety Act (CPSA) compliance and labeling precision.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   **Documentation Review:** Verify the Technical Specifications, Bill of Materials (BOM), and the Approved Sample (Golden Sample).
*   **Regulatory Alignment:** Confirm the shipment complies with the Japan Household Goods Quality Labeling Act. Ensure all labels are in Japanese (Katakana/Kanji) where applicable.
*   **Tool Calibration:** Ensure digital calipers, colorimeters, light boxes (D65 light source), and torque testers are calibrated and date-stamped.
*   **AQL Determination:** Establish the Acceptable Quality Level (AQL). For the Japanese market, a "Tightened" inspection level (typically S-3 or Level II with stricter zero-tolerance for critical defects) is recommended.

## Phase 2: On-Site Execution
*   **Visual Inspection (The "Three-Foot Rule"):** Inspect the product from a distance of 30-50cm under 1000 lux illumination. Any visible scratch, dust inclusion, or uneven paint must be flagged.
*   **Functional Testing:** Perform cycle tests (e.g., button presses, hinge durability) based on the manufacturer’s design life expectancy. 
*   **Packaging Compliance:** 
    *   Verify box integrity (no crushed corners).
    *   Check for the presence of silica gel packets.
    *   Ensure the Japanese instruction manual is error-free and contains correct contact information for local support.
*   **Barcode Verification:** Scan every SKU-linked barcode to ensure 100% readability by Japanese Point-of-Sale (POS) systems.

## Phase 3: Documentation and Reporting
*   **Defect Categorization:** Classify findings into Critical (safety/regulatory), Major (functional failure), and Minor (cosmetic). 
*   **Photographic Evidence:** Capture high-resolution images of all defects against a neutral background with a scale reference.
*   **Final Grading:** Issue a "Pass," "Pass with Minor Observations," or "Fail." A "Fail" requires a mandatory Root Cause Analysis (RCA) and a secondary inspection before shipment.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Shadow" Test:** Use a flashlight held at a 45-degree angle to the product surface to highlight micro-scratches in plastic or metal housings that are invisible under direct overhead light.
*   **Pro Tip: Cultural Context:** Japanese retailers prioritize the "unboxing experience." Even if the product functions perfectly, a scuffed inner tray or a misaligned manual will lead to high return rates.
*   **Pitfall: Language Neglect:** Do not use machine-translated Japanese for manuals or labels. Always employ a native speaker or a specialized technical translation agency to avoid embarrassing or confusing labeling errors.
*   **Pitfall: Assuming Global Standards:** Never assume that an AQL 2.5/4.0 standard is sufficient. Many Japanese buyers expect an AQL 0.65/1.0 or higher.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the most common reason for inspection failure in Japan?**
A: Minor cosmetic defects (scratches/smudges) and labeling errors. Japanese consumers hold a high aesthetic expectation; a product that works perfectly but looks "used" will be returned immediately.

**Q: Should I perform a pre-shipment inspection or a during-production inspection?**
A: For new suppliers, perform a "During Production" inspection to catch systemic issues early. For established suppliers, a final "Pre-shipment" inspection is mandatory to ensure the finalized, packaged goods are pristine.

**Q: How should I handle a disagreement with a factory regarding a "minor" defect?**
A: Always refer back to the "Golden Sample." If the defect is not present on the signed, approved sample, it must be addressed regardless of the factory's claim that it is "industry standard." In Japan, the "industry standard" is defined by the buyer's quality manual, not the factory's convenience.
