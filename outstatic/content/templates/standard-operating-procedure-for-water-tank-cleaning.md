# Standard Operating Procedure: Potable Water Tank Maintenance and Sanitation

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the cleaning, disinfection, and maintenance of potable water storage tanks. The objective is to eliminate microbial contamination, sediment accumulation, and biofilm growth to ensure water quality meets regulatory health and safety standards. All personnel performing these tasks must adhere to personal protective equipment (PPE) requirements and confined space entry protocols to prevent injury and contamination.

## Pre-Cleaning Preparations
*   **Safety Review:** Ensure all personnel are trained in confined space entry. Verify that oxygen levels and gas concentrations are safe for entry.
*   **Notification:** Notify all stakeholders, tenants, or facility users at least 48 hours in advance regarding the planned water supply interruption.
*   **Water Bypass:** Engage the bypass valve or activate auxiliary supply systems to maintain minimal service if applicable.
*   **Isolation:** Close the inlet valve completely to prevent water from entering the tank during the cleaning process.
*   **Drainage:** Drain the tank entirely using the scour valve. Ensure the discharge is directed to an approved drainage area to prevent environmental runoff issues.

## Cleaning and Sanitation Procedure
*   **Debris Removal:** Manually remove all loose sediment, silt, and sludge from the floor of the tank using non-corrosive, food-grade tools.
*   **Wall Scrubbing:** Scrub the interior walls, ceiling, and floor using stiff-bristled, food-grade brushes. Use a mild, food-safe detergent if necessary, ensuring no abrasive materials damage the tank lining.
*   **Rinsing:** Thoroughly rinse all interior surfaces with clean, potable water. Remove all wash water and debris through the drainage outlet.
*   **Disinfection:** Apply a food-grade chlorine solution (typically 50-100 ppm) to all interior surfaces using a pressure sprayer. Ensure complete coverage, including corners and piping connections.
*   **Contact Time:** Allow the disinfectant to dwell for the manufacturer-recommended contact time (usually 30 to 60 minutes).
*   **Final Flush:** Drain the disinfectant solution completely. Flush the tank with fresh potable water until the residual chlorine levels match the incoming supply levels.

## Restoration and Quality Assurance
*   **Inspection:** Conduct a final visual inspection to ensure the tank is free of debris, biofilm, and cleaning agent residues.
*   **Re-sealing:** Secure all manhole covers, gaskets, and access hatches. Ensure vent screens are intact and undamaged to prevent pest ingress.
*   **Refilling:** Open the inlet valve slowly to fill the tank, preventing pressure surges.
*   **Water Testing:** Collect water samples for bacteriological analysis by an accredited laboratory to verify that the water is safe for consumption.
*   **Documentation:** Update the maintenance logbook, noting the date, cleaning method, disinfectant used, technician name, and final test results.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a dedicated set of "potable-only" tools that are never used for general maintenance or exterior cleaning to prevent cross-contamination.
*   **Pro Tip:** Inspect the tank’s internal ladder and structural supports for signs of rust or corrosion during the cleaning process.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-chlorinating the tank can damage protective liners or stainless steel components. Always verify the ppm concentration before application.
*   **Pitfall:** Failure to properly seal manhole covers or vent screens is the #1 cause of post-cleaning contamination from insects and airborne dust.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a water tank be cleaned?**
A: Industry standards generally recommend a deep cleaning and disinfection at least once every 12 months. If the tank is located in a high-temperature environment or shows signs of rapid sediment buildup, semi-annual cleaning is advised.

**Q: Can I use high-pressure washers inside the tank?**
A: Use caution. High-pressure cleaning can damage internal protective coatings or liners. If pressure washing, use a low-to-medium pressure setting and ensure the nozzle is compatible with potable water infrastructure.

**Q: When is it safe to resume water service?**
A: Water service should only resume after the tank has been rinsed, refilled, and cleared by a successful bacteriological test result from a certified laboratory. Never rely on smell or appearance alone to determine potability.
