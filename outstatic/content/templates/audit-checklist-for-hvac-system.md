# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC System Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting a professional audit of Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems. The objective of this audit is to ensure optimal operational efficiency, maintain indoor air quality (IAQ), extend equipment service life, and ensure strict compliance with health and safety regulations. This checklist is intended for facility managers and maintenance technicians to execute scheduled performance evaluations to identify potential mechanical failures before they impact business operations.

## 1. Safety and Documentation Review
*   **Permit Verification:** Ensure all applicable permits for site access and work-at-heights are valid and signed.
*   **PPE Compliance:** Verify all personnel are equipped with required PPE, including gloves, safety glasses, and hard hats.
*   **Logbook Audit:** Review the maintenance logbook for the previous 12 months to identify recurring issues or "nuisance" alarms.
*   **Regulatory Compliance:** Confirm that refrigerant logs are up to date and that the facility is compliant with local environmental disposal regulations.

## 2. Air Handling Units (AHUs) & Filtration
*   **Filter Integrity:** Inspect all primary and secondary filters for dust buildup, tears, or bypass leakage; document pressure drop readings.
*   **Coil Inspection:** Check evaporator and condenser coils for oxidation, debris, or biological growth; verify fin straightness.
*   **Drain Pan & Condensate Line:** Inspect drain pans for standing water, sludge, or blockages; ensure P-traps are primed and venting correctly.
*   **Blower Assemblies:** Inspect fan blades for dirt accumulation (which causes imbalance) and check belt tension/wear.

## 3. Mechanical & Electrical Systems
*   **Compressor Performance:** Monitor suction and discharge pressures; verify they are within manufacturer-specified operational ranges.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Use an infrared thermographic camera to scan electrical panels and contactors for "hot spots" indicative of loose wiring.
*   **Motor Health:** Listen for abnormal bearing noise or vibration; verify motor amperage draw against the nameplate rating.
*   **Refrigerant Levels:** Check for signs of refrigerant leaks (oil spots around fittings) and verify subcooling/superheat values.

## 4. Control Systems & Calibration
*   **Thermostat Accuracy:** Compare wall-mounted sensor readings against a calibrated handheld digital thermometer.
*   **Sequence of Operations:** Trigger a manual override to ensure dampers, actuators, and valves respond correctly to commands.
*   **Building Management System (BMS) Integration:** Verify that alarms are correctly routed to the central monitoring station and that setpoints match facility requirements.
*   **Economizer Functionality:** Confirm that outside air dampers open and close based on enthalpy sensors to maximize "free cooling" when appropriate.

## 5. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The Infrared Advantage):** Always use a thermal imaging camera during your audit. You can detect failing contactors, loose electrical lugs, and restricted airflow patterns in minutes, which are invisible to the naked eye.
*   **Pro Tip (Log Trends):** Don't just look at the current reading; look at the trend. If the current draw is increasing by 5% every quarter, a motor or bearing failure is imminent, even if it is currently "within range."
*   **Pitfall (The "Filter Trap"):** Many auditors replace filters but fail to inspect the housing frame. If the frame is bent or the seal is missing, air will bypass the filter, rendering the clean media useless.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring Vibration):** Never ignore subtle vibrations in roof-top units. They are the leading indicator of blower shaft misalignment or motor mount fatigue, which leads to catastrophic failure if left unaddressed.

## 6. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a comprehensive HVAC audit be performed?**
A: For commercial facilities, a comprehensive audit should be performed at least semi-annually (before the heating and cooling seasons). High-traffic or critical environments (like server rooms) require quarterly audits.

**Q: What is the most critical item to check during an HVAC audit?**
A: While all components are vital, the "Airflow and Filtration" section is paramount. Restricted airflow is the leading cause of compressor failure, evaporator coil freezing, and poor indoor air quality.

**Q: If my audit reveals a minor refrigerant leak, should I just top it off?**
A: No. Under environmental regulations (such as the Clean Air Act in the US), you are legally required to find and repair the source of a leak rather than simply topping off the charge. Repeated topping off is both illegal and a sign of poor maintenance management.
