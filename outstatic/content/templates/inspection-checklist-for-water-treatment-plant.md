# Standard Operating Procedure: Water Treatment Plant Inspection

## Overview
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection protocols for a municipal water treatment facility. The primary objective of this inspection is to ensure compliance with health and safety standards, verify the integrity of mechanical and electrical infrastructure, and guarantee the delivery of safe, potable water. This procedure is designed to be performed daily by the Lead Operator or designated technician to maintain operational continuity and regulatory readiness.

## 1. Safety and PPE Verification
*   Confirm availability of calibrated multi-gas monitors (H2S, O2, LEL, CO).
*   Verify personal protective equipment (PPE) is on-site and in good condition (hard hats, safety glasses, steel-toed boots, high-visibility vests).
*   Check that all emergency stop buttons and pull-cords are unobstructed.
*   Ensure First Aid kits are stocked and fire extinguishers are within inspection expiration dates.

## 2. Influent and Pre-Treatment Inspection
*   **Intake Screens:** Inspect for debris, blockages, or excessive vegetative matter.
*   **Raw Water Pumps:** Check for unusual vibration, leaks at mechanical seals, or abnormal motor temperatures.
*   **Chemical Dosing (Pre-treatment):** Verify flow rates for coagulants and polymers; inspect pump tubing for leaks or crystallization.
*   **Flow Meters:** Record raw water inflow data and compare against historical benchmarks.

## 3. Filtration and Clarification Systems
*   **Clarifier Basins:** Observe sludge blanket levels and verify scrapers/collectors are moving smoothly without skipping.
*   **Filter Media:** Monitor differential pressure (head loss) across sand/anthracite filters. 
*   **Backwash Operations:** Verify backwash valve sequence and ensure clear effluent discharge during the cycle.
*   **Turbidity:** Cross-reference online turbidity readings with manual grab sample results.

## 4. Disinfection and Chemical Storage
*   **Chlorine/Disinfection Systems:** Inspect gas injectors or liquid feeders for leaks (test with ammonia vapor if applicable).
*   **Chemical Storage Tanks:** Check secondary containment areas for spills, leaks, or accumulated rainwater.
*   **Safety Interlocks:** Test low/high chlorine residual alarms and verify auto-shutoff functionality.
*   **Tank Levels:** Verify physical chemical levels match the telemetry/SCADA inputs.

## 5. Electrical and SCADA Infrastructure
*   **Control Panels:** Check for error codes, tripped breakers, or blown fuses.
*   **SCADA Interface:** Confirm all sensors are communicating correctly; ensure no "flatlined" readings.
*   **HVAC and Lighting:** Verify room temperatures (critical for electronic longevity) and confirm all emergency lighting is operational.
*   **Backup Power:** Inspect the generator fuel level, battery voltage, and "Auto-Start" status.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The "Listen" Test):** Never underestimate the human ear. A failing bearing often produces a high-pitched whine or a rhythmic thumping long before it triggers a vibration sensor. Conduct a "silent walk-through" where you switch off unnecessary equipment to listen to each motor.
*   **Pro Tip (The "Look Up" Habit):** Operators often focus on floor-level equipment. Develop the habit of scanning ceilings and overhead piping for condensation, slow drips, or sagging conduit supports.
*   **Pitfall (Alarm Fatigue):** Ignoring recurring minor alarms leads to complacency. Treat every alarm as a genuine failure until proven otherwise.
*   **Pitfall (Neglecting Documentation):** If it isn't documented in the logbook or digital management system, it didn't happen. In the event of a regulatory audit, gaps in your inspection history are treated as operational negligence.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I calibrate the sensors if readings seem stable?**
A: Regardless of apparent stability, adhere strictly to the manufacturer's calibration schedule (typically monthly or quarterly). Drift is often subtle and can lead to regulatory non-compliance before it becomes visibly obvious.

**Q: What is the most common cause of premature pump failure in treatment plants?**
A: Poorly maintained mechanical seals and alignment issues. Always prioritize the lubrication schedule and ensure shaft alignment is checked after any major maintenance or vibration events.

**Q: What should I do if a chemical containment area has a spill?**
A: Immediately initiate the Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) protocol. Do not attempt to clean up chemicals until the source of the leak is isolated and you are wearing the appropriate chemical-resistant PPE. Report the incident to your supervisor immediately for environmental record-keeping.
