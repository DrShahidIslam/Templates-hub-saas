# Standard Operating Procedure: Trust Audit Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for conducting a comprehensive audit of a trust. The objective is to ensure that all fiduciary duties, administrative mandates, and regulatory requirements are being executed in accordance with the trust instrument and applicable jurisdictional law. This audit is designed to mitigate legal risk, ensure tax compliance, and verify that the trustee is acting in the best interest of the beneficiaries.

## Phase 1: Documentation and Instrument Review
*   Obtain the original Trust Instrument, including all amendments, restatements, and codicils.
*   Verify the existence of the Certificate of Trust and confirm current successor trustee status.
*   Review the original "Letter of Wishes" or ancillary documents that guide discretionary distributions.
*   Confirm the presence of valid tax identification numbers (EIN/TIN) and relevant filings (Form 1041).

## Phase 2: Asset Verification and Valuation
*   Reconcile the opening balance (per the trust instrument or previous audit) with current holdings.
*   Perform a physical or digital verification of assets (real estate deeds, brokerage statements, private business interests).
*   Review independent appraisals for non-liquid assets to ensure they are current within the last 24 months.
*   Assess insurance coverage for high-value physical assets (e.g., property, fine art) to ensure the trust is named as the loss payee.
*   Audit the investment portfolio against the Investment Policy Statement (IPS) to ensure compliance with the Prudent Investor Rule.

## Phase 3: Fiduciary and Distributive Audit
*   Audit all discretionary distributions made during the period; verify they align with the "Ascertainable Standards" (Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support - HEMS).
*   Check for proof of receipt or acknowledgment from beneficiaries for all distributions.
*   Review board minutes or trustee notes to document the rationale behind significant discretionary decisions.
*   Verify that mandatory distributions (if applicable) were executed in the correct amount and timeframe.

## Phase 4: Compliance and Regulatory Reporting
*   Review all state and federal tax returns for the audit period.
*   Validate that the trustee has maintained a clear separation between trust assets and personal assets (Anti-commingling check).
*   Ensure that all mandatory beneficiary notices were sent within the legally required window.
*   Assess the "Duty of Account": Verify that annual accountings were prepared and distributed to qualified beneficiaries.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

**Pro Tips:**
*   **The Paper Trail:** Always document the "Why." If a trustee makes a large discretionary distribution, a contemporaneous memo explaining the financial need is the best defense against future beneficiary litigation.
*   **Leverage Technology:** Use automated accounting software that tracks transaction categorization to generate reports instantly, reducing manual reconciliation time by up to 40%.
*   **Independent Review:** Even if the trustee is a family member, engage a third-party accountant or legal counsel to review the audit findings to ensure neutrality.

**Pitfalls to Avoid:**
*   **The "Commingling" Trap:** Never pay personal expenses from the trust checking account, even if "reimbursing" the trust later. This is the fastest way to pierce the trust’s liability protection.
*   **Ignoring the IPS:** Failing to document why an investment was chosen (if it deviates from the general strategy) is a common failure point in litigation. 
*   **Stale Appraisals:** Using out-of-date valuations for real estate can lead to incorrect distribution calculations and potential tax reporting errors.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a trust audit be performed?**
A: Ideally, an informal review should occur annually during tax preparation, with a formal, professional audit occurring at least every three years or upon the resignation or death of a trustee.

**Q: What is the biggest risk identified during most trust audits?**
A: The most common risk is "Procedural Drift," where the trustee stops documenting decisions and distributing accountings to beneficiaries, which creates a vacuum of information that often leads to beneficiary mistrust and legal action.

**Q: Is it necessary to hire an outside firm for a trust audit?**
A: While small, simple trusts can be audited internally, any trust involving complex assets, multiple beneficiaries, or potential family conflict should always be audited by an independent third party to ensure objectivity and legal compliance.
