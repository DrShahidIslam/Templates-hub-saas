# Standard Operating Procedure: VA Loan Property Inspection & Minimum Property Requirements (MPR)

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for ensuring a property meets the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs). Unlike a standard home inspection—which evaluates the general condition of a home—a VA appraisal ensures the property is safe, structurally sound, and sanitary. As an operations professional, it is critical to understand that the VA appraiser’s primary focus is on habitability and compliance with federal standards to protect the veteran and the VA’s investment.

## Phase 1: Exterior & Structural Integrity
The exterior of the property must be free of hazards and exhibit long-term structural viability.

*   **Foundation:** Must be sound and free of major cracks or signs of movement.
*   **Roofing:** Must have at least two years of remaining life and be free of leaks.
*   **Exterior Siding/Cladding:** Must be painted (no peeling paint, specifically if the home was built before 1978) and free of rot.
*   **Grading & Drainage:** Soil must slope away from the foundation to prevent water pooling.
*   **Access:** The property must have direct, permanent, and safe access from a public or private street.

## Phase 2: Interior Safety & Habitability
The interior must be functional for daily living and free from health hazards.

*   **Mechanical Systems:** HVAC, electrical, and plumbing systems must be fully functional and safe.
*   **Electrical:** No exposed wires or open junction boxes. All outlets must have covers.
*   **Water Supply:** Must have a continuous supply of safe, potable water.
*   **Sanitation:** Functional sewage disposal system that meets local health department standards.
*   **Lead-Based Paint:** For homes built before 1978, all defective, peeling, or chipping paint must be mitigated/scraped and repainted.
*   **Safety Hazards:** No trip hazards, broken stairs, or missing handrails.

## Phase 3: Utilities & Infrastructure
The property must be capable of supporting the basic utilities required for a modern household.

*   **Water Heater:** Must be in good working condition and equipped with a pressure relief valve.
*   **Attic/Crawlspace:** Must be accessible and free of excessive dampness or wood-destroying insects (WDI).
*   **Windows/Doors:** Must be operational; security locks must be functional on all exterior entries.
*   **Kitchen:** Must contain a stove/range (or space for one) and a refrigerator space (or a functional unit).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **The "Lead Paint" Trap:** Many sellers fail to realize that peeling paint in pre-1978 homes is an automatic flag. Ensure any flaking paint is scraped and repainted professionally before the appraiser arrives to avoid a re-inspection delay.
*   **Don't Confuse the Home Inspection with the Appraisal:** A VA appraisal is *not* a home inspection. It does not check the life expectancy of every appliance or the minor condition of every outlet. It is highly recommended that the veteran *also* pays for a private home inspection to identify non-MPR issues.
*   **Crawlspace Access:** Ensure that the crawlspace and attic are accessible. If the appraiser cannot physically enter these areas due to locked doors or blocked entrances, they will mandate a re-inspection, costing time and money.
*   **WDI Reports:** In many regions, the VA requires a Wood-Destroying Insect (WDI) inspection. Confirm if this is required for the specific state/region of the property early in the process.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Does the VA require a perfect home?**
No. The VA requires that a home be "safe, sound, and sanitary." It does not need to be updated with modern aesthetics, provided the home is structurally stable and free of safety hazards.

**2. Can I use a VA loan for a "fixer-upper"?**
Standard VA loans are intended for move-in-ready properties. If a property requires significant repairs to meet MPRs, a "VA Renovation Loan" may be used, which allows for the cost of repairs to be financed into the mortgage.

**3. What happens if the appraiser finds a violation?**
If a violation is found, the appraiser will list it as a "condition" of the loan. The seller must typically remediate the issue, and the appraiser must perform a final re-inspection to verify the fix before the loan can proceed to closing.
