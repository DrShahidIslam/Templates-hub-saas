# Standard Operating Procedure: Package Unit Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance (PM) protocols for commercial rooftop package units. Regular maintenance is critical to extending equipment lifecycle, ensuring optimal thermal efficiency, and preventing costly emergency downtime. All technicians must strictly adhere to safety protocols, including Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures, before accessing internal electrical or mechanical components.

## Section 1: Pre-Maintenance Safety and Access
*   Perform a visual inspection of the roof and unit exterior for structural hazards.
*   Verify the unit is disconnected from the power source and apply LOTO tags to the disconnect switch.
*   Remove all service panels using appropriate tools, ensuring fasteners are secured to prevent roof damage.
*   Check for signs of wildlife intrusion or debris accumulation in the cabinet.

## Section 2: Airside and Filtration
*   Replace air filters with the specified MERV-rated media; verify airflow direction arrows.
*   Inspect the evaporator coil for dirt, mold, or "matting." Clean with an approved non-acidic coil cleaner if necessary.
*   Check the condensate drain pan for standing water or biological growth; clear the drain line and ensure proper trap priming.
*   Inspect the blower assembly for loose set screws, belt tension/alignment (if applicable), and debris on fan blades.

## Section 3: Condenser and Refrigerant Systems
*   Inspect condenser coils for obstructions (cottonwood, leaves, dust). Clean using low-pressure water or compressed air (always rinse from the inside out).
*   Check condenser fan motors and blades for vibration, noise, or blade damage.
*   Monitor refrigerant lines for oil streaks (indicative of leaks) and ensure insulation is intact on the suction line.
*   Verify refrigerant charge levels and monitor subcooling/superheat values against the manufacturer’s charging chart.

## Section 4: Electrical and Control Systems
*   Tighten all electrical terminal connections; heat cycles can cause wiring to loosen over time.
*   Inspect contactors for pitting or arc marks; replace if contact points are blackened or scorched.
*   Test capacitors for proper MFD (Microfarad) ratings; replace if values are +/- 10% of the rating.
*   Verify safety switches (High/Low pressure, Freeze stats) are functional and calibrated.
*   Perform a dry-run test: check thermostat calls for Heating, Cooling, and Fan-only modes.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always photograph the unit’s data plate during the first visit of the season. This ensures you have the correct model and serial numbers for warranty claims or parts procurement.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an infrared camera to check for "hot spots" on terminal blocks—this is the most effective way to identify a failing connection before it causes a total shutdown.
*   **Pitfall:** Never use high-pressure power washers on aluminum fins. This will collapse the fin structure and permanently impede airflow. Use a coil-specific cleaning wand.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "drain trap." A dry or clogged trap can cause water to back up into the blower, causing rust and mold growth inside the ductwork.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform this preventive maintenance?**
A: Industry standard for commercial package units is semi-annually (spring for cooling, autumn for heating). Units in high-dust or coastal environments should be serviced quarterly.

**Q: What is the most common reason for compressor failure?**
A: The most frequent cause is a dirty condenser coil. A restricted coil causes high head pressures, which forces the compressor to work harder, leading to overheating and premature electrical failure.

**Q: Is it necessary to check the gas heat section during a cooling PM?**
A: Yes. At minimum, perform a visual inspection of the heat exchanger for cracks and clean the burners. It is best practice to perform a full combustion analysis during every service visit to ensure safe operation.
