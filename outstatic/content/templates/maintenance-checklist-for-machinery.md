# Standard Operating Procedure: Machinery Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes a standardized framework for the scheduled inspection, cleaning, and upkeep of facility machinery. The primary objective of this protocol is to ensure operational continuity, extend asset lifecycle, and maintain strict compliance with occupational health and safety standards. All maintenance personnel must adhere to these steps to mitigate the risk of unplanned downtime and mechanical failure.

## 1. Safety and Preparation Protocols
*   **Lockout/Tagout (LOTO):** Verify that all machines are powered down and locked out in accordance with LOTO procedures before beginning inspection.
*   **PPE Verification:** Don appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, cut-resistant gloves, and steel-toed boots.
*   **Documentation Review:** Consult the original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) manual and the previous maintenance log to identify recurring issues.
*   **Area Clearing:** Ensure the workspace is clear of debris and that all emergency stops are functional and accessible.

## 2. Mechanical and Structural Inspection
*   **Hardware Integrity:** Check for loose, missing, or damaged bolts, screws, and fasteners. Torque to specifications where necessary.
*   **Drive Systems:** Inspect drive belts, chains, and pulleys for signs of excessive wear, fraying, or misalignment.
*   **Lubrication:** Verify that all grease points are adequately lubricated per the maintenance schedule. Wipe away old, contaminated grease.
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check hydraulic fluid, coolant, and oil reservoirs. Inspect for leaks at seals, gaskets, and hydraulic hoses.

## 3. Electrical and Control Systems
*   **Cable Inspection:** Examine external power cables for cracking, exposed wiring, or heat damage.
*   **Panel Health:** Ensure the electrical cabinet door is sealed properly and free of dust accumulation or moisture intrusion.
*   **Sensors and Limit Switches:** Clean optical lenses or sensors and verify that limit switches engage correctly without binding.
*   **Emergency Controls:** Perform a functional test of all E-stops and safety interlocks (ensure the machine is clear of personnel during this step).

## 4. Post-Maintenance Verification and Sign-off
*   **Cleanup:** Remove all tools, rags, and cleaning supplies from the machine housing.
*   **Operational Testing:** Perform a "dry run" at low speed to ensure mechanical components move without grinding or abnormal vibration.
*   **Log Completion:** Document the inspection date, findings, parts replaced, and the signature of the technician in the Master Maintenance Log.
*   **Return to Service:** Officially release the machine back to the production floor.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "Sensory Check" Pitfall:** Do not rely solely on visuals. Use your senses—listen for irregular clanking, smell for ozone or burning rubber, and feel for excessive heat on motor housings.
*   **Lubrication Management:** Do not over-lubricate. Excess grease can attract grit and damage seals. Follow OEM quantity recommendations precisely.
*   **Pro Tip:** Create a "Digital Twin" or photographic baseline of a well-maintained machine. Compare the current state of your machinery to this image to quickly identify "drift" or poor housekeeping.
*   **Avoid "Band-Aid" Repairs:** Never bypass a safety sensor or use improvised hardware to keep a machine running. If it requires a temporary fix, schedule a permanent repair immediately.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I perform these maintenance checks?**
A: Maintenance frequency should be determined by the OEM manual and the intensity of usage. Generally, high-output machinery requires daily minor inspections and monthly deep-dive preventive maintenance.

**Q: What should I do if I discover a part is out of stock during maintenance?**
A: Document the deficiency in the maintenance log, immediately notify the operations manager, and place an urgent order. If the part is safety-critical, the machine must remain locked out until the replacement is installed.

**Q: Does "cleaning" count as maintenance?**
A: Absolutely. Accumulated dust, debris, and oil mist are the leading causes of overheating and short-circuiting in industrial machinery. Cleaning is an essential component of every maintenance cycle.
