# Standard Operating Procedure: Wheel Loader Preventive Maintenance (PM)

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for conducting preventive maintenance on wheel loaders to ensure operational longevity, minimize unscheduled downtime, and maintain site safety. Regular preventive maintenance is critical for identifying mechanical fatigue, fluid degradation, and structural wear before they result in catastrophic failure. All operators and maintenance personnel must adhere to these guidelines to ensure the equipment meets OEM specifications and safety compliance standards.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Ensure the machine is parked on level ground with the bucket lowered to the ground.
*   Engage the parking brake and place the transmission in neutral.
*   Turn off the engine and remove the ignition key (Lock-Out/Tag-Out protocol).
*   Allow the engine and hydraulic system to cool sufficiently to prevent burns.
*   Don appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including steel-toed boots, high-visibility vest, safety glasses, and gloves.

## 2. Daily Fluid and Filter Inspection
*   **Engine Oil:** Check the dipstick; ensure the level is within the operating range. Note any signs of discoloration or burnt odor.
*   **Coolant:** Inspect the coolant reservoir level; ensure the cap is tightened and there are no signs of white residue around the radiator housing.
*   **Hydraulic Fluid:** Inspect the sight gauge while the bucket is in the carry position (or as per OEM manual). Ensure fluid is free of milky contaminants, which indicate water ingress.
*   **Fuel/Water Separator:** Drain any accumulated water from the separator bowl.
*   **Air Filters:** Inspect the dust ejector valve. If the service indicator shows red, replace the primary and secondary elements immediately.

## 3. Structural and Undercarriage Integrity
*   **Tires:** Inspect for sidewall cuts, embedded debris, or uneven wear patterns. Check inflation pressure against the load chart.
*   **Bucket and Linkage:** Check for cracked welds, bent pins, or excessive play in the bushings.
*   **Ground Engaging Tools (GET):** Inspect bucket teeth and cutting edges for excessive wear or missing bolts.
*   **Articulation Joint:** Ensure the steering cylinders and articulation pins are greased and show no signs of structural stress.

## 4. Electrical and Cabin Systems
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion and ensure cables are secure.
*   **Lighting:** Test all work lights, headlights, taillights, and turn signals.
*   **Safety Alarms:** Test the reverse travel alarm and the horn.
*   **Instrumentation:** Check the dash display for fault codes or warning lights upon start-up.
*   **Fire Extinguisher:** Ensure the unit is charged, mounted, and the safety pin is intact.

## 5. Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Clean-As-You-Go" Rule:** Always clear debris from the radiator grill and engine compartment. A clean engine runs cooler and allows you to spot leaks instantly.
*   **Pro Tip: Fluid Sampling:** Send oil and hydraulic fluid samples to a laboratory every 500 hours. This provides a "blood test" for your machine, revealing internal wear metals before a breakdown occurs.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Grease Intervals:** Never skip a greasing cycle. Pins and bushings are the most expensive non-engine components to replace; keeping them lubricated prevents "oval-ing" of bores.
*   **Pitfall - Over-greasing:** While greasing is vital, excessive pressure can blow out dust seals, allowing grit to enter the bearing surfaces. Apply until fresh grease just begins to appear.

## 6. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I replace the hydraulic oil?**
A: Follow the OEM manual intervals, typically every 1,000 to 2,000 hours. However, if the fluid appears milky or dark, perform an immediate analysis and change it regardless of hours.

**Q: Why does my machine lose power when climbing a slope?**
A: This is often a sign of a clogged fuel filter or low fuel rail pressure. Always check the fuel filter before suspecting more complex transmission or engine issues.

**Q: What is the most common cause of premature wheel loader failure?**
A: Lack of proper lubrication and failing to clean the radiator core. Overheating from a clogged radiator and friction from un-greased pins account for over 60% of avoidable heavy equipment repairs.
