# Standard Operating Procedure: Revenue Audit Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory steps for conducting a comprehensive revenue audit. The objective of this process is to ensure the accuracy, completeness, and validity of all revenue recorded in the general ledger. By adhering to this protocol, the finance department will mitigate the risk of revenue leakage, ensure compliance with accounting standards (such as ASC 606 or IFRS 15), and maintain the integrity of financial reporting for stakeholders.

## Phase 1: Revenue Recognition and Compliance
*   Verify that revenue is recognized in the correct accounting period based on the transfer of control or performance obligations.
*   Review a sample of high-value contracts to ensure that revenue recognition criteria align with signed terms.
*   Confirm that all revenue streams (subscriptions, professional services, hardware) are categorized according to the company’s Chart of Accounts.
*   Validate that any variable consideration (e.g., rebates, discounts, or performance bonuses) has been properly estimated and accrued.

## Phase 2: Transactional Accuracy and Cut-off Testing
*   **Sample Selection:** Extract a statistically significant sample of sales invoices from the audit period.
*   **Vouching:** Trace individual invoices back to underlying supporting documentation (e.g., Purchase Orders, Signed Contracts, Proof of Delivery).
*   **Cut-off Testing:** Examine transactions occurring five days before and five days after the period end to ensure they are recorded in the correct fiscal period.
*   **Mathematical Accuracy:** Re-calculate invoice totals, including tax, shipping, and currency conversion adjustments, to ensure they match the ledger entry.

## Phase 3: Accounts Receivable and Collections Reconciliation
*   Reconcile the Accounts Receivable (AR) sub-ledger to the General Ledger (GL) to identify discrepancies.
*   Review the Aged Receivables report; investigate any accounts past due by more than 90 days for potential impairment or write-off requirements.
*   Verify the accuracy of the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts calculation based on historical trends and current economic conditions.
*   Ensure that customer credit memos and returns are authorized by management and reflected accurately in the net revenue figures.

## Phase 4: System Integration and Data Integrity
*   Perform a "system walk-through" to ensure that data flows seamlessly from the CRM/Sales platform to the ERP/Accounting system.
*   Check for duplicate entries, missing invoice numbers, or gaps in the sequential numbering of revenue documents.
*   Validate that manual journal entries impacting revenue are supported by management approval and sufficient documentation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Automation:** Utilize data analytics tools to run "full population testing" rather than sampling, allowing for 100% visibility into transactions.
*   **Trend Analysis:** Compare monthly revenue against the budget and previous year's performance; document the "why" for any variance exceeding 5%.
*   **Cross-Functional Sync:** Hold a brief meeting with the Sales Operations team during the audit; they often have context on "side letters" or verbal agreements not present in formal contracts.

### Pitfalls to Avoid
*   **Confirmation Bias:** Do not assume a document is valid simply because it exists in the system; always cross-reference it with independent third-party evidence (e.g., bank receipts).
*   **Ignoring Non-Cash Revenue:** Failing to audit non-cash transactions (barter, trade credits) is a common cause of audit adjustments.
*   **Siloed Auditing:** Treating revenue as an isolated accounting function without understanding the underlying business model will lead to missed misclassifications.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should a revenue audit be performed?**
A: While external audits occur annually, internal revenue audits should be performed on a quarterly basis to identify and correct issues before year-end closing.

**Q: What is the most common cause of revenue audit adjustments?**
A: Timing errors (cut-off issues) and the incorrect application of multi-element arrangement accounting are the most frequent causes of financial restatements.

**Q: Should I involve the sales team in the audit process?**
A: Yes. Involving Sales Ops is critical, as they can provide clarity on contract amendments or specific pricing structures that may seem like errors to the finance team but are actually legitimate contractual nuances.
