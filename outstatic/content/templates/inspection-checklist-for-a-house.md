# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting a systematic inspection of a residential property. Whether for real estate due diligence, maintenance scheduling, or pre-move-in assessment, this protocol ensures that no critical structural or functional component is overlooked. By following this standardized methodology, stakeholders can identify potential hazards, estimate repair costs, and assess the long-term viability of the building’s systems.

## 1. Exterior and Structural Assessment
*   **Foundation:** Examine the perimeter for vertical or horizontal cracks, settling, or bowing.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect shingles for curling, missing pieces, or granular loss; check flashing around chimneys and vents for rust or gaps.
*   **Siding/Cladding:** Look for wood rot, cracked stucco, loose vinyl, or damaged brick mortar.
*   **Grading/Drainage:** Ensure the soil slopes away from the foundation; check that gutters and downspouts are clear and discharge at least six feet away from the structure.
*   **Windows/Doors:** Check exterior frames for water damage, peeling paint, and ensure seals are intact.

## 2. Interior Systems and Infrastructure
*   **Electrical:** Test outlets with a GFCI tester; ensure the breaker panel is labeled and free of oxidation or charred wires.
*   **Plumbing:** Operate all faucets and flush toilets to check for pressure and leaks; observe drain speed.
*   **HVAC:** Check air filters, verify that the unit kicks on/off correctly, and look for signs of rust in the condensate pan.
*   **Attic/Insulation:** Check for water stains (roof leaks) and ensure insulation is uniform and not blocking soffit vents.
*   **Basement/Crawlspace:** Look for efflorescence (white mineral deposits), dampness, or musty odors.

## 3. Interior Living Spaces
*   **Walls/Ceilings:** Inspect for water stains (past or present) and irregular cracking that may indicate shifting.
*   **Flooring:** Check for uneven surfaces, squeaky boards, or water damage under sinks and near windows.
*   **Windows/Doors:** Operate every window and door to ensure they latch, slide, and lock properly.
*   **Appliances:** Run a short cycle on the dishwasher; test burners on the stove; verify the refrigerator is maintaining temperature.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always bring a flashlight, a moisture meter, and a GFCI circuit tester. A high-lumen light is essential for seeing structural issues in dim corners.
*   **Pro Tip:** Take photos of everything, especially defects. Label photos immediately to ensure the inspection report is legally and functionally defensible.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not ignore the "minor" damp spot. In a crawlspace or basement, a small spot often indicates a chronic moisture issue that leads to mold or structural decay.
*   **Pitfall:** Overlooking "patch jobs." If a room is freshly painted but you notice bubbles or inconsistent texture, it may be a cosmetic attempt to hide water damage or recent drywall repairs.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How long should a professional-grade inspection take?**
A: Depending on the square footage and complexity of the property, a thorough inspection usually takes between two to four hours. Avoid rushing the process.

**Q: What is the most common "hidden" issue?**
A: Poor attic ventilation is a frequent oversight. It leads to moisture buildup, which causes roof decking rot and premature shingle failure that isn't visible from the ground.

**Q: Should I bring a professional contractor along?**
A: While a general home inspector is great for a broad overview, bringing a specialist (electrician or plumber) is recommended if the house is over 40 years old or shows signs of specific system failure.
