# Standard Operating Procedure: Maintenance Audit Protocol

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the requirements for conducting a comprehensive maintenance audit. The purpose of this audit is to ensure that all assets are performing within operational specifications, safety protocols are strictly adhered to, and maintenance records are accurate and up-to-date. By identifying gaps in maintenance execution or documentation, the organization mitigates the risk of unscheduled downtime, ensures regulatory compliance, and extends the overall lifecycle of critical infrastructure.

## Phase 1: Documentation and Compliance Review
*   Verify the existence and completeness of the Master Asset Register.
*   Audit Preventive Maintenance (PM) work orders for the last 12 months to ensure 100% completion rates.
*   Cross-reference completed work orders against the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) entries.
*   Review safety certifications and inspection logs for high-risk equipment (e.g., pressure vessels, lifting gear).
*   Check that all maintenance personnel possess valid certifications and training records for their assigned tasks.

## Phase 2: Physical Asset Inspection
*   Perform a visual walkthrough of all primary mechanical and electrical systems.
*   Identify visible signs of neglect, such as leaks, unusual vibrations, or excessive noise during operation.
*   Verify that physical equipment matches the "as-built" documentation and schematics.
*   Check for appropriate labeling and safety signage on all machinery.
*   Examine housekeeping standards around critical control points (e.g., no debris in electrical panels or oil spills near drives).

## Phase 3: Inventory and Spare Parts Audit
*   Conduct a cycle count on critical spare parts to verify the accuracy of inventory levels.
*   Inspect storage conditions for sensitive components (e.g., humidity control, temperature, and dust protection).
*   Identify and flag obsolete, expired, or damaged parts for disposal.
*   Verify that critical stock levels align with current lead times and lead-time-to-fail metrics.
*   Check for organized labeling systems and clear location mapping within the warehouse.

## Phase 4: Maintenance Workflow and Process Review
*   Evaluate the "Time-to-Repair" (MTTR) metrics for the previous quarter.
*   Assess the efficiency of the maintenance request intake process (from report to work order issuance).
*   Interview maintenance staff regarding tool availability and equipment access.
*   Verify the existence and usage of Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures during all site activities.
*   Check for the presence of Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) at the point of use for complex maintenance tasks.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Perform "Spot Checks" on equipment that has seen the highest frequency of breakdowns; these areas often hide chronic underlying issues that routine checks miss.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use digital audit forms. Real-time data entry minimizes human error and allows for instant reporting and trend analysis.
*   **Pitfall - The "Check-the-Box" Syndrome:** Avoid just checking items off a list. If a machine looks clean but hasn't been serviced according to the manual, flag it as non-compliant.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Soft Data:** Disregarding feedback from operators who use the equipment daily is a major mistake. They often know where the machine is failing long before it shows up in a logbook.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should a full maintenance audit be conducted?**
A: A comprehensive audit should be conducted at least annually. However, high-risk facilities or those subject to strict regulatory oversight should perform quarterly internal audits.

**Q: What is the most critical component to verify in the audit?**
A: Safety and compliance documentation are the most critical, as they prevent legal liability and, more importantly, ensure the physical safety of your staff.

**Q: If I find a discrepancy in the inventory, should I update it immediately?**
A: Do not update the records immediately. Document the discrepancy, investigate the root cause (e.g., unauthorized access, missing documentation), and then adjust the inventory after the investigation is closed to ensure accountability.
