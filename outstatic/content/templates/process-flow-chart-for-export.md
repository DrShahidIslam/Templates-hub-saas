# Standard Operating Procedure: Export Process Workflow

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic workflow required to execute an international export shipment. The objective is to ensure regulatory compliance, minimize transit delays, and maintain cost-efficiency from the receipt of a purchase order to the final delivery at the destination port. Adherence to this workflow is mandatory for all logistics and export documentation staff to mitigate risks associated with customs holds, non-payment, and supply chain disruptions.

## Section 1: Pre-Export Documentation & Compliance
*   **Verify Sales Contract/Proforma Invoice:** Confirm all terms of sale, including Incoterms, currency, and payment methods (e.g., Letter of Credit).
*   **Validate HS Classification:** Determine the correct Harmonized System (HS) code for the goods to ensure accurate duty calculations and regulatory compliance.
*   **Check Export Controls:** Cross-reference the destination country and the consignee against restricted party lists and check for necessary export licenses.
*   **Confirm Cargo Readiness:** Receive formal notification from the warehouse/production floor that goods are packed, labeled, and ready for inspection.

## Section 2: Logistics & Booking Coordination
*   **Select Freight Forwarder:** Evaluate quotes based on transit time, reliability, and cost; issue a formal booking request.
*   **Coordinate Inland Transport:** Schedule pick-up from the warehouse to the departure port/airport, ensuring the carrier has the correct container/cargo specifications.
*   **Issue Shipping Instructions (SI):** Provide the forwarder with the final SI, ensuring data matches the commercial invoice exactly to avoid bill of lading (BOL) errors.
*   **Verify Container/Load Security:** Ensure container seals are verified and recorded for compliance with international security standards (e.g., C-TPAT).

## Section 3: Customs Clearance & Final Documentation
*   **Draft Export Declaration:** File the Electronic Export Information (EEI) or equivalent automated filing through the customs portal.
*   **Prepare Export Documentation:** Finalize the Commercial Invoice, Packing List, Certificate of Origin, and any required health/phytosanitary certificates.
*   **Transmit Documents:** Send pre-alert documents to the consignee and the destination broker to facilitate import clearance prior to arrival.
*   **File Proof of Export:** Archive the stamped BOL and customs entry summary for audit and tax purposes.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The Golden Rule):** Always create a "Document Master Folder" for every shipment. If the Commercial Invoice, Packing List, and BOL do not match down to the decimal point on weights, your shipment will likely be flagged at customs.
*   **Pitfall (Incoterm Confusion):** Never assume "CIF" or "DDP" responsibilities without clear communication. Misunderstanding Incoterms is the leading cause of unexpected costs and disputes between buyers and sellers.
*   **Pro Tip (Pre-Clearance):** Send digital copies of all documents to the destination importer at least 48 hours before the vessel arrives. Proactive communication prevents demurrage and detention fees.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring HS Codes):** Relying on an old HS code can lead to incorrect tariff payments or, worse, an export ban on specific controlled goods. Always verify codes against the current year’s Customs Tariff Schedule.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What happens if there is a discrepancy between the Commercial Invoice and the Bill of Lading?**
A: This is a critical error. It can freeze the release of goods at the destination port. You must immediately request a "Letter of Indemnity" or an amendment from the carrier, which may incur administrative fees and cause significant transit delays.

**Q: At what point is the export considered "finalized"?**
A: An export is legally finalized once the cargo has physically left the customs territory and the export declaration has been accepted/stamped by the customs authority. You must retain these records for the duration mandated by your local tax authority (typically 5-7 years).

**Q: How do I handle hazardous materials (HazMat)?**
A: HazMat requires specialized labeling, a Dangerous Goods Declaration (DGD), and potentially a different freight carrier. Ensure you have the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) on file and that the forwarder is certified to handle the specific class of hazardous material you are exporting.
