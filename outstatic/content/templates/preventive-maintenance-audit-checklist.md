# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance (PM) Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the methodology for conducting a comprehensive Preventive Maintenance (PM) Audit. The objective of this audit is to verify that all machinery, infrastructure, and equipment are serviced according to the manufacturer’s specifications and organizational safety standards. By auditing these procedures, management ensures operational continuity, minimizes unplanned downtime, extends asset lifespan, and maintains regulatory compliance. This process applies to all maintenance personnel and facility managers responsible for oversight of the asset lifecycle.

## Phase 1: Documentation and Compliance Verification
*   Verify that current PM schedules align with the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) records.
*   Cross-reference completed work orders against the master maintenance calendar to identify missed or deferred tasks.
*   Check for the presence of up-to-date Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for all lubricants, chemicals, and consumables used during maintenance.
*   Confirm that all technician certifications (e.g., HVAC, electrical, welding) are valid and filed.
*   Review historical failure logs to determine if current PM intervals are sufficient or if they require adjustment based on Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF).

## Phase 2: Physical Equipment and Environment Inspection
*   Perform a visual inspection of equipment for leaks, abnormal wear, vibration, or audible noise.
*   Validate that asset tags/IDs are clearly visible and match the digital inventory registry.
*   Inspect storage areas for maintenance parts to ensure proper labeling, organization, and inventory levels of critical spares.
*   Audit the cleanliness of the immediate workspace to verify adherence to 5S methodology standards.
*   Verify that all safety guards, emergency stops, and warning signage are intact and functional.

## Phase 3: Data Integrity and Process Quality
*   Randomly select 10% of completed work orders to verify the quality of data entry (e.g., hours logged, parts used, "as-found" and "as-left" conditions).
*   Confirm that "Root Cause Analysis" (RCA) fields are completed for any reactive repairs identified during a PM inspection.
*   Audit the calibration status of measurement tools used by the maintenance team.
*   Review sign-off procedures to ensure that work orders have been closed by both the technician and the shift supervisor.

## Phase 4: Reporting and Corrective Action
*   Compile all audit findings into a formal "Gap Analysis Report."
*   Assign corrective actions with clear deadlines for any non-compliant findings identified during the audit.
*   Schedule a follow-up audit within 30 days to ensure that identified deficiencies have been remediated.
*   Present findings to the operations team to identify trends in equipment degradation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Audit While You Work:** Whenever possible, perform "shadow audits" where the auditor observes a technician performing a PM to verify that the task matches the written procedure.
*   **Focus on Critical Assets:** Prioritize audits for "bottleneck" equipment where downtime has a significant impact on revenue or safety.
*   **Use Digital Checklists:** Use mobile-based audit tools that allow for real-time photo uploads and GPS tagging to eliminate paperwork errors.

### Pitfalls
*   **Pencil Whipping:** Be wary of records that look too perfect. A lack of minor reported issues during PMs may indicate that technicians are not actually performing the physical checks.
*   **Scope Creep:** Avoid turning a PM audit into a full-scale mechanical overhaul; stay focused on procedural compliance and process integrity.
*   **Isolating Data:** Never audit PMs without comparing them to spare parts usage. If a PM shows everything is "good" but parts consumption is skyrocketing, there is a discrepancy in the reporting.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a preventive maintenance audit be performed?**
For high-criticality equipment, audits should be conducted quarterly. For standard facility equipment, an annual audit is typically sufficient to maintain compliance and identify process drift.

**2. What should I do if I find a recurring issue that isn't being addressed by the current PM?**
If a recurring issue persists despite adherence to the PM schedule, it is a clear indicator that the maintenance task itself is ineffective. You must initiate a "Job Plan Review" to update the frequency or the specific steps required to address the asset's actual failure mode.

**3. Who is responsible for correcting the deficiencies found in the audit?**
The immediate responsibility lies with the Maintenance Supervisor. However, the Operational Manager must oversee the resolution of these deficiencies to ensure that the necessary resources (budget, time, or parts) are allocated to correct the gaps.
