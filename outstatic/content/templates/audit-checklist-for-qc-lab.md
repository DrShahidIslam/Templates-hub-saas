# Standard Operating Procedure: Quality Control (QC) Laboratory Audit

This SOP establishes a comprehensive framework for conducting rigorous audits of a Quality Control (QC) laboratory. The objective is to ensure compliance with Good Laboratory Practices (GLP), internal standard operating procedures, and regulatory requirements (such as FDA, EMA, or ISO 17025 standards). This audit process focuses on data integrity, equipment reliability, personnel proficiency, and environmental controls to guarantee the accuracy and validity of all analytical testing results.

## 1. Documentation and Data Integrity
*   **Audit Trail Review:** Verify that all analytical software (CDS, LIMS) has enabled audit trails and that they are reviewed periodically.
*   **ALCOA+ Principles:** Confirm that all raw data is Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate, Complete, Consistent, Enduring, and Available.
*   **Logbook Verification:** Inspect manual and electronic logbooks for chronological entries, signatures, and timestamps without gaps or unauthorized erasures.
*   **SOP Compliance:** Ensure that the current version of all SOPs is accessible and that laboratory personnel are working to the latest approved revisions.

## 2. Equipment and Instrumentation
*   **Calibration Records:** Cross-check calibration certificates against the master equipment list to ensure all instruments are within their calibration cycle.
*   **Preventive Maintenance (PM):** Review PM logs for all critical instrumentation (HPLC, GC, Spectrophotometers, etc.) to confirm scheduled services were performed on time.
*   **Instrument Qualification:** Verify IQ/OQ/PQ documentation for new equipment or following major repairs/moves.
*   **Out-of-Service Protocol:** Confirm that "Out of Calibration" or "Out of Service" labels are correctly applied to non-compliant equipment and that unauthorized use is prevented.

## 3. Sample Management and Reagents
*   **Sample Receipt & Storage:** Audit the sample logging process. Ensure samples are stored in the correct environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, light exposure).
*   **Labeling Integrity:** Verify that all reagents, standards, and mobile phases are clearly labeled with concentration, expiration date, preparation date, and preparer initials.
*   **Inventory Control:** Check for expired reagents and ensure they are segregated from active stock.
*   **Chain of Custody:** Review the movement logs of samples to ensure accountability from receipt to disposal.

## 4. Personnel Training and Safety
*   **Training Files:** Audit employee training matrices. Ensure competency assessments (e.g., observed analytical runs) are documented and current for every procedure the analyst performs.
*   **Safety Compliance:** Verify the availability and condition of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
*   **Spill Kits and Eye Wash:** Check that safety stations are unobstructed and that emergency equipment is inspected monthly.
*   **Training on Deviations:** Ensure analysts have received training on any recently closed CAPAs (Corrective and Preventive Actions) or procedure updates.

## 5. Quality Systems and Investigations
*   **OOS/OOT Investigations:** Review the Out-of-Specification (OOS) and Out-of-Trend (OOT) investigation logs to ensure timely closure and root cause identification.
*   **Change Control:** Confirm that any changes to analytical methods or laboratory processes were vetted through the formal Change Control process before implementation.
*   **Self-Inspection:** Verify that the lab conducts internal self-audits and that findings from previous audits have been remediated.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Perform "vertical audits" where you select one finished product from the warehouse and trace its journey backward through the analytical testing records, raw data files, and reagent preparation logs.
*   **Pitfall - "Data Silos":** Do not rely solely on the LIMS. Always verify the electronic raw data (e.g., .raw files or chromatograms) directly on the instrument computer to ensure it matches the final report.
*   **Pitfall - The "Hidden" SOP:** Watch for analysts using "cheat sheets" or post-it notes with non-official instructions. All analytical steps must be documented in the controlled SOP.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a QC laboratory undergo an internal audit?**
Generally, internal audits should be performed at least annually. However, if there have been significant equipment changes, staff turnover, or a history of OOS results, a more frequent audit schedule (quarterly or semi-annually) is recommended.

**2. What is the most common finding during a QC lab audit?**
The most frequent finding is poor documentation practices, specifically regarding the "contemporaneous" requirement of ALCOA+. This often includes back-dating entries or failing to document a deviation immediately as it occurs.

**3. If an instrument is found to be out of calibration during the audit, what is the immediate action?**
The equipment must be immediately tagged as "Out of Service." An impact assessment must be initiated to evaluate all test results generated since the last successful calibration to determine if the integrity of the data is compromised.
