# Standard Operating Procedure: Comprehensive Condo Inspection

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a rigorous framework for conducting a detailed condo inspection. Whether performed for move-in, move-out, or routine maintenance, this protocol ensures that all physical, structural, and mechanical elements of the unit are documented. By following this guide, property managers and owners can mitigate liability, track long-term wear and tear, and ensure the asset meets health and safety standards. Consistency in this process is vital for maintaining property value and fostering positive landlord-tenant relationships.

## Section 1: Entry, Living Areas, and Flooring
*   **Entryway:** Inspect door locks, deadbolts, and doorbells for functionality. Check for signs of forced entry or damage to the door frame.
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Check for hairline cracks, water staining, peeling paint, or mold growth.
*   **Flooring:** Evaluate for scratches, loose tiles, creaky floorboards, or carpet stains/odors.
*   **Windows and Screens:** Ensure all windows operate smoothly, locks engage properly, and window screens are intact without holes.
*   **Lighting:** Test all light switches and verify that all fixtures are securely mounted and illuminated.

## Section 2: Kitchen and Appliance Suite
*   **Appliances:** Run the dishwasher through a short cycle; test the oven/range for heating; verify the refrigerator and freezer maintain consistent temperatures.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect under the sink for active leaks, corrosion on P-traps, or evidence of past moisture. Check water pressure and faucet functionality.
*   **Cabinetry:** Open and close all drawers and doors to ensure hinges and slides are functional and aligned. Check for water damage inside cabinetry.
*   **Countertops:** Check for chips, cracks, or burnt areas.

## Section 3: Bathrooms and Plumbing Fixtures
*   **Toilets:** Flush to check for proper cycle time and check the base for any signs of water leakage or rocking.
*   **Showers/Tubs:** Inspect grout and caulk lines for mold or separation. Test drainage speed.
*   **Ventilation:** Confirm the exhaust fan is running and effectively pulling air (verify with a piece of tissue paper).
*   **Fixtures:** Check faucets and showerheads for calcium buildup or low flow.

## Section 4: Electrical and HVAC Systems
*   **Outlets:** Use a GFCI outlet tester to ensure all outlets are grounded and functional, especially in wet zones (kitchen/bath).
*   **HVAC:** Check the air filter for cleanliness. Test the thermostat’s ability to call for both heat and cooling. 
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Depress the test button on every unit. Replace batteries if the unit is older than six months or if the chirping alert sounds.
*   **Breaker Panel:** Ensure the panel door opens easily and verify that all circuits are clearly labeled.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The Flashlight Test):** Always use a high-lumen flashlight to inspect walls at an angle. This "raking light" technique reveals dents, patch jobs, and hidden moisture stains that are invisible under direct overhead lighting.
*   **Pro Tip (Documentation):** Utilize time-stamped digital photography for every defect. High-resolution images are your primary defense in security deposit disputes.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring Odors):** Never dismiss a faint smell of mildew or mustiness. If you smell it, perform a deeper investigation behind large furniture or inside closets; these are often early indicators of a hidden leak.
*   **Pitfall (The "Quick Check"):** Do not simply look at appliances; operate them. A stove may look pristine but have a broken heating element that only becomes apparent during use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I perform a formal condo inspection?**
A: Ideally, perform a formal inspection at move-in and move-out. For long-term tenants, a semi-annual routine inspection (every 6 months) is recommended to catch preventative maintenance issues before they become expensive repairs.

**Q: What should I do if I find a major issue, like a ceiling leak, during an inspection?**
A: Cease the inspection immediately. Document the issue with photos, contact the building superintendent or management office if it involves building-wide infrastructure, and notify the tenant/owner in writing. Do not attempt to repair major structural or electrical issues yourself.

**Q: Should the tenant be present during the inspection?**
A: It is highly recommended. Having the tenant present promotes transparency, allows you to point out existing conditions, and often leads to a better mutual understanding of expectations regarding property maintenance.
