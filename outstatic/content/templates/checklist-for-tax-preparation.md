# Standard Operating Procedure: Annual Tax Preparation

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the systematic process for gathering, verifying, and organizing financial documentation required for the annual tax filing cycle. Adhering to this protocol ensures compliance with regulatory requirements, minimizes the risk of audit triggers, and maximizes potential deductions by ensuring no eligible financial data is overlooked. This document serves as both a workflow guide and a quality control checklist to maintain consistency and accuracy throughout the preparation period.

## Phase 1: Information Gathering and Documentation
*   **Identify Income Streams:** Collect all forms related to gross income, including W-2s (employment), 1099-NEC/MISC (contract work), 1099-INT (interest), 1099-DIV (dividends), and 1099-B (capital gains/losses).
*   **Verify Business Records:** For self-employed individuals or business owners, aggregate the Profit & Loss (P&L) statement, balance sheet, and a comprehensive ledger of gross receipts.
*   **Collect Deductible Expenses:** Gather receipts and invoices for business-related expenses, including office supplies, travel, equipment purchases, professional fees, and insurance premiums.
*   **Compile Personal Tax Documents:** Secure documentation for health savings account (HSA) contributions, student loan interest, mortgage interest (Form 1098), and property tax payments.

## Phase 2: Reconciliation and Review
*   **Cross-Reference Totals:** Reconcile all 1099 and W-2 totals against your internal bookkeeping records to ensure no discrepancies exist between reported income and actual cash inflows.
*   **Audit Expense Categories:** Review all listed business expenses for eligibility under current tax law. Ensure each expense is supported by an invoice, receipt, or bank statement.
*   **Verify Personal Information:** Confirm that all Social Security numbers, legal names, and current addresses are accurate for yourself, your spouse, and all dependents.
*   **Check Prior-Year Data:** Review the prior year’s tax return for carryover items, such as capital losses, net operating losses, or depreciation schedules.

## Phase 3: Submission and Finalization
*   **Final Data Integrity Check:** Perform a final review of the tax file to ensure all uploaded documents are legible and all numerical data is correctly entered into the filing software or provided to your CPA.
*   **Submit to Authorized Representative:** If using a tax professional, securely transmit all files via an encrypted portal rather than email. 
*   **Secure Filing Confirmation:** Once the return is e-filed, download and save the official IRS acknowledgment of receipt and the final PDF copy of the return.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Create a dedicated digital folder structure (e.g., "Tax Year 202X / Income / Deductions / Investments") at the beginning of the year to automate the gathering process.
*   **Pitfall - Mixing Accounts:** Never combine personal and business expenses in the same bank account. This makes audit reconciliation exponentially more difficult and risks the disallowance of deductions.
*   **Pro Tip:** If you missed a deduction, do not panic. Amended returns (Form 1040-X) can be filed to claim refunds for up to three years after the original filing deadline.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring 1099s:** Do not assume that if you did not receive a 1099, the income is not reportable. The IRS receives copies of these forms; failing to report income is a primary trigger for an automated notice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the most common reason for a tax audit?**
The most common trigger is a discrepancy between the income reported on your return and the information the IRS receives from third parties (employers, banks, and brokerages). Always ensure your W-2s and 1099s match your internal records.

**2. How long should I keep my tax documentation?**
The IRS recommends keeping records for at least three years from the date you filed your original return. However, if you are claiming a loss from worthless securities or bad debt, you should keep records for seven years.

**3. What should I do if I cannot file by the deadline?**
If you cannot meet the deadline, file Form 4868 to request an automatic six-month extension. Please note: an extension to file is not an extension to pay; any taxes owed must still be paid by the original deadline to avoid interest and late payment penalties.
