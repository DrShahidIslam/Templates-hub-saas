# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC Preventative Maintenance Inspection

### Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting a comprehensive HVAC inspection. Regular maintenance is critical to extending equipment lifespan, ensuring occupant comfort, maintaining indoor air quality, and optimizing energy efficiency. This procedure is intended for qualified technicians to identify potential failures before they result in costly downtime. All inspections must be performed in accordance with manufacturer specifications and local safety codes, with personal protective equipment (PPE) worn at all times.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Inspection Protocols
*   Ensure all lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedures are followed before accessing internal electrical components.
*   Verify that the technician has the necessary PPE, including safety glasses, work gloves, and voltage testers.
*   Review the equipment’s maintenance history log to identify recurring issues or recent repairs.
*   Confirm the thermostat is set to the correct mode for testing (Cooling/Heating).

## 2. Exterior Unit (Condenser) Inspection
*   **Clear Debris:** Remove leaves, grass, and dirt from around the unit perimeter to ensure at least 24 inches of clearance.
*   **Coil Inspection:** Inspect condenser coils for physical damage or heavy debris buildup; clean with an approved coil cleaner if necessary.
*   **Fan Operation:** Verify the fan motor is running smoothly without unusual vibration, rattling, or grinding noises.
*   **Refrigerant Lines:** Inspect lines for signs of oil or leakage, which may indicate a refrigerant leak. Ensure insulation on suction lines is intact.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Check the contactor for pitted points and ensure all wire terminals are tight and free of corrosion.

## 3. Interior Unit (Air Handler/Furnace) Inspection
*   **Filter Assessment:** Check the air filter for saturation. Replace with the specified MERV-rated filter if airflow is restricted.
*   **Blower Assembly:** Inspect the blower wheel for dust accumulation and confirm the motor operates at the correct amperage.
*   **Evaporator Coil:** Check for ice buildup or mold growth; clean if accessibility permits.
*   **Condensate Management:** Ensure the condensate drain pan is clear of algae/debris and the drain line is draining freely into the designated outlet.
*   **Heat Exchanger (Gas Units):** Perform a visual inspection for cracks, rust, or soot buildup which could indicate a carbon monoxide hazard.

## 4. Operational and Performance Testing
*   **Temperature Differential:** Measure the supply and return air temperatures (Delta T) to ensure the system is operating within the manufacturer’s specified range.
*   **Amperage/Voltage:** Measure and record the electrical draw of the compressor and motors against the nameplate ratings.
*   **Safety Controls:** Test high-pressure, low-pressure, and rollout switches to confirm the unit shuts down appropriately when triggered.
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Verify that the thermostat signal accurately matches the system performance.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - Over-cleaning:** Avoid using high-pressure washers on aluminum condenser fins, as they can easily bend and restrict airflow. Always use a low-pressure spray or dedicated coil fin comb.
*   **Pro Tip - Refrigerant Gauges:** Minimize the connection and disconnection of manifold gauges to prevent unnecessary refrigerant loss (even in small amounts). Use low-loss fittings.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Noise:** Never ignore "soft" sounds. A slight hum from a contactor or a faint metallic rattle in a motor housing is often the precursor to a catastrophic mid-season breakdown.
*   **Pro Tip - Infrared Thermal Imaging:** Use a thermal camera to identify hot spots on electrical terminal blocks, which often indicate loose or oxidizing connections before they fail.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should a commercial HVAC system be inspected?**
A: Industry best practice is a minimum of semi-annual inspections—once before the cooling season and once before the heating season.

**Q: What is the most common cause of HVAC system failure during an inspection?**
A: Restricted airflow due to dirty filters or fouled evaporator coils is the most frequent cause, leading to compressor overheating and reduced efficiency.

**Q: If the Delta T (temperature difference) is outside the manufacturer's range, what is the first thing I should check?**
A: Check the airflow first. A dirty filter or a dirty evaporator coil is the most common reason for an incorrect temperature differential; ensure the air side is clean before adjusting refrigerant charges.
