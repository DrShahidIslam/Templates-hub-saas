# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for Water Treatment Plant (WTP)

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the systematic protocol for the preventive maintenance (PM) of a municipal or industrial water treatment plant. The primary objective of this PM program is to ensure consistent water quality, extend the operational lifespan of critical mechanical and electrical assets, and mitigate the risk of unplanned system failures. Adherence to this checklist is mandatory for all maintenance staff to ensure compliance with safety regulations and environmental health standards.

## 1. Daily Inspection & Monitoring
*   **Visual Site Inspection:** Walk the perimeter and facility interior to check for leaks, unusual noises, or odors.
*   **Pumps and Motors:** Monitor vibration levels, bearing temperatures (via infrared thermography), and check for seal leakage.
*   **Chemical Feed Systems:** Verify levels in storage tanks and confirm dosing pumps are stroking within the calibrated range.
*   **Instrumentation Readings:** Log turbidity, pH, residual chlorine, and flow rates; compare against historical baselines for anomalies.
*   **Emergency Power:** Ensure the backup generator is in "Auto" mode and fuel levels are sufficient.

## 2. Weekly Mechanical & Filtration Maintenance
*   **Filter Beds:** Inspect surface conditions for mud-ball formation or uneven media distribution; backwash if differential pressure exceeds set points.
*   **Valves and Actuators:** Cycle all major isolation and control valves to prevent freezing/seizing; inspect for packing leaks.
*   **Blowers:** Check intake filters for debris; inspect belt tension and lubricate couplings as per manufacturer specifications.
*   **Chemical Lines:** Inspect all tubing and injection quills for signs of scale buildup or crystallization.

## 3. Monthly Electrical & Instrumentation Calibration
*   **Sensors/Probes:** Clean and recalibrate pH, ORP, and turbidity sensors using standard buffer solutions.
*   **Control Panels:** Inspect electrical cabinets for dust, moisture, or vermin; vacuum debris and tighten all loose terminal connections.
*   **SCADA Connectivity:** Verify data signal integrity and ensure alarm set points are active and responding correctly.
*   **Battery Backup:** Test UPS systems for critical PLC hardware to ensure battery health under load.

## 4. Quarterly/Semi-Annual Preventive Overhauls
*   **Pump Servicing:** Change lubricating oil in gearboxes and grease bearings; check alignment of couplings (laser alignment recommended).
*   **Chemical Tank Cleaning:** Drain and clean sludge/sediment from bulk storage tanks.
*   **Backflow Preventers:** Test all backflow prevention devices and verify certification documents.
*   **Structural Inspection:** Inspect concrete basins, walkways, and railings for signs of corrosion or structural degradation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement a "Predictive Maintenance" strategy by using vibration analysis and oil sampling; it is significantly cheaper than replacing a failed motor.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always tag out/lock out (LOTO) equipment before performing mechanical work; never trust a "soft" software lockout.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-greasing motors. Too much grease can blow out bearing seals and cause motor overheating. Follow the manufacturer’s specific gram weight recommendations.
*   **Pitfall:** Neglecting documentation. If a maintenance task is not logged, for audit purposes, it did not happen. Keep a centralized, digitized maintenance log.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I calibrate the turbidity meters?**
A: Turbidity meters should generally be verified weekly and calibrated monthly, or whenever readings deviate by more than 5% from laboratory reference samples.

**Q: What is the most critical component to monitor for long-term plant health?**
A: The pump and motor assemblies. They are the "heart" of the plant; improper maintenance here leads to flow restriction, pressure spikes, and catastrophic downtime.

**Q: Can I postpone PM if the plant is running at peak capacity?**
A: While operational demand is high, critical PM (like chemical dosing safety checks) should never be skipped. Non-critical tasks should be rescheduled in the CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) for the next available low-demand window, never simply ignored.
