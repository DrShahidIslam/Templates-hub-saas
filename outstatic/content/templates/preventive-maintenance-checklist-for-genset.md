# Standard Operating Procedure: Generator Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection and maintenance protocols required to ensure the operational readiness, longevity, and reliability of standby power generation systems. Regular preventive maintenance (PM) is critical to mitigating the risks of mechanical failure, fuel degradation, and control system malfunctions. All maintenance activities must be conducted by certified technicians in strict accordance with the Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) guidelines and site-specific safety regulations.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Maintenance Protocols
*   **Lock-Out/Tag-Out (LOTO):** Ensure the generator is in "Off" mode and the starting battery is disconnected to prevent accidental cranking.
*   **PPE:** Personnel must wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment, including safety glasses, oil-resistant gloves, and steel-toed boots.
*   **Environment:** Ensure the engine is cool to the touch and the area is well-ventilated to prevent inhalation of exhaust fumes or fumes from chemicals.

## 2. Lubrication and Cooling Systems
*   **Engine Oil:** Check oil levels using the dipstick. If levels are low, top up with the OEM-specified lubricant. Note: If the maintenance interval is reached, drain oil, replace filters, and refill with fresh oil.
*   **Coolant System:** Inspect coolant levels in the radiator/expansion tank. Check hoses for signs of cracking, bulging, or brittleness. Inspect the radiator core for debris or fin damage.
*   **Leaks:** Inspect the engine block, oil pan, and cooling lines for any signs of seepage or active leaks.

## 3. Fuel and Air Intake Systems
*   **Fuel Levels:** Verify fuel tank levels are at the required standby capacity.
*   **Fuel Filters:** Inspect water separators and fuel filters; drain any water found in the separator bowl. Replace fuel filters per the hourly service interval.
*   **Air Intake:** Check the air cleaner indicator. If the filter is clogged or shows "red," replace the air filter element. Check air intake piping for loose clamps or holes.

## 4. Electrical and Battery Systems
*   **Battery Condition:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion. Clean terminals and apply dielectric grease if necessary. Test the battery voltage and electrolyte level (if applicable).
*   **Battery Charger:** Verify the charger is operational and the float voltage is within the manufacturer's specified range.
*   **Control Panel:** Check for any "Alarm" or "Warning" lights on the digital controller. Clear history logs if necessary and ensure all display readings are within normal parameters.

## 5. Exhaust and Mechanical Integrity
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust manifold and piping for leaks or carbon soot deposits, which indicate a seal failure.
*   **Drive Belts:** Check belt tension and look for signs of fraying, cracking, or glazing. Adjust or replace as needed.
*   **Engine Mounts:** Visually inspect vibration isolators for cracks or excessive compression.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always perform a "Load Bank Test" annually. Running a generator without load for long periods leads to "wet stacking," where unburned fuel accumulates in the exhaust system.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a detailed maintenance logbook inside the generator enclosure. This is essential for compliance audits and troubleshooting intermittent issues.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not overfill the oil. Overfilling can lead to aeration, which reduces lubrication effectiveness and can cause severe engine damage.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore "small" leaks. A minor coolant weep often indicates a failing hose or gasket that will inevitably rupture under the high-pressure conditions of a real power emergency.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a standby generator be exercised?**
A: Industry best practice suggests running the generator under load for 30 minutes at least once a month to ensure the engine reaches operating temperature and that fuel hasn't degraded.

**Q: Why is "wet stacking" dangerous?**
A: Wet stacking occurs when an engine operates under light loads for too long. Unburned fuel accumulates in the exhaust, creating a fire hazard and reducing the engine's power output efficiency over time.

**Q: What is the most common cause of generator failure?**
A: Battery failure. Because batteries degrade even when idle, they are the single most common reason a generator will fail to start during an outage. Always replace standby batteries every 24–36 months regardless of appearance.
