# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Equipment Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for conducting routine inspections of electrical equipment to ensure operational safety, regulatory compliance, and the mitigation of fire or shock hazards. All personnel performing these inspections must be qualified, wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), and follow Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures where applicable. The objective is to identify deteriorating components, improper installations, and environmental threats before they result in equipment failure or workplace injury.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Safety Protocols
*   **Verify Authorization:** Ensure you are qualified for the voltage level of the equipment being inspected.
*   **PPE Requirements:** Don appropriate PPE, including arc-rated clothing, insulated gloves (tested/certified), safety glasses, and non-conductive footwear.
*   **Documentation:** Carry the specific equipment maintenance manual, a calibrated multimeter, an infrared thermal imager, and the inspection log sheet.
*   **De-energization:** Where possible and required, apply LOTO procedures. If inspecting while energized, maintain appropriate approach boundaries.

## Phase 2: External Housing and Physical Integrity
*   **Enclosure Inspection:** Check for dents, corrosion, rust, or physical damage to the equipment cabinet.
*   **Seal Integrity:** Verify that gaskets and seals are intact to prevent moisture, dust, or pest ingress.
*   **Fasteners:** Ensure all enclosure screws, bolts, and locking mechanisms are tight and accounted for.
*   **Labeling:** Confirm all safety signage, warning labels, and equipment ID tags are present, legible, and current.
*   **Ventilation:** Check air intakes and exhaust vents for obstructions, dust buildup, or debris.

## Phase 3: Wiring, Connections, and Termination
*   **Cable Condition:** Inspect insulation for signs of cracking, fraying, discoloration, or melting.
*   **Connection Points:** Check for signs of overheating (discoloration of lugs or wires). Use a thermal imager to identify hot spots under load.
*   **Torque Check:** Ensure that terminations are tight and that there is no evidence of loose strands or poor contact.
*   **Grounding:** Verify that the grounding conductor is securely attached, corrosion-free, and undamaged.
*   **Conduit Integrity:** Ensure conduits are properly supported, sealed at entry points, and free from crushing or sagging.

## Phase 4: Operational and Internal Components
*   **Circuit Breakers/Fuses:** Check for signs of tripping or "reset" history. Ensure the correct fuse ratings are installed.
*   **Contactors/Relays:** Inspect for carbon buildup, pitted contacts, or excessive humming/vibration during operation.
*   **Internal Cleanliness:** Inspect the interior for dust, moisture, or foreign objects. Use a non-conductive vacuum to remove dust—never use compressed air, as it pushes debris into delicate components.
*   **Control Indicators:** Verify that pilot lights, digital displays, and emergency stop buttons function as intended.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Thermal Imaging):** Always perform thermography while the equipment is under at least 40% load. Cold connections are often missed during static inspections.
*   **Pro Tip (The "Smell" Test):** Never ignore the smell of ozone or "burnt plastic." It is often the first indicator of an impending electrical fault that physical sight cannot detect.
*   **Pitfall (Compressed Air):** Avoid using compressed air for cleaning internal cabinets. It creates an electrostatic hazard and forces conductive dust into sensitive control circuitry.
*   **Pitfall (Over-tightening):** Do not over-torque lugs. Always follow the manufacturer’s specified torque values; over-tightening can strip threads or crack terminal blocks.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should these inspections be performed?**
Inspections should be based on the manufacturer’s recommendations and the criticality of the equipment. Generally, critical infrastructure should be inspected quarterly, while general-purpose equipment may suffice with an annual inspection.

**2. What should I do if I find a potential hazard?**
If a hazard is identified, immediately isolate the equipment using LOTO procedures, report the finding to the facilities supervisor, and tag the equipment as "Out of Service" until repairs are completed and verified.

**3. Is it necessary to wear gloves if the equipment is off?**
Yes. Always treat equipment as energized until it has been physically verified as de-energized using a calibrated test instrument. PPE should remain on until the "zero energy" state is confirmed.
