# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocols required to ensure vehicle longevity, operational safety, and optimal performance. Regular maintenance minimizes the risk of mechanical failure, enhances fuel efficiency, and preserves the asset’s resale value. Technicians and vehicle owners are expected to follow this systematic checklist according to the vehicle manufacturer’s recommended service intervals.

## 1. Under-the-Hood Inspections
*   **Engine Oil:** Check levels via dipstick; inspect for discoloration (milky appearance indicates coolant contamination). Ensure oil meets the viscosity requirements defined in the owner’s manual.
*   **Coolant System:** Check reservoir levels while the engine is cool. Inspect hoses for cracks, bulges, or signs of dry rot.
*   **Brake Fluid:** Verify level is between 'Min' and 'Max' markers. Ensure the fluid is clean (translucent/yellowish rather than dark brown).
*   **Transmission Fluid:** Check fluid levels and odor. Burnt odors may indicate internal component wear.
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion. Ensure cables are tight and secure. Verify the mounting bracket holds the battery firmly in place.
*   **Drive Belts:** Check for fraying, glazing, or missing teeth on the serpentine belt. Ensure tension is within specifications.

## 2. Exterior & Safety Systems
*   **Lighting:** Test all high beams, low beams, turn signals, brake lights, reverse lights, and hazard flashers.
*   **Windshield Wipers:** Inspect blades for streaks or worn rubber. Replace if they leave gaps in clearing.
*   **Tires:** Measure tread depth (minimum 4/32" for safety). Check sidewalls for bubbles or cuts. Verify tire pressure matches the door-jamb placard (not the max pressure listed on the tire).
*   **Glass:** Inspect the windshield and mirrors for cracks or pitting that could compromise visibility.

## 3. Undercarriage & Chassis
*   **Suspension:** Visually inspect shocks and struts for fluid leaks. Check bushings for significant cracking or play.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust pipe and muffler for rust-through, holes, or loose hangers that cause rattling.
*   **Brakes:** Inspect brake pads for friction material thickness. Check rotors/discs for scoring or deep grooves.
*   **Fluid Leaks:** Observe the ground underneath the vehicle for fresh spots of oil, transmission fluid, or coolant.

## 4. Interior & Operational Checks
*   **Dashboard Indicators:** Confirm no check engine, ABS, or airbag lights are illuminated after start-up.
*   **Brake Pedal Feel:** Ensure the pedal provides firm resistance and does not sink to the floor under steady pressure.
*   **Parking Brake:** Test the engagement to ensure it holds the vehicle securely on an incline.
*   **Climate Control:** Verify that the A/C, heater, and defroster functions are operational.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a physical or digital logbook of all maintenance tasks, including dates, mileage, and receipts. This is essential for warranty claims and future resale.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always follow the "Severe Driving Conditions" schedule in your manual if you frequently drive in stop-and-go traffic, extreme temperatures, or dusty environments.
*   **Pitfall:** Never open the radiator cap while the engine is hot. Pressurized coolant can cause severe burns.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not ignore "minor" leaks. Small drips often indicate a failing seal that can lead to catastrophic component failure if left unaddressed.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid using "universal" fluids if your manufacturer specifies proprietary formulations, particularly for transmission and power steering systems.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I change my engine oil?**
A: Modern synthetic oils often allow for 7,500 to 10,000-mile intervals, but you must strictly adhere to the schedule provided by your vehicle manufacturer, as every engine’s design requirements differ.

**Q: What do the different colors of fluid leaks mean?**
A: Generally, brown/black indicates oil; red/pink indicates transmission or power steering fluid; green/orange/blue indicates engine coolant; and clear/water-like is typically just A/C condensation.

**Q: Can I check my own tire pressure if I don't have a gauge?**
A: No. Visual inspection is highly unreliable. Always use an accurate tire pressure gauge to measure the PSI, as tires can be dangerously under-inflated even when they look "normal" to the naked eye.
