# Standard Operating Procedure: Comprehensive Home Inspection

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional methodology for conducting a systematic home inspection. Whether you are performing a pre-purchase assessment, a seasonal maintenance check, or a property turnover evaluation, this document ensures consistency, safety, and thoroughness. Following this protocol mitigates the risk of overlooking structural defects, safety hazards, or deferred maintenance issues that could lead to significant financial liability.

## Exterior Structural and Site Inspection
*   **Foundation:** Inspect for horizontal or vertical cracks, signs of spalling, or moisture seepage.
*   **Siding and Trim:** Check for rot, peeling paint, loose boards, or insect infestation (look for mud tubes).
*   **Roofing:** Evaluate the condition of shingles (curling, missing, or granular loss), flashing around chimneys/vents, and gutter clearance.
*   **Drainage:** Ensure downspouts direct water at least six feet away from the foundation; check for low spots in the yard causing pooling.
*   **Hardscaping:** Verify walkway and driveway integrity, looking for trip hazards and proper slope away from the dwelling.

## Interior Systems and Mechanicals
*   **Electrical Panel:** Check for tripped breakers, signs of scorching, or double-tapped wires. Ensure all circuits are clearly labeled.
*   **HVAC System:** Test both heating and cooling cycles. Inspect filters, check for unusual noises, and verify the condensation line is clear.
*   **Plumbing:** Operate all faucets to check for water pressure and drainage speed. Inspect under sinks and around toilets for active leaks or signs of past water damage.
*   **Water Heater:** Check the age (via serial number), look for corrosion at connections, and ensure the pressure relief valve is correctly piped.
*   **Attic/Crawlspace:** Inspect for signs of moisture, mold, pest activity, or compromised insulation.

## Interior Living Spaces
*   **Windows and Doors:** Operate every window to ensure smooth opening/closing and functional locks. Check door frames for squareness (indicators of settling).
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Scan for hairline cracks, water stains (indicating roof or pipe leaks), or bulging drywall.
*   **Flooring:** Look for unevenness, loose tiles, squeaky floorboards, or signs of moisture damage near external walls.
*   **Safety Devices:** Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide alarms. Verify the presence and expiration dates of fire extinguishers.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Use Your Senses.** A moldy smell (mustiness) in a basement is often a more reliable indicator of issues than a visual check. Trust your nose as much as your eyes.
*   **Pro Tip: Leverage Technology.** Use a high-lumen LED flashlight to look for shadows or irregularities in surfaces and a simple moisture meter to identify hidden leaks behind walls.
*   **Pitfall: The "Cosmetic Trap."** Do not let fresh paint or staging distract you from structural realities. A newly painted ceiling often hides active roof leaks.
*   **Pitfall: Neglecting Utility Access.** Always ensure you have the necessary clearance to access the main water shut-off and electrical main; you do not want to be searching for these in an emergency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should a homeowner conduct a full inspection?**
A: A comprehensive "deep-dive" inspection should be performed twice a year—typically in the spring and autumn—to address seasonal transitions.

**Q: What is the most critical area to inspect for long-term home value?**
A: Water intrusion is the primary enemy of any structure. Prioritize the roof, gutters, and foundation, as these are the most expensive items to repair.

**Q: Should I perform these inspections myself or hire a pro?**
A: You can perform routine maintenance inspections yourself, but always hire a licensed, certified home inspector for pre-purchase assessments or major concerns regarding structural integrity or electrical safety.
