# Standard Operating Procedure: New Home Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting a thorough inspection of a new residential property. Whether performing a pre-drywall walkthrough, a final punch-list inspection, or a move-in assessment, this document ensures that all structural, mechanical, and cosmetic elements are vetted against quality standards. Following this protocol mitigates the risk of latent defects, ensures contractor accountability, and guarantees that the property meets all safety and habitability requirements before final occupancy.

## 1. Exterior & Building Envelope
*   **Foundation:** Check for cracks, settling patterns, or signs of water pooling near the perimeter.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect shingles for proper alignment, damage, or missing granules; verify that flashing around chimneys and vents is properly sealed.
*   **Siding/Cladding:** Ensure uniform installation, no gaps, and that moisture barriers are intact.
*   **Grading:** Confirm that the ground slopes away from the foundation to prevent basement flooding.
*   **Windows/Doors:** Check seals, weather stripping, and ensure all locks operate smoothly without resistance.

## 2. Interior Structural & Systems
*   **Electrical:** Test every outlet with a circuit tester. Verify all light switches operate the correct fixtures and check the breaker panel for clear labeling.
*   **HVAC:** Run the system in both heating and cooling modes. Ensure airflow is consistent in all rooms and check for unusual noises or odors.
*   **Plumbing:** Fill sinks and tubs to capacity, then release to check drainage speed. Flush all toilets and inspect under cabinets for leaks.
*   **Attic/Crawlspace:** Inspect for signs of insulation gaps, pest intrusion, or moisture staining on rafters.

## 3. Cosmetic & Finishings
*   **Walls/Ceilings:** Check for drywall seams, "nail pops," or uneven paint application.
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for creaky boards, carpet lifting, or tile grout consistency.
*   **Cabinetry/Millwork:** Verify that all cabinet doors and drawers align correctly and that latches function.
*   **Fixtures:** Inspect faucets, showerheads, and towel bars for stability and finish quality.

## 4. Safety & Compliance
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test each unit manually; ensure they are positioned according to local building codes.
*   **Stairways:** Verify handrail stability and ensure tread depth/riser height feels safe.
*   **Garage Door:** Test the auto-reverse sensor by placing an object (e.g., a 2x4 piece of wood) in the door path.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Lighting" Strategy:** Always conduct inspections during daylight hours. Bring a high-lumen flashlight to inspect dark corners and under-sink areas, as shadows hide surface defects.
*   **Pro Tip: Document Everything:** Take high-resolution photos of every issue. Create a digital folder categorized by room to simplify the punch-list submission to your contractor.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Leaks:** Never dismiss a "minor" drip under a sink. It is often indicative of a poor connection or improper installation that could lead to mold or water damage within weeks.
*   **Pitfall: Rushing the Walkthrough:** New homeowners often get caught up in the excitement. Dedicate at least 3–4 hours to the process; do not let the builder rush you.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I hire a professional inspector even for a brand-new home?**
A: Yes. Even new construction is subject to human error. A professional third-party inspector brings specialized equipment (such as thermal imaging) that can identify hidden insulation gaps or electrical issues you might miss.

**Q: What if the builder refuses to fix a minor cosmetic issue?**
A: Prioritize items that affect safety or structural integrity. For minor cosmetic issues, document them in writing via email to create a paper trail, and negotiate a "hold-back" of final payment until the issues are resolved.

**Q: Is it necessary to test every single electrical outlet?**
A: Absolutely. It is common for contractors to leave a wire disconnected or miswired during the final trim-out. Using a $10 outlet tester can save you from a major electrical headache later.
