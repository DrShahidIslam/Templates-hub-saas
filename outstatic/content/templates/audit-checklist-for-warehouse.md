# Standard Operating Procedure: Warehouse Audit Protocols

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic approach to conducting a comprehensive warehouse audit. The primary objective is to verify inventory accuracy, assess facility safety compliance, evaluate operational efficiency, and identify potential risks to supply chain continuity. By adhering to this rigorous audit framework, management ensures high-level inventory integrity, mitigates shrinkage, and maintains compliance with OSHA and industry-standard safety regulations. This process should be executed quarterly to maintain peak operational performance.

## Phase 1: Physical Inventory and Accuracy Verification
*   **Cycle Count Reconciliation:** Compare physical counts against ERP/WMS system records for a statistically significant sample of SKUs.
*   **Discrepancy Analysis:** Document all variances; categorize them by overages, shortages, or damaged goods.
*   **Bin Location Verification:** Ensure items are stored in their designated zones and that WMS location data matches physical reality.
*   **Dead Stock Identification:** Flag inventory that has not moved in the last 180 days for potential liquidation or disposal.
*   **Unit of Measure (UOM) Audit:** Confirm that items are being counted in the correct UOM (e.g., eaches vs. cases).

## Phase 2: Safety and Facility Compliance
*   **Egress and Aisles:** Confirm that all fire exits, walkways, and aisle ways are clear of debris, pallets, or unauthorized storage.
*   **PPE Compliance:** Audit personal protective equipment usage (e.g., high-visibility vests, steel-toed boots, safety glasses) among all personnel.
*   **Equipment Inspection:** Check forklift and pallet jack maintenance logs; ensure pre-shift inspection checklists are completed and signed.
*   **Hazardous Materials (HazMat):** Verify that all MSDS/SDS documentation is accessible and that hazardous substances are stored in secondary containment.
*   **Signage and Marking:** Ensure floor markings, speed limit signage, and load-bearing rack labels are visible and in good condition.

## Phase 3: Operational Efficiency and Process Flow
*   **Receiving Procedures:** Verify that incoming shipments are inspected, logged, and staged within the established KPI timeframe.
*   **Picking/Packing Accuracy:** Review error logs and verify that packing slips match the items processed for shipment.
*   **Warehouse Layout:** Assess traffic patterns for bottlenecks and confirm the flow from Receiving to Staging to Shipping is logical.
*   **Security Controls:** Inspect perimeter fencing, security cameras, access badge readers, and restricted-access zones for integrity.

## Phase 4: Documentation and Administrative Audit
*   **Record Retention:** Ensure all shipping, receiving, and return documentation is archived according to company policy.
*   **Employee Training:** Audit digital training records to ensure all staff are certified for current equipment and safety protocols.
*   **Maintenance Logs:** Review logs for conveyors, dock levelers, and racking structural repairs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Surprise" Factor:** Conduct "spot" audits on specific high-value zones without prior announcement to get a realistic view of daily maintenance habits.
*   **Pro Tip: Photographic Evidence:** Always document non-compliance with timestamps and photos; this is vital for insurance claims and management reporting.
*   **Pitfall: The "Clean-up" Bias:** Avoid letting the warehouse team perform a "deep clean" specifically for the audit; it masks the true operational state of the facility.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Variances:** Don't dismiss minor inventory discrepancies as "rounding errors." These often signify larger process failures in receiving or picking.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a full warehouse audit be conducted?**
A: While cycle counting should be continuous, a full-scale operational and safety audit should be conducted at least quarterly to keep processes aligned with KPIs and compliance standards.

**Q: What is the most effective way to handle inventory shrinkage findings?**
A: If shrinkage exceeds the company-defined threshold, immediate root-cause analysis is required. Investigate check-out logs, security footage, and system access logs to determine if the loss is administrative or due to theft.

**Q: Should warehouse staff be involved in the audit process?**
A: Yes. Involving floor staff in the audit process promotes accountability and provides them with a clearer understanding of why specific procedures (like bin-to-bin transfers) are critical to overall success.
