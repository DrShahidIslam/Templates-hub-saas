# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Routine Management for Autistic Children

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is designed to provide a structured, predictable, and supportive framework for managing the daily routine of an autistic child. By establishing consistent transitions and clear visual expectations, this routine aims to reduce anxiety, minimize sensory overwhelm, and foster independence. The following protocol serves as a baseline that should be customized to the child’s specific sensory needs, communication style, and developmental milestones.

## Morning Routine: Establishing Predictability
*   **Waking & Sensory Reset:** Wake the child at the same time daily. Allow 5–10 minutes of quiet transition time before requiring verbal interaction.
*   **Visual Schedule Review:** Review the physical or digital visual schedule together to confirm the day’s flow.
*   **Hygiene Protocol:** Use visual timers for tasks like brushing teeth to provide a concrete "end" point.
*   **Dressing:** Lay out clothes the night before to eliminate decision fatigue. Use sensory-friendly fabrics (remove tags/seams if necessary).
*   **Breakfast Check:** Provide a predictable, low-sensory environment. If the child has selective eating habits, prioritize consistency over variety.

## Mid-Day & School/Activity Transitions
*   **Transition Warnings:** Provide a "5-minute warning" before any shift in activity using a visual timer or a countdown clock.
*   **Task Chunking:** Break complex tasks (e.g., "clean up toys") into micro-steps (e.g., "put blocks in the bin," then "put the bin on the shelf").
*   **Sensory Breaks:** Schedule mandatory 10-minute "down-time" periods in a sensory-safe space (low lighting, noise-canceling headphones, or weighted blankets) to prevent meltdowns.
*   **Hydration & Nutrition:** Monitor blood sugar and hydration levels, as dysregulation often stems from physical needs the child may not be able to articulate.

## Evening Routine: Decompression & Regulation
*   **Environment Dimming:** 60 minutes before bed, lower the lights and reduce ambient noise to trigger melatonin production.
*   **"Brain Dump" or Reflection:** Use a communication board or simplified verbal check-in to allow the child to express any high-stress moments from the day.
*   **Predictable Hygiene Sequence:** Maintain the exact same sequence for showering/bathing and pajamas every night.
*   **Sleep Hygiene:** Ensure the bedroom environment remains consistent, utilizing white noise machines or blackout curtains to stabilize the sleep environment.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "First/Then" Strategy:** Use the "First/Then" visual method (e.g., "First, brush teeth; Then, listen to music") to negotiate non-preferred tasks effectively.
*   **Pro Tip: Incorporate Special Interests:** Use the child's special interest as a reward or a transition tool (e.g., watching a clip of a preferred topic after completing homework).
*   **Pitfall: Over-Scheduling:** Avoid "back-to-back" activities without scheduled transition time. Fatigue is cumulative for neurodivergent children.
*   **Pitfall: Inconsistent Visuals:** Do not change the visual schedule layout frequently. Consistency is more important than aesthetics.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should I do if my child resists the schedule?**
Validate their frustration, ensure their sensory needs are being met (e.g., check for noise or discomfort), and offer a controlled choice (e.g., "Do you want to put on your left shoe first or your right shoe first?") to return a sense of agency.

**2. How do I handle unexpected changes to the routine (e.g., doctor appointments)?**
Introduce the change as far in advance as possible using a visual calendar. Create a "Social Story" (a brief document explaining the change) to help them understand what to expect during the event.

**3. Are visual schedules necessary if my child is high-functioning?**
Yes. Even for highly verbal or neurotypical-passing individuals, a visual schedule functions as an external "executive function" tool, reducing the cognitive load of remembering daily obligations and lowering anxiety.
