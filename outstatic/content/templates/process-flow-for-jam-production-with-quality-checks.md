# Standard Operating Procedure: Commercial Jam Production

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized process for the commercial production of fruit jam, ensuring consistency, food safety, and optimal shelf-life. Adherence to these protocols is mandatory to maintain compliance with health regulations (HACCP/FDA standards) and to ensure that every batch meets our quality benchmarks for texture, flavor, and stability. All personnel must wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) and follow strict sanitation guidelines throughout the production cycle.

## Phase 1: Raw Material Inspection and Preparation

*   **Supplier Verification:** Cross-check incoming fruit/pectin/sugar batches against Purchase Orders.
*   **Quality Inspection:** Inspect fruit for signs of mold, rot, or foreign objects. Reject any sub-standard materials immediately.
*   **Sanitization:** Wash all fruit using approved food-grade sanitizing solutions.
*   **Preparation:** Hull, peel, and chop fruit to standardized sizes to ensure consistent cooking times.
*   **Weighing:** Precisely measure fruit, sugar, pectin, and acidity regulators (citric acid) according to the Master Batch Recipe.

## Phase 2: Cooking and Thermal Processing

*   **Blending:** Combine fruit and measured sugar in the steam-jacketed kettle.
*   **Heat Application:** Bring the mixture to a slow boil while stirring continuously to prevent scorching.
*   **Pectin Activation:** Incorporate pectin/acid premix at the specified temperature (typically 180°F - 190°F) to ensure proper gelation.
*   **Refractometer Test:** Verify Total Soluble Solids (Brix level). Aim for the standard 65% Brix or as specified by the product formula.
*   **Temperature Logging:** Maintain a continuous digital log of core temperature to ensure kill-step requirements are met for pathogens.

## Phase 3: Filling, Capping, and Cooling

*   **Jar Preparation:** Ensure jars are pre-heated/sterilized to prevent thermal shock upon filling.
*   **Filling:** Fill jars to the specified "headspace" requirement to allow for vacuum seal formation.
*   **Capping:** Apply lids using an automated vacuum capper. Ensure torque levels are consistent with manufacturer specifications.
*   **Cooling Tunnel:** Pass jars through a cooling tunnel or water bath to lower the temperature to 100°F before labeling to prevent thermal stress on the product.

## Phase 4: Final Quality Assurance (QA) and Packaging

*   **Seal Integrity:** Perform a vacuum seal check (audible pop or visual depression of the safety button).
*   **Labeling:** Apply labels featuring the lot number, expiration date, and nutritional information.
*   **Random Sampling:** Extract three units per batch for sensory evaluation (color, texture, spreadability) and retained sample storage.
*   **Case Packing:** Pack finished goods into shipping cartons and store in a cool, dry, climate-controlled warehouse.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Always bloom your pectin separately in a small portion of sugar if using high-methoxyl pectin to avoid clumping in the main kettle.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a digital refractometer rather than a visual “spoon test” for consistent Brix readings across different shifts.
*   **Pitfall - The Scorching Trap:** Neglecting the bottom of the kettle in the final stages of cooking is the leading cause of "burnt" flavor profiles. Invest in automated paddle scrapers.
*   **Pitfall - pH Sensitivity:** If the pH is not low enough (ideally 3.0–3.4), the pectin will fail to set, resulting in a syrup rather than a jam. Always verify pH after adding citric acid.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How do we handle a batch that fails the Brix test?**
A: If the Brix is too low, the batch must be returned to the kettle for further evaporation. If too high (potentially causing crystallization), the batch must be analyzed for sugar-to-acid ratios before any corrective measures are attempted.

**Q: Why is headspace so critical in the jar?**
A: Headspace is vital for the creation of a vacuum. Too much headspace results in excessive oxygen, leading to oxidation/discoloration; too little headspace prevents a vacuum seal, compromising shelf safety.

**Q: How long should we keep retained samples?**
A: Retained samples should be stored in a cool, dark location for a minimum of 12 months beyond the product's expiration date to assist in any future quality investigations.
