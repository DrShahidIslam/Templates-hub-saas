# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the rigorous requirements for the preventive maintenance of industrial diesel generators. Consistent maintenance is critical to ensuring operational readiness, extending the lifespan of engine components, and preventing costly mechanical failures during power outages. All personnel performing these tasks must be qualified technicians following appropriate Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) and Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) protocols.

## 1. Safety and Preparation (Pre-Task)
*   **Safety Audit:** Ensure the generator is in "OFF" or "Maintenance" mode and the engine is cool to the touch.
*   **LOTO:** Apply lockout/tagout devices to the main circuit breaker and the engine start control.
*   **PPE:** Utilize safety glasses, chemical-resistant gloves, and hearing protection if testing is required.
*   **Containment:** Ensure spill containment kits are staged to capture accidental oil or coolant drainage.

## 2. Fluid Systems Inspection
*   **Engine Oil:** Check oil levels using the dipstick. Inspect for signs of water contamination (milky appearance) or excessive fuel dilution.
*   **Cooling System:** Inspect coolant levels and test for proper inhibitor concentration. Check radiator fins for debris or obstructions.
*   **Fuel System:** Drain water separators. Inspect fuel lines for cracks, leaks, or loose fittings. Ensure the fuel tank level is sufficient and the vent is clear.

## 3. Electrical and Battery Maintenance
*   **Battery Condition:** Inspect terminals for corrosion and ensure connections are tight. Verify battery voltage and electrolyte levels (if lead-acid).
*   **Charging System:** Verify the battery charger is operational and the alternator belt tension is within manufacturer specifications.
*   **Control Panel:** Check for error codes, battery voltage indicators, and ensure all gauge readings are within normal parameters.

## 4. Mechanical and Air Induction
*   **Air Intake:** Check air filter restriction indicators. Inspect air intake hoses for leaks or dry rot.
*   **Belts and Hoses:** Inspect all serpentine or fan belts for fraying or cracking. Check radiator hoses for bulges or signs of brittleness.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the exhaust manifold and piping for leaks. Ensure the exhaust rain cap is functioning correctly.

## 5. Operational Load Test
*   **Start-Up:** Clear the area and initiate a manual start. Listen for abnormal knocks, vibrations, or smoke.
*   **Load Application:** Apply a facility load (if possible) or use a resistive load bank to ensure the generator can handle rated capacity.
*   **Monitoring:** Record voltage, frequency, oil pressure, and temperature at 15-minute intervals during the load run.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a digital logbook or cloud-based maintenance software for every inspection to identify performance trends before a failure occurs.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always prioritize high-quality OEM filters and fluids; aftermarket parts often lack the filtration efficiency required for critical standby power.
*   **Pitfall:** Neglecting the "dry run." Starting a generator once a month is insufficient if you do not apply a load. Running at low loads (wet stacking) can cause carbon buildup in the cylinder walls and exhaust system.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring fuel quality. Diesel fuel degrades over time. If the generator is not used frequently, utilize a fuel stabilizer or plan for periodic fuel polishing.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform a full preventive maintenance inspection?**
A: For standby generators, a full inspection is recommended every 6 months or every 250–500 operating hours, whichever comes first.

**Q: What is "wet stacking" and how can I prevent it?**
A: Wet stacking occurs when a diesel engine runs at light loads for extended periods, causing unburnt fuel to accumulate in the exhaust system. It is prevented by running the engine under a significant load (at least 30–50% of capacity) during routine testing.

**Q: Why is it important to keep the fuel tank full?**
A: A full tank minimizes the volume of air inside the reservoir, reducing the potential for condensation to form on the tank walls, which leads to water contamination and microbial growth in the fuel.
