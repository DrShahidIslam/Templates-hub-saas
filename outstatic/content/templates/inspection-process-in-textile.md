# Standard Operating Procedure: Textile Quality Inspection Process

## Introduction
The textile inspection process is a critical quality control gate designed to ensure that fabric batches meet specified technical requirements, aesthetic standards, and safety compliance before entering the cutting or manufacturing phase. By adhering to a systematic 4-point or 10-point inspection system, the operations team can minimize waste, prevent production delays, and ensure brand integrity. This document outlines the mandatory procedures for incoming fabric verification, non-conformity reporting, and final acceptance criteria.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Verify the Purchase Order (PO) against the supplier packing list and physical delivery.
*   Check the environmental conditions of the inspection room (must be well-lit with standardized D65 lighting).
*   Ensure all calibrated measuring equipment (tape measures, GSM scales, and pick glasses) is ready.
*   Confirm availability of the "Approved Master Sample" for color, hand-feel, and texture comparison.

## Phase 2: Physical Fabric Examination
*   **Roll Identification:** Record the roll number, batch number, and total yardage/meters received.
*   **Fabric Width Measurement:** Measure the usable fabric width at three points along the roll (beginning, middle, end) to ensure consistency.
*   **GSM (Grams per Square Meter) Check:** Cut a sample from the roll using a GSM cutter and weigh it to ensure it matches the technical specification sheet (allowance: ±5%).
*   **Visual Inspection:** Unroll the fabric on the inspection table at a steady speed (not exceeding 15 meters per minute).
*   **Defect Identification:** Mark all defects using adhesive stickers. Typical defects include:
    *   Holes, slubs, or yarn breakages.
    *   Color shading variations (side-to-center or end-to-end).
    *   Soiling, oil stains, or contamination.
    *   Skewing or bowing of the grain line.

## Phase 3: Evaluation and Scoring
*   Apply the agreed-upon grading system (typically the American 4-Point System).
*   Calculate total penalty points per 100 square yards/meters.
*   Compare results against the Acceptable Quality Limit (AQL) defined in the client contract.
*   Determine status: **Pass, Fail, or Re-grade.**

## Phase 4: Documentation and Disposition
*   Generate a detailed Quality Inspection Report (QIR).
*   If the roll fails: Isolate the stock in the "Non-Conforming Material" (NCM) quarantine area.
*   Notify the Procurement/Sourcing department to initiate a return or claim process with the vendor.
*   Update the Inventory Management System (IMS) with the final status of each roll.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always inspect fabric at a 45-degree angle to the light source to detect subtle shading variations that are invisible when viewed head-on.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "Retained Sample" library (small swatches from every roll) for at least six months post-production for post-sale claim resolution.
*   **Pitfall:** Overlooking "side-to-side" shading. Fabric may look perfect individually but will result in mismatched panels on a finished garment if not checked for consistency.
*   **Pitfall:** Rushing the inspection speed. Faster inspection leads to missed defects, which result in higher rejections during the cutting process—costing significantly more than an extra hour of inspection time.

## FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the standard light source required for textile inspection?**
Standard industrial practice requires D65 (Daylight) lighting, which provides a color temperature of approximately 6500K to simulate natural daylight and ensure accurate color assessment.

**2. How should defects be handled if they are within the acceptable tolerance?**
If defects fall within the AQL tolerance, they must still be recorded. If a roll exceeds the point threshold, it must be flagged for "cutting around" (where the pattern is adjusted to avoid the defect) or rejected entirely.

**3. Why is GSM testing so important for fabric?**
GSM (Gram per Square Meter) is the primary indicator of fabric density and quality consistency. If the GSM is too low, the garment may appear sheer or lack durability; if too high, it may alter the intended drape or fit of the garment design.
