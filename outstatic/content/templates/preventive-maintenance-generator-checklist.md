# Standard Operating Procedure: Generator Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the periodic inspection and preventive maintenance of standby and prime power generator sets. Adherence to these guidelines ensures optimal equipment reliability, extends the operational lifecycle of the engine, and guarantees power continuity during critical outages. All maintenance activities must be performed by qualified personnel in compliance with manufacturer specifications and local safety regulations.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Inspection Protocols
*   **De-energize:** Ensure the generator is in "OFF" or "Manual" mode to prevent unexpected automatic start-up.
*   **Lockout/Tagout (LOTO):** Apply LOTO procedures to the starter battery and the main circuit breaker.
*   **PPE:** Wear appropriate personal protective equipment, including safety glasses, heat-resistant gloves, and steel-toed boots.
*   **Cool-Down:** Ensure the engine is cool to the touch before removing radiator caps or touching exhaust components.

## 2. Engine Systems Inspection
*   **Lubrication System:** Check oil levels; inspect for leaks around the oil filter, drain plug, and valve covers. Note the color and viscosity of the oil.
*   **Cooling System:** Inspect coolant levels and check hoses for cracks, bulges, or signs of dry rot. Verify that the radiator fins are free of debris and obstructions.
*   **Fuel System:** Check fuel lines for leaks or signs of seepage. Ensure fuel filters are free of sediment and that the fuel tank is filled to the required level.
*   **Air Intake:** Inspect air filter indicators; replace if the service indicator shows "red." Check intake hoses for secure connections.
*   **Exhaust System:** Inspect the muffler and piping for leaks or carbon build-up. Ensure the rain cap is functional.

## 3. Electrical and Control Systems
*   **Battery Maintenance:** Clean battery terminals of corrosion; ensure connections are tight. Verify battery voltage and electrolyte levels (if applicable).
*   **Control Panel:** Check for error codes or warning lights on the generator control unit. Verify that all gauges (voltage, frequency, oil pressure, temp) are calibrated and readable.
*   **Wiring:** Inspect external wiring harnesses for fraying, rodent damage, or loose connections.
*   **Transfer Switch:** Visually inspect the Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) cabinet for dust, moisture, or signs of arcing.

## 4. Operational Testing
*   **Load Bank Test:** If required by the maintenance schedule, perform a load bank test to ensure the generator can handle its rated capacity.
*   **Transfer Test:** Conduct a simulated utility power failure to ensure the ATS triggers the start signal and transfers the load correctly.
*   **Observation:** Monitor the engine for unusual vibrations, high-pitched noises, or exhaust smoke color (black/blue/white) during the first 15 minutes of operation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always maintain a detailed maintenance logbook. Tracking trends in oil consumption or start-up time is the most effective way to predict component failure before it happens.
*   **Pro Tip:** If the generator is housed outside, install a mesh screen over intake vents to prevent small animals from nesting in the engine compartment.
*   **Pitfall:** Never "top off" fuel tanks with old fuel. Diesel fuel degrades over time; ensure your fuel maintenance program includes fuel polishing or the addition of stabilizers.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening battery terminals is a common error; it can strip the lead posts and cause high-resistance connections. Use a torque wrench per manufacturer specs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I perform a full preventive maintenance inspection?**
A: Standard industry practice is monthly for visual inspections and quarterly for comprehensive service, though this should be adjusted based on the manufacturer’s manual and the generator's total run hours.

**Q: Why does the generator need to run if it is just a standby unit?**
A: Running the generator weekly under load (or at least exercising it) prevents the "wet stacking" effect (unburned fuel accumulation), lubricates internal components, and ensures the battery remains charged.

**Q: What do I do if I find a leak during my inspection?**
A: Immediately document the location and severity of the leak, tag the unit as "Maintenance Required," and escalate to a lead technician. Do not operate the generator if the leak involves pressurized fuel or coolant.
