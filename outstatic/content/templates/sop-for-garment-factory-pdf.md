# Standard Operating Procedure: Garment Production & Quality Control

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a comprehensive framework for managing garment factory operations, from raw material procurement to final packaging. By standardizing these workflows, the facility ensures consistent product quality, optimizes throughput, reduces material wastage, and maintains compliance with global safety and labor standards. All production personnel are expected to adhere to these guidelines to ensure operational excellence.

## Phase 1: Pre-Production & Sourcing
*   **Fabric Inspection:** Conduct a 4-point system inspection on all incoming fabric rolls to identify defects (holes, streaks, shading).
*   **Pattern Grading:** Ensure digital patterns are graded accurately for all sizes before marker creation.
*   **Marker Planning:** Optimize markers to ensure minimum fabric waste; verify efficiency percentages are within the 85-90% range.
*   **Shrinkage Testing:** Wash test fabric swatches to confirm shrinkage rates align with pattern allowances.

## Phase 2: Cutting Room Operations
*   **Spreading:** Maintain consistent tension during fabric laying; ensure ply count matches the cutting order precisely.
*   **Cutting:** Use automated cutting machines for precision; perform manual checks on complex curves and small components.
*   **Bundling:** Group cut pieces by size and color; attach identification tickets to every bundle to ensure batch traceability.
*   **Fusing:** Check adhesion strength of interlinings using a peel test; monitor temperature and pressure settings of the fusing press.

## Phase 3: Sewing & Assembly
*   **Line Balancing:** Arrange workstations to ensure an even flow of work, minimizing bottlenecking between operators.
*   **In-Line Quality Audit:** Assign roving quality inspectors to check every 10th garment at each critical operation (e.g., collar attachment, armhole joining).
*   **Needle Management:** Maintain a strict needle log; all broken needles must be accounted for and documented to ensure no metal fragments remain in garments.
*   **Work-in-Progress (WIP) Tracking:** Use RFID or manual log sheets to track bundle progression through the sewing line.

## Phase 4: Finishing & Packaging
*   **Thread Trimming:** Perform a 100% inspection for loose threads on both the inside and outside of the garment.
*   **Metal Detection:** Pass all finished goods through a calibrated needle detector machine to ensure safety.
*   **Pressing/Ironing:** Utilize steam-suction tables to prevent moisture buildup; ensure the temperature is appropriate for the fabric composition.
*   **Final Audit (AQL):** Perform a final inspection based on the Acceptable Quality Limit (AQL) 2.5 standard before packing.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement a "Traffic Light" system at each sewing station (Green = Working, Yellow = Small Issue, Red = Stopped/Blocked) to provide real-time visibility to supervisors.
*   **Pitfall - Poor Communication:** Failing to reconcile pattern discrepancies between the design team and the cutting room is the leading cause of garment sizing issues.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always keep a "Golden Sample" (the approved perfect garment) at the start of the sewing line for operators to reference throughout the shift.
*   **Pitfall - Maintenance:** Overlooking the regular lubrication of sewing machines leads to oil stains on light-colored fabrics, resulting in high rework rates.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should the needle detector machine be calibrated?**
A: The needle detector should be calibrated at the start of every shift and every time a technician changes, using a standard test card to ensure accuracy.

**Q: What is the most effective way to reduce fabric waste in the cutting room?**
A: Use high-end nesting software to optimize marker efficiency and ensure the "spreaders" are trained to minimize fabric end-loss through proper alignment.

**Q: What should be done if a critical quality defect is discovered during the final AQL audit?**
A: The entire lot must be quarantined immediately. A "100% sort" must be performed on the affected shipment, and a root cause analysis (RCA) must be conducted to prevent recurrence in the next production run.
