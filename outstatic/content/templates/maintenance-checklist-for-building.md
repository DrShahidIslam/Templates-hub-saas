# Standard Operating Procedure: Comprehensive Building Maintenance

## Overview
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a structured framework for the routine inspection and maintenance of commercial and residential building assets. The objective of this protocol is to ensure operational continuity, extend the lifecycle of building systems, mitigate liability risks, and maintain a safe environment for all occupants. This document should be executed by qualified facility personnel or contracted maintenance vendors on a recurring monthly and quarterly basis to ensure compliance with safety codes and high-quality facility standards.

## Monthly Maintenance Checklist

## Exterior & Building Envelope
*   **Roofing System:** Inspect for debris, ponding water, or signs of membrane degradation. Clear all roof drains and scuppers.
*   **Landscaping:** Confirm pathways are clear of trip hazards; prune overhanging branches that may damage siding or roofing.
*   **Signage & Lighting:** Verify all exterior building and parking lot lighting is functional; replace burnt-out bulbs or damaged fixtures immediately.
*   **Foundation & Siding:** Check for cracks, water staining, or evidence of pest ingress.

## Mechanical & HVAC Systems
*   **Filter Replacement:** Inspect and replace air intake filters in all HVAC units.
*   **Drain Lines:** Flush condensate pans and lines to prevent mold growth and water backup.
*   **System Testing:** Run a diagnostic check on heating and cooling cycles; listen for unusual vibrations or mechanical noise.
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Ensure all zones are reporting temperatures accurately and responding to setpoint changes.

## Plumbing & Life Safety
*   **Water Leaks:** Inspect all accessible pipes, under-sink valves, and water heaters for corrosion or minor drips.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Verify pressure gauge is in the "green" zone and that the inspection tag is current.
*   **Emergency Lighting:** Depress the test button on all emergency exit signs and battery backup lights to ensure they hold a charge.
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test the alarm functionality and vacuum dust from sensors.

## Interior & Common Areas
*   **Doors & Hardware:** Lubricate hinges and door closers; check that all electronic locks and panic bars operate smoothly.
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for loose tiles, frayed carpet edges, or slip hazards in entryways.
*   **Electrical Panels:** Check that panels are free from obstructions and clearly labeled.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Implement a CMMS.** Utilize a Computerized Maintenance Management System to digitize these checklists. Automated reminders ensure no task is skipped, and it provides an audit trail for insurance purposes.
*   **Pro Tip: The "Two-Senses" Rule.** When inspecting equipment, always use two senses. If a motor sounds okay but feels excessively hot to the touch, it requires immediate investigation.
*   **Pitfall: Deferring Small Repairs.** The most common failure in facility management is "deferred maintenance." A $50 worn-out seal, if ignored, can turn into a $5,000 water damage restoration project.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Vendor Documentation.** Always store original manuals and warranty documents in a central location. Attempting DIY fixes on high-end HVAC units without adhering to manufacturer specs can void your warranty.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I replace HVAC filters if the building is in a dusty environment?**
A: In high-dust environments or during heavy construction, you should transition from a monthly schedule to a bi-weekly inspection schedule. Clogged filters force HVAC motors to work harder, significantly increasing energy costs and risk of premature mechanical failure.

**Q: What is the most critical item on this list?**
A: Life safety equipment (Fire extinguishers, smoke detectors, and emergency lighting) is the highest priority. Failure to maintain these items poses a severe risk to occupants and can result in significant legal liability and regulatory fines.

**Q: Should I perform all these tasks myself?**
A: Basic inspections should be handled by your facility team; however, specialized tasks—such as electrical panel internal inspections, gas pressure testing, or elevator servicing—must be performed by licensed, insured professionals to ensure compliance with local building codes.
