# Standard Operating Procedure: Building Inspection and Facility Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements for conducting a comprehensive building inspection. The objective of this audit is to identify structural, mechanical, and safety deficiencies, ensure regulatory compliance, and maintain the operational integrity of the asset. This protocol should be utilized by facility managers, inspectors, or site supervisors to standardize reporting, minimize liability, and establish a preventative maintenance schedule based on empirical data.

## 1. Exterior and Structural Integrity
*   **Foundation:** Inspect for visible cracks, shifting, or signs of water intrusion at the perimeter.
*   **Roofing System:** Check for missing shingles, damaged flashing, pooling water, or clogged drainage systems.
*   **Walls and Siding:** Examine masonry for mortar gaps and evaluate siding for rot, warping, or infestation.
*   **Windows and Doors:** Ensure all exterior frames are sealed/caulked and weather-stripping is intact.
*   **Landscaping and Grading:** Confirm that ground slopes away from the building to prevent drainage issues.

## 2. Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing (MEP)
*   **HVAC Systems:** Verify filter status, check for unusual vibration or noise, and confirm thermostat functionality.
*   **Electrical Panels:** Ensure panels are labeled clearly and are free of "double-tapped" breakers or signs of overheating/scorching.
*   **Lighting:** Test all interior and exterior light fixtures, including emergency exit lighting and path-of-egress illumination.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect visible pipes for leaks or corrosion, and test water pressure at designated points.
*   **Fire Suppression:** Confirm fire extinguishers are charged/tagged and sprinkler heads are unobstructed.

## 3. Interior Spaces and Safety Compliance
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for trip hazards, loose carpeting, or uneven transitions.
*   **Ceilings and Walls:** Scan for water stains, mold growth, or peeling paint (particularly in high-humidity areas).
*   **Life Safety:** Confirm clear access to fire exits and verify that egress paths are free of storage or obstructions.
*   **ADA Accessibility:** Ensure ramps, handrails, and wide-clearance doorways meet current ADA accessibility standards.
*   **Signage:** Verify that all safety, directional, and emergency signage is legible and mounted correctly.

## 4. Documentation and Reporting
*   **Photo Evidence:** Capture high-resolution images of all deficiencies identified during the walk-through.
*   **Priority Categorization:** Assign a severity level to each issue: Critical (Immediate action), High (Within 30 days), or Low (Include in annual budget).
*   **Maintenance Log Update:** Input findings into the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) or project tracking software.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Utilize a digital tablet to record notes and photos simultaneously; this prevents data loss and simplifies the post-inspection report generation.
*   **Pro Tip:** Conduct inspections during varied weather conditions. A roof leak may not be visible during a dry-weather inspection.
*   **Pitfall (Neglecting Hidden Spaces):** Often, inspectors focus on main areas and ignore mechanical closets or crawlspaces. Always carry a high-lumen flashlight to inspect these "hidden" zones.
*   **Pitfall (Rushing):** A superficial walkthrough is a liability. If a specific system (like HVAC) requires expert verification, note it and schedule a specialized contractor immediately.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a full building inspection be conducted?**
A: A comprehensive audit should be performed at least semi-annually. However, high-traffic commercial buildings may require quarterly inspections to address wear and tear.

**Q: What is the most common safety violation found during inspections?**
A: Improperly blocked egress paths and obstructed fire extinguishers are the most frequently cited safety violations by fire marshals and insurance auditors.

**Q: Should I perform the inspection alone or with a team?**
A: While a solo inspector is efficient for routine checks, it is recommended to conduct annual audits with a maintenance technician or a specialized contractor to provide expert insight into MEP systems.
