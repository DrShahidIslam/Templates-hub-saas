# Standard Operating Procedure: South Korea Student Visa (D-2) Application

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for successfully securing a D-2 Student Visa for South Korea. As an operations manager, I emphasize that the South Korean immigration process is strictly document-based; minor discrepancies in financial proof or academic verification are common causes for rejection. Adherence to this structured checklist is critical to ensuring compliance with the Ministry of Justice (MOJ) requirements and avoiding processing delays.

## Phase 1: Pre-Application & Documentation
*   **Receive Certificate of Admission (CoA):** Ensure the university has issued the official CoA. Do not initiate the visa process until the original or high-quality scanned copy is in your possession.
*   **Validate Passport:** Ensure your passport has at least 6 months of validity remaining and at least two blank visa pages.
*   **Academic Verification:** Collect notarized or apostilled copies of your highest degree (high school diploma or bachelor’s degree, depending on the program level).
*   **Financial Solvency Evidence:** Prepare official bank statements covering the last 6 months. The balance must meet the university’s minimum threshold (often $18,000–$20,000 USD, subject to specific embassy requirements).
*   **Study Plan/Statement of Purpose:** Draft a concise, formal essay explaining your objective in studying in Korea, your academic background, and your long-term post-graduation plans.

## Phase 2: Application Submission
*   **Complete Visa Application Form:** Fill out the "Application for Visa Issuance" form accurately. Ensure all dates, passport numbers, and personal details match your official documents perfectly.
*   **Photograph Requirements:** Provide one color passport-sized photo (3.5cm x 4.5cm) taken against a white background within the last 6 months.
*   **Fee Payment:** Determine the visa application fee at your local Korean Consulate or Embassy. Fees are usually non-refundable and payable in local currency.
*   **Submission Strategy:** Book an appointment if required by your local jurisdiction. If submitting through a Visa Application Center (VAC), confirm if they offer courier return services.

## Phase 3: Post-Submission & Arrival
*   **Track Status:** Use the Korea Visa Portal (visa.go.kr) to monitor the status of your application using your passport number and tracking code.
*   **Visa Collection:** Once approved, verify the visa sticker details (validity dates and entry type).
*   **Alien Registration Card (ARC):** Upon arrival in Korea, prioritize the appointment at the local Immigration Office to apply for your ARC within 90 days of entry.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Financial Consistency:** Avoid large, unexplained deposits into your bank account right before the application. Immigration officers look for stable financial history. If a parent is sponsoring you, include their employment letter and a signed affidavit of support.
*   **The "Apostille" Requirement:** Depending on your country of origin, your academic documents must be apostilled or notarized by the Korean Embassy in your home country. Failure to do this will result in immediate rejection.
*   **TB Testing:** Many consulates now require a Tuberculosis (TB) test result from a designated hospital. Check your local consulate's website specifically, as this is a frequent cause of incomplete applications.
*   **Document Integrity:** Do not use correction tape or cross out errors on the application form. If a mistake is made, print a fresh form.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How long does the visa processing take?**
A: Typically, it takes 14 to 21 working days. However, during peak enrollment months (January/February and July/August), processing times can extend significantly due to high volume.

**Q: Can I change my study plan after getting the visa?**
A: Your visa is tied to the institution stated on your Certificate of Admission. If you switch universities, you will generally be required to update your status with the immigration office or potentially re-apply.

**Q: Do I need to be fluent in Korean to get a student visa?**
A: Generally, no. If your program is taught in English, you must provide proof of English proficiency (IELTS/TOEFL). If the program is in Korean, you must provide a TOPIK score, though some language-learning (D-4) programs allow entry without prior language knowledge.
