# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC System Maintenance and Operation

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the operation, routine inspection, and preventive maintenance of the facility’s Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems. Adherence to these procedures ensures optimal climate control, extends equipment lifecycle, minimizes energy consumption, and maintains compliance with indoor air quality (IAQ) safety standards. All maintenance activities must be logged in the facility maintenance management system (CMMS) upon completion.

## Section 1: Pre-Operational Checks and Monitoring
*   Verify the Building Management System (BMS) shows no active error codes or system alarms.
*   Check temperature and humidity setpoints against current facility occupancy requirements.
*   Inspect outdoor air intake louvers for obstructions (e.g., debris, bird nests, vegetation).
*   Confirm that all physical unit access panels are securely latched and gasketed to prevent air bypass.
*   Check the mechanical room for unusual vibration, rattling sounds, or persistent odors (e.g., burning, mildew).

## Section 2: Routine Preventive Maintenance
*   **Air Filtration:** Inspect air filters monthly. Replace filters if the differential pressure exceeds the manufacturer’s recommended threshold or if visual inspection shows heavy dust loading.
*   **Coil Cleaning:** Inspect evaporator and condenser coils for dirt accumulation. Use approved coil cleaner or compressed air to ensure optimal heat transfer.
*   **Condensate Management:** Inspect condensate pans for debris, biofilm, or standing water. Verify that drain lines are clear and that the P-trap is primed.
*   **Drive Systems:** Inspect blower belts for proper tension and signs of fraying or glazing. Adjust tension or replace belts according to the preventative maintenance schedule.
*   **Lubrication:** Verify that motor bearings and fan shafts are lubricated as specified by the equipment manufacturer’s technical manual.
*   **Electrical Inspection:** Tighten electrical connections in the control panel to prevent arcing and ensure stable power delivery.

## Section 3: Seasonal Startup and Shutdown
*   **Spring Transition:** Switch from heating mode to cooling mode. Test cooling stage cycling, check refrigerant pressures, and verify condensate pump operation.
*   **Fall Transition:** Switch from cooling mode to heating mode. Inspect heat exchangers for cracks or signs of oxidation. Test ignition systems and safety flame sensors.
*   **Seasonal Calibration:** Recalibrate thermostats and sensors to account for shifts in ambient temperature and humidity levels.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the date, time, and specific actions taken during each inspection. Historical data is critical for predictive maintenance and identifying failing components before they cause downtime.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement a "Clean-as-you-go" policy. Keeping the mechanical room floor and the exterior of the HVAC unit free of dust and debris prevents cross-contamination of air intakes.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-lubricating bearings is a common mistake. Excess grease can blow out seals and cause motor failure. Always use a calibrated grease gun and follow manufacturer volume specifications.
*   **Pitfall:** Neglecting condensate traps. A clogged trap during cooling season is the number one cause of water damage in commercial HVAC systems.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should HVAC filters be changed in a high-occupancy facility?**
Filters should generally be inspected monthly. In high-occupancy or dusty environments, quarterly replacement is the baseline, but pressure drop readings should dictate the exact frequency.

**2. What should I do if the system makes a loud banging noise?**
Immediately isolate power to the unit and inspect the blower assembly or fan blades for obstructions or loose components. If the issue is not external, contact a certified HVAC technician to avoid motor burnout.

**3. Why is there water pooling around the HVAC unit base?**
This is typically caused by a restricted condensate drain line or an improperly vented P-trap. Clear the line using a vacuum or compressed air and verify that the unit is slightly pitched toward the drain port to ensure gravity flow.
