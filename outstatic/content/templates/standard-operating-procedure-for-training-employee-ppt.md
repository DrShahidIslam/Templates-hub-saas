# Standard Operating Procedure: Employee Training Presentation Development

This document outlines the professional standards and procedural requirements for developing instructional PowerPoint (PPT) presentations for employee training. Adhering to these guidelines ensures consistent knowledge transfer, brand alignment, and high learner engagement across all departments. The objective is to convert complex organizational information into structured, visually accessible, and actionable training modules that support both synchronous and asynchronous learning.

## Phase 1: Planning and Content Structuring
* Define learning objectives using the SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
* Conduct a target audience analysis to determine prior knowledge, role-specific needs, and preferred learning styles.
* Create a storyboard or outline document before opening PowerPoint software to ensure logical flow.
* Identify key takeaways that must be achieved by the end of the session.
* Establish a timeline for drafting, stakeholder review, and final production.

## Phase 2: Design and Visual Strategy
* Apply the corporate branding guidelines (colors, fonts, logo placement, and imagery).
* Adhere to the "6x6 Rule": No more than six bullet points per slide and no more than six words per bullet point to prevent cognitive overload.
* Use high-quality, professional imagery or iconography; avoid low-resolution clip-art.
* Ensure high contrast between text and background for accessibility (WCAG compliance).
* Implement consistent slide layouts using the "Slide Master" function to maintain structural integrity.

## Phase 3: Content Integration and Interactivity
* Incorporate interactive elements such as polling, knowledge checks, or scenario-based questions every 5–7 slides.
* Write clear, concise presenter notes that provide context without simply reading the text on the slide.
* Embed media (videos or audio) using native PowerPoint integration rather than external links when possible to avoid connectivity issues.
* Include a clear "Call to Action" or next steps slide at the conclusion of the presentation.
* Review all text for clarity, brevity, and tone of voice (must be professional, encouraging, and objective).

## Phase 4: Quality Assurance and Finalization
* Perform a "Click-Through" test to ensure all animations, transitions, and links function correctly.
* Share the draft with a Subject Matter Expert (SME) for technical accuracy validation.
* Conduct a pilot run with a small test group to gather feedback on pacing and clarity.
* Export the final file as a read-only PDF for distribution and save the source file in the centralized repository.
* Run the Accessibility Checker within PowerPoint to verify screen-reader compatibility.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Use the "Morph" transition for smooth, professional movement between complex diagrams.
* **Pro Tip:** Always build a "hidden" appendix section for advanced technical questions that fall outside the core training scope.
* **Pitfall:** Avoid "Death by PowerPoint"—do not overload slides with dense paragraphs; keep the focus on the presenter’s delivery.
* **Pitfall:** Never use generic stock photos that appear staged; use authentic company photos to build trust and relatability.
* **Pitfall:** Do not overlook font size; ensure body text is at least 24pt to be legible in a classroom or remote environment.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How long should an ideal training presentation be?**
Training sessions should ideally be 45–60 minutes. If the material requires more time, break the presentation into modular "micro-learning" segments rather than one long, exhaustive slide deck.

**2. Should I include audio narration in the slides?**
For self-paced (asynchronous) training, audio narration is highly recommended to improve accessibility. For live (synchronous) training, it is generally discouraged, as the live facilitator should provide the narration.

**3. What is the best way to handle technical failures during a presentation?**
Always have a secondary, static PDF version of your presentation saved to a local drive or cloud-based folder that can be opened instantly if the PPT file becomes corrupted or the software fails to render correctly.
