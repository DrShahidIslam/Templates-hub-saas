# Standard Operating Procedure: Clinical Assessment & Documentation for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional workflow for conducting a comprehensive evaluation and documentation process for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). The objective of this protocol is to ensure that clinicians follow a standardized, evidence-based approach that maintains high diagnostic accuracy while ensuring patient comfort. This procedure emphasizes a multi-disciplinary approach, incorporating direct observation, informant reports, and standardized diagnostic tools to provide a holistic profile of the individual’s neurodevelopmental needs.

## Phase 1: Intake and Pre-Screening
*   **Initial Triage:** Review referral notes for primary concerns (social-communication delays, repetitive behaviors, or sensory processing issues).
*   **Information Gathering:** Collect formal history from parents/guardians, including developmental milestones (speech, motor, and social).
*   **Record Review:** Audit previous academic reports, pediatrician notes, or prior speech/occupational therapy evaluations.
*   **Consent:** Obtain signed informed consent for all diagnostic assessments and potential video recording (if applicable).

## Phase 2: Core Diagnostic Assessment
*   **Direct Observation (ADOS-2):** Administer the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule-2 (ADOS-2) in a quiet, low-stimulus environment.
*   **Informant Interview (ADI-R/SRS-2):** Conduct a structured caregiver interview to capture behaviors that may not manifest in a clinical setting.
*   **Cognitive Evaluation:** Administer a Wechsler scale (WPPSI/WISC) to assess intellectual functioning and identify co-occurring learning profiles.
*   **Adaptive Behavior Assessment:** Utilize the Vineland-3 to measure daily living skills and personal autonomy.

## Phase 3: Differential Diagnosis & Synthesis
*   **Rule-Outs:** Evaluate potential overlaps with ADHD, Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder, Generalized Anxiety, or Sensory Processing Disorder.
*   **Functional Impact Analysis:** Document how behaviors impact daily functioning in home, school, and community environments.
*   **Clinical Summary:** Synthesize findings into a final report, ensuring the inclusion of specific DSM-5-TR diagnostic criteria met.
*   **Severity Leveling:** Assign severity levels (1, 2, or 3) based on the level of support required for social communication and restricted, repetitive behaviors.

## Phase 4: Feedback and Post-Assessment Planning
*   **Parent/Patient Briefing:** Review assessment findings with the family in a supportive, jargon-free session.
*   **Recommendation Mapping:** Provide a customized roadmap for interventions, including Speech Therapy, Occupational Therapy, and ABA or CBT support.
*   **Resource Distribution:** Provide a curated folder of local support networks, educational advocacy resources, and community programs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Sensory Calibration):** Always ask about sensory sensitivities *before* the appointment. Adjust lighting, noise levels, and seating arrangements to minimize the chance of a meltdown.
*   **Pro Tip (The "Masking" Variable):** Be aware that high-functioning individuals, particularly females and adults, may "mask" or camouflage symptoms during the assessment. Rely more heavily on collateral informant reports for these cases.
*   **Pitfall (Cultural Bias):** Avoid over-pathologizing differences in social communication that may be attributed to cultural or linguistic backgrounds. 
*   **Pitfall (Fragmented Data):** Do not rely solely on standardized test scores. The clinical "gestalt"—how the person interacts with you in the room—is often more diagnostic than the numerical output of a questionnaire.

## FAQ

**1. Does a diagnosis of ASD guarantee funding for therapy?**
Not necessarily. While a clinical diagnosis is a prerequisite for most insurance coverage, authorization is typically based on "medical necessity" and functional deficits rather than the label alone.

**2. At what age can a reliable ASD diagnosis be made?**
While screening can identify flags as early as 18 months, professional diagnostic stability increases significantly after age 3, as the core social-communication markers become more consistent.

**3. What should I do if the assessment results are inconclusive?**
If the data is ambiguous, do not force a diagnosis. Schedule a follow-up observation session, consult with a multidisciplinary panel, or refer to a specialist in neurodevelopmental conditions to ensure the patient is not misdiagnosed.
