# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Operations

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the safe and efficient operation of industrial diesel generators. Adherence to these procedures is critical to ensure personnel safety, maintain equipment longevity, and guarantee power reliability during grid outages or scheduled maintenance. All operators must be trained and authorized before interacting with generator controls or fuel systems.

## 1. Pre-Start Inspection Checklist
Before initiating the startup sequence, conduct a physical walk-around to ensure the mechanical integrity of the unit.

*   **Fuel Levels:** Verify fuel tank levels are above the minimum threshold (typically >25%). Check for leaks under the tank and fuel lines.
*   **Engine Oil:** Check the dipstick. Oil level must be within the cross-hatched safe zone. Inspect for signs of milky discoloration (coolant contamination).
*   **Coolant System:** Verify the radiator coolant level is sufficient. Ensure the radiator fins are clear of debris.
*   **Battery & Connections:** Check battery terminals for corrosion and ensure cables are tight. Verify battery voltage on the control panel.
*   **Air Intake:** Check air filters for blockages and ensure the air intake dampers are fully open.
*   **Exhaust System:** Confirm the exhaust vent is unobstructed and the rain cap is functional.
*   **Surroundings:** Ensure the area surrounding the generator is free of flammable materials and tools.

## 2. Startup Procedures
Follow these steps to transition the generator from standby to operational mode.

*   **Mode Selection:** Ensure the control panel is set to "Manual" if starting locally. 
*   **Pre-Heat (If Applicable):** Activate the glow plugs or jacket water heater bypass if operating in cold ambient temperatures.
*   **Initiate Start:** Press the "Start" button. Listen for unusual grinding or mechanical noises during the crank cycle.
*   **Warm-up Period:** Allow the engine to idle for 3–5 minutes to reach optimal operating temperature before applying a load.
*   **Voltage and Frequency Check:** Confirm the output voltage (e.g., 480V/277V) and frequency (60Hz or 50Hz) match equipment requirements on the digital display.

## 3. Loading and Monitoring
Once the generator is at steady state, transition to load operations.

*   **Transfer Switch:** If using an Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS), ensure the "Auto" mode is engaged. If manual, close the main circuit breaker only after verifying steady voltage.
*   **Load Application:** Apply electrical loads incrementally to prevent engine surging. 
*   **Constant Monitoring:** Observe gauges for oil pressure, coolant temperature, and exhaust gas temperature (EGT).
*   **Load Balance:** Ensure that the load is balanced across all three phases to prevent overheating of individual windings.

## 4. Shutdown Procedures
Proper shutdown prevents engine damage caused by rapid cooling or residual heat soak.

*   **Unload:** Gradually reduce the electrical load and disconnect the main circuit breaker.
*   **Cool-Down Cycle:** Allow the generator to run at no-load for 5–10 minutes. This allows the turbocharger and engine block to cool down to prevent thermal fatigue.
*   **Stop Command:** Press the "Stop/Reset" button.
*   **Post-Shutdown:** Set the controller to "Auto" mode for standby readiness unless maintenance is required.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip (The Load Bank):** If your generator rarely sees a load above 30% of its capacity, schedule a load bank test every 6 months to prevent "wet stacking," where unburnt fuel accumulates in the exhaust system.
*   **Pitfall (Cold Starts):** Never apply full load immediately upon starting. Cold engines have poor oil viscosity, and immediate load application leads to premature cylinder liner wear.
*   **Pitfall (Fuel Quality):** Diesel degrades over time. Ensure you have a fuel polishing schedule and use biocides if fuel remains in the tank for more than 6 months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I perform a load test?**
A: Industry standards (such as NFPA 110) generally recommend a weekly run for 30 minutes under at least 30% load to ensure the unit is operational and to circulate fluids.

**Q: What should I do if the "Low Oil Pressure" alarm triggers?**
A: Immediately initiate an emergency stop or manual shutdown. Do not attempt to restart the unit until you have checked for leaks and verified the oil level. Running with low pressure can destroy an engine in seconds.

**Q: Can I use automotive-grade diesel?**
A: Only if it meets the ASTM D975 standard for your specific engine manufacturer's requirements. High-sulfur or improperly filtered fuel can damage fuel injectors and emission control systems.
