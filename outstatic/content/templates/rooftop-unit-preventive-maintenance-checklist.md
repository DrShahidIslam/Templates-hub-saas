# Standard Operating Procedure: Rooftop Unit (RTU) Preventive Maintenance

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory maintenance protocols for Rooftop Units (RTUs) to ensure peak operational efficiency, extend equipment lifespan, and maintain indoor air quality. Regular preventive maintenance is critical to preventing catastrophic mechanical failure, reducing energy consumption, and ensuring regulatory compliance. All technicians must adhere to safety protocols, including Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures, before accessing internal components.

## Preventive Maintenance Checklist

## 1. Safety and Pre-Inspection
* Perform a visual inspection of the roof area for debris or water pooling.
* Verify the unit is powered down and apply Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures.
* Inspect unit cabinet for structural integrity, missing screws, or loose panels.
* Check for signs of water infiltration or rodent damage.

## 2. Airside Components
* Inspect and replace air filters; note the MERV rating and installation date.
* Inspect blower motor assembly for signs of wear or abnormal vibrations.
* Clean and inspect blower wheels/fans for dust buildup; balance if necessary.
* Inspect drive belts for tension, cracking, or fraying; replace as a set if worn.
* Lubricate blower motor bearings if they are not permanently sealed.
* Clean the evaporator coil using approved foaming cleaner and rinse thoroughly.

## 3. Refrigeration System
* Inspect condensate pans and drain lines; clear all blockages and treat with algaecide.
* Measure refrigerant pressures (suction and liquid lines); compare against manufacturer subcooling/superheat charts.
* Check for oil streaks at brazed joints or flare fittings, indicating a refrigerant leak.
* Inspect compressor contactor for pitted points or signs of arcing.
* Verify proper operation of the crankcase heater.

## 4. Condenser Section
* Inspect condenser coils for debris, cottonwood, or bent fins; straighten fins with a fin comb.
* Clean condenser coils using a non-acidic coil cleaner.
* Verify condenser fan motors are operational and free of obstructions.
* Ensure fan blades are tight on the motor shaft.

## 5. Heating/Electrical Section
* Inspect gas burners for clean, blue flame and proper ignition.
* Inspect the heat exchanger for cracks, rust, or oxidation (critical safety check).
* Test safety switches, including high-limit controls and rollout switches.
* Tighten all electrical terminal connections; inspect wires for insulation degradation.
* Verify the operation of the economizer damper and sensors.

## 6. Final Commissioning
* Remove LOTO and restore power.
* Start unit and verify all stages (cooling/heating/fan) cycle correctly.
* Measure voltage and amperage on motors and compressors; compare with nameplate ratings.
* Record all data in the site maintenance logbook.
* Re-secure all service panels.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a calibrated thermometer/manometer. Digital gauges provide faster, more accurate readings for subcooling and superheat calculations.
* **Pro Tip:** When cleaning coils, always spray from the inside out to push debris off rather than further into the fins.
* **Pitfall:** Over-tightening belts. This places excessive stress on motor bearings, leading to premature motor failure.
* **Pitfall:** Skipping the condensate drain flush. Stagnant water in pans leads to organic growth, odors, and potential leaks into the building interior.
* **Pitfall:** Ignoring "nuisance" trips. If a unit trips a breaker or high-pressure switch, find the root cause; do not simply reset and walk away.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should RTU preventive maintenance be performed?**
A: Industry standards dictate a minimum of quarterly maintenance. However, high-occupancy buildings or units located in harsh environments (coastal areas, heavy dust) should be serviced monthly or bi-monthly.

**Q: What is the most common cause of RTU failure?**
A: Dirty coils. Blocked airflow restricts heat transfer, causing the compressor to work harder, run hotter, and eventually burn out due to excessive head pressure.

**Q: Can I use high-pressure water to clean coils?**
A: No. High-pressure water or improper nozzle usage can easily flatten or damage delicate aluminum fins, which permanently restricts airflow and decreases unit efficiency. Always use low-pressure spray and manufacturer-approved cleaning agents.
