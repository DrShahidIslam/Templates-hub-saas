# Standard Operating Procedure: Manual Handling

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory safety protocols for the manual handling of objects in the workplace. Manual handling—defined as the transporting or supporting of a load by hand or bodily force—remains a leading cause of workplace injury. The primary objective of this procedure is to reduce the risk of musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs) and acute injuries by ensuring that all personnel assess, plan, and execute lifting operations in accordance with ergonomic best practices. Compliance with these procedures is mandatory for all employees.

## 1. Pre-Lift Assessment and Planning
Before initiating any manual handling task, personnel must perform a comprehensive T.I.L.E. assessment (Task, Individual, Load, Environment).

*   **Task Assessment:** Determine if mechanical assistance (e.g., trolleys, pallet jacks, or hoists) can be used instead of manual lifting.
*   **Load Inspection:** Check the weight, stability, and contents of the object. Is it sharp, hot, or leaking?
*   **Clearance:** Ensure the intended path is clear of debris, spills, or obstacles.
*   **Destination Check:** Verify that the landing area is stable, clear, and capable of supporting the weight of the load.
*   **Team Lifting:** If the load exceeds 20kg (or is awkward/unbalanced), designate a two-person lift or utilize mechanical aids.

## 2. Proper Lifting Technique
Adherence to biomechanical safety standards is required to protect the spine and joints during the lifting motion.

*   **Stable Base:** Stand as close to the load as possible with feet shoulder-width apart, one foot slightly forward to maintain balance.
*   **Posture:** Keep the back straight (maintaining the natural S-curve of the spine), chest out, and tuck the chin in.
*   **Grip:** Ensure a firm grip using the palms of the hands rather than just the fingertips. 
*   **Power Source:** Use the strength of the legs to drive the lift. Push off with the back leg, keeping the load close to the center of gravity (the waist).
*   **Avoiding Rotation:** Never twist the torso while carrying a load. If a change in direction is required, move the feet to pivot the entire body.
*   **Vision:** Ensure the load does not obstruct your line of sight.

## 3. Placement and Unloading
The placement of the load is as critical as the initial lift to prevent strain on the lower back.

*   **Controlled Descent:** Lower the load by bending at the knees, not the waist.
*   **Positioning:** Place the load on the edge of the destination surface and slide it into the final position if necessary.
*   **Finger Protection:** Be mindful of finger placement to ensure they are not trapped between the load and the landing surface.
*   **Final Alignment:** Once released, ensure the load is stable and does not pose a tipping or falling hazard.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

**Pro Tips:**
*   **The "Hug" Rule:** Keep the load as close to your torso as possible. Doubling the distance from your body effectively multiplies the strain on your lower back by a factor of ten.
*   **Micro-Breaks:** If performing repetitive handling tasks, take regular stretching breaks to release muscle tension.
*   **Footwear:** Always wear approved safety footwear with non-slip soles and adequate ankle support.

**Pitfalls to Avoid:**
*   **"Jerk" Lifting:** Avoid sudden or jerky movements; all lifting should be fluid and controlled.
*   **Ignoring Fatigue:** Do not attempt heavy lifting if you are fatigued or rushing; fatigue decreases focus and physical stability.
*   **Ignoring PPE:** Failing to wear gloves when handling objects with rough or splintered surfaces can lead to infections or serious lacerations.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should I do if a load feels too heavy once I have started lifting it?**
If you feel the load is beyond your physical capability or is causing strain, stop immediately. Lower the load in a controlled manner, find a partner for assistance, or secure mechanical lifting equipment. Never compromise your health to finish a task.

**2. Are there specific weight limits for manual handling?**
While there is no single universal weight limit, regulatory guidelines generally advise caution for any load exceeding 20-25kg. Any load over this threshold must be subject to a secondary risk assessment to determine if mechanical assistance is required.

**3. What is the most common mistake made during manual handling?**
The most common mistake is twisting the torso while holding a load. The human spine is designed for vertical load-bearing, not rotational torque. Always pivot your feet to turn your body rather than twisting your waist.
