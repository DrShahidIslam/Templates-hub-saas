# Standard Operating Procedure: Laboratory Process Flow Diagramming

## Introduction
A process flow diagram (PFD) is a vital operational tool used to visualize, analyze, and optimize laboratory workflows. By mapping the movement of samples, personnel, and data through the facility, a PFD identifies bottlenecks, ensures compliance with regulatory standards (such as ISO 15189 or GLP), and mitigates cross-contamination risks. This SOP provides a standardized framework for documenting laboratory processes to ensure consistency, safety, and operational efficiency across all functional zones.

## Step-by-Step Execution Checklist

## Phase 1: Preparation and Scoping
* Define the specific process boundaries (e.g., "From Sample Receipt to Result Authorization").
* Identify the target audience (e.g., auditors, lab technicians, or facility engineers).
* Gather necessary documentation: current SOPs, equipment manuals, and safety protocols.
* Assemble a subject matter expert (SME) team, including bench scientists and quality assurance personnel.

## Phase 2: Information Gathering and Mapping
* Conduct a "Gemba Walk" to observe the real-time workflow without interruptions.
* Interview operators to capture "hidden" steps or non-standard workarounds.
* List all major inputs (reagents, samples, metadata) and outputs (results, waste, reports).
* Draft the initial map using standard BPMN (Business Process Model and Notation) symbols.
* Map decision points clearly (e.g., "Is the sample quality sufficient? Yes/No").

## Phase 3: Validation and Standardization
* Verify the map against actual lab performance to ensure no steps were omitted.
* Ensure all hazardous zones and directional flows (clean to dirty) are clearly marked.
* Assign ownership to specific roles for every step in the process.
* Format the diagram using professional software (e.g., Visio, Lucidchart) for accessibility.

## Phase 4: Review and Implementation
* Circulate the draft for peer review by the lab supervisor and Safety Officer.
* Update existing SOPs to reference the newly created PFD.
* Train staff on the diagram and store a hard copy at the relevant workstation.
* Establish a schedule for annual review or whenever equipment/processes change.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
* **Color Coding:** Use specific colors to differentiate between personnel paths, sample paths, and waste paths to prevent cross-contamination.
* **Granularity:** Do not over-complicate the chart. If a single step has 10 sub-steps, create a secondary, detailed "level 2" diagram.
* **Digital Integration:** Embed QR codes on physical lab equipment that link directly to the relevant section of the digital PFD.

### Pitfalls
* **The "As-Designed" vs. "As-Performed" Trap:** Never draw the process as it is *supposed* to happen according to the handbook; always draw it as it *actually* happens, then address discrepancies in a separate project.
* **Ignoring Safety:** Omitting emergency exits or biohazard containment zones renders the PFD useless for safety audits.
* **Static Documents:** Storing the PFD as a static PDF on a drive that no one checks. Keep it live and accessible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should the Process Flow Diagram be updated?**
A: The PFD should be reviewed at least annually, or immediately following any significant changes in instrumentation, facility layout, or regulatory requirements.

**Q: Should I include administrative tasks, or just technical bench work?**
A: Both. Including administrative "gates" (e.g., data entry, verification, sign-off) is critical to identifying why a sample might be delayed in the pre-analytical or post-analytical phases.

**Q: What software is recommended for creating laboratory PFDs?**
A: Industry standards include Lucidchart, Microsoft Visio, or draw.io. These tools allow for version control and collaborative editing, which are essential for maintaining accurate documentation.
