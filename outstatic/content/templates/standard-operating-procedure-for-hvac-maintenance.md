# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC Preventative Maintenance

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting routine preventative maintenance on Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems. The objective is to maximize equipment longevity, ensure indoor air quality, maintain energy efficiency, and minimize unscheduled downtime. This procedure applies to all technicians responsible for the upkeep of commercial and residential HVAC units, including split systems, packaged units, and air handling units (AHUs).

## Phase 1: Preparation and Safety
* [ ] Verify the system status and record the initial setpoint temperature and current operating mode.
* [ ] De-energize the unit at the disconnect switch and perform Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures where required.
* [ ] Don appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, cut-resistant gloves, and respiratory protection if working in dusty environments.
* [ ] Confirm that all necessary tools—gauges, multimeter, vacuum cleaner, coil cleaner, and refrigerant scales—are sanitized and calibrated.

## Phase 2: Outdoor Unit Inspection (Condenser)
* [ ] Remove debris, leaves, and obstructions from the immediate perimeter of the condenser.
* [ ] Inspect the condenser coil for dirt buildup or damage; clean using approved coil cleaner and low-pressure water.
* [ ] Check the fan blade for tightness, balance, and signs of fatigue.
* [ ] Inspect the contactor for signs of pitting or arcing; replace if contact points show heavy oxidation.
* [ ] Verify the integrity of electrical connections and tighten terminal screws.
* [ ] Check the run capacitor for signs of swelling or leakage and test with a capacitance meter to ensure it falls within the manufacturer's specified tolerance.

## Phase 3: Indoor Unit Inspection (Evaporator & Airflow)
* [ ] Inspect the air filter; replace if dirty or according to the facility’s change-out schedule.
* [ ] Examine the evaporator coil for biofilm or debris; clean if necessary using a non-acidic coil cleaner.
* [ ] Inspect the condensate drain pan for standing water, sludge, or blockages.
* [ ] Flush the condensate drain line to ensure clear flow and prevent water backup.
* [ ] Inspect the blower motor assembly; clean fan blades and check motor bearings for excessive noise or vibration.
* [ ] Check the integrity of supply and return ductwork for air leaks or insulation degradation.

## Phase 4: Operational Testing and Performance Verification
* [ ] Restore power to the system.
* [ ] Measure system pressures (suction and liquid lines) to ensure they are within the manufacturer’s specified range based on outdoor ambient temperatures.
* [ ] Calculate superheat and subcooling values to verify proper refrigerant charge.
* [ ] Check the voltage and amperage draw of the compressor and motors against the nameplate data.
* [ ] Test the thermostat functionality, including all modes (Heat/Cool/Auto/Fan).
* [ ] Confirm that the system cycles on and off correctly per thermostat commands.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Always document the "Before and After" readings for pressure and amperage. This data is critical for identifying "trend drift," which allows for predictive maintenance before a part fails.
* **Pro Tip:** If you find a dirty filter, never just replace it. Check the coil and blower wheel. A dirty filter often acts as a precursor to biological growth on the evaporator coil.
* **Pitfall:** Never use a pressure washer on condenser coils. The high pressure will fold the aluminum fins, significantly reducing heat exchange efficiency. Use a specialized coil brush or low-pressure sprayer.
* **Pitfall:** Ignoring electrical terminal corrosion. Even if the system is running, loose or corroded terminals lead to resistance, which creates heat and eventually causes catastrophic component failure.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should preventative maintenance be performed?**
For most commercial and high-usage residential systems, maintenance should be performed at least twice a year: once before the cooling season (Spring) and once before the heating season (Fall).

**2. What should I do if the system is low on refrigerant?**
Never "top off" the refrigerant without first identifying the source of the leak. Refrigerant is a closed-loop system; if levels are low, there is a leak that must be located, repaired, and leak-tested before recharging.

**3. Is it necessary to check the ductwork during every visit?**
While a full duct cleaning is not part of every routine maintenance, a visual inspection of the accessible plenums and connections is mandatory to ensure there are no air leaks, as these significantly degrade HVAC efficiency.
