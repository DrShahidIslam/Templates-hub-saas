# Standard Operating Procedure: Store Department Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for conducting a comprehensive audit of the store/inventory department. The objective of this audit is to ensure inventory accuracy, optimize storage conditions, verify compliance with safety protocols, and identify discrepancies in stock valuation. By adhering to these procedures, the organization minimizes shrinkage, reduces carrying costs, and ensures operational continuity.

## 1. Pre-Audit Preparation
*   **Documentation Review:** Gather the previous audit report, current inventory ledger, and the last cycle count summary.
*   **Access Verification:** Ensure the audit team has authorized access to the warehouse management system (WMS) or ERP modules.
*   **Safety Briefing:** Review the site’s Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) requirements and evacuation protocols.
*   **Notification:** Provide store personnel with a 24-hour notice of the audit to ensure documentation is staged and areas are accessible.

## 2. Physical Inventory & Storage Standards
*   **Stock Organization:** Verify that items are stored according to the SKU/bin location mapping in the system.
*   **Labeling:** Confirm that all racks, shelves, and bins are clearly labeled and that labels match the physical items.
*   **Housekeeping:** Check for aisle obstructions, dust accumulation, and adequate lighting in all storage zones.
*   **Segregation:** Ensure hazardous materials are stored in designated areas with proper secondary containment.
*   **Damaged Goods:** Identify items marked as "damaged" or "expired" and confirm they are quarantined from sellable stock.

## 3. Documentation & System Integrity
*   **Stock Reconciliation:** Perform a spot check by selecting 10 random items; count physical units and cross-reference with ERP/WMS balances.
*   **Inward/Outward Records:** Audit the last 20 GRNs (Goods Receipt Notes) and Issue Slips to verify they have been signed, dated, and entered into the system.
*   **Approval Authority:** Verify that all high-value inventory releases have documented approval from authorized management.
*   **Return Process:** Review the "Return to Vendor" (RTV) documentation to ensure credits are being captured in the accounting system.

## 4. Safety & Compliance
*   **Fire Safety:** Inspect fire extinguishers for current inspection tags and ensure clear access to emergency exits.
*   **Equipment Maintenance:** Verify that forklifts, pallet jacks, and scanners have completed their recent preventative maintenance cycles.
*   **Security Controls:** Test the integrity of locks, security cameras, and verify the visitor log for the warehouse floor.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The "Shadow Count"):** Always perform a "blind count" where the stock taker does not see the system balance until after they submit their physical count; this prevents "lazy counting" where personnel just confirm the system number.
*   **Pro Tip (Focus on Velocity):** Prioritize auditing "A-class" items (high-value or high-turnover) more frequently than "C-class" (low-value) items to maximize ROI on audit time.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring "Zero" Balances):** Auditors often skip items showing a zero balance in the system. Always verify that these areas are truly empty; they are the most common hiding spots for "phantom" stock.
*   **Pitfall (The Paper-Trail Gap):** Never accept verbal assurances for missing stock. If it isn't documented in the system (or on a signed physical form), it doesn't exist.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a full warehouse audit be conducted?**
A: A full "wall-to-wall" audit is typically conducted annually or semi-annually. However, high-performing departments should utilize a "Cyclical Counting" strategy, where a portion of inventory is audited every day or week.

**Q: What is the acceptable variance threshold for inventory?**
A: This depends on the industry, but a general industry standard is a variance of less than 1% of total inventory value. Anything exceeding 2% usually triggers an immediate investigation into pilferage or process failure.

**Q: What should I do if a large discrepancy is found during the audit?**
A: Do not force an adjustment in the system immediately. First, initiate a "Root Cause Analysis" (RCA) to determine if the error was a data entry mistake, a theft, or a miscount. Once documented, require a department manager’s sign-off before executing a system adjustment.
