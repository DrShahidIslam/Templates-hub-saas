# Standard Operating Procedure: Rental Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as the definitive protocol for conducting thorough rental property inspections. Consistent, high-quality inspections are critical for protecting asset value, ensuring tenant safety, and maintaining clear documentation for security deposit reconciliations. This document outlines the professional standards required for move-in, move-out, and periodic routine inspections to minimize liability and maximize property longevity.

## 1. Preparation and Documentation
*   **Notify the Tenant:** Provide formal written notice at least 24–48 hours prior to entry, per local landlord-tenant laws.
*   **Gather Equipment:** Ensure you are equipped with a high-resolution camera/smartphone, the original move-in inspection report, a flashlight, a moisture meter, and a professional inspection checklist.
*   **Verify Access:** Ensure you have the master key or secure lockbox code.
*   **Coordinate Utilities:** Confirm that all utilities (electricity, water, gas) are active to test appliances and fixtures.

## 2. Exterior and Structural Assessment
*   **Roof and Gutters:** Inspect for missing shingles, debris buildup, or damaged downspouts.
*   **Foundations/Walls:** Check for major cracks, water pooling near the foundation, or structural settling.
*   **Landscaping:** Ensure grass is mowed, hedges are trimmed, and there are no dead trees posing a fall hazard.
*   **Exterior Lighting:** Test all porch lights, motion sensors, and path lighting.
*   **Entryways:** Check door integrity, screen door tension, and the functionality of deadbolts and handle sets.

## 3. Interior Systems and Safety
*   **HVAC:** Check for clean filters, proper airflow, and verify the thermostat is responsive.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect under all sinks for leaks, verify toilet flush strength, and check for water pressure issues in showers.
*   **Electrical:** Test every outlet with a plug-in tester; verify all light switches function.
*   **Life Safety:** Test every smoke detector and carbon monoxide alarm; check expiration dates. Ensure fire extinguishers are present and charged.

## 4. Room-by-Room Condition Report
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Document scuffs, holes, water stains, or peeling paint.
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for carpet stains/burns, hardwood scratches, or cracked tile/grout.
*   **Windows:** Ensure all locks engage, screens are intact, and blinds/curtains function properly.
*   **Appliances:** 
    *   **Kitchen:** Run the dishwasher cycle, test burners, verify oven temp, and check the freezer seal.
    *   **Laundry:** Inspect washing machine hoses for cracks and check dryer vent exhaust.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Timestamp Everything.** Use a camera app that embeds dates and times into the metadata or directly onto the photos. This is your primary evidence in legal disputes.
*   **Pro Tip: Document the "Hidden" Areas.** Don’t just look at the walls. Open the vanity cabinets, check the corners of closets, and look behind the doors where tenants often hide damage.
*   **Pitfall: Relying on Memory.** Never assume you will "remember" the condition of a room. If you didn't document it, it didn't happen.
*   **Pitfall: Neglecting Tenant Input.** If a tenant points out a minor issue during the inspection, record it immediately. Ignoring it signals that maintenance is not a priority, often leading to tenant resentment.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I conduct routine inspections?**
A: It is recommended to perform routine inspections every 6 months to catch potential maintenance issues (like small leaks) before they turn into major, expensive repairs.

**Q: What do I do if I find damage caused by the tenant?**
A: Document the damage with high-quality photos, write a detailed description, and notify the tenant in writing. Request that they address the repair or inform them that the cost will be deducted from their security deposit upon move-out.

**Q: Can I use video instead of photos?**
A: Video is an excellent supplemental tool for a "walk-through" feel, but it should not replace high-resolution still photos, which are easier to archive and reference for specific damage claims.
