# Standard Operating Procedure: Comprehensive Building Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes a systematic framework for maintaining the physical integrity, safety, and functionality of building assets. Effective maintenance management ensures a safe environment for occupants, extends the lifecycle of mechanical and structural systems, and mitigates the risk of costly emergency repairs. This document is intended for facilities managers, maintenance technicians, and custodial staff to ensure consistent, high-quality operations.

## Phase 1: Preventative Maintenance (PM) Scheduling
*   **Asset Audit:** Maintain a master registry of all building assets (HVAC, fire suppression, electrical panels, plumbing, and roofing).
*   **Calendar Integration:** Populate the facilities management software with recurring maintenance tasks based on manufacturer recommendations and local code requirements.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Ensure necessary parts, consumables (filters, lubricants, bulbs), and specialized labor are available 48 hours prior to the scheduled PM date.
*   **Documentation:** Record the condition of assets pre- and post-maintenance to track wear patterns over time.

## Phase 2: Reactive & Emergency Response
*   **Intake Protocol:** All non-emergency maintenance requests must be logged through the official ticketing system with a priority level (P1-P4).
*   **Immediate Triage:** For P1 emergencies (e.g., major water leaks, fire system failure, total power loss), deploy technicians immediately and secure the area to protect occupants.
*   **Communication:** Provide the requester with a tracking number and an estimated time of arrival (ETA) for the technician.
*   **Close-Out:** Ensure the work order is signed off by the building manager or the requester before marking the task as "Complete."

## Phase 3: Life Safety & Compliance Inspections
*   **Fire & Life Safety:** Conduct monthly visual inspections of fire extinguishers, emergency exit lighting, and path-of-egress signage.
*   **Regulatory Compliance:** Coordinate with third-party vendors for annual certifications (e.g., elevator inspections, backflow prevention tests, fire sprinkler dry-pipe tests).
*   **Safety Barriers:** Ensure all roof hatches, electrical rooms, and janitorial closets are locked and restricted to authorized personnel only.

## Phase 4: Building Exterior & Aesthetic Maintenance
*   **Groundskeeping:** Inspect exterior lighting, walkways, and parking lots for trip hazards or illumination outages.
*   **Building Envelope:** Perform semi-annual inspections of the roof, gutter systems, window seals, and siding to prevent water ingress.
*   **Cleaning Standards:** Execute daily and weekly janitorial tasks, focusing on high-touch surfaces, common areas, and waste management.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The 80/20 Rule:** Dedicate 80% of your labor budget to preventative maintenance. This significantly reduces the 20% that is typically spent on chaotic, high-cost emergency repairs.
*   **Pro Tip: Digital Logs:** Use a cloud-based Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) rather than paper logs. This provides an audit trail for insurance and resale purposes.
*   **Pitfall: Deferred Maintenance:** Never delay small repairs (like a dripping pipe or flickering light). Deferred maintenance often compounds into structural damage, leading to exponential cost increases later.
*   **Pitfall: Underestimating Documentation:** If a task isn’t documented, it didn't happen. In the event of a liability claim, maintenance logs are your primary defense.

## FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How do I prioritize maintenance tasks when resources are limited?**
Prioritize by the "Risk to Occupant" and "Risk to Asset." Life safety systems (fire, electrical, water supply) always take precedence over cosmetic or comfort-related repairs.

**2. How often should we review the preventative maintenance schedule?**
Perform a formal review of your PM schedule every six months. Adjust frequencies based on actual equipment performance data—if a filter is still clean at the 3-month mark, you may extend the interval to 4 months to save on labor and parts.

**3. What should be done if a tenant or employee reports a hazardous condition?**
The area must be isolated immediately. Use safety cones, caution tape, or lock off the room. Notify the Facilities Manager and the Safety Officer instantly, regardless of the time of day, to conduct a formal risk assessment before any repairs begin.
