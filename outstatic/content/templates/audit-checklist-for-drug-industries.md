# Standard Operating Procedure: Quality Audit Protocol for Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the comprehensive requirements for conducting internal and external quality audits within pharmaceutical manufacturing facilities. The primary objective of this audit is to ensure rigorous adherence to Current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMP), 21 CFR Part 210/211, and ICH Q10 guidelines. By following this systematic approach, the organization maintains product integrity, patient safety, and regulatory compliance, ensuring the facility remains in a constant state of inspection readiness.

## 1. Quality Management System (QMS) & Documentation
*   Verify the existence and periodic review of the Quality Manual.
*   Ensure all SOPs are current, approved, and accessible to relevant personnel.
*   Audit the Change Control process for adequacy in risk assessment and impact analysis.
*   Review the CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) system for timely closure and effectiveness verification.
*   Check the Document Control system for proper versioning and archival of records.

## 2. Facilities, Utilities, and Equipment
*   Inspect cleanroom classification, air pressure differentials, and HVAC monitoring logs.
*   Verify water system (purified/WFI) performance, including microbial and chemical testing trends.
*   Review equipment calibration schedules and certificates for all measuring instruments.
*   Audit equipment maintenance logs to ensure preventative maintenance is conducted per the master schedule.
*   Confirm existence of validated cleaning procedures to prevent cross-contamination.

## 3. Personnel and Training
*   Confirm current organizational charts and clear definition of responsibilities.
*   Verify that training matrices are up-to-date for all roles, including GMP and safety training.
*   Ensure that personnel performing critical tasks (e.g., aseptic filling) have documented competency assessments.
*   Review health and hygiene records for employees working in controlled environments.

## 4. Production and Process Control
*   Audit Batch Production Records (BPR) for completeness, including signature/date requirements and absence of errors.
*   Verify adherence to Master Batch Records (MBR) and that all deviations are documented and investigated.
*   Check labeling and packaging controls to ensure proper reconciliation and prevention of mix-ups.
*   Review In-Process Control (IPC) testing data to ensure compliance with predefined specifications.

## 5. Material Management and Storage
*   Review supplier qualification files and audit reports for Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (API) and excipient vendors.
*   Confirm proper segregation of quarantined, released, and rejected materials.
*   Audit warehouse environmental monitoring (temperature/humidity) logs.
*   Verify FIFO (First-In, First-Out) and FEFO (First-Expired, First-Out) stock rotation practices.

## 6. Laboratory Controls
*   Review analytical method validation/verification protocols and reports.
*   Check the Out-of-Specification (OOS) and Out-of-Trend (OOT) investigation process.
*   Verify data integrity controls for laboratory instruments (e.g., audit trails, password protections).
*   Ensure stability testing programs are compliant with ICH guidelines.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Walk-through" Test.** Always dedicate 20% of your audit time to an unannounced walk-through of the shop floor. Observe actual behavior versus written procedure; discrepancies here are the highest risk areas.
*   **Pro Tip: Data Integrity Focus.** Regulators prioritize data integrity. Ensure you verify that electronic records have audit trails enabled and that signatures are unique.
*   **Pitfall: "Paper-compliance."** A common failure is having beautiful documentation that does not reflect actual work practices. Always cross-reference batch records with raw data logs.
*   **Pitfall: Poor CAPA Follow-through.** Audits frequently fail because companies identify the problem but do not provide evidence that the long-term corrective action actually fixed the root cause.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a pharmaceutical facility conduct internal quality audits?**
A: Standard industry practice is to perform a comprehensive internal audit at least annually. However, high-risk areas or departments with repeated deviations should be audited on a semi-annual or quarterly basis.

**Q: What is the most critical component to verify during a regulatory inspection?**
A: Data Integrity (ALCOA+ principles: Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate). If the data is deemed untrustworthy, the regulators will assume the product is not compliant, regardless of the physical plant condition.

**Q: If I find a discrepancy during the audit, should I fix it immediately?**
A: You should record the discrepancy immediately. If it poses an imminent patient safety risk, stop the process and initiate a deviation report. Do not try to "hide" or "fix" it retrospectively before documentation; this is considered fraudulent and can lead to severe regulatory enforcement.
