# Standard Operating Procedure: Small Business Audit Readiness

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a comprehensive framework for small business owners and managers to prepare for financial, operational, or tax audits. Maintaining an organized, audit-ready environment minimizes disruption to daily business operations, reduces the risk of non-compliance penalties, and provides stakeholders with confidence in the accuracy of financial reporting. This guide outlines the essential documentation and procedural steps required to ensure a seamless audit process.

## 1. Financial Documentation & Reconciliation
The foundation of any audit is the integrity of the financial records. Ensure all entries are current and supported by primary documentation.

*   **Income Statements:** Verify that all revenue reported matches bank deposits and invoices.
*   **Balance Sheet Verification:** Reconcile all bank accounts, credit card statements, and loan balances against the general ledger.
*   **Accounts Payable/Receivable:** Ensure aging reports are current; confirm that all outstanding invoices are legitimate and all payments are accounted for.
*   **Expense Substantiation:** Cross-reference business expense reports with original receipts, particularly for travel, meals, and entertainment.
*   **Payroll Records:** Confirm payroll tax filings (Forms 941/940), W-2s, and 1099s match salary and contractor payments.

## 2. Operational & Legal Compliance
Auditors often review the structural and legal health of the business to ensure operational legitimacy.

*   **Corporate Documents:** Maintain original copies of Articles of Incorporation, Bylaws, Operating Agreements, and board meeting minutes.
*   **Contractual Agreements:** Organize all active vendor contracts, lease agreements, and client service agreements (MSAs).
*   **Insurance Policies:** Compile proof of current coverage for general liability, professional liability, and workers' compensation.
*   **Regulatory Filings:** Keep records of business licenses, permits, and state-specific tax filings (Sales Tax/Use Tax).

## 3. Personnel & HR Records
HR audits focus on the classification of employees and compliance with labor laws.

*   **Employee Files:** Ensure all files contain signed offer letters, I-9 forms, W-4 forms, and performance reviews.
*   **Contractor Classification:** Verify that 1099 workers meet the criteria for independent contractors (vs. employees) to avoid misclassification penalties.
*   **Labor Posters & Handbooks:** Confirm that the current employee handbook and mandatory labor law posters are accessible to all staff.

## 4. Digital Security & Internal Controls
Assess the systems in place to prevent fraud and protect sensitive business data.

*   **Access Logs:** Document who has authorization to access accounting software and banking portals.
*   **Segregation of Duties:** Verify that the person who writes checks is not the same person who reconciles the bank statements.
*   **Data Backup:** Provide evidence of a regular, tested data backup protocol for financial records.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Audit Folder" Method:** Maintain a digital "Audit" folder throughout the year. As transactions occur or documents are signed, move copies into this folder immediately. This prevents the "scramble" when an audit notice arrives.
*   **Pro Tip: Document the "Why":** For unusual financial entries (e.g., a large unexpected write-off), attach a brief memo explaining the logic. This saves time during the auditor's inquiry phase.
*   **Pitfall: Discrepancy Ignoring:** Never ignore a small unreconciled variance. Auditors often treat small, recurring unexplained variances as indicators of larger systemic control failures.
*   **Pitfall: Defensive Communication:** Avoid volunteering information beyond what is requested. Provide clear, concise answers to specific questions; do not speculate on auditor intent.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How far back should I keep audit records?**
A: Generally, you should retain financial records for at least seven years. Tax records, specifically, should be kept for at least three years after filing, but many experts recommend seven years to cover the maximum statute of limitations for potential audits.

**Q: What is the first thing I should do when I receive an audit notice?**
A: Contact your CPA or tax professional immediately. Do not respond to the auditor directly until you have conferred with your advisor to determine the scope of the audit and the best strategy for documentation delivery.

**Q: Can I request an extension for an audit?**
A: Yes, if you receive an audit notice, you can often request a one-time extension for the document submission deadline if the timeframe is unreasonable. Submit this request in writing to the auditor as early as possible.
