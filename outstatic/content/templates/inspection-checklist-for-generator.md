# Standard Operating Procedure: Standby Generator Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection and maintenance protocols for facility standby power systems. Regular inspections are critical to ensure equipment reliability during utility power failures, extend the operational life of the generator, and maintain compliance with NFPA 110 standards. All inspections must be performed by authorized personnel using appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including insulated gloves, safety glasses, and hearing protection.

## Section 1: Pre-Inspection Safety & Administrative Protocols
*   Verify the generator is in "Auto" mode before beginning; switch to "Off/Manual" only for physical inspections or maintenance.
*   Ensure the area around the generator is clear of debris, flammable materials, or obstructions.
*   Check for any "Active Alarm" indicators on the control panel. Record all fault codes before resetting.
*   Review the previous inspection log to track recurring issues.

## Section 2: Exterior & Enclosure Inspection
*   Inspect the enclosure panels for signs of corrosion, dents, or loose fasteners.
*   Verify that all air intake and exhaust louvers are free of nests, leaves, or snow blockage.
*   Check the base frame for signs of fuel, oil, or coolant leaks.
*   Ensure the fuel tank vent is unobstructed.

## Section 3: Fluid Levels & Cooling System
*   **Engine Oil:** Check the dipstick. Ensure the oil is at the "Full" mark and free of discoloration (milky appearance indicates coolant contamination).
*   **Coolant:** Inspect the radiator coolant level. Ensure the cap is tight and verify the coolant concentration with a refractometer if required by the seasonal schedule.
*   **Fuel System:** Check fuel levels. If using diesel, check the water separator and drain any collected water into an approved container. Inspect fuel lines for cracks or wet spots.
*   **Battery:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion. Ensure the battery charger is providing a steady float voltage (typically 13.5V–14.2V).

## Section 4: Mechanical & Electrical Components
*   **Air Filter:** Check the filter condition indicator (if equipped) or visually inspect for dust accumulation.
*   **Belts:** Inspect fan belts and alternator belts for cracking, fraying, or improper tension.
*   **Wiring:** Examine exposed electrical wiring for insulation damage or signs of heat stress/arcing.
*   **Control Panel:** Confirm the battery voltage, oil pressure, and water temperature readings are within the manufacturer’s nominal ranges while the unit is at idle.

## Section 5: Load Test & Operational Verification
*   Execute a brief (5–15 minute) "No-Load" exercise run to verify startup capability and pressure stability.
*   Listen for abnormal engine sounds (knocking, whistling, or excessive vibration).
*   Verify that the "Run" indicator light illuminates and the unit shuts down automatically once the timer expires.
*   Return the system to "Auto" mode immediately upon completion.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always keep a dedicated "Generator Logbook" attached to the unit. Documenting the date, hour meter reading, and fluid levels provides a critical audit trail for insurance and compliance inspections.
*   **Pro Tip:** Replace air filters and fuel filters based on run-hours rather than calendar time if the unit is used frequently.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore a small drip. A minor oil or coolant weep often indicates a failing gasket that will lead to a catastrophic system failure during a real-world outage.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not leave the generator in "Manual" mode after an inspection. This is the #1 cause of generator failure during power outages.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I inspect the generator if it is rarely used?**
A: Even if the unit does not run during power outages, you should perform a visual inspection monthly and a load-test run at least once a month to ensure the battery is charged and the engine components remain lubricated.

**Q: What should I do if I find water in the fuel water separator?**
A: Immediately drain the separator into a designated waste container. If water continues to accumulate frequently, contact a professional fuel-polishing service to inspect the main fuel tank for contamination.

**Q: Can I perform a load test with the facility connected to the generator?**
A: A full-load test (transferring the facility load) should only be conducted by a certified technician or trained facility engineer, as it involves the Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) and poses significant electrical safety risks. Stick to no-load tests for routine daily/monthly checks.
