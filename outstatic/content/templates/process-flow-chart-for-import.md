# Standard Operating Procedure: International Import Process Flow

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized workflow for managing international import operations. The objective of this document is to ensure regulatory compliance, minimize transit delays, optimize landed costs, and maintain seamless coordination between suppliers, freight forwarders, and internal stakeholders. Adherence to this process is mandatory for all logistics and procurement personnel to prevent customs penalties and supply chain disruptions.

## Section 1: Pre-Shipment Documentation & Compliance
*   **Verify Incoterms:** Confirm the agreed-upon Incoterms (e.g., FOB, DDP, EXW) to define liability and cost transfer points.
*   **Regulatory Review:** Check the Harmonized System (HS) codes for the goods to determine applicable duties, taxes, and potential import restrictions or license requirements.
*   **Supplier Documentation:** Secure the Pro-forma Invoice, Packing List, and Certificate of Origin from the supplier at least 5 days prior to vessel/flight departure.
*   **Validation:** Audit documents for accuracy; ensure quantities, descriptions, and values match the Purchase Order (PO).

## Section 2: Logistics & Transportation Coordination
*   **Booking Confirmation:** Receive the Booking Confirmation from the freight forwarder and ensure the vessel/flight schedule aligns with the "Must Arrive By Date" (MABD).
*   **Insurance Coverage:** Confirm cargo insurance is active; if shipping under FOB, ensure the policy covers the transit from the port of loading.
*   **Tracking Updates:** Input shipment details into the internal TMS (Transportation Management System) and set milestone alerts for departure, arrival, and customs clearance.
*   **Carrier Communication:** Maintain open lines with the forwarder to anticipate potential transshipment delays or space constraints.

## Section 3: Customs Clearance & Entry Filing
*   **ISF/AMS Filing:** Ensure the Importer Security Filing (ISF) is submitted at least 24 hours prior to vessel loading (for ocean freight).
*   **Document Submission:** Transmit the Commercial Invoice, Packing List, and Bill of Lading (BOL) to the Customs Broker.
*   **Duty Payment:** Authorize payment of duties, taxes, and fees via the designated payment portal to avoid clearance holds.
*   **PGA Coordination:** If the goods are regulated (e.g., FDA, FCC, USDA), ensure all Participating Government Agency (PGA) documents are submitted and approved.

## Section 4: Final Mile Delivery & Documentation Archiving
*   **DO Issuance:** Secure the Delivery Order (DO) once freight charges and port fees are settled.
*   **Drayage/Cartage Scheduling:** Coordinate with the local carrier for container pickup or LTL (Less-Than-Truckload) delivery from the bonded warehouse to the destination facility.
*   **Receiving Verification:** Conduct a physical inspection of the cargo upon arrival; document any damages or shortages on the Proof of Delivery (POD).
*   **Record Retention:** File all digital copies of the entry summary (Form 7501), commercial invoices, and proof of payment in the centralized cloud storage for audit readiness (standard requirement: 5 years).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always maintain a "Master Compliance Binder" for each vendor. This should include pre-filled manufacturer declarations, saving significant time during high-volume periods.
*   **Pro Tip:** Request a "Pre-Clearance" review from your broker. Sending documents 48 hours before arrival allows the broker to catch errors before the goods hit the port, preventing costly demurrage charges.
*   **Pitfall:** Never rely solely on the supplier's provided HS code. Customs classifications are the importer’s legal responsibility. A misclassified item can lead to severe fines and retroactive duty audits.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "Free Time" at the port. Monitor your demurrage and detention clock daily; the most expensive logistics mistakes are often incurred while goods sit in a container yard.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What is the most common reason for an import shipment to be held at customs?**
A: Missing or inaccurate documentation, specifically discrepancies between the commercial invoice and the actual cargo manifests, or missing PGA (Participating Government Agency) certifications.

**Q: Why is the choice of Incoterm critical for the import process?**
A: Incoterms define who is responsible for the freight charges, insurance, and legal risks at every stage. For example, under EXW (Ex Works), you take on all risks from the factory floor; under DDP (Delivered Duty Paid), the seller takes on all import risks.

**Q: How can I minimize the risk of paying excessive demurrage fees?**
A: Ensure your customs broker is authorized to file entries "pre-arrival" and maintain a proactive schedule with your drayage carrier to ensure trucks are dispatched the moment cargo is released by customs.
