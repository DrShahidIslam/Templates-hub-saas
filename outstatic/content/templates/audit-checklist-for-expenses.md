# Standard Operating Procedure: Expense Audit Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for auditing corporate expenses to ensure financial accuracy, policy compliance, and the mitigation of fraudulent activity. The objective is to verify that all expenditures are authorized, supported by valid documentation, and aligned with company fiscal policy. Consistent adherence to this audit procedure protects the organization’s bottom line, ensures tax compliance, and maintains internal audit readiness.

## Phase 1: Verification of Documentation
Before reviewing figures, ensure the integrity of the submission by verifying the existence and validity of supporting evidence.

*   **Receipt Completeness:** Verify that every line item has an associated itemized receipt (not just a credit card slip).
*   **Legibility:** Ensure scans or photos are clear and show the vendor name, date, transaction amount, and itemized list of goods/services.
*   **Authenticity:** Check for signs of tampering, such as inconsistent fonts, altered dates, or overlapping data in digital documents.
*   **Submission Timeliness:** Confirm that the expense was submitted within the company’s mandated filing window (e.g., within 30 days of the transaction).

## Phase 2: Policy Compliance & Approval
Evaluate the expenditure against the corporate Travel and Expense (T&E) policy to ensure it adheres to predefined spending limits.

*   **Categorization Accuracy:** Validate that the expense category (e.g., Travel, Meals, Office Supplies) matches the nature of the transaction.
*   **Spending Caps:** Check the amount against per-diem limits or budget caps for that specific role or department.
*   **Authorized Approval:** Ensure the submission has been digitally approved by the correct budget holder or department head.
*   **Duplicate Detection:** Run an automated or manual cross-check against prior submissions to ensure the same receipt has not been expensed twice.

## Phase 3: Financial Integrity & Taxation
This phase focuses on the accuracy of the accounting entries and tax-related requirements.

*   **Tax Applicability:** Verify if Sales Tax or VAT is correctly itemized and if recoverable tax has been properly flagged.
*   **Personal vs. Business:** Inspect for non-reimbursable items such as personal toiletries, movies, or unauthorized alcohol charges.
*   **Currency Conversion:** For international travel, ensure the exchange rate used corresponds to the date of the transaction.
*   **GL Code Alignment:** Ensure the expense is mapped to the correct General Ledger (GL) code for accurate monthly reporting.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Implement Spot Checks:** Don't audit 100% of every report if volume is high. Implement a risk-based approach where reports above a certain dollar threshold are audited manually, while smaller reports undergo random sampling.
*   **Pro Tip: Utilize Software:** Leverage expense management platforms (e.g., Expensify, Concur) to automate the detection of duplicate receipts and policy violations.
*   **Pitfall: Lack of Business Purpose:** The most common audit failure is the missing "Business Purpose" field. A receipt proves *what* was bought, but the description must prove *why* it was necessary for the business.
*   **Pitfall: Retroactive Approval:** Never process an expense that was approved after the fact by someone who lacks the authority to do so. This creates a breakdown in internal controls.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if a receipt is lost?**
A: Employees should complete a "Missing Receipt Affidavit." If this becomes a frequent occurrence for a specific employee, it should be flagged as a recurring policy violation requiring management intervention.

**Q: How do we handle expenses that exceed policy limits?**
A: Expenses exceeding limits should be rejected for reimbursement at the full amount. The employee should be reimbursed only up to the policy limit, and the excess should be identified as a personal expense.

**Q: Why is it important to audit "low-dollar" expenses?**
A: While individual small expenses are low risk, they are the most common source of "expense creeping" and cumulative fraud. Regular audits of small-dollar items discourage employees from testing the system's boundaries.
