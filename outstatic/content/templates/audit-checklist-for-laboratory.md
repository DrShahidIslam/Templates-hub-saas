# Standard Operating Procedure: Laboratory Quality and Compliance Audit

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements and procedural framework for conducting a comprehensive audit of laboratory operations. The objective of this audit is to ensure adherence to Good Laboratory Practice (GLP), ISO/IEC 17025 standards, and internal safety protocols. Regular auditing is critical to maintaining data integrity, minimizing operational risk, and ensuring a safe environment for all personnel. This checklist serves as the baseline for internal audits, which should be performed bi-annually or upon significant changes to laboratory infrastructure.

## 1. Quality Management and Documentation
*   Verify the existence and accessibility of the current Quality Manual.
*   Ensure all SOPs are version-controlled, signed by authorized personnel, and located within immediate reach of relevant workstations.
*   Confirm that all training records are up-to-date, including certifications for new equipment and safety training.
*   Audit the logbooks for traceability: check for entries involving sample receipt, unique identifier assignment, and chain of custody.
*   Review internal audit reports from the previous cycle to confirm that identified non-conformities have been effectively remediated.

## 2. Equipment Calibration and Maintenance
*   Confirm every piece of instrumentation has a valid, current calibration sticker or certificate.
*   Review the preventative maintenance (PM) schedule to ensure all service intervals are met and documented.
*   Inspect the "Out of Service" labeling process for broken or faulty equipment.
*   Verify that calibration weights and reference standards are certified by an accredited external body.
*   Assess environmental monitoring logs (e.g., temperature/humidity) for refrigerators, freezers, and incubators to ensure data consistency.

## 3. Laboratory Safety and Environment
*   Verify that Safety Data Sheets (SDS) are accessible, indexed, and current for all chemicals on-site.
*   Ensure emergency equipment (eyewash stations, safety showers, fire extinguishers) is inspected monthly and free of obstructions.
*   Audit Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) compliance, ensuring appropriate stock of gloves, lab coats, and eye protection.
*   Check that all chemical storage units are correctly labeled, segregated by compatibility, and secured.
*   Inspect waste disposal logs to confirm hazardous waste is being labeled, stored, and removed by authorized contractors.

## 4. Analytical Procedures and Data Integrity
*   Perform a "spot check" on raw data: compare reported results against the original instrument output to ensure accuracy.
*   Confirm that data back-up protocols are functioning and that electronic records are protected against unauthorized modification (21 CFR Part 11 compliance).
*   Verify the validation status of analytical methods; ensure that any method modifications have been documented and re-validated.
*   Assess reagent inventory for expiration dates; ensure "first-in, first-out" (FIFO) practices are in use.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use the "Audit Trail" approach. Select one finished product or test result at random and trace it backward through the entire process—from final report back to raw data, instrument logs, and sample arrival. This is the fastest way to identify process gaps.
*   **Pitfall - The "Clean-up" Rush:** Avoid auditing only when a client or regulatory visit is announced. Audits are most effective when they reflect "day-to-day" operations, not a scrubbed lab.
*   **Pitfall - Overlooking Digital Integrity:** Many labs focus heavily on physical chemicals but fail to verify password security and audit trails on analytical software. Ensure user roles/permissions are properly restricted.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a laboratory internal audit be performed?**
A: Industry standards typically dictate at least an annual audit. However, high-throughput labs or those in heavily regulated sectors (pharma/clinical) should conduct internal audits every six months to identify drift in compliance.

**Q: What is the most common finding in lab audits?**
A: Documentation errors are the most frequent finding. This includes missing signatures, undated entries, or "white-out" usage on logs, which violates data integrity principles.

**Q: If I find a non-conformity during an audit, what is the next step?**
A: Initiate a Corrective and Preventive Action (CAPA) report immediately. Document the root cause, the immediate fix, and the long-term changes implemented to prevent the issue from recurring.
