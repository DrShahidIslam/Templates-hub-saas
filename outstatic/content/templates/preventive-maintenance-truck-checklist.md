# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance (PM) for Commercial Trucking Fleet

Effective preventive maintenance is the backbone of fleet reliability, safety, and operational longevity. This SOP outlines the systematic approach required to inspect and service commercial trucks to minimize downtime, prevent catastrophic mechanical failure, and ensure compliance with Department of Transportation (DOT) safety standards. By adhering to this checklist, maintenance teams ensure that every vehicle remains road-ready and cost-efficient over its lifecycle.

## Section 1: Pre-Inspection Documentation & Safety
*   Verify the current mileage against the maintenance schedule.
*   Review the Driver Vehicle Inspection Report (DVIR) for reported issues from the previous shift.
*   Ensure the truck is parked on level ground, the parking brake is engaged, and the wheels are chocked.
*   Utilize proper Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures if working in the engine bay.
*   Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, steel-toed boots, and gloves.

## Section 2: Engine & Fluid Systems
*   **Oil System:** Drain oil and replace the oil filter. Check for metal shavings or abnormal debris in the used oil.
*   **Coolant:** Check coolant levels and test for proper freezing/boiling point protection. Inspect hoses for cracks, bulges, or leaks.
*   **Air Intake:** Inspect the air filter; replace if restricted. Check intake piping for loose clamps or holes.
*   **Belts:** Inspect serpentine and accessory belts for fraying, glazing, or cracks. Verify tensioner function.
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check and top off transmission fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer fluid.

## Section 3: Chassis, Suspension, & Drivetrain
*   **Lubrication:** Grease all zerk fittings (kingpins, tie rod ends, U-joints, and slack adjusters).
*   **Suspension:** Inspect leaf springs for cracks, shifting, or broken pins. Check air bags for leaks or dry rot.
*   **Drivetrain:** Inspect the driveshaft for play in the U-joints and ensure the carrier bearing is secure.
*   **Exhaust:** Check the exhaust system for leaks, loose hangers, or signs of carbon buildup.

## Section 4: Brake & Tire Systems
*   **Tires:** Measure tread depth (ensure compliance with minimum DOT requirements). Check for uneven wear patterns, cuts, or sidewall bulges.
*   **Tire Pressure:** Inflate all tires to manufacturer-specified PSI.
*   **Brakes:** Inspect brake pads/shoes for lining thickness. Check drums/rotors for scoring or heat spotting.
*   **Air System:** Check brake chambers for leaks and verify that the air dryer is functioning correctly (drain air tanks to check for moisture).

## Section 5: Electrical, Lighting, & Cab Interior
*   **Lighting:** Test all exterior lights, including turn signals, brake lights, hazards, clearance lights, and high/low beams.
*   **Batteries:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion; clean and apply dielectric grease as needed. Secure battery hold-downs.
*   **Dashboard:** Start the engine and monitor the instrument cluster for warning lights.
*   **Safety Equipment:** Confirm that the fire extinguisher is charged and the emergency reflective triangles are present.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the "why" behind every repair. If a hose is replaced early, note if it was due to heat degradation or external abrasion to identify root-cause fleet issues.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement an oil analysis program every 50,000 miles to detect internal engine wear before it becomes a failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore "minor" leaks. A small oil leak often points to a failing seal that will cause significant environmental damage and engine failure if left unaddressed.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "shortcut" greasing. Ensure grease is visible at the seals to confirm the old contaminated grease has been successfully purged.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a full preventive maintenance inspection be performed?**
Industry standards typically recommend a comprehensive PM inspection every 10,000 to 20,000 miles, depending on the severity of the duty cycle and manufacturer recommendations.

**2. What should I do if I find a critical safety issue during a routine PM?**
Immediately tag the vehicle as "Out of Service" (OOS). Communicate the findings to the Fleet Manager and the assigned driver; do not clear the vehicle for operation until the repair is validated by a certified technician.

**3. Is the DVIR sufficient to replace a PM inspection?**
No. The DVIR is a driver-reported snapshot of daily conditions, whereas a PM inspection is a proactive, deep-dive technical audit designed to find hidden wear that a driver may not notice during normal operation.
