# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for Diesel Generator (DG) Sets

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocols for industrial Diesel Generator (DG) sets. The primary objective of this procedure is to ensure the mechanical integrity, fuel efficiency, and operational readiness of the equipment during power failures. Strict adherence to this schedule minimizes the risk of catastrophic engine failure, reduces long-term maintenance costs, and ensures compliance with safety and environmental regulations. All maintenance tasks must be recorded in the equipment logbook upon completion.

## 1. Safety and Pre-Maintenance Preparations
* Ensure the DG set is in the "OFF" position and the control panel is locked (Lock-Out/Tag-Out protocol).
* Disconnect the starting battery (negative terminal first) to prevent accidental startup.
* Ensure the engine has cooled to ambient temperature before touching any hot surfaces.
* Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, gloves, and steel-toed boots.
* Keep a fire extinguisher readily accessible in the service area.

## 2. Lubrication System Maintenance
* Check the engine oil level using the dipstick; ensure it sits between the "Min" and "Max" markers.
* Inspect the oil filter for leaks or signs of bypass; replace the filter element according to the manufacturer’s service hour intervals.
* Check for oil leaks around the sump gasket, oil cooler, and crankshaft seals.
* Analyze the oil condition; if the oil appears milky or excessively dark/viscous, schedule an immediate oil and filter change.

## 3. Cooling System Inspection
* Check the coolant level in the radiator/expansion tank. Top up with the manufacturer-recommended coolant/water mix if required.
* Inspect radiator fins for debris, dust, or insect nesting; use low-pressure compressed air to clean if necessary.
* Check the condition of all rubber coolant hoses for cracking, bulging, or hardening.
* Inspect the water pump for signs of leakage or bearing noise during the test run.
* Verify the integrity of the fan belt tension; replace if fraying or slack is detected.

## 4. Fuel and Air Intake Systems
* Inspect the fuel tank for condensation and water ingress; drain the water separator/sediment bowl.
* Check fuel lines and connections for signs of leaks or sweating.
* Inspect the primary and secondary fuel filters; replace elements based on the manufacturer’s hourly recommendation.
* Inspect the air filter indicator; if red, replace or clean the air filter element.
* Ensure the air intake manifold is free from obstructions.

## 5. Electrical and Control Systems
* Check battery terminal connections; clean off any corrosion and apply a light coat of petroleum jelly.
* Measure battery voltage under load to ensure it maintains cranking current.
* Inspect the control panel wiring for loose terminations or burnt smells.
* Verify the functionality of the Emergency Stop button.
* Test the Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) functionality to ensure seamless load transfer.

## 6. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Always perform a 10-15 minute "no-load" test run after maintenance to check for leaks and ensure all sensors are providing accurate data.
* **Pro Tip:** Maintain a detailed digital logbook of "Hours Run" versus "Fuel Consumed" to detect hidden engine efficiency losses.
* **Pitfall:** Never mix different brands of coolant, as chemical reactions can cause sludge buildup in the radiator core.
* **Pitfall:** Do not overtighten fan belts; excessive tension can cause premature wear on the alternator and water pump bearings.

## 7. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform preventive maintenance?**
A: Preventive maintenance should be performed at least every 250-500 operating hours or every 6 months, whichever comes first, regardless of usage.

**Q: Why does my DG set smoke during the test run?**
A: White smoke often indicates coolant leaking into the combustion chamber (head gasket issue), while black smoke typically suggests incomplete combustion due to clogged air filters or faulty fuel injectors.

**Q: Can I use tap water in the radiator?**
A: No. Tap water contains minerals that cause scale buildup, leading to poor heat transfer and eventual engine overheating. Always use distilled water mixed with the manufacturer-specified corrosion inhibitor/coolant.
