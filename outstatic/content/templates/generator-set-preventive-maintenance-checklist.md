# Standard Operating Procedure: Generator Set Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance (PM) protocols for industrial generator sets. Adherence to this checklist ensures operational readiness, extends equipment lifecycle, and minimizes the risk of catastrophic failure during power outages. All maintenance tasks must be performed by certified technicians following the manufacturer’s specific service manual and local safety regulations (including Lockout/Tagout procedures).

## 1. Safety and Preparation
*   Ensure the generator is set to "OFF" or "Maintenance Mode" on the control panel.
*   Engage the emergency stop button.
*   Disconnect the engine starter battery negative cable to prevent accidental cranking.
*   Verify that the fuel supply valve is closed (if required for specific component access).
*   Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, gloves, and ear protection.

## 2. Lubrication System Inspection
*   Check engine oil level; add oil if necessary.
*   Inspect oil filter for signs of leakage or corrosion.
*   Check for oil leaks around the engine block, seals, and drain plugs.
*   Review the oil change interval log to determine if a full drain and flush is required.

## 3. Cooling System Maintenance
*   Inspect coolant levels in the radiator or expansion tank; top off with a 50/50 mixture of distilled water and coolant if required.
*   Check radiator fins for debris, dust, or blockages; clean with compressed air if necessary.
*   Inspect all radiator hoses for cracks, swelling, or signs of wear.
*   Verify the integrity of hose clamps and check for cooling system leaks.
*   Check the drive belt tension and inspect for fraying or cracking.

## 4. Fuel System Assessment
*   Check fuel levels and refill if below 75% capacity to prevent condensation buildup.
*   Inspect fuel lines and fittings for signs of sweating or leaks.
*   Drain water and sediment from the primary fuel/water separator.
*   Check fuel filters for signs of contamination or clogging.

## 5. Electrical and Starting System
*   Inspect the starter battery; check terminal connections for tightness and corrosion.
*   Test battery voltage and specific gravity (for lead-acid batteries).
*   Clean any corrosion from battery terminals with a wire brush and apply terminal protector spray.
*   Examine the battery charger for proper operation and output.
*   Inspect the control panel wiring for loose connections or damaged insulation.

## 6. Air Intake and Exhaust
*   Check air cleaner element for dirt or obstruction; replace if restriction indicator shows red.
*   Inspect air intake piping for secure connections and leaks.
*   Check the exhaust system for leaks, structural damage, or excessive soot buildup.
*   Ensure the exhaust rain cap is functioning correctly.

## 7. Operational Test (Post-Maintenance)
*   Reconnect the battery and disengage the emergency stop.
*   Start the generator in "Manual" mode.
*   Monitor oil pressure, coolant temperature, and frequency/voltage during a 15-minute "no-load" run.
*   Listen for unusual mechanical noises or vibrations.
*   Return the unit to "Auto" mode once confirmed operational.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always perform a "Load Bank Test" annually to prevent "wet stacking" (unburned fuel buildup) caused by running a generator without a sufficient load.
*   **Pro Tip:** Keep a digital logbook for every unit. Consistency in tracking pressure and temperature trends can help predict a failure weeks before it happens.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening battery terminals often leads to cracked battery cases; use a torque wrench to manufacturer specifications.
*   **Pitfall:** Never use water to clean a hot radiator; the thermal shock can cause the fins to crack.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should I conduct preventive maintenance?**
A: Standard industrial practice is at least every 6 months or every 250 operating hours, whichever comes first. However, critical mission environments may require monthly inspections.

**Q: Can I use tap water in the coolant mixture?**
A: No. Tap water contains minerals that create scale deposits inside the radiator, significantly reducing cooling efficiency. Always use distilled water.

**Q: What should I do if I find a leak during maintenance?**
A: Identify the source of the leak immediately. If it is a minor gasket seep, note it for repair in the next service window. If it is a pressurized fuel or oil line leak, the generator must be tagged out of service until the repair is completed.
