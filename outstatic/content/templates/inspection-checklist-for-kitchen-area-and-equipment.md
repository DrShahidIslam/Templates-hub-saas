# Standard Operating Procedure: Kitchen and Equipment Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for inspecting the kitchen environment and operational equipment. The objective is to maintain the highest standards of food safety, employee welfare, and equipment longevity. Regular inspections are critical to mitigating fire hazards, preventing cross-contamination, and ensuring compliance with local health department regulations. All designated personnel must adhere to this checklist daily to maintain audit-readiness and operational efficiency.

## 1. Environmental & Sanitation Inspection
*   **Floors and Drains:** Ensure floors are free of standing water, grease buildup, and debris. Verify that floor drains are clear of blockages and drain covers are secure.
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Check for signs of water damage, mold growth, or peeling paint/surfaces. Ensure no dust or grease is accumulating on overhead ventilation covers.
*   **Waste Management:** Inspect trash and recycling bins to ensure they are not overflowing, have functioning lids, and are properly lined.
*   **Pest Control:** Scan for signs of pest activity (droppings, gnaw marks, or sightings). Ensure all exterior doors are sealed properly and screens are intact.
*   **Storage Areas:** Verify all dry goods are stored at least six inches off the floor on sanitized shelving. Confirm "First-In, First-Out" (FIFO) rotation.

## 2. Refrigeration & Temperature Control
*   **Unit Temperatures:** Verify all walk-in coolers and reach-in freezers are within the safe zone (40°F/4°C or below for coolers; 0°F/-18°C for freezers).
*   **Door Seals:** Inspect gaskets for cracks, tears, or debris buildup that prevents a tight seal.
*   **Condenser Coils:** Check for dust buildup on coils; ensure proper airflow around the unit to prevent overheating.
*   **Organization:** Confirm that raw proteins are stored on bottom shelves to prevent drip-contamination of ready-to-eat foods.

## 3. Cooking Equipment & Appliances
*   **Cooking Surfaces:** Verify stovetops, flat-tops, and fryers are clear of heavy carbon buildup. Check that grease traps are emptied.
*   **Gas Lines & Connections:** Inspect gas hoses for signs of wear or cracking. Ensure the Emergency Shut-Off valve is clearly marked and accessible.
*   **Ventilation Hoods:** Check hood filters for excessive grease saturation. Ensure the fire suppression system links are clean and unobstructed.
*   **Electrical Outlets:** Inspect for frayed cords, scorched plates, or loose connections. Ensure no equipment cords are running across high-traffic walkways.

## 4. Safety & Sanitation Stations
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Ensure pressure gauges are in the "Green" zone and the safety pin is intact. Check that the inspection tag is current.
*   **Handwashing Stations:** Verify the presence of soap, paper towels, and a functioning hot water supply at all designated hand sinks.
*   **Chemical Storage:** Confirm all chemicals are labeled correctly and stored away from food-contact surfaces.
*   **PPE Availability:** Ensure that cut-resistant gloves, aprons, and non-slip floor mats are in good condition and available for use.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "Cleaning Clock." Assign specific equipment tasks to specific times of the day rather than leaving all deep cleaning for the end of the shift. This prevents staff burnout and ensures consistency.
*   **Pro Tip:** Keep a digital log of temperature readings. This creates a historical data trail that is invaluable during a health inspection or if a refrigeration unit fails.
*   **Pitfall - The "Normalization of Deviance":** Do not overlook "small" issues like a slightly dripping faucet or a flickering light. These often escalate into major equipment failures or safety hazards if left unaddressed.
*   **Pitfall - Improper Chemical Storage:** A common health code violation is storing unlabeled spray bottles or storing cleaning chemicals above food prep areas. Always store chemicals on low shelves.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should the deep cleaning of hood filters occur?**
A: Depending on the volume of cooking, hood filters should be degreased at least weekly, though high-volume kitchens may require a daily soak.

**Q: What is the immediate action if a walk-in cooler temperature exceeds 41°F?**
A: First, check if the door was left ajar. If not, log the temperature, move perishable food to a backup unit, and contact the maintenance department immediately for a repair.

**Q: Are staff allowed to use personal cleaning supplies from home?**
A: No. All cleaning chemicals must be approved by the safety manager and accompanied by an SDS (Safety Data Sheet) to ensure they are food-safe and stored correctly.
