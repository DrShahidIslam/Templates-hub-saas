# Standard Operating Procedure: Maintenance Department Audit

## Purpose and Scope
This document outlines the standardized procedure for auditing the Maintenance Department to ensure operational efficiency, regulatory compliance, and equipment reliability. This audit is designed to assess documentation integrity, preventative maintenance (PM) execution, inventory management, and workplace safety standards. It is intended to be used by internal auditors, operations managers, or safety officers to verify that maintenance activities align with company policy and industry best practices.

---

## Maintenance Department Audit Checklist

## Section 1: Documentation and Compliance
*   Verify the existence and current status of all mandatory legal and safety permits for facility equipment (boilers, elevators, pressure vessels, etc.).
*   Confirm that the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) is updated in real-time with labor hours, parts usage, and work completion notes.
*   Review training records for all maintenance personnel to ensure all certifications (e.g., HVAC, Electrical, OSHA-10/30) are current and documented.
*   Check for the presence of up-to-date Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for all critical machinery and equipment.

## Section 2: Preventative Maintenance (PM) Execution
*   Compare the PM schedule against actual completion reports for the previous quarter.
*   Randomly select five work orders to verify that the work performed matches the reported time and materials used.
*   Assess the "Backlog Report" to identify high-priority outstanding tasks and determine if resources are being allocated correctly.
*   Verify that emergency/unplanned maintenance is being tracked separately from routine scheduled maintenance to measure "reactive vs. proactive" ratios.

## Section 3: Inventory and Spare Parts Management
*   Perform a spot-check inventory of high-value or critical spare parts against the CMMS database.
*   Examine the storage area for organization, labeling, and climate control (if required).
*   Review the "Obsolete Parts" list to ensure the department is not carrying excessive stock of components for retired equipment.
*   Verify the procurement process for new parts, ensuring that requisitions are approved and properly logged.

## Section 4: Safety and Housekeeping (5S)
*   Inspect the maintenance workshop for adherence to 5S principles (Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain).
*   Verify that Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) kits are complete, accessible, and inspected regularly.
*   Ensure all personal protective equipment (PPE) is available, in good condition, and utilized by staff.
*   Check for hazardous waste disposal logs to confirm compliance with environmental regulations.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **The "Shadow" Method:** Shadow a maintenance technician for two hours. You will learn more about the reality of the workflow than you will from any report.
*   **Focus on the "Big Three":** Always track your Meantime Between Failures (MTBF), Meantime to Repair (MTTR), and PM Compliance percentage. These metrics are the heartbeat of your department.
*   **Photographic Evidence:** During the audit, take photos of both non-compliant areas and "gold standard" areas to provide clear visual feedback during the close-out meeting.

### Pitfalls
*   **The "Paperwork Perfection" Trap:** A department might have perfect documentation but poor mechanical outcomes. Never let paperwork replace the physical inspection of the equipment.
*   **Ignoring Informal Communication:** Relying solely on the CMMS often ignores the "tribal knowledge" communicated via text or verbal requests. Ensure all work is being funneled through the system.
*   **Retaliatory Auditing:** Auditors must approach this as a process-improvement exercise, not a disciplinary one. If staff feels targeted, transparency will drop significantly.

## FAQ: Maintenance Auditing

**Q1: How often should a formal maintenance audit be conducted?**
A: A comprehensive audit should be conducted at least bi-annually. However, smaller "spot audits" focusing on a specific area (like inventory or safety compliance) should be performed monthly to ensure consistent adherence.

**Q2: What is the most common reason for a failing audit score?**
A: Failure to close out work orders in the CMMS is the most common issue. When technicians forget to close tasks or record parts, the department loses visibility into true costs and asset health.

**Q3: How do I handle resistance from the maintenance team during an audit?**
A: Frame the audit as a tool to justify their resource requests. By proving that the department is understaffed or under-funded through objective data, you empower the team rather than policing them.
