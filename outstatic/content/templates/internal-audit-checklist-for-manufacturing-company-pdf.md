# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Audit for Manufacturing Operations

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting a comprehensive internal audit within a manufacturing environment. The objective is to evaluate operational efficiency, ensure strict compliance with quality management systems (e.g., ISO 9001), verify safety standards, and identify supply chain vulnerabilities. By adhering to this audit framework, the organization mitigates risk, maintains regulatory compliance, and fosters a culture of continuous improvement. This document serves as the master guide for audit leads and departmental managers.

## Phase 1: Planning and Documentation Review
*   **Audit Scope Definition:** Clearly define the departments (e.g., procurement, floor production, quality control, logistics) and processes under review.
*   **Regulatory Alignment:** Gather current versions of SOPs, ISO certifications, OSHA guidelines, and internal work instructions.
*   **Historical Data Analysis:** Review findings from the previous audit cycle to ensure identified non-conformities have been remediated.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Confirm availability of key personnel and ensure access to digital logs (ERP/MES systems).

## Phase 2: Production Floor and Equipment Audit
*   **Machine Calibration:** Verify that all production machinery has up-to-date calibration certificates and maintenance logs.
*   **Preventative Maintenance (PM):** Audit the PM schedule versus actual completion dates.
*   **Work-in-Progress (WIP) Integrity:** Inspect storage areas to ensure WIP is properly labeled, organized, and protected from environmental degradation.
*   **Safety Compliance:** Conduct a walkthrough to verify the presence of PPE, clear egress routes, machine guarding, and visible hazard signage.

## Phase 3: Quality Control and Supply Chain
*   **Raw Material Inspection:** Audit incoming inspection records to ensure compliance with material specifications.
*   **Non-Conformance Management:** Review the log of rejected parts; verify that root cause analysis (RCA) was performed and corrective actions (CAPA) were implemented.
*   **Traceability Audit:** Perform a "traceability drill" by selecting a finished good and tracing its history back to raw material lot numbers.
*   **Supplier Compliance:** Verify that all active vendors are on the "Approved Supplier List" and that recent performance reviews have been filed.

## Phase 4: Financial and Inventory Controls
*   **Inventory Accuracy:** Perform spot-check cycle counts on high-value components and compare them against the ERP system.
*   **Scrap Analysis:** Review scrap logs to ensure waste is accurately documented and that high scrap rates are being addressed.
*   **Procurement Audit:** Ensure purchase orders (POs) are authorized per the company’s spending authority matrix.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement "Gemba Walks" (going to the actual place of work) rather than relying solely on desktop audits. You will see process deviations that never appear on a report.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use digital audit software to capture timestamped photos of non-conformities to build an indisputable evidence trail.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-reliance on "Interviews." Employees may provide the "ideal" process rather than the "actual" process. Always verify verbal claims against physical records or observation.
*   **Pitfall:** "Checklist Mentality." Do not treat the audit as a box-ticking exercise. If you see a process that works but is not documented correctly, treat the documentation—not the process—as the non-conformity.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a manufacturing internal audit be conducted?**
A: High-compliance environments (e.g., medical or aerospace) should conduct full internal audits quarterly. Standard manufacturing facilities typically perform a full audit annually, supplemented by monthly focused spot-checks.

**Q: What is the most critical item to verify during an audit?**
A: Traceability. Being able to track a product from raw material batch to the end-customer is the backbone of risk management, recall efficiency, and regulatory compliance.

**Q: Should the audit findings be shared with the floor operators?**
A: Yes. Transparency is vital for a "just culture." Sharing findings helps operators understand the *why* behind quality protocols and encourages buy-in for the necessary corrective actions.
