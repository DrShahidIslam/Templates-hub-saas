# Standard Operating Procedure: Scissor Lift Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance requirements for all scissor lift equipment. Regular, systematic inspections are critical to ensuring personnel safety, maintaining compliance with OSHA and ANSI/SAIA standards, and extending the operational lifespan of the machinery. This checklist must be completed by a qualified technician prior to the commencement of each shift or in accordance with the manufacturer's recommended maintenance interval. Failure to address deficiencies discovered during these inspections will result in the immediate removal of the unit from service.

## 1. Visual Inspection & Structural Integrity
*   **Chassis and Frame:** Inspect for visible cracks, weld failures, deformation, or corrosion in the base frame.
*   **Scissor Arm Assembly:** Check all pivot pins, bushings, and bolts for signs of wear, shifting, or missing retention hardware. 
*   **Platform Condition:** Ensure the floor decking is secure, free of oil/debris, and that the toe-boards are intact.
*   **Guardrails:** Verify that all railings are secure, locking gates function correctly, and mid-rails are not bent.
*   **Decals and Labels:** Ensure all safety, capacity, and instruction decals are present, legible, and match the specific model.
*   **Tires and Wheels:** Inspect for cuts, chunks missing, or flat spots. Ensure lug nuts are torqued to manufacturer specifications.

## 2. Hydraulic and Fluid Systems
*   **Hydraulic Fluid Levels:** Check fluid level with the platform in the fully lowered position. Ensure levels are within the "Full" range.
*   **Leak Detection:** Inspect hoses, cylinders, fittings, and the pump unit for signs of weeping, puddles, or pressurized spray.
*   **Cylinders:** Verify there is no "drift" or leaking at the seal glands. Ensure lift cylinders are free of pits or scoring on the chrome rods.
*   **Hose Routing:** Confirm that hoses are not rubbing against sharp metal edges or showing signs of abrasion/kinking.

## 3. Electrical and Battery Systems
*   **Battery Maintenance:** For lead-acid batteries, check electrolyte levels and top off with distilled water if necessary. Ensure terminals are clean and free of corrosion.
*   **Cabling:** Inspect all battery cables for frayed insulation or loose connections.
*   **Emergency Stop:** Test the E-stop on both the platform and the ground controls. Verify that the unit loses power immediately upon activation.
*   **Controls and Joysticks:** Ensure the joystick returns to center (neutral) smoothly and does not "stick." Test all directional and speed functions.
*   **Alarms and Beacons:** Verify that the motion alarm, tilt alarm, and flashing amber strobe are functional.

## 4. Operational/Load Test
*   **Function Test (Ground):** Raise and lower the unit using ground controls. Listen for unusual noises (grinding, squealing, or cavitation).
*   **Function Test (Platform):** Test steering, drive speed, and elevation functions from the platform. 
*   **Limit Switches:** Verify that the "drive speed cut-out" engages when the lift is elevated. 
*   **Braking Test:** Perform a drive-and-stop test on a level surface to ensure the friction brakes engage instantly upon release of the joystick.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall: Over-greasing.** While lubrication is vital, excessive grease on pivot points can attract dust and grit, creating an abrasive paste that accelerates wear. Wipe away excess after application.
*   **Pro Tip: Document Everything.** Maintain a physical or digital logbook for every lift. Consistent documentation is your primary defense during an OSHA audit and essential for warranty claims.
*   **Pro Tip: The "Cold Start" Check.** Perform your primary inspection in the morning while the unit is cold. Hydraulic leaks are often easier to spot before the ambient temperature rise or the engine heat obscures early-stage weeping.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Leaks.** A minor weep today is a blown hose tomorrow. Never "top off" and hope for the best; always trace the source of the leak and repair the seal or fitting immediately.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a deep-dive, annual inspection be performed?**
A: In addition to daily pre-start inspections, an annual or 500-hour (whichever comes first) intensive maintenance inspection should be performed by a factory-trained technician, including a load test and hydraulic pressure verification.

**Q: What should I do if I find a defect during the inspection?**
A: If any safety-critical component (brakes, steering, E-stops, or structural integrity) is compromised, "Red Tag" the machine immediately, disconnect the batteries, and remove the key to prevent unauthorized operation until repairs are verified.

**Q: Can I use aftermarket hydraulic fluid?**
A: Only use the hydraulic fluid specified in your manufacturer’s manual. Using the wrong viscosity or chemical composition can destroy pump seals and cause sluggish operation in extreme temperature ranges.
