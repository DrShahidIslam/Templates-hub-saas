# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Building Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes a systematic framework for the recurring inspection and maintenance of facility infrastructure. The objective of this protocol is to extend the service life of building assets, ensure regulatory compliance, minimize unplanned downtime, and maintain a safe, high-performance environment for all occupants. This checklist should be executed by qualified facilities personnel on a quarterly basis, with specific high-frequency tasks performed monthly as indicated.

## 1. Exterior & Building Envelope
*   **Roofing System:** Inspect for membrane punctures, debris accumulation in drains, and signs of ponding water. Ensure flashing and seals around roof penetrations are intact.
*   **Building Facade:** Check for masonry cracks, spalling, or loose architectural elements. Ensure weep holes are clear of obstructions.
*   **Windows & Glazing:** Inspect caulking and weather-stripping for degradation. Confirm all operable windows latch and seal correctly.
*   **Hardscapes & Drainage:** Clear storm drains and gutters of organic debris. Inspect concrete walkways for trip hazards (gaps >0.5 inches) or structural heaving.

## 2. Mechanical, Electrical, & Plumbing (MEP)
*   **HVAC Systems:** Replace air filters (MERV-13 or higher). Inspect belts for tension and wear. Clean condenser coils and check refrigerant levels.
*   **Electrical Panels:** Utilize thermal imaging to detect "hot spots" indicating loose connections. Ensure all panel labels are legible and circuit breakers are clearly identified.
*   **Lighting:** Perform a site-wide "burn-out" check. Replace failing ballasts or flickering LED drivers. Test emergency egress lighting and exit signs for battery backup functionality.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect all visible piping for signs of corrosion or slow leaks. Test shut-off valves for ease of movement. Verify water heater temperature and pressure relief valve (PRV) status.

## 3. Life Safety & Security
*   **Fire Suppression:** Verify that fire extinguishers are mounted, charged, and have current inspection tags. Ensure path-of-travel to fire pulls and exits is unobstructed.
*   **Smoke & Carbon Monoxide Detectors:** Perform a physical test of all hard-wired units. Replace batteries in non-hardwired units annually.
*   **Access Control:** Test exterior door hardware, including strikes and closers. Ensure key-fob systems and electronic magnetic locks engage properly upon door closure.

## 4. Interior Finishes & Housekeeping
*   **Flooring:** Inspect high-traffic areas for carpet fraying or loose floor tiles that could pose safety risks.
*   **Walls & Ceilings:** Check for signs of water intrusion (staining or bubbling paint). Repair minor drywall damage to maintain aesthetic integrity.
*   **Door Assemblies:** Adjust door closers to ensure proper latching without excessive force. Lubricate hinges to prevent squeaking or misalignment.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Implement Digital Logs.** Move away from paper checklists. Use a Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) to track time-stamped photos and historical trends of equipment performance.
*   **Pro Tip: Schedule Off-Hours.** Conduct invasive inspections (like electrical panel testing or water shut-offs) during low-occupancy hours to mitigate disruption to building operations.
*   **Pitfall: Deferred Maintenance.** The most common mistake is skipping "minor" repairs. Small leaks or loose hinges rarely fix themselves and almost always lead to costly structural damage or injury if ignored.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Manufacturer Specs.** Always reference the specific O&M (Operations & Maintenance) manuals for your HVAC and electrical equipment; generic maintenance can sometimes void warranties.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I review and update this SOP?**
A: This SOP should be reviewed annually or whenever a major infrastructure upgrade (e.g., HVAC system replacement) occurs to ensure the checklist remains relevant to the current asset inventory.

**Q: What is the best way to handle maintenance items found during an inspection that require specialized contractors?**
A: Immediate triage is required. If the issue poses a safety risk, isolate the area and contact a licensed contractor immediately. For non-urgent issues, log the defect in your CMMS and solicit at least three quotes before scheduling the repair.

**Q: Should I keep maintenance records if the building is a small office?**
A: Absolutely. Maintaining a thorough paper trail is critical for insurance claims, liability protection, and future resale value. Even a simple Excel spreadsheet tracking the "Date, Item, Action, and Technician" is sufficient for smaller properties.
