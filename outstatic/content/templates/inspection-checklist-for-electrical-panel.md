# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Panel Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory safety protocols and technical procedures for conducting a comprehensive inspection of electrical distribution panels. The objective of this inspection is to identify thermal degradation, loose connections, physical damage, and compliance violations to ensure personnel safety and operational uptime. All inspections must be performed by qualified personnel using appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) and calibrated diagnostic tools.

## Section 1: Pre-Inspection Safety & Preparation
*   **Verify PPE Requirements:** Ensure use of arc-rated clothing, safety glasses, insulated gloves (rated for the voltage), and leather protectors.
*   **Clear the Work Zone:** Remove obstructions, floor mats, or equipment blocking access to the panel (minimum 36-inch clearance).
*   **Tool Calibration Check:** Verify that multimeters and thermal imaging cameras are calibrated and functional.
*   **Communication:** Notify facility stakeholders of the inspection window, as this may require temporary load management or downtime.

## Section 2: Exterior Physical Inspection
*   **Integrity Check:** Inspect the panel enclosure for signs of rust, water damage, or physical impact/dents.
*   **Labels & Signage:** Ensure all warning labels ("Arc Flash Hazard," "High Voltage") are present, legible, and current.
*   **Mounting:** Confirm the panel is securely fastened to the wall or structure with no loose bolts.
*   **Environmental Assessment:** Check for signs of moisture, pests, or unauthorized chemical storage in the immediate vicinity.

## Section 3: Interior Component Inspection
*   **Dead Front Removal:** Use insulated tools to carefully remove the panel cover. Inspect for scorch marks or carbon tracking on the inside of the cover.
*   **Thermal Imaging (Under Load):** Utilize a thermal imaging camera to scan for "hot spots." Temperatures should be uniform; any localized heat indicates high resistance.
*   **Conductor Connections:** Visually check for discolored or brittle wire insulation. If permitted by site safety protocols, perform a torque check on terminal lugs to ensure they meet manufacturer specifications.
*   **Circuit Breakers:** Check for signs of "tripped" status, pitting, or arc damage on breaker faces.
*   **Busbar Integrity:** Examine the busbars for signs of oxidation, dust accumulation, or foreign debris that could cause arcing.

## Section 4: Testing & Verification
*   **Voltage Consistency:** Measure voltage across phases to ensure load balancing and stability.
*   **Grounding/Bonding:** Confirm that the grounding conductor is intact and properly secured to the grounding bus.
*   **Labeling Accuracy:** Verify that the directory card matches the physical circuit breakers. Correct any outdated or missing labels.

## Section 5: Documentation & Closeout
*   **Photo Documentation:** Capture high-resolution images of the interior and any identified deficiencies.
*   **Report Generation:** Log all readings, thermal findings, and maintenance actions in the facility management software.
*   **Panel Re-energization:** Secure the panel cover, tighten fasteners, and ensure the workspace is clean before clearing the area.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always perform thermal imaging *while the system is under load*. Testing an idle panel will fail to reveal resistance-based issues.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a torque screwdriver rather than a standard one to ensure connections meet the exact manufacturer requirements—too tight or too loose can both lead to failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Never touch internal components even if you believe the power is off. Always test for voltage ("test before touch") using a secondary, independent meter.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid the "dusty" trap. If you find significant dust buildup, do not use compressed air, as this can force conductive particles into sensitive mechanisms; use a HEPA-rated vacuum instead.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should an electrical panel be inspected?**
A: NFPA 70B recommends electrical equipment inspections at least annually, though high-load or mission-critical panels should be inspected quarterly or bi-annually.

**Q: What should I do if I find a "hot spot" during thermal imaging?**
A: Immediately document the finding, assess the severity, and schedule a repair. If the temperature exceeds 40°C above ambient, de-energize the circuit immediately until the connection is cleaned or tightened.

**Q: Can I use a regular vacuum cleaner for an electrical panel?**
A: No. Standard household vacuums are not anti-static and can create a static discharge, potentially triggering an arc flash. Always use an industrial-grade, ESD-safe, HEPA-filtered vacuum.
