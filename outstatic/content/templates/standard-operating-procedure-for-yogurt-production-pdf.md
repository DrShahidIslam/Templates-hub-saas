# Standard Operating Procedure: Yogurt Production

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the rigorous process for manufacturing high-quality, food-safe yogurt. Adherence to these protocols ensures consistency in texture, flavor profile, and microbial stability while maintaining strict compliance with Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) and Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point (HACCP) standards. All operators must sanitize equipment and verify temperature calibrations prior to commencing each batch.

## 1. Preparation and Sanitization
*   Verify that all processing equipment (vats, homogenizers, transfer lines) has been cleaned and sanitized (CIP - Clean-in-Place).
*   Conduct an ATP swab test on critical contact surfaces to ensure microbial load is within safety parameters.
*   Calibrate all thermometers and pH meters against a known buffer solution.
*   Ensure all staff are wearing full PPE, including hairnets, beard guards, sanitized gloves, and dedicated footwear.

## 2. Standardization and Pasteurization
*   Standardize the milk to the target fat and solids-not-fat (SNF) content based on the specific yogurt formulation.
*   Heat the milk mixture to 85°C – 95°C (185°F – 203°F) for 5–10 minutes to denature whey proteins and ensure structural stability.
*   Perform homogenization at 2,000–2,500 psi to break down fat globules, preventing cream separation.

## 3. Inoculation and Fermentation
*   Cool the milk mixture to the incubation temperature (42°C – 45°C / 108°F – 113°F).
*   Add the specific yogurt starter culture (Lactobacillus bulgaricus and Streptococcus thermophilus) using aseptic techniques.
*   Transfer the mixture to incubation tanks and hold at the target temperature until the desired pH (typically 4.5–4.6) is reached.
*   Monitor pH levels every 30 minutes during the final stages of fermentation to prevent over-acidification.

## 4. Cooling and Finishing
*   Once the target pH is achieved, activate the cooling system to drop the temperature rapidly to 5°C (41°F) to halt the fermentation process.
*   If producing "stirred-style" yogurt, gently break the curd and incorporate fruit preps, stabilizers, or flavorings at this stage.
*   Perform visual and sensory quality checks for texture, viscosity, and absence of off-notes.

## 5. Packaging and Storage
*   Maintain a sterile environment during the filling process.
*   Apply hermetic seals to containers to prevent contamination and ensure shelf-life.
*   Apply tracking labels including Batch Number, Production Date, and Expiration Date.
*   Transfer final product to a refrigerated cold-storage unit maintained at 1°C – 4°C (34°F – 39°F).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "culture rotation" schedule to prevent bacteriophage buildup in the processing facility.
*   **Pro Tip:** For a creamier texture, increase the holding time during the pasteurization phase to allow for greater protein hydration.
*   **Pitfall - Whey Separation (Syneresis):** Often caused by mechanical agitation that is too aggressive post-fermentation or insufficient denaturation of whey proteins.
*   **Pitfall - Inconsistent Texture:** Usually linked to fluctuations in incubation temperature; ensure your thermal regulation system is serviced quarterly.
*   **Pitfall - Bitter Aftertaste:** Frequently a sign of "over-fermentation" where proteolysis has occurred due to leaving the yogurt at warm temperatures for too long.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I determine the correct amount of starter culture to use?**
A: Always refer to the manufacturer’s technical data sheet provided with your specific starter culture, as concentration levels vary significantly between suppliers and strain types.

**Q: Why does the pH drop too slowly during fermentation?**
A: The most common causes are an incorrect incubation temperature (too low) or the presence of antibiotic residues in the raw milk, which inhibit culture activity.

**Q: Can I recycle left-over yogurt into a new batch?**
A: No. Using "back-slopping" (using previous batches as starter) increases the risk of contamination and results in rapid strain mutation, leading to inconsistent product quality and potential safety hazards.
