# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Audit for Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs)

This internal audit procedure serves as a critical governance tool to ensure operational transparency, financial integrity, and programmatic accountability within an NGO. Designed to mitigate risks associated with donor funding, regulatory compliance, and organizational mismanagement, this SOP provides a structured framework for verifying that institutional policies are being executed effectively. By adhering to this checklist, the organization ensures it maintains its tax-exempt status, builds donor trust, and demonstrates maximum impact for its beneficiaries.

## 1. Financial Governance and Internal Controls
*   **Segregation of Duties:** Verify that the person who approves expenditures is different from the person who processes payments and reconciles bank statements.
*   **Cash Handling:** Review cash disbursement records. Ensure all petty cash has a capped limit, a logged voucher system, and documented physical counts.
*   **Authorization Thresholds:** Confirm that all expenditures above the established limit have received proper board or executive sign-off as per the Financial Policy Manual.
*   **Bank Reconciliations:** Ensure all bank statements are reconciled within 10 days of month-end and that any unexplained variances are documented and resolved.

## 2. Donor Compliance and Grant Management
*   **Restricted Funds Tracking:** Validate that funds provided for specific projects are not used for general operational expenses unless explicitly permitted by the donor agreement.
*   **Reporting Timelines:** Cross-reference financial reports submitted to donors against the actual ledger entries to ensure no discrepancy in reporting.
*   **Grant Conditions:** Confirm that specific donor requirements (e.g., procurement guidelines, advertising standards, or audit clauses) were met throughout the project lifecycle.
*   **Asset Register:** Verify that all capital assets purchased with grant funds are tagged, recorded, and physically located.

## 3. Procurement and Vendor Management
*   **Competitive Bidding:** For high-value contracts, ensure at least three quotes were obtained and that the selection process was documented impartially.
*   **Vendor Due Diligence:** Confirm that vendors are legitimate, active, and not on any "Debarred Parties" lists (especially important for USAID or EU-funded NGOs).
*   **Invoice Verification:** Ensure that all invoices are original, dated, detailed, and matched against an approved Purchase Order (PO) and proof of delivery/service.

## 4. Human Resources and Payroll
*   **Payroll Accuracy:** Audit payroll against employment contracts, ensuring tax withholdings and benefits are calculated correctly and paid to government authorities on time.
*   **Timesheet Compliance:** For employees working on multiple grants, verify that timesheets are signed, dated, and accurately allocate time to the correct cost centers.
*   **Conflict of Interest:** Ensure that staff and board members have signed an annual Conflict of Interest disclosure statement.

## 5. Governance and Regulatory Compliance
*   **Statutory Filings:** Confirm that all annual reports, tax filings, and NGO registration renewals were submitted to regulatory bodies by their respective deadlines.
*   **Board Minutes:** Review board meeting minutes to ensure that major financial decisions, budget approvals, and executive appointments are formally recorded.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Implement Continuous Auditing.** Instead of waiting for an annual audit, perform "spot checks" on one specific category (e.g., travel expenses) every month. This prevents small errors from accumulating into systemic issues.
*   **Pro Tip: Use Digital Trail.** Migrate to paperless systems (ERP or Accounting software) where digital logs of who changed a transaction are automatically saved. 
*   **Pitfall: The "Charity Mindset."** Many NGOs mistakenly believe their "good intentions" exempt them from rigorous documentation. Auditors do not measure impact by good intentions; they measure them by evidence. If it isn't documented, it didn't happen.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Expenditures.** Staff often neglect receipts for small items. Ensure a culture of "no receipt, no reimbursement," regardless of the amount.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should an NGO perform an internal audit?**
At a minimum, a comprehensive internal audit should be conducted annually. However, high-risk areas like payroll and procurement should ideally be audited on a quarterly basis.

**2. Who should lead the internal audit process?**
Ideally, the audit should be conducted by an internal audit committee or a dedicated internal auditor who reports directly to the Board of Directors, rather than the Executive Director, to ensure independence.

**3. What happens if the audit reveals a significant discrepancy?**
Immediate disclosure is the best policy. Prepare a corrective action plan (CAP) that outlines the root cause of the error, the steps taken to fix it, and the new controls implemented to prevent recurrence. If funds were misappropriated, legal counsel should be consulted immediately.
