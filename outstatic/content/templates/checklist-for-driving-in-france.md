# Standard Operating Procedure: Driving in France

Driving in France requires strict adherence to local regulations and an understanding of unique European road etiquette. This SOP provides a comprehensive guide for drivers—whether operating a personal or rental vehicle—to ensure compliance with French traffic laws (*Code de la route*), prioritize safety, and navigate the French road network efficiently. Failure to adhere to these standards can result in significant on-the-spot fines and impoundment of the vehicle.

## 1. Pre-Departure Compliance & Documentation
Before entering the driver’s seat, ensure all legal requirements are met to avoid administrative penalties.

*   **Driver’s License:** Carry a valid, full driver’s license. Non-EU residents should carry an International Driving Permit (IDP) alongside their domestic license.
*   **Registration & Insurance:** Ensure the vehicle’s registration document (*carte grise*) and valid proof of insurance are physically present in the vehicle.
*   **Rental Documentation:** Keep the rental agreement and emergency contact number for the rental agency in the glove compartment.
*   **Proof of Identity:** Carry a valid passport or national ID card, as police may request this during routine traffic stops.

## 2. Mandatory On-Board Equipment
French law dictates specific safety items must be present and accessible in the cabin at all times.

*   **Warning Triangle:** A red reflective triangle must be stored in the boot for use in the event of a breakdown.
*   **Reflective Safety Vests:** A high-visibility vest for every occupant must be stored inside the cabin (not the boot) to be worn immediately upon exiting the vehicle during an emergency.
*   **Crit'Air Sticker:** If driving in major cities (e.g., Paris, Lyon, Bordeaux), ensure the mandatory "Clean Air" sticker is displayed on the windshield, indicating the vehicle’s emission category.
*   **Breathalyzer:** While the legal requirement to carry a disposable breathalyzer remains on the books, enforcement is currently low; however, carrying one is still recommended as a safety precaution.
*   **Headlamp Beam Deflectors:** If driving a right-hand drive vehicle (from the UK/Ireland), apply beam deflectors to prevent blinding oncoming French drivers.

## 3. Rules of the Road & Etiquette
Understanding the *Code de la route* is essential for smooth navigation and accident prevention.

*   **Priority to the Right (*Priorité à droite*):** Unless otherwise marked with yellow diamond signs, you must give way to traffic entering from the right at intersections. This rule is strictly enforced in urban areas.
*   **Speed Limits:** 
    *   **Motorways (*Autoroutes*):** 130 km/h (reduced to 110 km/h in rain).
    *   **Dual Carriageways:** 110 km/h.
    *   **Main Roads:** 80 km/h.
    *   **Urban Areas:** 50 km/h (often reduced to 30 km/h in city centers).
*   **Overtaking:** Always overtake on the left. Stay in the right-hand lane on motorways unless passing.
*   **Mobile Devices:** Using a phone while driving is strictly prohibited, including the use of Bluetooth earpieces/headphones. Only integrated hands-free systems are permitted.
*   **Tolls (*Péages*):** Most motorways are toll-funded. Prepare your credit card or cash. Note that lanes marked with a green 't' are for automated electronic tag holders only; do not enter these unless equipped.

## 4. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - Radar Detectors:** It is illegal to use GPS/navigation software that warns of speed cameras. Ensure your GPS settings have "Speed Camera Alerts" disabled while in France.
*   **Pro Tip - Roundabouts:** Traffic already inside the roundabout has priority. Always indicate when exiting.
*   **Pitfall - Drinking and Driving:** France has a strict limit of 0.05% BAC. If you have consumed even one glass of wine, it is safer to avoid driving.
*   **Pro Tip - Fueling:** Fuel stations in rural areas or during holidays may be automated and require a "Chip & PIN" credit card. Keep your tank at least half-full if traveling through remote regions.

## 5. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I use my home country's GPS for navigation?**
A: Yes, but you must ensure that features displaying the location of speed cameras are deactivated, as these are illegal in France and can result in heavy fines.

**Q: What should I do if I have an accident?**
A: If the accident is minor, fill out the *constat amiable* (European Accident Statement) provided by your rental agency. If there are injuries, move to a safe area, dial 112 (the European emergency number), and wait for the police.

**Q: Are there any specific rules for driving in Paris?**
A: Yes. Paris has strict Low Emission Zone (LEZ) rules. You must have a Crit'Air sticker, and parking is notoriously difficult and expensive. It is highly recommended to park on the outskirts and use the Metro for city center transit.
