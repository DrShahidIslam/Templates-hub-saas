# Standard Operating Procedure: Intensive Care Unit (ICU) Patient Management

The Intensive Care Unit (ICU) is a high-acuity environment requiring rigorous adherence to clinical protocols to ensure patient safety and optimal outcomes. This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the essential workflow for the bedside management of critically ill patients, encompassing systematic assessment, monitoring, and interdisciplinary communication. Adherence to these steps is mandatory for all clinical staff to maintain standardized care and mitigate risks associated with hemodynamic instability and multi-organ failure.

## 1. Initial Assessment and Handoff
*   **Receive Handoff:** Obtain a comprehensive verbal report from the transferring unit using the SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) framework.
*   **Verify Identification:** Confirm patient identity using two identifiers (full name and date of birth) against the patient’s ID bracelet and EMR.
*   **Rapid Primary Survey (ABCDE):**
    *   **Airway:** Verify patency of ET tube or tracheostomy; confirm position (cm marking at teeth).
    *   **Breathing:** Assess bilateral breath sounds, ventilator settings, and SpO2.
    *   **Circulation:** Check heart rate, rhythm, and peripheral pulses; ensure invasive lines are functioning.
    *   **Disability:** Assess GCS (Glasgow Coma Scale) and sedation levels (RASS).
    *   **Exposure:** Conduct a thorough skin assessment for pressure injuries, rashes, or surgical sites.

## 2. Equipment and Safety Check
*   **Ventilator Safety:** Ensure manual resuscitation bag (Ambu bag) is present at the bedside, connected to oxygen, and functional.
*   **Monitor Alarms:** Customize alarm parameters (HR, SpO2, BP, RR) based on patient acuity and physician orders.
*   **Invasive Lines:** Label all IV lines, arterial lines, and central venous catheters; ensure tubing is flush and free of air bubbles.
*   **Emergency Trolley:** Confirm the "Crash Cart" location and verify the presence of specialized equipment (e.g., suction, defibrillator, airway kit).

## 3. Daily Clinical Rounds and Care
*   **Multi-disciplinary Rounds:** Attend daily rounds with the intensivist, pharmacist, respiratory therapist, and nutritionist to define the "Goal of the Day."
*   **Medication Reconciliation:** Review all continuous infusions (vasopressors, sedation, analgesia) for titration accuracy and appropriateness.
*   **Sedation Vacation:** Implement daily sedation interruption (DSI) for ventilated patients, as indicated by clinical status.
*   **Skin and Mouth Care:** Perform oral care (chlorhexidine) every 4 hours to prevent Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP) and perform full skin turn/pressure relief every 2 hours.
*   **Nutrition:** Verify that enteral or parenteral nutrition orders are being followed and assess gastric residuals if required by facility policy.

## 4. Documentation and Discharge Planning
*   **Flowsheet Integrity:** Ensure real-time documentation of vitals, fluid balance (I/Os), and medication administration.
*   **Communication:** Update family members daily regarding the patient's progress and changes in the plan of care.
*   **Transfer Readiness:** Maintain an updated transfer checklist once the patient no longer meets ICU criteria to facilitate timely step-down.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The "Check-Back" Rule):** Always repeat back verbal orders to the physician to ensure accuracy, especially during high-stress code situations.
*   **Pro Tip (Bundle Compliance):** Consistently use "Care Bundles" (e.g., VAP bundle, CLABSI bundle) as they are the gold standard for reducing preventable hospital-acquired infections.
*   **Pitfall (Alarm Fatigue):** Never silence an alarm without investigating the patient first. Always check the patient before you check the monitor.
*   **Pitfall (Documentation Lag):** Avoid "batch charting" at the end of the shift. In the ICU, physiological status changes rapidly; documenting in real-time is a legal and clinical necessity.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should I check the ventilator settings against the physician’s orders?**
A: Ventilator settings should be verified at the start of every shift, after any patient repositioning, and immediately following any change in clinical status.

**Q: What is the priority if an invasive line (e.g., Central Line) becomes dislodged?**
A: Apply immediate firm, direct pressure to the site to prevent hemorrhage or air embolism, call for assistance, and notify the attending physician immediately.

**Q: How should I handle a discrepancy between the monitor and the patient's physical presentation?**
A: Always treat the patient, not the monitor. If the monitor indicates a catastrophic event but the patient appears stable, assess for lead displacement or equipment malfunction before escalating, but never ignore a potential crisis.
