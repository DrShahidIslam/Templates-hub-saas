# Standard Operating Procedure: Maintenance of Dry-Type Transformers

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection and maintenance protocols for dry-type transformers to ensure optimal operational efficiency, safety, and longevity. Dry-type transformers, while low-maintenance compared to oil-filled units, are susceptible to dust accumulation, moisture ingress, and thermal stress. Regular maintenance is critical to preventing insulation degradation, localized overheating, and catastrophic electrical failure. This procedure must be performed by qualified electrical personnel following all Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) and arc flash safety requirements.

## 1. Safety and Preparation Checklist
*   **LOTO Verification:** Ensure the transformer is fully de-energized, isolated from both primary and secondary circuits, and grounded according to facility safety standards.
*   **Arc Flash PPE:** Utilize appropriate PPE (Level 2 or higher, depending on the fault current) during initial inspection.
*   **Work Area Clearance:** Verify that all flammable materials and debris are cleared from the transformer enclosure area.
*   **Equipment Staging:** Prepare a calibrated torque wrench, clean dry rags, lint-free cloths, a soft-bristled brush, a vacuum cleaner with a HEPA filter, and an infrared thermal imager (for post-maintenance testing).

## 2. External and Enclosure Inspection
*   **Ventilation Path Check:** Inspect all louvers and cooling vents for blockages. Remove cobwebs, dust mats, or nesting materials that obstruct airflow.
*   **Enclosure Integrity:** Check the cabinet for signs of rust, corrosion, or physical damage. Verify that door gaskets are intact to prevent moisture/vermin ingress.
*   **Foundation and Mounting:** Inspect floor bolts for tightness and check for signs of cracked concrete or loose vibration isolators.
*   **Warning Labels:** Ensure that "High Voltage" and "Danger" warning placards are legible and securely attached.

## 3. Internal and Coil Cleaning
*   **Surface Dust Removal:** Use a HEPA-filtered vacuum to remove loose dust from the top of the coils, barriers, and support insulators.
*   **Insulator Cleaning:** Wipe down porcelain or epoxy insulators with a lint-free cloth. If heavy contamination exists, use a manufacturer-approved non-conductive solvent.
*   **Cooling Duct Inspection:** Inspect vertical cooling ducts between coils. Ensure there are no foreign objects lodged in the spacers.
*   **Evidence of Tracking:** Examine coil surfaces for "tracking" (carbon paths) or white powdery deposits, which indicate corona discharge or insulation degradation.

## 4. Electrical and Mechanical Connections
*   **Torque Verification:** Apply a calibrated torque wrench to all bolted electrical connections (HV and LV terminals, neutral, and ground). Refer to the manufacturer's torque table for specific values.
*   **Visual Thermal Inspection:** Inspect cable lugs for signs of discoloration or "heat tinting," which are early indicators of loose connections.
*   **Grounding Continuity:** Verify the integrity of the main grounding lug and ensure the ground cable is free of fraying or corrosion.
*   **Tap Changer Check:** If applicable, verify the tap changer is locked in the correct position. Do not operate the tap changer under load.

## 5. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Avoid Compressed Air:** Never use high-pressure compressed air to clean a transformer. It forces conductive dust into the deep windings where it cannot be removed, potentially causing an internal arc flash.
*   **The "Lint-Free" Rule:** Always use lint-free cloths. Cotton or paper towels can leave fibers behind, which act as a bridge for moisture and dust, leading to tracking.
*   **Document Temperature:** If possible, take an infrared thermal scan under load *before* shutting down for maintenance. This identifies "hot spots" that may not be visible during a cold inspection.
*   **Humidity Warning:** Avoid performing open-enclosure maintenance on days with high humidity (above 60%) to prevent moisture absorption by the dry-type insulation.

## 6. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform this maintenance?**
A: Typically, dry-type transformers should undergo a visual inspection every 6 months and a full cleaning/torquing procedure annually. In high-dust or industrial chemical environments, quarterly maintenance is recommended.

**Q: Can I use electrical contact cleaner on the coils?**
A: Only if the product is explicitly rated for the specific resin or epoxy insulation used by your transformer manufacturer. Many generic contact cleaners contain solvents that can degrade insulation coatings.

**Q: What do I do if I find "tracking" on the coil surface?**
A: Surface tracking is a sign of dielectric breakdown. Light carbon deposits can sometimes be sanded off with ultra-fine abrasive, but deep tracking usually indicates the need for a professional diagnostic test (e.g., TTR, Megger/Insulation Resistance test) or replacement of the unit. Consult an expert before attempting to energize.
