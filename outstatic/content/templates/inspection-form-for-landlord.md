# Standard Operating Procedure: Rental Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a standardized framework for conducting comprehensive rental property inspections. Consistent documentation is essential for protecting the landlord’s asset, ensuring tenant compliance with lease terms, and maintaining a clear historical record of the property’s condition. This procedure is designed to be used during move-in, periodic (routine) check-ups, and move-out transitions to minimize liability and maximize property value.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Safety
*   **Documentation Retrieval:** Review the signed lease agreement and the previous inspection report to identify specific areas of concern or pre-existing damage.
*   **Safety Equipment:** Ensure the inspector is equipped with necessary safety gear, including flashlight, multi-tool, and protective footwear.
*   **Legal Compliance:** Provide the tenant with the legally required notice of entry (typically 24–48 hours) as per local jurisdiction.
*   **Tools:** Bring a smartphone or camera for timestamped documentation, a moisture meter, and a draft of the inspection form.

## Phase 2: Interior Systems and Utilities
*   **HVAC:** Test thermostat responsiveness. Check for unusual noises, odors, or debris around the intake vents.
*   **Plumbing:** Operate all faucets, check under sinks for active leaks or signs of water damage (warped cabinetry/mold), and flush toilets to ensure proper seal and suction.
*   **Electrical:** Test GFCI outlets in kitchens and bathrooms. Verify that all light switches function and ensure no outlets show signs of scorch marks.
*   **Safety Devices:** Verify all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors are operational by pressing the "Test" button. Check expiration dates.

## Phase 3: Room-by-Room Physical Condition
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for stains, tears in carpet, deep scratches in hardwood, or cracked tiles.
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Document nail holes, scuffs, water stains, or cracks. Differentiate between structural cracks and drywall settlement.
*   **Windows and Doors:** Ensure all windows lock securely and screens are intact. Verify that doors close/latch without friction and that locks are functional.
*   **Cabinetry:** Check drawers and cabinet doors for proper alignment and smooth hinge operation.

## Phase 4: Exterior and Curb Appeal
*   **Structural Integrity:** Visually inspect the roofline (where accessible) and siding for missing pieces or signs of pests.
*   **Landscaping:** Confirm that the tenant is maintaining the lawn, removing weeds, and keeping gutters clear of major debris.
*   **Pest Control:** Check for signs of insect or rodent activity around entry points or in dark storage areas.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Digital Timestamping):** Use an inspection app that automatically embeds the date and time into photos. This is the gold standard for evidence in small claims court.
*   **Pro Tip (The "Hidden" Spots):** Never skip the back of the refrigerator, inside the oven, and the top of ceiling fan blades—these are the most common areas where excessive grease/dust accumulation is overlooked.
*   **Pitfall (Subjectivity):** Avoid vague notes like "looks bad." Use specific, descriptive language such as: "12-inch circular water stain on living room ceiling, yellowed, dry to the touch."
*   **Pitfall (Rushing):** Never conduct an inspection while the tenant is actively distracting you. Schedule a time when you have the space to document the property thoroughly.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should I perform routine inspections?**
A: A biannual (every 6 months) schedule is standard. This allows you to check for "silent" issues like slow plumbing leaks before they cause major structural damage, without feeling intrusive to the tenant.

**Q: Am I legally required to have the tenant present during the inspection?**
A: Generally, no. As long as you have provided proper legal notice, you are entitled to enter the property. However, having the tenant present can be useful if you wish to discuss maintenance issues in real-time.

**Q: What do I do if I find damage caused by the tenant?**
A: Document it clearly with photos. Send a formal written notice to the tenant stating the specific damage, referencing the relevant lease clause, and setting a reasonable deadline for them to remedy the issue or be billed for the repair.
