# Standard Operating Procedure: Kitchen Equipment Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for the routine inspection, cleaning, and maintenance of commercial kitchen equipment. The objective of this procedure is to extend the operational lifespan of high-value assets, ensure compliance with health and safety regulations, mitigate the risk of fire hazards, and prevent costly downtime. Adherence to this checklist is required for all designated kitchen staff and facility maintenance personnel to maintain a safe and efficient culinary environment.

## Refrigeration Units (Walk-ins, Reach-ins, Prep Tables)
*   **Condenser Coils:** Clean dust, grease, and debris from condenser coils using a soft brush or vacuum to ensure optimal heat exchange.
*   **Gaskets:** Inspect door seals (gaskets) for tears, cracks, or improper suction. Test the "dollar bill" test to ensure a tight seal.
*   **Drain Lines:** Flush evaporator drain lines to prevent blockages, mold growth, and water backup.
*   **Temperature Calibration:** Verify that internal temperatures match digital readouts; check if the unit cycles correctly without excessive icing on evaporator coils.
*   **Door Hinges:** Lubricate hinges and ensure doors self-close properly to prevent air leaks.

## Cooking Equipment (Ranges, Griddles, Fryers, Ovens)
*   **Burners & Pilot Lights:** Inspect gas burners for debris. Ensure pilot lights are burning blue; yellow flames indicate a need for professional air-mixture adjustment.
*   **Fryer Boiling Out:** Perform a full "boil out" of deep fryers to remove carbonized oil and sediment buildup from the tank walls.
*   **Oven Calibration:** Check oven thermostat accuracy using a secondary, independent probe thermometer.
*   **Ventilation Interlocks:** Ensure the Ansul system links and hood fans are engaging correctly when cooking equipment is powered on.
*   **Gas Hoses:** Check flexible gas connectors for kinks, cracks, or signs of wear. Ensure the safety restraint cable is attached.

## Dishwashing & Sanitation Systems
*   **Spray Arms:** Remove and clear debris from dish machine spray nozzles to ensure consistent water pressure and cleaning coverage.
*   **Chemical Levels:** Verify that detergent, sanitizer, and rinse aid dispensers are calibrated and full.
*   **Lime/Scale Removal:** Inspect heating elements for mineral buildup; perform a de-liming cycle if scale is visible.
*   **Strainers & Filters:** Remove and clean the scrap screens and pump filters daily to prevent debris from entering the pump assembly.

## Electrical & Safety Components
*   **Cord Integrity:** Inspect all power cords for exposed wiring, fraying, or damaged insulation.
*   **Emergency Stops:** Verify that all E-stop buttons are accessible and functional.
*   **GFCI Outlets:** Test all Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter outlets near wet areas to ensure they trip correctly.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Log Everything:** Maintain a digital or physical maintenance logbook. Tracking the date of service and the technician who performed it is critical for warranty claims and health department audits.
*   **Pro Tip: Inventory Spare Parts:** Keep common wear-and-tear items on-site, such as replacement gaskets, pilot thermocouples, and grease filters, to minimize downtime during minor failures.
*   **Pitfall: Over-Cleaning:** Avoid using abrasive steel wool or harsh chemicals on stainless steel surfaces, as these can cause "pitting" and eventually rust. Always use manufacturer-recommended cleaners.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Leaks:** A minor leak in a faucet or refrigeration line is rarely an isolated issue; it is usually a precursor to a component failure that could lead to water damage or compressor burnout.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should preventive maintenance be performed?**
Routine visual inspections should be daily, while deep maintenance (cleaning coils, de-liming, professional calibration) should be performed quarterly at a minimum. Heavy-use kitchens may require monthly intervals.

**2. Should I attempt to repair gas equipment myself?**
No. While you can perform basic cleaning and inspection, any repair involving gas lines, internal burners, or electrical circuitry should be handled by a certified, licensed technician to ensure safety and code compliance.

**3. Why is my refrigerator ice-cold but the product is spoiling?**
This is often caused by poor airflow. Ensure that product is not stacked against the rear wall or blocking the evaporator fans, as this prevents the cold air from circulating effectively throughout the cabinet.
