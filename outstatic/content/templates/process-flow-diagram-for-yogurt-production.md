# Standard Operating Procedure: Yogurt Production Process Flow

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized industrial workflow for the production of high-quality yogurt. The objective is to maintain consistent texture, acidity, and microbial safety while ensuring operational efficiency from raw milk intake to final packaging. Adherence to these protocols is critical for regulatory compliance (HACCP/FDA/ISO) and the preservation of product shelf-life.

## Section 1: Raw Material Preparation and Standardization
*   **Milk Reception:** Verify temperature (≤ 4°C) and conduct antibiotic residue testing.
*   **Clarification/Separation:** Pass raw milk through a centrifugal separator to remove impurities and standardize fat content to the target percentage (e.g., 0%, 2%, or 3.5%).
*   **Formulation:** Add sugar, stabilizers, or milk solids (non-fat dry milk) to reach desired viscosity and total solids content.

## Section 2: Thermal Processing and Homogenization
*   **Pre-heating:** Heat milk to 60°C to prepare for homogenization.
*   **Homogenization:** Pass mixture through a high-pressure homogenizer (2,000–2,500 psi) to prevent cream layer separation and ensure a smooth, creamy mouthfeel.
*   **Pasteurization:** Heat the mixture to 85°C–90°C for 5–10 minutes. This denatures whey proteins and creates an inhospitable environment for competing spoilage bacteria.

## Section 3: Inoculation and Fermentation
*   **Cooling:** Rapidly cool the mixture to the inoculation temperature (42°C–45°C).
*   **Inoculation:** Add the specific yogurt starter culture (*Lactobacillus bulgaricus* and *Streptococcus thermophilus*) under sterile conditions.
*   **Incubation:** Hold in a fermentation tank at a steady 42°C–45°C until the target pH (usually 4.5–4.6) is achieved.
*   **Cooling/Breaking:** Once the pH goal is met, cool the yogurt to 20°C to arrest fermentation and prevent over-acidification.

## Section 4: Post-Processing and Packaging
*   **Flavoring/Fruit Inclusion:** If producing flavored yogurt, add fruit preps or flavorings via a static mixer or dosing pump.
*   **Packaging:** Fill sanitized containers in a HEPA-filtered environment to prevent post-process contamination.
*   **Cold Storage:** Move product immediately to a blast chiller, then transfer to a refrigerated warehouse (≤ 5°C) to firm up the gel structure before shipping.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - Temperature Fluctuations:** Even a minor drop in incubation temperature will lead to extended fermentation times, resulting in a grainy, acidic product. Ensure PID controllers on fermentation tanks are calibrated monthly.
*   **Pro Tip - Whey Syneresis:** To prevent the "watery" layer on top (syneresis), ensure proper hydration of stabilizers and avoid mechanical agitation after the yogurt has begun to set.
*   **Pitfall - Bacteriophage Contamination:** Yogurt starters are sensitive to phage. If fermentation times suddenly increase, sanitize the entire line immediately; the starter strain may have been compromised by viral interference.
*   **Pro Tip - pH Monitoring:** Use calibrated online pH probes rather than just timing the fermentation process; milk protein quality varies seasonally, affecting the rate of acidification.

## FAQ
**Q: How do I know if my yogurt is too acidic?**
A: If the pH drops below 4.4, the yogurt may taste excessively sour and experience higher levels of whey separation. Always stop fermentation at 4.55 to allow for a slight pH drop during the cooling phase.

**Q: Can I re-use the yogurt from the previous batch as a starter?**
A: No. Commercial production requires high-count, standardized lyophilized or frozen cultures to ensure a consistent bacterial balance. Using "back-sloping" introduces a high risk of microbial contamination and texture failure.

**Q: Why does my yogurt appear grainy?**
A: Graininess is usually a sign of improper homogenization or a temperature spike during the heating phase that caused localized protein burning. Check your homogenizer seals and pasteurizer pressure valves.
