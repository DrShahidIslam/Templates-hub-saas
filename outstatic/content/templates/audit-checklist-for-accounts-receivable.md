# Standard Operating Procedure: Accounts Receivable (AR) Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the formal process for conducting a comprehensive audit of the Accounts Receivable function. The objective is to verify the accuracy, existence, and collectability of trade receivables, ensure adherence to internal control policies, and validate that revenue recognition complies with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) or IFRS. This audit serves to identify potential bad debt risks, process inefficiencies, and fraudulent activities within the revenue cycle.

## Phase 1: Revenue Recognition and Invoicing Verification
*   **Sample Testing:** Select a statistically significant sample of invoices from the audit period and trace them back to underlying shipping documents, service logs, or signed contracts.
*   **Cut-off Testing:** Verify that revenue is recorded in the correct accounting period by reviewing shipping documents dated immediately before and after the period-end.
*   **Mathematical Accuracy:** Re-calculate invoice totals (quantity x price) and verify that applicable taxes, discounts, and freight charges are correctly applied.
*   **Authorization Check:** Ensure all credit memos and price adjustments are backed by authorized documentation and have been approved by management.

## Phase 2: Accounts Receivable Ledger and Aging Analysis
*   **Sub-ledger Reconciliation:** Agree the AR sub-ledger balance to the General Ledger (GL) control account. Investigate any discrepancies greater than zero.
*   **Aging Accuracy:** Review the Accounts Receivable Aging Report. Verify that the "days past due" for all balances are categorized correctly based on invoice dates.
*   **Credit Balance Review:** Analyze accounts with negative balances (credit balances). Determine if these are due to overpayments, returns, or potential data entry errors.
*   **Concentration Risk:** Identify any single customer who accounts for more than 5-10% of total receivables to assess exposure to insolvency.

## Phase 3: Cash Receipts and Collection Controls
*   **Cash Application:** Trace a sample of cash receipts back to bank deposit slips and bank statements to ensure timely and accurate recording.
*   **Segregation of Duties:** Confirm that employees responsible for recording receivables do not have access to incoming cash or bank reconciliation functions.
*   **Collection Efforts:** Review documentation of collection calls, emails, and payment plans for accounts overdue by 60+ days.
*   **Write-off Procedures:** Review all accounts written off during the period. Ensure formal write-off requests are approved by authorized personnel and supported by evidence of collection attempts.

## Phase 4: Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (ADA)
*   **Methodology Review:** Evaluate the logic behind the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts calculation. Ensure the methodology is consistent with historical bad debt trends.
*   **Provision Adequacy:** Assess whether the ADA balance is sufficient to cover high-risk accounts identified in the aging report.
*   **Subsequent Events:** Review post-audit-period cash receipts to determine if balances previously considered "uncollectible" were recovered.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Utilize Data Analytics:** Use software to run a "duplicate payment" report or identify invoices generated on weekends/holidays to flag potential anomalies.
*   **External Confirmations:** If material balances exist, perform a direct confirmation process by mailing or emailing statements to major customers to verify their outstanding balances.
*   **Trend Analysis:** Compare the current year’s Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) against the previous year. A significant increase may indicate collection issues or aggressive sales tactics.

### Common Pitfalls
*   **Ignoring Small Write-offs:** Auditors often overlook small write-offs, but a high volume of small, unauthorized write-offs can be a indicator of employee fraud.
*   **Static ADA:** Failing to adjust the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts during economic downturns is a common regulatory red flag.
*   **Poor Documentation:** Assuming that a "clean" sub-ledger means the AR process is healthy; always focus on the existence of the source documents.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should an AR audit be conducted?**
A: Internal audits should occur at least annually. However, high-volume businesses with high-risk customer bases should perform quarterly "mini-audits" focusing on aging reports and cash applications.

**Q: What is the most common reason for a discrepancy between the Sub-ledger and the General Ledger?**
A: The most frequent cause is timing differences, such as cash receipts posted to the sub-ledger that have not yet been reflected in the bank/GL, or manual journal entries made directly to the GL that bypass the sub-ledger.

**Q: Should I verify every single account during the audit?**
A: In most cases, a risk-based sampling approach is sufficient. Focus your testing on high-value accounts, accounts with long-standing overdue balances, and accounts with frequent credit memos. Only use 100% testing if the internal control environment is deemed weak or high-risk.
