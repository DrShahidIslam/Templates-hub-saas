# Standard Operating Procedure: Air Compressor Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection protocols for industrial air compressors to ensure operational safety, peak efficiency, and the longevity of mechanical components. Regular inspections are critical to identifying potential failure points—such as oil leaks, belt degradation, or pressure buildup—before they result in costly downtime or catastrophic system failure. All technicians must strictly adhere to Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures when performing internal inspections or maintenance.

## Pre-Inspection Safety Requirements
*   Ensure the compressor is powered down and the LOTO device is applied.
*   Verify the system is fully depressurized via the manual blow-down valve.
*   Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, hearing protection, and gloves.

## Section 1: Exterior and Environmental Assessment
*   **Ambient Conditions:** Inspect the area for excessive dust, debris, or moisture buildup that could obstruct intake filters or ventilation.
*   **Vibration Analysis:** Check the unit's base bolts and anchor points for signs of loosening or fatigue.
*   **External Leakage:** Visually inspect the unit for pools of oil or coolant under the crankcase or cooler assembly.
*   **Labels and Signage:** Confirm that all safety warnings, pressure ratings, and maintenance stickers remain legible.

## Section 2: Fluid and Mechanical Systems
*   **Oil Levels:** Check the sight glass or dipstick; ensure the oil is at the manufacturer-recommended level and is not milky or discolored (indicating moisture contamination).
*   **Belt Tension:** Apply light pressure to belts; there should be no more than 1/2 inch of deflection. Inspect for glazing, cracks, or fraying.
*   **Air Intake Filters:** Remove the filter housing and inspect the element. If heavy dust or debris is present, replace or clean the filter according to manufacturer specs.
*   **Drive Coupling:** Inspect the coupling for alignment issues, excessive heat discoloration, or rubber bushing degradation.

## Section 3: Pneumatic and Electrical Components
*   **Hoses and Fittings:** Check all discharge lines for cracks, bubbles, or weeping at the connections.
*   **Condensate Drain:** Manually cycle the automatic drain valve to ensure it is not clogged and is effectively expelling moisture from the receiver tank.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Inspect control panels and motor wiring terminals for charred insulation or loose connections (use thermal imaging if available).
*   **Pressure Relief Valve:** Gently pull the test ring on the safety relief valve to ensure it is not seized (caution: ensure system is depressurized).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain an "Equipment Logbook" attached to the compressor to document every inspection date, oil change, and observed vibration levels. This data creates a baseline that makes identifying "drift" in performance much easier.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an ultrasonic leak detector to find "silent" air leaks in piping that may not be audible over background shop noise.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore "small" oil leaks. Air compressors operate under high heat; even minor oil leaks can lead to a fire hazard or internal damage due to low lubrication levels.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not over-tighten belts. Excessive tension places undue stress on motor bearings and pump shafts, leading to premature bearing failure.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should a daily inspection be performed?**
A basic "walk-around" inspection should be performed at the start of every shift. A deep-dive inspection (following this SOP) is recommended monthly, or every 250 operational hours.

**2. What does milky oil indicate in my air compressor?**
Milky oil is a clear indicator of moisture contamination. This usually happens when the compressor is operated for short intervals, preventing it from reaching the operating temperature required to evaporate condensation.

**3. Why is my safety relief valve "popping" during normal operation?**
This is a critical warning sign that your pressure switch or unloader valve is failing to cut off the motor when the tank reaches maximum pressure. If this occurs, shut the unit down immediately and inspect the control circuit.
