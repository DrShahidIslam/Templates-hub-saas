# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential HVAC Preventative Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory technical and safety protocols for conducting a comprehensive preventative maintenance (PM) service on residential HVAC systems. The objective of this procedure is to ensure system longevity, peak operational efficiency, verified safety, and improved indoor air quality. Technicians are expected to adhere to these steps strictly to minimize emergency service calls and maximize equipment lifespan.

## Phase 1: Pre-Service Safety & System Diagnostics
*   **Safety Check:** Confirm power is isolated before inspecting electrical components. Ensure the technician is wearing appropriate PPE (safety glasses, gloves, and non-conductive footwear).
*   **Operational Baseline:** Turn the thermostat to "Cool" or "Heat" mode. Verify the system starts, check for unusual noises, and record baseline temperature splits (Supply vs. Return).
*   **Thermostat Verification:** Ensure the thermostat is level, calibrated, and the digital display is functioning correctly.

## Phase 2: Indoor Unit (Air Handler/Furnace)
*   **Filter Inspection:** Inspect air filters; replace with high-quality, correct-sized filters. Document the MERV rating.
*   **Blower Assembly:** Clean the blower wheel to remove dust accumulation, which ensures proper airflow. Check the motor for signs of overheating.
*   **Evaporator Coil:** Inspect the coil for debris or biological growth. Clean if visible fouling is present.
*   **Condensate Management:** Flush the condensate drain line with an approved algaecide to prevent clogs. Verify the operation of the float switch.
*   **Heat Exchanger (Furnace):** Perform a visual inspection for cracks, rust, or soot buildup. Verify burner ignition and flame color (should be steady blue).

## Phase 3: Outdoor Unit (Condenser)
*   **Coil Cleaning:** Remove exterior debris from the condenser fins. Use a coil-cleaning solution to remove organic matter, ensuring unrestricted airflow.
*   **Contactors & Relays:** Inspect the contactor for pitting or burned points. Vacuum out the control box to remove insect debris.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Tighten all high-voltage and low-voltage electrical connections. Verify wire insulation is intact.
*   **Capacitor Testing:** Measure the microfarad (MFD) rating of the run capacitor. Replace if the reading is +/- 10% of the manufacturer’s rating.
*   **Refrigerant System:** Check for visible oil spots on lines, which indicate a potential refrigerant leak. Record suction and liquid line pressures (if applicable by company policy).

## Phase 4: Post-Service Verification
*   **System Re-test:** Re-start the system and monitor for a full five-minute cycle.
*   **Final Documentation:** Complete the digital service report, including the delta-T (temperature difference) reading and the "passed/failed" status of all critical components.
*   **Client Briefing:** Review maintenance findings with the homeowner and provide recommendations for any required repairs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pitfall - Over-Cleaning:** Avoid using high-pressure sprayers on delicate aluminum fins, as this can flatten them and permanently restrict airflow. Use a fin comb to repair any accidental damage.
*   **Pro Tip - Temperature Delta:** Always measure temperature split at the closest register and the return air grill. A properly functioning system should typically produce a 15°F to 20°F split.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Vibration:** Never ignore slight motor vibrations; these are often the precursor to premature bearing failure.
*   **Pro Tip - Photography:** Take photos of dirty coils or worn components. Providing visual proof of issues significantly increases the conversion rate for necessary repair recommendations.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should residential HVAC maintenance be performed?**
A: Industry standards recommend twice annually—once in the spring for the cooling season and once in the fall for the heating season—to account for seasonal wear and tear.

**Q: Why is a clean condenser coil so important?**
A: A dirty coil acts as a thermal insulator, forcing the compressor to work harder, which leads to higher electricity bills, decreased cooling capacity, and potential compressor burnout.

**Q: If my system is running fine, is maintenance still necessary?**
A: Yes. Many HVAC issues (such as refrigerant leaks or capacitor degradation) are "silent." Preventative maintenance catches these minor issues before they manifest as costly system failures during peak temperature weeks.
