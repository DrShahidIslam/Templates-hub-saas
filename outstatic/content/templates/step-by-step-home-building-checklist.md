# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Construction Management

This document serves as the master operational framework for overseeing the lifecycle of a residential home build. Adhering to this structured approach ensures regulatory compliance, fiscal discipline, and the maintenance of high-quality craftsmanship throughout the project. The following protocol is designed to mitigate risk, manage sub-contractor timelines, and ensure that every phase of construction passes inspection before advancing to the next.

## Phase 1: Pre-Construction & Entitlements
*   **Site Survey & Geotechnical Report:** Secure a professional survey and soil test to determine foundation requirements.
*   **Architectural Blueprints:** Finalize structural engineering, floor plans, and elevation drawings.
*   **Permitting:** Submit all plans to the local building department; obtain necessary zoning, septic/sewer, and building permits.
*   **Budget & Financing:** Secure construction loan; establish a 10-15% contingency fund for unforeseen site conditions.
*   **Utility Coordination:** Apply for temporary power, water, and gas hookups for the job site.

## Phase 2: Site Prep & Foundation
*   **Site Clearing:** Excavate topsoil, remove vegetation, and install silt fencing for erosion control.
*   **Layout:** Stake out the building footprint using professional grade lasers.
*   **Excavation:** Dig footings to the depth specified by the structural engineer.
*   **Rough-in Plumbing (Sub-slab):** Install sewer lines and main water entry points before pouring concrete.
*   **Inspection:** Schedule foundation footing and sub-slab plumbing inspections.
*   **Concrete Pour:** Pour footings and slab; monitor curing time according to weather conditions.

## Phase 3: Framing & Exterior Envelope
*   **Framing:** Erect wall studs, joists, and roof trusses.
*   **Sheathing:** Install plywood or OSB on walls and roof for structural rigidity.
*   **Roofing:** Install felt/underlayment and finish roofing material (shingles/metal).
*   **Windows & Exterior Doors:** Install windows and doors to "dry-in" the structure.
*   **House Wrap:** Apply moisture-resistant barrier to protect against exterior elements.

## Phase 4: MEP Rough-Ins & Interior Closures
*   **MEP Rough-in:** Mechanical (HVAC ducts), Electrical (wiring/boxes), and Plumbing (supply/drain lines) installation.
*   **Insulation:** Install thermal insulation (batts, spray foam, or blown-in) in walls and attic.
*   **Inspections:** Complete structural, electrical, and plumbing rough-in inspections.
*   **Drywall:** Hang, tape, mud, and sand all interior wall surfaces.

## Phase 5: Interior Finishes & Final Completion
*   **Trim & Millwork:** Install baseboards, door casings, and window trim.
*   **Painting:** Prime and paint interior walls, ceilings, and trim.
*   **Cabinetry & Countertops:** Install kitchen and bathroom cabinetry and stone/laminate surfaces.
*   **Flooring:** Lay hardwood, tile, or carpet throughout the home.
*   **Final Trim-out:** Install light fixtures, plumbing faucets, and HVAC registers.
*   **Final Inspection:** Obtain a Certificate of Occupancy (CO) from the building department.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The Contingency Trap:** Never skip the 10-15% contingency fund. Site excavation often reveals hidden groundwater or ledge rock that can deplete your budget early.
*   **Sequential Ordering:** Do not order custom finishes (tile, appliances) until the structure is fully "dried-in" to prevent theft and moisture damage.
*   **Paper Trail:** Document all change orders in writing with signed cost-impact statements. Verbal agreements are the primary source of litigation in residential construction.
*   **Site Cleanliness:** A clean job site is a safe job site. Mandate weekly waste removal to prevent injuries and material damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the most critical inspection during the build?**
A: The "Framing/Rough-in" inspection is arguably the most critical, as it confirms the structural integrity of the home before the walls are closed up with drywall, making future corrections extremely expensive.

**Q: How do I manage sub-contractor scheduling effectively?**
A: Utilize a "Critical Path Method" schedule. Create a digital project board where each trade knows their start date and how their delay impacts the trades that follow them.

**Q: Should I live on-site during the build?**
A: Generally, no. Living on-site presents significant liability issues, health hazards (dust/chemicals), and logistical challenges that typically slow down contractor productivity.
