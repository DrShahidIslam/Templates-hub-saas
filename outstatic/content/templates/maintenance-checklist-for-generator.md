# Standard Operating Procedure: Generator Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory maintenance protocols for industrial and commercial standby generators. Regular preventive maintenance is critical to ensuring operational readiness, extending the equipment lifecycle, and guaranteeing power availability during grid failure. All inspections must be performed by qualified personnel, following manufacturer-specific guidelines and site safety regulations. Failure to adhere to these intervals may result in mechanical failure or invalidation of warranty.

## 1. Safety and Preparation Protocols
* Ensure the generator is set to "OFF" or "Maintenance Mode" at the control panel to prevent accidental starting.
* Disconnect the starter battery (negative terminal first) before performing internal mechanical work.
* Utilize appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety goggles, heat-resistant gloves, and hearing protection.
* Verify the area is well-ventilated to prevent carbon monoxide or fuel vapor buildup.
* Keep an oil-absorbent spill kit accessible at all times.

## 2. Engine Systems Maintenance
* **Oil Level and Quality:** Check dipstick levels; inspect oil for signs of metal shavings, milky discoloration (coolant intrusion), or excessive carbon buildup.
* **Oil/Filter Change:** Replace oil and filters according to the manufacturer’s hourly run-time intervals or semi-annual schedule.
* **Coolant System:** Check coolant levels and test for proper freezing/boiling point protection using a refractometer. Inspect hoses for cracks, swelling, or dry rot.
* **Air Intake:** Inspect the air filter element. Replace if dirty or at recommended intervals to ensure proper air-to-fuel ratios.
* **Fuel System:** Inspect fuel lines for leaks. Drain water separators if equipped. Ensure the fuel tank is at least 90% full to prevent condensation buildup.

## 3. Electrical and Control Systems
* **Battery Inspection:** Clean terminals of corrosion. Check voltage with a multimeter; ensure electrolyte levels (if applicable) are correct.
* **Battery Charger:** Verify the charger is operational and properly maintaining the float voltage.
* **Control Panel:** Inspect for error codes, alarm logs, or "check engine" indicators. Test the emergency stop (E-stop) button for functionality.
* **Wiring Harness:** Inspect all exposed wiring for insulation degradation, rodent damage, or loose connections.

## 4. Operational Testing (Load Bank or Transfer Test)
* **Start-up:** Manually start the engine and monitor for abnormal noises, vibrations, or exhaust smoke patterns.
* **Transfer Switch (ATS):** Perform a functional test of the Automatic Transfer Switch to ensure the system shifts to generator power and back to utility power correctly.
* **Readings:** Record frequency (Hz), voltage (V), and oil pressure under load.
* **Cool Down:** Allow the generator to run in an unloaded state for at least 5 minutes before shutting down to stabilize internal temperatures.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Implement an "Oil Analysis" program. Sending an oil sample to a laboratory once a year can detect internal engine wear (like bearing failure) long before a catastrophic event occurs.
* **Pro Tip:** Keep a logbook inside the generator enclosure. Digital records are great, but a physical log inside the unit ensures technicians have immediate access to historical maintenance data.
* **Pitfall: Neglecting the Battery:** The #1 cause of generator failure is a dead starting battery. Even if the generator is in perfect mechanical shape, it is useless if it cannot crank.
* **Pitfall: Wet Stacking:** Running a diesel generator for long periods without a load can cause unburned fuel to accumulate in the exhaust system. Always perform a load test if the unit hasn't run under heavy demand recently.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I perform a full preventive maintenance inspection?**
A: Industry standard is semi-annually for standby units, or every 250–500 run-hours, whichever comes first. Always refer to your specific manufacturer’s manual for the primary interval.

**Q: Can I perform these checks while the generator is running?**
A: Basic checks like oil pressure and voltage monitoring should be done while running, but all physical inspections (fluid topping, belt checks, and filter changes) must be performed while the engine is cold and locked out/tagged out.

**Q: Why is it important to run the generator under load?**
A: Running the unit under load ensures the alternator can handle the site’s electrical demand and prevents "wet stacking," which can lead to permanent damage to the engine’s exhaust components and internal seals.
