# Standard Operating Procedure: Venous Blood Collection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for performing venipuncture to ensure specimen integrity, patient safety, and adherence to infection control standards. Adherence to these steps is critical to preventing pre-analytical errors, such as hemolysis or clotting, and minimizing the risk of complications like hematoma or nerve injury. All personnel must follow these guidelines strictly to maintain diagnostic accuracy and high-quality clinical outcomes.

## 1. Preparation and Patient Identification

*   **Verification:** Confirm the patient’s identity using two unique identifiers (e.g., full name and date of birth). Match identifiers against the laboratory requisition form or electronic order.
*   **Consent:** Explain the procedure to the patient, address concerns, and obtain verbal consent.
*   **Hand Hygiene:** Perform thorough hand hygiene using soap and water or an alcohol-based hand sanitizer immediately before donning non-sterile gloves.
*   **Equipment Setup:** Gather all necessary supplies:
    *   Disposable vacuum tube holder and needle (appropriate gauge).
    *   Required collection tubes (verify expiration dates and additive types).
    *   Tourniquet.
    *   Antiseptic wipes (70% isopropyl alcohol).
    *   Gauze pads and adhesive bandage.
    *   Sharps disposal container.

## 2. Site Selection and Preparation

*   **Positioning:** Ensure the patient is seated or reclined comfortably with the arm extended and supported.
*   **Application:** Apply the tourniquet 3–4 inches above the intended puncture site. Do not leave the tourniquet on for more than one minute to avoid hemoconcentration.
*   **Palpation:** Inspect the antecubital fossa for a suitable vein (median cubital is preferred). Palpate to assess size, depth, and "bounce."
*   **Cleansing:** Cleanse the site with a 70% isopropyl alcohol pad using a circular motion, moving from the center outward. Allow the site to air-dry completely; do not wave or blow on the area.

## 3. Venipuncture Execution

*   **Anchoring:** Anchor the vein by pulling the skin taut below the puncture site with the thumb of your non-dominant hand.
*   **Insertion:** Hold the needle bevel-up at a 15- to 30-degree angle. Enter the skin and vein in one smooth motion.
*   **Collection:** Once blood flow is established, push the collection tube into the holder. Follow the correct Order of Draw to prevent cross-contamination of additives.
*   **Mixing:** Gently invert each tube (do not shake) according to manufacturer instructions (usually 5–10 times) immediately after removal.
*   **Release:** Release the tourniquet as soon as blood flow is established or once the final tube has begun filling.

## 4. Completion and Post-Care

*   **Withdrawal:** Withdraw the needle smoothly at the same angle of entry.
*   **Hemostasis:** Apply immediate pressure to the site with a clean gauze pad. Keep the arm extended (do not bend the elbow, as this increases hematoma risk).
*   **Disposal:** Activate the needle safety device immediately and drop the needle into the designated sharps container.
*   **Labeling:** Label all tubes at the bedside in the presence of the patient, including your initials, date, and time of collection.
*   **Assessment:** Check the site for cessation of bleeding before applying an adhesive bandage.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Avoid Hemolysis:** Never pull back on a syringe plunger forcefully. Always allow the vacuum tube to fill automatically. Ensure alcohol is dry before puncturing, as residual alcohol causes hemolysis.
*   **Tourniquet Time:** Extended tourniquet use alters potassium and protein levels. If a patient is difficult to stick, release the tourniquet after 60 seconds and re-apply after a brief interval.
*   **Patient Fainting (Syncope):** If a patient complains of dizziness, immediately withdraw the needle, lower the patient's head, and monitor their vitals. Never leave a dizzy patient unattended.
*   **Nerve Damage:** If a patient reports a "sharp, electric shock" sensation, withdraw the needle immediately. This indicates nerve contact.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if blood flow stops mid-collection?**
A: First, try slightly adjusting the needle depth or angle without digging. If that fails, check if the tube has lost its vacuum (try a new tube). If the vein has collapsed, release the tourniquet and try a smaller gauge needle or a different site.

**Q: Can I label the tubes before performing the venipuncture?**
A: No. Pre-labeling increases the risk of misidentification errors. Always label tubes at the bedside immediately after the blood has been drawn and before the patient leaves your view.

**Q: Why is the "Order of Draw" so important?**
A: The order is standardized to prevent the carryover of chemical additives (like EDTA or heparin) from one tube to the next. Incorrect order can significantly alter test results, particularly for coagulation studies and potassium levels.
