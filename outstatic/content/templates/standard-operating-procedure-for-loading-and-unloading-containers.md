# Standard Operating Procedure: Container Loading and Unloading

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for loading and unloading intermodal shipping containers. The objective is to ensure the safety of personnel, maintain the integrity of the cargo, optimize space utilization, and adhere to international maritime and logistics compliance standards. All personnel involved in these operations must prioritize weight distribution, load securement, and documentation accuracy to prevent transit damage and regulatory fines.

## 1. Pre-Operational Preparation

*   **Safety Briefing:** Conduct a "toolbox talk" to confirm all personnel are wearing required Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including high-visibility vests, steel-toed boots, and gloves.
*   **Container Inspection:** Perform a "Container Inspection Report" (CIR). Check for holes, structural damage, light leaks, and ensure the floor is dry and free of debris.
*   **Documentation Review:** Cross-reference the cargo manifest with the booking sheet to confirm weight limits and destination requirements.
*   **Equipment Check:** Verify that forklifts, pallet jacks, and dock levelers are in good working condition and rated for the specific load weight.
*   **Dock Safety:** Ensure the trailer/container is secured to the dock using wheel chocks or automatic vehicle restraints to prevent "trailer creep."

## 2. Loading Procedures

*   **Weight Distribution:** Position the heaviest items on the floor of the container, centered along the longitudinal axis to ensure stability.
*   **Pallet Patterning:** Utilize a "pinwheel" or "interlocking" pattern to maximize floor space and minimize shifting during transit.
*   **Load Securement:** Use dunnage, air bags, strapping, or bracing to fill voids. Ensure all cargo is anchored to the container’s internal lashing points.
*   **Weight Compliance:** Weigh the container on a certified scale post-loading to ensure it does not exceed the Maximum Payload Weight (refer to the CSC safety plate).
*   **Seal Application:** Once loading is complete, apply a high-security bolt seal and record the seal number on the Bill of Lading (BOL).

## 3. Unloading Procedures

*   **Seal Verification:** Before opening, verify that the container seal is intact and matches the number listed on the shipping documents.
*   **Atmospheric Check:** If the container has been in transit for an extended period, open the doors slightly to allow for ventilation; monitor for signs of chemical leaks or hazardous fumes.
*   **Structural Assessment:** Inspect the load for shifting, leaning pallets, or damaged goods. Take photographs of any damage *before* unloading to facilitate insurance claims.
*   **Systematic Unloading:** Remove cargo in reverse order of the loading sequence. Ensure that forklift tines do not strike the container walls.
*   **Final Inspection:** Once empty, sweep the container clean, remove all labels/placards, and inspect for any damage that may have occurred during the transit.

## 4. Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize "Weight Centers" software or calculators if the load is dense; container rollovers are frequently caused by improper side-to-side balance.
*   **Pro Tip:** Keep a "Damage Log" digital folder. High-quality photographic evidence of how a container arrived is your primary defense against vendor liability claims.
*   **Pitfall - The "Lumpy" Load:** Avoid loading heavy items only at the nose or the doors. This creates unstable handling for the driver and potential structural failure for the container.
*   **Pitfall - Forgetting to Check Humidity:** For sensitive goods (electronics/textiles), always check the container for condensation or moisture before loading. Damp containers lead to mold and ruined shipments.

## 5. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if the container seal is broken upon arrival?**
A: Do not open the doors. Notify your supervisor immediately, take photos of the broken seal, and contact the carrier or customs authority to request an inspection before breaking the seal further.

**Q: Is it mandatory to use a professional weight scale?**
A: Yes, under SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea) regulations, the Verified Gross Mass (VGM) must be determined and submitted for every container before it can be loaded onto a vessel.

**Q: What is the best way to handle "voids" or empty space?**
A: Voids should never be left empty. Use inflatable dunnage bags or honeycomb paper bracing to fill the space. If the load shifts during transit, it can become impossible to unload safely at the destination.
