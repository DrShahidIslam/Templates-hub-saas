# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Diabetic Management Routine

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is designed to provide a structured, evidence-based framework for managing diabetes on a daily basis. Maintaining consistent glucose levels requires a disciplined approach to blood glucose monitoring, nutritional intake, physical activity, and medication adherence. By following this standardized routine, patients can minimize the risk of hypoglycemic or hyperglycemic episodes and optimize long-term health outcomes. Please note that this document serves as a operational guide and should be customized in collaboration with your primary care physician or endocrinologist.

## Morning: Activation and Baseline Assessment

*   **Glucose Measurement:** Perform a fasting blood glucose test immediately upon waking to establish your baseline for the day.
*   **Hydration:** Consume 8–12 ounces of water to aid metabolic function and rehydrate after the overnight fast.
*   **Medication Administration:** Take prescribed insulin or oral hypoglycemic agents according to your physician’s specific timing requirements (e.g., "with food" vs. "before food").
*   **Foot Inspection:** Visually inspect the soles, sides, and between the toes for any signs of blisters, redness, or cuts, using a mirror if necessary to ensure skin integrity.
*   **Nutritional Planning:** Consume a balanced breakfast rich in complex carbohydrates and fiber to prevent early-day glucose spikes.

## Mid-Day: Sustenance and Activity Maintenance

*   **Pre-Meal Testing:** Check blood glucose levels approximately 15 minutes before lunch to determine appropriate insulin dosing or carbohydrate intake.
*   **Balanced Nutrition:** Prioritize "The Plate Method"—filling half the plate with non-starchy vegetables, one-quarter with lean protein, and one-quarter with fiber-rich carbohydrates.
*   **Activity Integration:** If possible, engage in 10–15 minutes of light physical activity (such as brisk walking) post-lunch to improve insulin sensitivity.
*   **Hydration Check:** Maintain consistent water intake throughout the day to support kidney function.

## Evening: Monitoring and Recovery

*   **Post-Dinner Evaluation:** Test blood glucose levels two hours after the evening meal to assess how your body responded to your dietary choices.
*   **Final Medication Check:** Administer evening doses of long-acting insulin or other medications as prescribed.
*   **Logbook Maintenance:** Record all glucose readings, food intake, and physical activity in a mobile app or physical journal to identify long-term patterns for your next provider visit.
*   **Preparation for Tomorrow:** Lay out medications, testing supplies, and exercise clothing to reduce friction in your morning routine.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **The 15-15 Rule:** If you experience hypoglycemia (glucose below 70 mg/dL), consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (e.g., 4 oz of juice), wait 15 minutes, and retest. Repeat until stable.
*   **Consistency is Key:** Try to eat and exercise at similar times daily; biological rhythms play a significant role in insulin effectiveness.
*   **Kit Maintenance:** Always keep a "Go Bag" with backup testing strips, glucose tabs, and a snack when leaving the house.

### Pitfalls
*   **Ignoring "Invisible" Symptoms:** Never dismiss irritability, extreme fatigue, or brain fog as stress; these are often early indicators of glucose instability.
*   **Over-Correcting:** Avoid "stacking" insulin doses if a reading is high; always wait for the recommended interval specified by your doctor to avoid dangerous drops.
*   **Skipping Foot Checks:** Never assume a small cut or blister is insignificant. Diabetic neuropathy can mask pain, leading to severe infections if ignored.

## FAQ

**1. What should I do if I forget to take my medication?**
Do not "double up" on a dose to make up for the missed one unless explicitly instructed by your doctor. Contact your healthcare provider or pharmacist immediately to receive guidance on how to safely resume your schedule.

**2. How often should I calibrate my glucose monitor?**
Most modern continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) require little to no calibration. However, if you are using a traditional finger-prick glucometer, ensure you calibrate according to the specific manufacturer’s instructions (usually with every new batch of test strips).

**3. Is it normal for my glucose to be high in the morning if I didn't eat late?**
This may be the "Dawn Phenomenon," where the body releases hormones (cortisol, growth hormone) in the early morning to prepare for waking, causing the liver to release stored glucose. Consult your doctor, as this may require a medication dosage adjustment.
