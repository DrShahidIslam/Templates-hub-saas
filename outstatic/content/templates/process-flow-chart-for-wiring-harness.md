# Standard Operating Procedure: Wiring Harness Process Mapping

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the methodology for designing, documenting, and optimizing a process flow chart for wiring harness assembly. A well-constructed flow chart serves as the architectural blueprint for production, ensuring consistency, reducing manufacturing defects (such as crimping errors or routing inconsistencies), and improving throughput. By standardizing this workflow, the operations team can identify bottlenecks in wire preparation, terminal insertion, and electrical testing, ultimately driving higher quality and operational efficiency.

## Phase 1: Pre-Mapping Preparation and Requirements
*   **Identify Scope:** Define whether the flow chart covers the entire lifecycle (from cable cutting to end-of-line testing) or a specific sub-assembly.
*   **Gather Documentation:** Collect the Bill of Materials (BOM), engineering drawings, harness schematics, and the current Quality Control (QC) plan.
*   **Define Process Boundaries:** Establish clear start points (e.g., raw wire receipt) and end points (e.g., final inspection/packaging).
*   **Select Mapping Software:** Utilize industry-standard software (e.g., Microsoft Visio, Lucidchart, or specialized PLM tools) to ensure clarity and version control.

## Phase 2: Detailed Process Mapping
*   **Wire Preparation:** Map the sequence for de-reeling, measuring, cutting, and stripping of individual conductors.
*   **Termination Stage:** Document the crimping process, including terminal selection, applicator settings, and crimp height validation.
*   **Sub-Assembly Integration:** Define the sequence for inserting terminals into housings (connector assembly) and applying seals or plugs.
*   **Harness Routing:** Map the assembly on the formboard, including wire twisting, braiding, or taping sequence to ensure ergonomic access.
*   **Quality Gates:** Clearly mark Inspection Points (IP), such as pull-tests, continuity checks, and visual housing checks.

## Phase 3: Validation and Finalization
*   **Workflow Simulation:** Conduct a "walk-through" of the flow chart on the production floor to verify that the mapped sequence matches physical constraints.
*   **Stakeholder Review:** Secure sign-off from Engineering, Production Supervisors, and Quality Assurance leads.
*   **Versioning:** Apply a unique document control number and revision date to ensure operators are always using the latest process version.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use color-coding in your flow chart to distinguish between manual tasks (e.g., manual insertion) and automated tasks (e.g., automated cutting/crimping).
*   **Pro Tip:** Include "Decision Diamonds" for rework loops. If a harness fails a continuity test, the flow chart must clearly define the path for troubleshooting versus scrapping.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-complicating the chart. If a chart is too dense, operators will ignore it. Use "Hyperlinked Sub-processes" to keep the main view clean and professional.
*   **Pitfall:** Forgetting the environment. Ensure the flow chart accounts for cable management to prevent tangling during the assembly of complex multi-branch harnesses.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should the wiring harness flow chart be reviewed?**
A: The flow chart should be reviewed at least annually, or immediately following any Engineering Change Order (ECO) that alters wire gauge, terminal type, or harness routing.

**Q: What is the most critical quality gate to include in the flow chart?**
A: The Crimp Quality Validation is the most critical; improper crimping accounts for the majority of field failures. Always include a formal pull-test and cross-section inspection checkpoint.

**Q: Should I document the tool calibration in the flow chart?**
A: While calibration schedules are usually held in a separate Master Maintenance Log, the flow chart should include an "Equipment Readiness Check" step before the start of each production shift.
