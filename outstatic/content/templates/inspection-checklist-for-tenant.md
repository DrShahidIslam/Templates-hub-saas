# Standard Operating Procedure: Tenant Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting move-in and move-out inspections. Consistent, thorough inspections are the primary defense for property owners and managers against property damage claims, security deposit disputes, and maintenance neglect. By utilizing this standardized process, you ensure objective documentation of the property’s condition, protecting the landlord's asset and providing a fair, transparent experience for the tenant.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   **Documentation Retrieval:** Pull the previous move-in/move-out reports and the initial lease agreement.
*   **Digital Tools:** Ensure your mobile device is charged, camera/video functionality is clear, and you have access to your property management software or inspection app.
*   **Safety Gear:** Bring a flashlight (high-lumen), a screwdriver (for plate covers if necessary), a moisture meter, and a socket tester for electrical outlets.
*   **Tenant Notification:** Ensure the tenant has received 24–48 hours’ notice (per local laws) and confirm their attendance if applicable.

## Phase 2: Interior Inspection Checklist

## General Living Areas (Living Room, Dining Room, Hallways)
*   **Flooring:** Check for stains, tears, scratches, or uneven surfaces.
*   **Walls/Ceiling:** Note any scuffs, holes (nail/anchor points), water stains, or peeling paint.
*   **Windows:** Ensure all windows operate smoothly, locks engage, and screens are intact.
*   **Electrical:** Test all light switches and wall outlets using a socket tester.
*   **HVAC:** Verify the thermostat responds correctly and airflow is consistent.

## Kitchen & Food Preparation
*   **Appliances:** Run the dishwasher cycle, test refrigerator cooling, verify oven temperature, and ensure stovetop burners ignite.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect under the sink for leaks, check faucet water pressure, and test the garbage disposal.
*   **Cabinetry:** Check drawers for sticking or broken tracks and verify cabinet doors are properly aligned.
*   **Countertops:** Look for chips, deep cuts, or burn marks.

## Bathrooms
*   **Plumbing:** Flush toilets to check for proper seals and fill speed; check sinks and showers for drainage speed.
*   **Surfaces:** Inspect shower tiles/grout for mold or water damage.
*   **Ventilation:** Turn on exhaust fans to ensure they are functional and clear of debris.
*   **Fixtures:** Inspect towel bars and toilet paper holders for secure wall attachment.

## Exterior & Safety Systems
*   **Safety:** Verify smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors are operational. Test the fire extinguisher gauge.
*   **Locks:** Confirm all deadbolts and window locks operate without resistance.
*   **Exterior:** Check for debris, pest activity, or damage to exterior siding and decking.

## Phase 3: Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip – The "Document Everything" Rule:** If you see a scratch, take a wide-angle shot to show context and a close-up shot to show detail. Timestamp your photos automatically.
*   **Pro Tip – Timestamped Video:** Conduct a "walk-through" video with narration. Verbalizing the condition of items (e.g., "The wall shows no damage") provides a powerful secondary layer of proof.
*   **Pitfall – Skipping the "Invisible" Items:** Many managers check floors but forget to check inside the dishwasher, behind the refrigerator, or inside the HVAC filter compartment. These are common areas for hidden damage or neglect.
*   **Pitfall – Subjectivity:** Avoid using vague terms like "dirty" or "bad condition." Be specific: "White paint on master bedroom wall has a 2-inch dark scuff near the light switch."

## Phase 4: Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. Is it mandatory to have the tenant present during the inspection?**
While not strictly legally required in all jurisdictions, it is highly recommended. Having the tenant present minimizes "he-said/she-said" disputes regarding the condition of the property and allows for immediate clarification of concerns.

**2. What should I do if the tenant disagrees with my assessment?**
Always provide a section for "Tenant Comments" on your inspection form. If there is a dispute, document their side of the story clearly and take additional photos of the area in question. Do not get into an argument; note that the report will be reviewed by management.

**3. How long should I keep inspection records?**
You should maintain inspection records for the duration of the tenancy, plus the statute of limitations for your state regarding security deposit returns (typically 3 to 7 years). Digital backups in a secure cloud environment are the industry standard.
