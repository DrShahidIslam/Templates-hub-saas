# Standard Operating Procedure: NGO Financial and Operational Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a comprehensive framework for conducting an internal or external audit of a Non-Governmental Organization (NGO). The objective is to ensure transparency, regulatory compliance, donor accountability, and operational efficiency. By adhering to this audit checklist, the organization mitigates financial risk, secures institutional trust, and guarantees that all programmatic activities align with the mission and legal mandates of the NGO.

## 1. Governance and Compliance Documentation
*   Verify the existence and validity of the NGO’s registration certificate and tax-exempt status documents.
*   Review the Memorandum of Association and Articles of Association to ensure all current activities align with original bylaws.
*   Examine minutes from Board of Trustees/Directors meetings to confirm quorum and proper authorization of major financial decisions.
*   Check for the filing of annual returns with relevant government regulatory bodies.
*   Review conflict-of-interest disclosures signed by board members and key personnel.

## 2. Financial Management and Internal Controls
*   Verify bank reconciliations for all accounts are performed monthly and signed off by the finance manager.
*   Check the authorization hierarchy for expenditures (e.g., dual-signature requirements for checks or electronic transfers).
*   Review the petty cash log for accuracy, physical cash counts, and proper supporting documentation (receipts/invoices).
*   Examine payroll records to ensure compliance with tax withholdings, social security contributions, and salary structure policies.
*   Confirm that all assets (laptops, vehicles, equipment) are tracked in an asset register and periodically physically verified.

## 3. Donor Reporting and Grant Management
*   Cross-reference grant agreements with actual project expenditures to ensure funds are restricted appropriately.
*   Review the "Burn Rate" analysis for each grant to identify under-utilization or over-spending issues.
*   Validate that procurement processes for donor-funded projects adhere to specific donor guidelines (e.g., three-quote rule).
*   Verify that narrative reports submitted to donors align chronologically and financially with the financial reports provided.
*   Ensure that indirect cost (overhead) recovery is calculated according to the approved budget line items.

## 4. Operational and Programmatic Audit
*   Review beneficiary selection criteria to ensure they are transparent, non-discriminatory, and documented.
*   Check for "Proof of Delivery" documents (e.g., distribution lists, photos, sign-off sheets) for aid or services provided in the field.
*   Assess whether project monitoring and evaluation (M&E) data supports the reported impact metrics.
*   Verify HR files to ensure employment contracts, job descriptions, and performance appraisals are up to date.
*   Confirm that data protection policies are in place to secure beneficiary sensitive information.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "Grant Folder" for every donor, containing the contract, budget, approved amendments, and all corresponding invoices from Day 1 of the project.
*   **Pro Tip:** Automate your financial reporting using cloud-based accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks Online, Xero) to create a real-time audit trail.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "In-Kind" donations. Ensure non-cash contributions (donated goods/services) are valued at fair market rate and recorded as both revenue and expenditure.
*   **Pitfall:** Failing to document verbal changes to grant budgets. Any shift in funds must be approved in writing by the donor to avoid "disallowed costs" during an audit.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should an NGO perform an internal audit?**
Best practice dictates a quarterly internal "health check" to identify errors early, with a formal, comprehensive internal audit occurring at least once a year, ahead of the external statutory audit.

**2. What are "disallowed costs" and how can they be avoided?**
Disallowed costs are expenditures the donor refuses to reimburse due to lack of documentation or non-compliance with the grant agreement. These can be avoided by strictly adhering to the procurement policy and ensuring every transaction has a clear, physical paper trail.

**3. Is it mandatory to audit restricted funds separately from general funds?**
Yes. To maintain donor transparency and avoid commingling of funds, the financial system must be able to track and report on each restricted fund’s income and expenditure independently of the general operating budget.
