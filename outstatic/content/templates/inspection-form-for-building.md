# Standard Operating Procedure: Building Inspection Protocols

This document outlines the standardized procedure for conducting comprehensive building inspections to ensure structural integrity, occupant safety, and operational efficiency. The objective of this SOP is to provide a consistent methodology for facility managers and inspectors to identify maintenance needs, mitigate liability, and uphold regulatory compliance. Adherence to this protocol ensures that all physical assets are documented accurately, allowing for proactive capital planning and risk management.

## 1. Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   **Documentation Review:** Review the previous inspection report, open work orders, and floor plans.
*   **Safety Gear:** Ensure the inspector is equipped with appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including hard hats, high-visibility vests, and non-slip footwear.
*   **Tools Required:** Bring a digital camera/tablet, flashlight, laser distance meter, moisture meter, and a clipboard with the standardized inspection checklist.
*   **Notification:** Notify department heads or site tenants 24–48 hours in advance to ensure accessibility to restricted areas (e.g., mechanical rooms, roof access).

## 2. Exterior Envelope and Site Grounds
*   **Roofing System:** Inspect for damaged flashing, ponding water, debris in drains, or loose shingles/membrane tears.
*   **Facades:** Check for cracks in masonry, spalling concrete, or signs of water intrusion around window seals.
*   **Drainage:** Verify that gutters and downspouts are clear and directed away from the foundation.
*   **Hardscapes:** Examine sidewalks, parking lots, and loading docks for trip hazards, potholes, or uneven surfaces.
*   **Landscaping:** Confirm that vegetation is trimmed away from the building exterior to prevent moisture buildup and pest nesting.

## 3. Interior Common Areas and Structure
*   **Egress Paths:** Verify that all stairwells are free of storage, emergency lighting is functional, and fire doors close/latch properly.
*   **Wall/Ceiling Integrity:** Inspect for signs of water stains, mold growth, or peeling paint which could indicate roof or plumbing leaks.
*   **Flooring:** Check for loose tiles, worn carpeting, or uneven transitions that pose a liability risk.
*   **HVAC Performance:** Observe interior comfort levels; listen for abnormal noises in air handlers or ventilation grilles.

## 4. Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing (MEP)
*   **Electrical Panels:** Ensure panels are not obstructed, labels are current, and there is no evidence of charring or buzzing sounds.
*   **Plumbing:** Inspect visible pipes for leaks or corrosion; ensure all restroom fixtures are operational.
*   **Life Safety Systems:** Check the expiration dates on fire extinguishers and verify that fire alarm control panels show no active trouble codes.
*   **Mechanical Rooms:** Confirm that equipment maintenance logs are up to date and that the area is clean of flammable debris.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Photo Documentation):** Always take a "wide shot" followed by a "close-up" of any deficiency to provide context for repair technicians.
*   **Pro Tip (The "Touch" Test):** Don't just look; test door latches, light switches, and sink faucets. Minor operational failures are often missed during visual-only inspections.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring "Minor" Cracks):** Do not dismiss hairline fractures as cosmetic. Document them; if they widen in the next inspection, you have evidence of structural shifting.
*   **Pitfall (Assumption of Compliance):** Never assume a system is safe because it was fine last month. Always verify current status, specifically for fire suppression and electrical components.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a full building inspection be conducted?**
A: A comprehensive interior and exterior inspection should be performed quarterly, with a formal, detailed audit conducted annually. High-traffic facilities may require monthly walkthroughs.

**Q: What should I do if I discover a high-risk safety hazard during an inspection?**
A: Stop the inspection immediately. Secure the area, report the hazard to the building owner or safety director, and initiate an emergency work order before proceeding with the rest of the inspection.

**Q: Is it necessary to photograph areas that appear to be in good condition?**
A: Yes. "Baseline photos" provide vital evidence in the event of an insurance claim or a tenant dispute regarding the condition of a space at a specific point in time.
