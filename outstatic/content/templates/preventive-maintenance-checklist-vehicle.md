# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Preventive Maintenance

Effective fleet management relies on a proactive approach to vehicle maintenance. This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory steps to perform a comprehensive preventive maintenance (PM) inspection. Adhering to this protocol minimizes vehicle downtime, reduces long-term repair costs, ensures driver safety, and extends the operational lifecycle of company assets. All maintenance personnel must document findings within the fleet management system upon completion of the inspection.

## 1. Exterior and Body Integrity
*   **Lighting System:** Test high/low beams, turn signals, hazard lights, brake lights, and reverse lamps. Replace any dimmed or non-functional bulbs immediately.
*   **Tires and Wheels:** Measure tread depth across all tires (including spare). Inspect sidewalls for bulges, cracks, or embedded debris. Verify tire pressure matches the door placard specifications. Check lug nuts for tightness.
*   **Wiper Blades:** Inspect for rubber degradation or streaking. Test spray nozzles for proper alignment and fluid delivery.
*   **Body Inspection:** Document any new dents, scratches, or rust. Ensure all doors, locks, and latches operate smoothly.

## 2. Under-the-Hood Diagnostics
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check and top off engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer reservoir.
*   **Battery Health:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion. Ensure the battery is securely mounted and cables are tight. Perform a load test if the battery is over two years old.
*   **Belts and Hoses:** Inspect serpentine belt for fraying or cracking. Check cooling hoses for bulges, leaks, or sponginess.
*   **Air Filtration:** Inspect engine air filter and cabin air filter. Replace if dust/debris saturation exceeds 50%.

## 3. Chassis and Drivetrain
*   **Brake System:** Inspect pads and rotors for wear. Check brake lines for signs of corrosion or leakage.
*   **Suspension:** Visually inspect struts, shocks, and bushings for leaks or abnormal compression.
*   **Exhaust System:** Check for visible rust, holes, or loose hangers that could cause excessive noise or vibration.
*   **Undercarriage:** Scan for oil or transmission fluid leaks pooling on the vehicle frame.

## 4. Interior and Safety Equipment
*   **Dashboard Indicators:** Confirm there are no active "Check Engine" or warning lights. Clear any stored fault codes after addressing issues.
*   **Safety Gear:** Verify the presence and expiration date of the fire extinguisher and first aid kit. Ensure reflective triangles/emergency flares are in the vehicle.
*   **Seatbelts:** Test the locking mechanism of all seatbelts to ensure they retract and lock under tension.
*   **Controls:** Ensure the A/C, heater, defroster, and horn are fully operational.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a digital tracking system to alert you 500 miles before the recommended service interval to avoid "maintenance drift."
*   **Pro Tip:** Document "minor" issues even if they don't require immediate repair; this creates a trend analysis that helps predict future component failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore fluid leaks, even small ones. A minor weep from a gasket is often the precursor to a catastrophic system failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-torquing lug nuts can lead to stud failure; always use a calibrated torque wrench rather than an impact gun for final tightening.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should a preventive maintenance inspection be performed?**
A: Standard intervals are every 5,000 to 7,500 miles or every 6 months, whichever comes first. Always defer to the manufacturer’s specific service manual for heavy-duty or high-idle usage.

**Q: What should I do if a safety-critical issue is identified during the inspection?**
A: The vehicle must be immediately tagged as "Out of Service" (OOS). Remove the keys, notify the fleet manager, and ensure the vehicle is not operated until the repair is verified by a lead technician.

**Q: Is it necessary to replace fluids if they aren't low?**
A: Yes. Fluids degrade over time due to heat cycles and contamination. Follow the manufacturer's time-based intervals for fluid flushes (e.g., coolant or brake fluid) even if the mileage threshold has not been met.
