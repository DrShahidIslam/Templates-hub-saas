# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Safety Inspection

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements and methodology for conducting comprehensive electrical safety inspections. The objective is to identify potential hazards—such as frayed wiring, overloaded circuits, or improper grounding—to mitigate the risk of electrical shock, arc flashes, and facility fires. This procedure applies to all personnel responsible for facility maintenance and applies to all permanent and portable electrical infrastructure within the site.

---

## Electrical Safety Inspection Checklist

## 1. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) & Preparation
*   Verify presence of non-conductive footwear (ASTM F2413 rated).
*   Ensure inspection tools (multimeter, non-contact voltage tester, thermal camera) are calibrated and intact.
*   Confirm availability of insulated gloves (Class 00/0 rated).
*   Verify that the "Lockout/Tagout" (LOTO) kit is accessible for hazardous areas.
*   Ensure adequate lighting is available to inspect dark electrical panels or crawlspaces.

## 2. Main Distribution Boards & Circuit Breakers
*   Inspect panel covers for signs of heat damage (discoloration or melting).
*   Verify that all circuit breakers are clearly labeled and that the directory is current.
*   Check for "tripped" breakers and investigate the root cause before resetting.
*   Ensure no combustible materials are stored within 36 inches of the electrical panel.
*   Confirm the panel door closes and latches properly to prevent dust/debris ingress.
*   Verify that unused breaker slots are properly sealed with filler plates.

## 3. Wiring, Cords, and Outlets
*   Inspect all power cords for visible damage, cracks, or exposed copper conductors.
*   Check that extension cords are not being used as permanent facility wiring.
*   Test Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) outlets using the "Test/Reset" buttons.
*   Ensure outlet faceplates are securely fastened and not cracked.
*   Check that "daisy-chaining" of power strips is not occurring.
*   Verify that cords are routed away from high-traffic areas to prevent tripping hazards.

## 4. Equipment and Environment
*   Confirm that all portable electrical tools are grounded or double-insulated.
*   Ensure high-voltage equipment enclosures are properly grounded and bonded.
*   Inspect ventilation around motors and heavy machinery to prevent overheating.
*   Verify that emergency disconnects (E-Stops) are unobstructed and functional.
*   Check that damp/wet locations are equipped with moisture-resistant electrical enclosures (NEMA 3R or higher).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Use Thermal Imaging.** Conduct inspections while equipment is under normal load. Thermal cameras can identify "hot spots" in connections that are not yet visible to the naked eye but indicate impending failure.
*   **Pro Tip: Document with Photos.** Maintain a digital log of your inspection. If a hazard is corrected, take a "post-fix" photo to ensure your compliance records are bulletproof during an audit.
*   **Pitfall: Over-Reliance on "Testing".** A GFCI might test successfully, but the ground connection itself could be faulty. Always use a receptacle tester to verify correct wiring (Hot/Neutral/Ground orientation).
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Auditory Cues.** Never ignore a humming, buzzing, or crackling sound coming from a panel. This is often the sound of arcing and requires immediate de-energization.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a facility-wide electrical inspection be conducted?**
A: A formal inspection should be conducted at least annually. However, high-use areas or environments prone to moisture/dust should be inspected on a quarterly basis.

**Q: What should I do if I find a frayed wire on a piece of office equipment?**
A: Immediately remove the equipment from service, apply a "Do Not Use" tag, and isolate it from the power source. Do not attempt to patch the cord with electrical tape; it must be replaced or professionally repaired by a certified electrician.

**Q: Are power strips allowed to be plugged into other power strips?**
A: No. This is known as "daisy-chaining" and is a severe fire hazard. It creates an accumulation of electrical resistance and poses a high risk of overloading the circuit, which can lead to overheating and electrical fires.
