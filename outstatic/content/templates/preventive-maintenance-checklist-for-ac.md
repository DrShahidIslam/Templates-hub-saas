# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for HVAC Systems

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocol for HVAC units to ensure optimal performance, energy efficiency, and extended equipment lifespan. Regular maintenance is critical to preventing compressor failure, improving indoor air quality, and reducing emergency repair costs. All maintenance must be performed by certified technicians following established safety protocols, including Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures where applicable.

## 1. Safety and Preparation
*   Confirm the system is fully powered down via the service disconnect switch.
*   Verify that all personal protective equipment (PPE), including gloves and safety glasses, is utilized.
*   Clear the immediate area of debris to ensure safe access to the interior and exterior units.
*   Inspect the unit for any visible signs of damage, refrigerant leaks, or burning odors.

## 2. Airflow and Filtration
*   **Inspect/Replace Filters:** Remove and inspect air filters. Replace disposable filters if dirty or clean reusable electrostatic filters.
*   **Clear Obstructions:** Ensure return air vents and supply registers are unobstructed by furniture or interior decor.
*   **Inspect Blower Assembly:** Check the blower wheel for dust accumulation and signs of imbalance. Clean blades if necessary to maintain airflow efficiency.
*   **Check Evaporator Coil:** Inspect the coil for dust, mold, or debris. If dirty, clean with an approved coil cleaner to prevent icing and restricted airflow.

## 3. Electrical and Mechanical Components
*   **Tighten Connections:** Inspect all electrical terminals and tighten loose connections. Replace any wires showing signs of heat damage or fraying.
*   **Contactor Inspection:** Check the contactor for pitted or burnt points that could lead to compressor failure.
*   **Capacitor Testing:** Measure the microfarad (MFD) rating of the run and start capacitors; replace if they deviate by more than +/- 10% of the manufacturer’s specified rating.
*   **Motor Health:** Check the fan motor for unusual vibration or noise. Lubricate bearings if the motor is not permanently sealed.

## 4. Condensate Management
*   **Clean Drain Line:** Flush the condensate drain line with an algaecide or a mild bleach solution to prevent sludge buildup and water damage.
*   **Inspect Drain Pan:** Check the condensate pan for rust or stagnant water. Ensure the pan is properly sloped toward the drain outlet.
*   **Float Switch Test:** Test the emergency float switch to ensure it successfully cuts power to the unit if the drain line becomes clogged.

## 5. Exterior Unit (Condenser)
*   **Coil Cleaning:** Remove leaves, grass, and debris from the condenser fins. Use a low-pressure water spray to clean the fins from the inside out to ensure heat transfer efficiency.
*   **Fan Check:** Verify the condenser fan motor is clear of debris and spinning freely.
*   **Refrigerant Line Insulation:** Inspect the suction line insulation (black foam piping) for cracks or degradation; replace as needed to prevent energy loss and condensation issues.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a high-quality, non-acidic coil cleaner for indoor evaporator coils to avoid damaging delicate aluminum fins.
*   **Pro Tip:** Document your static pressure readings before and after cleaning the coils; this provides a baseline to measure the effectiveness of the maintenance.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening electrical connections can strip terminals. Always use a calibrated torque screwdriver when working on sensitive control boards.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid using high-pressure power washers on condenser coils; the high force can easily bend the aluminum fins, which restricts airflow and forces the compressor to work harder.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I perform this maintenance?**
A: Preventive maintenance should be performed at least twice a year—once in the spring before the cooling season and once in the fall before the heating season.

**Q: Why does my unit smell like mildew after maintenance?**
A: This usually indicates that the evaporator coil or drain pan still harbors biological growth. Ensure you are using an EPA-approved antimicrobial coil cleaner during your cleaning process.

**Q: Is it necessary to check refrigerant levels during every visit?**
A: No. HVAC systems are closed-loop systems. If the refrigerant levels are low, it indicates a leak. Only check pressures if the system performance is sub-optimal or if the delta T (temperature difference across the coil) is outside of the manufacturer’s specifications.
