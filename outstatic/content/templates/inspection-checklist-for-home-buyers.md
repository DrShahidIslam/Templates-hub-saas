# Standard Operating Procedure: Home Buyer Property Inspection

This SOP provides a comprehensive framework for prospective home buyers to conduct a methodical inspection of a residential property. While a professional third-party home inspector is essential for technical assessment, this guide empowers the buyer to perform a preliminary evaluation to identify potential deal-breakers, maintenance red flags, and areas requiring professional scrutiny. By following this standardized process, buyers can minimize financial risk and make data-driven decisions during the acquisition process.

## Section 1: Exterior & Structural Integrity
*   **Foundation:** Look for stair-step cracking in brickwork or concrete, which may indicate settlement.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect for missing shingles, excessive granule loss, or signs of sagging. Note the age and material of the roof.
*   **Grading:** Ensure the ground slopes away from the foundation to prevent water pooling.
*   **Siding/Cladding:** Check for rotting wood, cracked stucco, or loose panels that could allow moisture infiltration.
*   **Gutters:** Ensure they are securely attached and directed away from the house via downspout extensions.

## Section 2: Interior Systems & Utilities
*   **HVAC:** Check the age of the furnace and A/C unit. Run the heat and cooling systems to verify they reach desired temperatures.
*   **Electrical:** Ensure all visible outlets are grounded (use a three-prong tester). Check the circuit breaker panel for signs of scorched wires or outdated fuse boxes.
*   **Plumbing:** Turn on all faucets simultaneously to test water pressure. Check under sinks for active leaks or signs of water damage.
*   **Water Heater:** Note the manufacture date and check for signs of rust or leakage at the base.

## Section 3: Interior Rooms & Finishes
*   **Windows:** Operate every window to ensure they open, close, and lock properly. Check for condensation between double-pane glass (sign of seal failure).
*   **Flooring:** Walk the perimeter of rooms to detect soft spots in subfloors, which may indicate past water damage.
*   **Attic:** Look for signs of roof leaks (water stains) or inadequate insulation.
*   **Basement/Crawlspace:** Use a flashlight to inspect for efflorescence (white, powdery residue) on walls, which indicates moisture penetration.

## Section 4: Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Smell Test":** Be wary of heavy air freshener usage; it is often deployed to mask the scent of mold, mildew, or pet damage.
*   **Pro Tip: Bring a Marble:** Carry a marble to test if floors are level. If the marble rolls rapidly in one direction, the floor may be uneven due to foundation issues.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Stains:** A small yellow spot on the ceiling is rarely "just a small leak"; it is often the surface symptom of a larger, chronic roof or plumbing issue.
*   **Pitfall: Skipping the Attic/Crawlspace:** Many buyers fear these areas due to dirt or discomfort. These two spaces provide the most honest story about the home’s actual condition.

## FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I conduct an inspection without a professional?**
A: No. While this checklist helps you spot obvious issues, a certified home inspector uses tools like thermal cameras and moisture meters to detect issues hidden behind walls that you cannot see. Use this checklist as a precursor to hiring a pro.

**Q: What if the seller says the home is sold "as-is"?**
A: "As-is" means the seller will not perform repairs. It does not mean you cannot perform an inspection. You still need an inspection to determine if the property is worth the price or if the repairs required are beyond your budget.

**Q: Should I take photos during my inspection?**
A: Absolutely. Create a digital folder for the property and document any area of concern. These photos are invaluable for reference when negotiating repairs or asking a professional contractor for a quote on potential work.
