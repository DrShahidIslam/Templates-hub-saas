# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Property Evaluation

Buying a home is one of the most significant capital investments an individual will make. This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is designed to move beyond emotional reactions and provide a systematic, data-driven framework for property evaluation. By utilizing this checklist, a prospective buyer can mitigate risk, identify deferred maintenance, and accurately assess the long-term viability of a potential acquisition.

## Phase 1: Exterior & Structural Integrity
*   **Foundation:** Scan for horizontal cracks, stair-step masonry cracks, or signs of water pooling against the slab/foundation.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect for curled, missing, or worn shingles. Check the age of the roof and look for sagging in the ridge line.
*   **Siding/Cladding:** Look for rot, insect damage, or peeling paint. Ensure there is adequate clearance between the siding and the ground to prevent moisture wicking.
*   **Grading/Drainage:** Observe if the ground slopes away from the house. Ensure gutters are clean and downspouts discharge water at least 5–6 feet away from the foundation.
*   **Windows & Exterior Doors:** Check for fogging between panes (seal failure) and ensure all locks are functional.

## Phase 2: Interior Systems & Utilities
*   **HVAC:** Check the manufacture date on the furnace and AC unit. Turn them on to verify airflow and heat/cooling output.
*   **Electrical:** Inspect the panel (ensure it is a breaker box, not fuses). Check for GFCIs in kitchens, bathrooms, and exterior outlets. Test a sampling of outlets.
*   **Plumbing:** Turn on all faucets simultaneously to check for water pressure drops. Check under sinks for signs of current or previous leaks (water stains/mildew).
*   **Water Heater:** Note the age and capacity. Look for signs of corrosion or leakage at the base.
*   **Attic/Crawlspace:** Look for evidence of pests, water intrusion, or inadequate insulation. Check for proper ventilation to prevent mold growth.

## Phase 3: Interior Living Conditions
*   **Walls & Ceilings:** Look for hairline cracks or water stains, which may indicate structural settling or active roof leaks.
*   **Flooring:** Walk the perimeter of rooms to check for soft spots or uneven leveling.
*   **Kitchen & Bath:** Test all appliances. Verify that exhaust fans vent to the exterior, not into the attic space.
*   **Odors:** Be hyper-aware of "masking" scents (heavy candles or plug-ins), which may be covering up pet dander, mold, or smoke damage.

## Phase 4: Neighborhood & External Factors
*   **Noise Pollution:** Note proximity to train tracks, highways, or flight paths.
*   **Traffic Flow:** Observe parking availability and the speed of traffic during your arrival.
*   **Neighbor Maintenance:** Take note of the general state of adjacent properties; poor maintenance nearby can negatively impact future resale value.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "Flashlight Rule":** Always bring a high-lumen flashlight. Even in daylight, it is essential for inspecting dark corners of basements, crawlspaces, and attics.
*   **Pitfall - Cosmetic Distraction:** Do not let staging (nice furniture, fresh paint) distract you from underlying structural issues. Paint is cheap; foundation repair is expensive.
*   **Pro Tip - The "Water Test":** Run the taps in the master bath while flushing the toilet. This reveals potential pressure issues and drainage backups that a quick walk-through will miss.
*   **Pro Tip - Neighborhood Recon:** Visit the property at two different times of day (e.g., Saturday morning and Tuesday evening) to get an accurate read on noise and activity levels.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. Should I bring a professional home inspector to the initial viewing?**
Generally, no. Initial viewings are for qualifying a property for your "shortlist." A formal professional inspection should be a contingency in your purchase offer once you have entered into a contract.

**2. What are "red flags" that should immediately disqualify a house?**
Significant foundation shifts, active termite infestation, non-permitted structural modifications, and severe water damage (black mold) are often deal-breakers that suggest systemic neglect.

**3. How do I know if the systems (HVAC/Roof) are at the end of their lifespan?**
Check the serial numbers on equipment; manufacturers often hide the year of production in the code. For roofs, if you see "cupping" or large amounts of granules in the gutters, it likely needs replacement within 3–5 years.
