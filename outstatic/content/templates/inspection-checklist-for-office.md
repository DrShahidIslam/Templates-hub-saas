# Standard Operating Procedure: Office Facilities & Safety Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory protocol for conducting a comprehensive office facility inspection. The objective of this procedure is to ensure a safe, functional, and professional work environment that meets organizational standards and regulatory compliance requirements. Regular inspections mitigate operational risks, prolong asset lifespan, and ensure employee well-being. This procedure applies to all facility managers, office administrators, and designated safety officers responsible for site maintenance.

## 1. Safety and Egress Systems
*   **Emergency Exits:** Ensure all exit pathways are clear of boxes, furniture, or equipment. Confirm that "Exit" signs are illuminated and functional.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Verify that pressure gauges are in the green zone, the safety seal is intact, and the inspection tag is current.
*   **Fire Alarms & Detectors:** Perform a visual sweep for dust accumulation; ensure nothing is hanging from or obstructing smoke sensors.
*   **First Aid Kits:** Check that kits are fully stocked, unexpired, and accessible.
*   **Emergency Lighting:** Test battery backups in stairwells and corridors to ensure they activate during a simulated power loss.

## 2. Workplace Ergonomics and Workspace Standards
*   **Desk Organization:** Confirm that workstation surfaces are free of excessive clutter that could pose a fire or tripping hazard.
*   **Furniture Integrity:** Inspect office chairs for loose bolts, faulty hydraulic lifts, or damaged fabric.
*   **Cable Management:** Ensure all power cords and data cables are secured in cable trays or floor covers; check for frayed wires or overloaded power strips.
*   **Lighting Quality:** Identify and report flickering ballasts, burnt-out bulbs, or inadequate task lighting.

## 3. General Sanitation and Building Maintenance
*   **HVAC Performance:** Check for unusual odors, excessive noise, or blocked air intake vents. Ensure temperatures are within the approved building climate range.
*   **Common Areas:** Inspect breakrooms and kitchens for sanitary food storage, functional appliances, and leak-free plumbing.
*   **Floor Condition:** Identify tripping hazards such as torn carpeting, uneven floor transitions, or wet spots.
*   **Waste Management:** Confirm that trash and recycling bins are emptied regularly and that biohazard/shred bins are locked and secure.

## 4. Security and IT Infrastructure
*   **Physical Access:** Test the integrity of server room locks, badge readers, and restricted-area door latches.
*   **Hardware Security:** Verify that shared printers, copiers, and docking stations are properly mounted or secured.
*   **Entry Points:** Check that all windows are closed/locked and the reception area displays appropriate visitor signage.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Use Digital Documentation.** Use a mobile-friendly inspection app to capture photos of deficiencies. This creates an immediate audit trail and simplifies work-order generation for maintenance teams.
*   **Pro Tip: The "Fresh Eyes" Rotation.** Rotate the person performing the inspection every quarter. Familiarity breeds blindness; someone new to the task is more likely to notice subtle wear and tear.
*   **Pitfall: Checking the Box.** Avoid "pencil-whipping" the checklist. If you are not physically testing doors or checking under desks, you are missing the most common points of failure.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Items.** Never defer "minor" issues like a loose screw or a flickering light. These are often precursors to larger, more expensive safety incidents.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should this inspection be performed?**
A: A comprehensive walk-through should be conducted at a minimum of once per month. High-traffic areas or large facilities may require bi-weekly inspections.

**Q: What should I do if I find a high-risk safety hazard?**
A: Immediately cordon off the affected area, document the hazard with a photograph, and notify the Facilities Manager or Safety Officer. Do not wait for the next scheduled report to address imminent safety threats.

**Q: Are these inspections legally required?**
A: In many jurisdictions, local fire codes and occupational health and safety (OSHA) regulations require regular inspections of emergency equipment and egress routes. Always ensure your checklist aligns with local municipal requirements.
