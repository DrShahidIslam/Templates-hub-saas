# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Psoriasis Management Routine

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines a structured, consistent daily regimen designed to manage psoriasis symptoms, maintain skin barrier integrity, and minimize inflammatory flare-ups. Consistency is the primary factor in therapeutic success; this routine is engineered to integrate seamlessly into daily hygiene habits to ensure that dermatological treatment is prioritized without causing operational fatigue. Adherence to these protocols assists in reducing scaling, alleviating pruritus (itching), and protecting compromised dermal tissue.

## Daily Routine Checklist

## Section 1: Morning Stabilization
*   **Gentle Cleansing:** Use a lukewarm, non-soap, fragrance-free cleanser. Avoid hot water, which strips natural oils and exacerbates dryness.
*   **Pat-Dry Protocol:** Use a soft, cotton towel to gently pat the skin dry. Do not rub, as friction can trigger the Koebner phenomenon (new lesions appearing at sites of skin injury).
*   **Strategic Emollient Application:** Apply a thick, fragrance-free moisturizer (preferably containing ceramides or urea) while the skin is still slightly damp to "lock in" hydration.
*   **Medication Adherence:** Apply prescribed topical treatments (e.g., corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors) precisely to lesion sites. Allow 5–10 minutes for absorption before applying clothing.
*   **Sunlight Exposure (If Prescribed):** If phototherapy or controlled natural UV exposure is part of your plan, perform this early to avoid peak mid-day intensity.

## Section 2: Mid-Day Maintenance
*   **Hydration Check:** Consume adequate water to support systemic skin health from within.
*   **Environmental Awareness:** Monitor skin for irritation caused by sweat or synthetic fabrics. If working out, shower immediately afterward to remove sweat, which can irritate plaques.
*   **Spot Treatment:** If itching occurs, avoid scratching. Use a cooling, fragrance-free anti-itch lotion or a chilled compress to manage the sensation.

## Section 3: Evening Recovery & Repair
*   **Temperature-Controlled Bathing:** Take a 10–15 minute lukewarm bath. Optional: Add colloidal oatmeal or Epsom salts to soothe inflammation and loosen stubborn scales.
*   **Debridement (If Necessary):** Gently remove softened scales using a soft cloth. Never force-remove scales, as this causes bleeding and inflammation.
*   **Active Treatment Application:** Apply high-potency prescribed topicals as directed by your physician. 
*   **Occlusive Layering:** For stubborn areas (e.g., elbows, knees), apply a thick layer of petroleum jelly or a specialized heavy-duty ointment to create an occlusive barrier overnight.
*   **Environment Preparation:** Ensure the bedroom air is sufficiently humidified, especially during winter months, to prevent nocturnal transepidermal water loss.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: The "Fridge Hack":** Store your topical creams or anti-itch lotions in the refrigerator. The cold temperature provides an immediate, drug-free anesthetic effect on itchy plaques.
*   **Pro Tip: Cotton First:** When using heavy-duty occlusive treatments, wear 100% cotton pajamas. This allows the skin to breathe while preventing medication from rubbing off onto linens.
*   **Pitfall: Over-Exfoliation:** Avoid harsh loofahs or chemical scrubs. Psoriatic skin is hyper-proliferative but fragile; aggressive scrubbing triggers a reactive inflammatory response.
*   **Pitfall: Fragrance Traps:** Many products labeled "natural" contain essential oils (like lavender or citrus) that are common contact allergens. Stick to strictly hypoallergenic, fragrance-free medical-grade products.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Should I prioritize prescription medication over moisturizing?**
No. Moisturizing is the foundation of the routine. It repairs the skin barrier, allowing your prescription medications to penetrate more effectively. Apply prescriptions first, then moisturize over them unless your dermatologist instructs otherwise.

**2. How do I know if I am over-treating an area?**
If your skin becomes thin, starts bruising easily, or develops stretch marks (striae), you may be using high-potency steroids too frequently. Consult your dermatologist to discuss a "tapering" schedule or a "drug holiday."

**3. Does diet play a role in my daily routine?**
While not a replacement for medical treatment, many patients find that reducing systemic inflammation through an anti-inflammatory diet (low sugar, high omega-3 fatty acids) supports the topical routine and reduces the frequency of flare-ups.
