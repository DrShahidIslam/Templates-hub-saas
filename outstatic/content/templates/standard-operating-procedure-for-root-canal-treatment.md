# Standard Operating Procedure: Root Canal Treatment (RCT)

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the clinical protocol for performing endodontic treatment, commonly known as a root canal. The objective is to achieve a predictable, sterile, and biologically sound outcome by effectively cleaning, shaping, and sealing the root canal system. Adherence to this protocol minimizes procedural errors, reduces post-operative complications, and ensures long-term tooth preservation. All clinical staff must adhere to strict aseptic techniques and ergonomic standards throughout the procedure.

---

## Step-by-Step Clinical Checklist

## Phase 1: Pre-Operative Assessment
*   [ ] Confirm patient identity and surgical site (tooth number).
*   [ ] Review relevant medical history, specifically allergies to local anesthetics or latex.
*   [ ] Analyze diagnostic radiographs (Periapical and/or CBCT) for canal morphology and periapical pathology.
*   [ ] Perform clinical testing: Percussion, palpation, and vitality testing.
*   [ ] Explain the procedure, risks (fracture, missed canals), and expected prognosis to the patient. Obtain informed consent.

## Phase 2: Anesthesia and Isolation
*   [ ] Administer local anesthetic (infiltration or nerve block). Confirm profound anesthesia prior to proceeding.
*   [ ] Apply the dental dam. Isolation is mandatory to prevent cross-contamination and protect the airway.
*   [ ] Disinfect the tooth surface and the surrounding rubber dam with a 0.12% Chlorhexidine or equivalent solution.

## Phase 3: Access and Cleaning
*   [ ] Perform an endodontic access cavity preparation, ensuring all pulp horns are unroofed.
*   [ ] Locate all canal orifices using an endodontic explorer.
*   [ ] Coronal flaring: Create a straight-line path to the canal orifice.
*   [ ] Determine working length (WL) using an electronic apex locator (EAL) and confirm with a radiograph.
*   [ ] Perform mechanical cleaning and shaping using rotary or reciprocating instrumentation, maintaining a continuous irrigation protocol with 5.25% Sodium Hypochlorite (NaOCl).

## Phase 4: Obturation and Restoration
*   [ ] Verify the final master cone fit radiographically.
*   [ ] Dry the canals with sterile paper points.
*   [ ] Place the sealer and master gutta-percha point; compact using a warm vertical or lateral condensation technique.
*   [ ] Seal the access cavity with an intermediate restorative material or a permanent bonded restoration.
*   [ ] Take a final post-operative radiograph to verify the density and extent of the obturation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Irrigation is Key:** Mechanical instrumentation only cleans about 40-50% of the canal walls. The chemical action of sodium hypochlorite is essential for dissolving organic tissue in inaccessible areas.
*   **Patency:** Always maintain apical patency with a small K-file (size 08 or 10) to prevent the formation of a dentin plug at the apex.
*   **The Microscope Advantage:** If available, use a dental operating microscope to identify calcified canals and ensure no debris remains in the pulp chamber.

### Pitfalls
*   **Incomplete Access:** Leaving pulp horns unroofed often leads to discoloration and failed treatments due to residual bacteria.
*   **Forgetting the Irrigation:** Relying solely on mechanical files leads to "bacteriological failures." Never skip the irrigation sequence.
*   **Ignoring Coronal Seal:** A poorly sealed access cavity allows for coronal leakage, which is the leading cause of RCT failure within the first 6 months.

## FAQ

**Q1: How long should a patient expect to be in the chair for a root canal?**
A: Depending on the complexity, canal configuration, and tooth location (molar vs. anterior), a typical root canal procedure takes between 60 to 90 minutes.

**Q2: When is a second visit required for an RCT?**
A: A multi-visit approach is generally indicated if there is persistent exudate, acute apical abscess with severe symptoms, or if the canal system is highly complex or calcified, requiring calcium hydroxide inter-appointment medication.

**Q3: What should I do if I cannot find a canal?**
A: Re-evaluate your access cavity design. Use an endodontic explorer and consider using an ultrasonic tip to remove dentin shelves. If you still cannot locate the canal, consider a CBCT scan to visualize calcified pathways before proceeding further to avoid perforation.
