# Standard Operating Procedure: Heat Treatment Process Flow

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the rigorous control parameters required for the heat treatment of metallic components. The objective is to ensure metallurgical consistency, structural integrity, and compliance with mechanical specifications through a systematic, repeatable process. Adherence to these steps is mandatory to prevent material degradation, surface oxidation, and dimensional instability, ensuring that every batch meets the defined hardness, ductility, and tensile requirements.

## Phase 1: Pre-Process Preparation and Verification

*   Verify the work order against the physical material (alloy grade, serial number, and quantity).
*   Perform a visual inspection of parts to ensure freedom from oils, coolants, rust, or debris.
*   Confirm the furnace calibration is current and within the permitted variance of the last TUS (Temperature Uniformity Survey).
*   Clean all fixtures and racks to prevent cross-contamination or chemical reaction with the workpiece.
*   Load components into the furnace fixture ensuring proper spacing to allow for uniform heat distribution and airflow.

## Phase 2: Execution of Thermal Cycle

*   Set the furnace controller to the specific ramp-up, soak, and cooling parameters outlined in the project-specific heat treat specification.
*   Initiate the atmospheric control system (if applicable) to prevent decarburization or oxidation.
*   Monitor the thermocouple readouts throughout the ramp-up phase to ensure the heating rate does not exceed the maximum allowed thermal shock limits.
*   Maintain the soak duration starting only after the entire load reaches the target temperature range.
*   Execute the quenching process (oil, water, brine, or gas) within the defined "transfer time" window to avoid undesirable microstructural transformations.

## Phase 3: Post-Processing and Quality Assurance

*   Perform a thorough cleaning of the quenched parts to remove residual quenchant, preventing staining or corrosion.
*   Conduct tempering immediately following quenching to relieve internal stresses and stabilize the microstructure.
*   Allow for full ambient cooling before performing final inspections.
*   Verify hardness using a calibrated Rockwell, Brinell, or Vickers hardness tester as specified in the work instructions.
*   Log all temperature charts, cooling logs, and hardness readings into the Quality Management System (QMS).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize "witness coupons" of the same material batch as the production parts; these provide a non-destructive way to verify internal properties without compromising final components.
*   **Pro Tip:** Standardize your quenching agitation rates; inconsistent turbulence leads to inconsistent cooling rates and resulting hardness variations across the batch.
*   **Pitfall - Surface Contamination:** Failure to properly degrease parts before loading is the primary cause of soot formation and uneven surface finishing in vacuum or controlled-atmosphere furnaces.
*   **Pitfall - The Transfer Window:** Lingering too long during the transfer from the furnace to the quench tank is a common oversight that results in pearlite formation, significantly reducing the final hardness of high-carbon steels.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should thermocouple probes be checked for accuracy?**
Thermocouples should be checked or replaced based on your facility's AMS2750 or equivalent standards, typically every 3 to 12 months, depending on the operating temperature range and frequency of use.

**2. What is the most common cause of component cracking during heat treatment?**
Cracking is most often caused by excessive heating rates (thermal shock) or improper geometry leading to stress concentrations during the rapid quench phase. Ensure parts are properly supported to allow for uniform contraction.

**3. Why must tempering be performed immediately after quenching?**
As-quenched martensite is extremely brittle and contains high internal stress. Delaying the temper increases the risk of "quench cracking" or spontaneous failure of the component while sitting in inventory.
