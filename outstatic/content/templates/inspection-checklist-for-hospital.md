# Standard Operating Procedure: Hospital Facility Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the rigorous protocols required for conducting a comprehensive facility inspection within a hospital environment. The primary objective of this inspection is to ensure compliance with healthcare safety standards (e.g., The Joint Commission, CMS, OSHA), maintain infection control integrity, and guarantee optimal patient safety. This document is intended for use by facility managers, safety officers, and clinical leadership to identify hazards, verify equipment functionality, and maintain environmental standards.

## 1. Infection Control & Environmental Hygiene
*   **Surface Integrity:** Inspect walls, ceilings, and flooring for cracks, water damage, or peeling paint that could harbor pathogens.
*   **Hand Hygiene Stations:** Verify all alcohol-based hand rub dispensers are functional, filled, and accessible. Ensure sink areas are free of debris and soap dispensers are operational.
*   **Waste Management:** Confirm that biohazard and sharps containers are not overfilled (do not exceed the fill line) and are properly labeled and secured.
*   **Air Quality & HVAC:** Check for visible dust or mold in air vents. Ensure negative pressure rooms (for airborne precautions) are displaying correct monitoring status.
*   **Cleaning Documentation:** Audit logbooks in patient rooms and common areas to ensure compliance with terminal cleaning schedules.

## 2. Life Safety & Fire Prevention
*   **Egress Paths:** Verify that all corridors are free of "clutter" (carts, wheelchairs, or equipment) to maintain the required clear width for patient transport and emergency evacuation.
*   **Fire Doors:** Test that all fire and smoke doors latch correctly and are never propped open with wedges.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Ensure units are charged, the pin is intact, the gauge is in the green zone, and they have been inspected within the last 30 days.
*   **Sprinkler Clearance:** Verify that no storage or decorative items are within 18 inches of sprinkler heads.
*   **Exit Signage:** Confirm all emergency exit signs are illuminated and visible from all points in the hallway.

## 3. Clinical Equipment & Biomedical Safety
*   **Electrical Cords:** Inspect power cords for fraying, cuts, or exposed wires. Ensure no "daisy-chaining" of power strips or extension cords.
*   **Emergency Power (Red Outlets):** Confirm that life-critical equipment is plugged into red (generator-backed) outlets.
*   **Crash Carts:** Verify that the tamper-evident seal is intact and that the expiration date of the earliest-expiring item is clearly visible.
*   **Calibration Labels:** Check medical devices (e.g., vitals monitors, infusion pumps) for current calibration/preventative maintenance stickers.

## 4. Patient Care Area & Security
*   **HIPAA Compliance:** Ensure no Protected Health Information (PHI) is left unattended on counters or whiteboards in public view.
*   **Medication Security:** Verify that medication rooms/carts are locked when unattended and that access logs are maintained.
*   **Patient Fall Hazards:** Ensure call buttons are functional and within reach, and that bed brakes are engaged when the bed is stationary.
*   **Visitor Management:** Check that signage directing visitors to the check-in desk is clear and security protocols are being followed.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Shadow" Method:** Always perform inspections at different times of the day. A unit may look immaculate during a morning shift but become cluttered and disorganized during the high-stress turnover of a shift change.
*   **Pro Tip: Staff Engagement:** Treat the inspection as a "consultative" process rather than a policing one. Ask staff, "What is one thing that makes your job harder or less safe?" to uncover issues the checklist might miss.
*   **Pitfall: The "Clipboarding" Trap:** Do not just tick boxes. If you see a deficiency, document the corrective action immediately. A checklist with 100% "Pass" marks in a busy hospital is often an indicator of a lazy inspector.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Ceiling Tiles:** Inspectors frequently forget to look up. Missing or misaligned ceiling tiles are a major infection control violation (dust migration) and a fire code issue.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should a full facility inspection be conducted?**
A: While daily "rounds" should be conducted by unit leads, a formal, comprehensive facility-wide inspection should occur at least monthly to ensure long-term compliance.

**Q: What should I do if I find a critical violation (e.g., a blocked fire exit)?**
A: Address it immediately. Clear the obstruction yourself or contact the facilities department to remove it before leaving the area. Do not wait to report it in a document; the risk to patient safety is immediate.

**Q: Is it acceptable to mark an item as "Not Applicable"?**
A: Yes, provided the reason is documented. For example, a non-patient care area would not require a crash cart inspection. However, ensure that the justification is noted in the inspection log to satisfy auditors.
