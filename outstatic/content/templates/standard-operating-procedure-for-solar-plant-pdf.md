# Standard Operating Procedure: Solar Photovoltaic (PV) Plant Operations

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the daily, monthly, and periodic maintenance of a grid-connected solar photovoltaic plant. The objective is to maximize energy yield, ensure personnel safety, and maintain the longevity of critical assets including PV modules, inverters, and switchgear. All site technicians must adhere to these guidelines to ensure compliance with Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) specifications and safety regulations.

## 1. Daily Inspection & Monitoring
*   **SCADA/Remote Monitoring:** Review the central control room dashboard to verify inverter output vs. expected yield based on pyranometer data.
*   **Alarm Audit:** Investigate any "Communication Loss" or "Low Insulation Resistance" alerts immediately.
*   **Visual Site Walkthrough:** Check for perimeter fence integrity and ensure no unauthorized debris is near the transformer yard.
*   **Weather Log:** Record local irradiance and temperature data to correlate with daily generation trends.

## 2. Weekly Preventive Maintenance
*   **Inverter Vents:** Inspect inverter cooling fans and air intake filters for dust accumulation; vacuum or replace as needed.
*   **Cable Ties:** Check for loose or sagging DC/AC cabling; secure all hanging wires with UV-resistant cable ties.
*   **Vegetation Management:** Ensure grass and shrubs are trimmed to prevent shading of the lower module rows.
*   **Pest Control:** Inspect conduit entries and electrical cabinets for rodent damage or nesting materials.

## 3. Monthly Mechanical & Electrical Checks
*   **Module Cleaning:** Perform wet cleaning of panels if soiling ratios exceed 3%, ensuring water hardness levels are within manufacturer specs to prevent mineral deposits.
*   **Tightening Torque:** Use a calibrated torque wrench to check the tightness of module mounting clamps and structural bolts.
*   **String Monitoring:** Compare voltage and current levels across all strings to identify potential PID (Potential Induced Degradation) or shading issues.
*   **Combiner Box Inspection:** Inspect DC combiner boxes for signs of heat stress, moisture ingress, or burnt busbar terminals.

## 4. Quarterly Safety & Compliance Audit
*   **PPE Verification:** Audit the site safety kit, including insulated mats, arc-flash gloves, and fire extinguishers (CO2/Dry Powder).
*   **Grounding/Earthing:** Measure earthing pit resistance using a clamp-on earth tester; values must remain below 1 Ohm.
*   **Emergency Stop Test:** Physically trigger the Inverter Emergency Stop button to ensure the grid disconnect functions properly.
*   **Document Management:** Update the site logbook and upload digital reports to the centralized asset management system.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Use thermal imaging (infrared thermography) during peak sun hours at least once a quarter. This is the fastest way to identify "hot spots" on panels or loose connections in terminal blocks before they result in fire or catastrophic failure.
*   **Pitfall - Micro-cracking:** Avoid walking on PV panels during cleaning or inspections. Even if the glass doesn't break, the weight can cause microscopic cell fractures that reduce efficiency by 10–15% over time.
*   **Pitfall - Water Quality:** Never use high-pressure washers directly on the panels, as high-impact water can compromise the edge seals, leading to delamination and moisture ingress.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "Golden String" log. Keep a history of the highest-performing string on-site to use as a baseline for comparing the health of other strings.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should solar panels be cleaned?**
A: Frequency depends on your location. In arid or high-dust areas, monthly cleaning is recommended. In regions with frequent rainfall and low dust, quarterly cleaning is usually sufficient.

**Q: What is the most common cause of inverter failure?**
A: Overheating due to clogged air filters or blocked ventilation intakes is the leading cause. Regular cleaning of intake filters is critical to extending inverter life.

**Q: Can I use tap water for module cleaning?**
A: Only if the water is tested to be "soft." High mineral content (hard water) leaves a calcium carbonate film on the glass, which creates a permanent "tint" that reduces light transmission and invites faster soiling. Use deionized or demineralized water for optimal results.
