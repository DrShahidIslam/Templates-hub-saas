# Standard Operating Procedure: Diesel Generator Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance procedures for diesel-powered backup generators. Regular maintenance is critical to ensure operational reliability, extend the lifespan of the equipment, and ensure the generator starts instantly during a power failure. This document is intended for qualified technicians; all procedures must be performed in compliance with site safety regulations and manufacturer-specific service manuals.

## 1. Safety and Preparation
*   Ensure the generator is in the "OFF" or "MANUAL" mode to prevent accidental startup during maintenance.
*   Disconnect the starter battery negative terminal.
*   Allow the engine to cool down completely before touching components.
*   Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety goggles, gloves, and ear protection if testing.
*   Keep a spill kit nearby to manage potential oil or coolant leaks.

## 2. Lubrication and Cooling System
*   **Engine Oil:** Check oil levels using the dipstick. Verify the oil is free of milky discoloration (a sign of coolant contamination). Change oil and filters according to the manufacturer’s hourly interval (typically every 250–500 hours).
*   **Coolant System:** Inspect radiator fins for debris or blockages. Check coolant levels in the expansion tank and verify the concentration of antifreeze/corrosion inhibitor. Inspect all hoses for signs of brittleness, cracking, or swelling.
*   **Belts:** Check drive belts for proper tension and signs of fraying or cracking. Replace if wear is evident.

## 3. Fuel System
*   **Fuel Levels:** Verify that the main tank is filled to at least 90% capacity to prevent condensation buildup.
*   **Fuel Filters:** Drain water separators daily (if applicable) or replace primary and secondary fuel filters per the maintenance schedule.
*   **Fuel Lines:** Inspect all fuel lines and fittings for signs of leakage or "weeping."
*   **Fuel Quality:** Check for signs of microbial growth or sludge in the fuel tank; utilize fuel additives if the generator experiences long periods of inactivity.

## 4. Electrical and Starting System
*   **Battery:** Clean battery terminals and apply a thin layer of petroleum jelly to prevent corrosion. Check the state of charge using a hydrometer or digital battery tester.
*   **Charger:** Verify that the battery charger is functioning and maintaining the float voltage recommended by the battery manufacturer.
*   **Wiring:** Inspect all control panel wiring, sensors, and terminal blocks for loose connections or signs of heat damage/arcing.

## 5. Air Intake and Exhaust
*   **Air Filters:** Inspect the air cleaner element. Replace if dirty or clogged, especially in dusty environments. Ensure the air intake housing is sealed tightly.
*   **Exhaust:** Check for exhaust leaks at the manifold and along the entire piping run. Ensure the rain cap on the exterior exhaust stack is functioning correctly to prevent water entry.

## 6. Functional Testing (Post-Maintenance)
*   Reconnect the battery and set the generator to "AUTO."
*   Perform a "no-load" run for at least 15 minutes to verify temperature and oil pressure stability.
*   Check the control panel for any active alarms or warnings.
*   Record all findings, parts replaced, and running hours in the maintenance logbook.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always perform a "Load Bank Test" annually. This ensures the generator can handle its rated output and prevents "wet stacking" (unburnt fuel buildup in the exhaust) caused by only running the generator at low or no load.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not ignore small oil leaks. A minor leak often indicates a degrading seal that could fail catastrophically under the vibration of a full-load startup.
*   **Pro Tip:** Keep a log of fuel delivery dates. If diesel sits in a tank for more than 12 months without treatment, it can degrade and clog fuel injectors.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I run my generator if it doesn't experience power outages?**
A: It is recommended to run the generator under load at least once a month for 30 minutes to ensure components remain lubricated and the battery remains charged.

**Q: What is "wet stacking" and how can I avoid it?**
A: Wet stacking is the buildup of unburnt fuel in the exhaust system. It is avoided by ensuring the generator is periodically run at a significant percentage of its rated capacity (Load Bank Testing).

**Q: Can I use automotive-grade engine oil for my diesel generator?**
A: No. Always use the specific oil viscosity and API (American Petroleum Institute) service classification recommended by the generator manufacturer, as diesel engines operate at higher pressures and temperatures than gasoline engines.
