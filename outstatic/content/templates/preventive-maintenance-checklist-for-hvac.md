# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting comprehensive preventive maintenance on commercial and residential HVAC systems. The objective of this procedure is to ensure optimal equipment efficiency, extend the operational lifecycle of units, improve indoor air quality, and mitigate the risk of catastrophic system failure. Technicians are expected to adhere to these safety standards and verification steps during every scheduled service interval.

## Section 1: Safety & Initial Assessment
*   **Safety Gear:** Ensure all personnel are equipped with appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including safety glasses, cut-resistant gloves, and voltage testers.
*   **Power Isolation:** Confirm the electrical disconnect is set to the "OFF" position and utilize Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures before opening cabinet panels.
*   **System Functionality Test:** Prior to shutdown, run the system in both heating and cooling modes to identify any existing performance anomalies or abnormal noises.
*   **Refrigerant Check:** Visually inspect for oil stains or signs of refrigerant leaks at joints and connections.

## Section 2: Air Handling & Filtration
*   **Filter Replacement:** Replace or clean air filters. Verify the correct MERV rating is utilized for the specific equipment requirements.
*   **Coil Inspection:** Inspect evaporator and condenser coils for dirt, debris, or biological growth. Clean coils using an approved non-acidic foaming cleaner.
*   **Drainage Systems:** Clear the condensate drain line using a shop-vac or pressurized air. Verify the drain pan is free of standing water and algae buildup; treat with an antimicrobial tablet.
*   **Blower Assembly:** Inspect the blower motor and wheel. Clean dust from blades to maintain balance and airflow efficiency.

## Section 3: Electrical & Mechanical Components
*   **Connection Integrity:** Inspect all electrical terminals and connectors for signs of overheating, charring, or loose wiring. Tighten connections as needed.
*   **Capacitor Testing:** Measure microfarad (MFD) ratings on capacitors. Replace any capacitor that is outside of the manufacturer’s rated tolerance (typically ±5-10%).
*   **Contactors:** Inspect contactor points for pitting or arcing. Replace if heavy carbon buildup is detected.
*   **Belts & Pulleys:** Check fan belts for proper tension and signs of cracking or fraying. Replace belts every 12 months or if tension cannot be properly maintained.
*   **Lubrication:** If applicable, apply manufacturer-recommended lubricant to motor bearings.

## Section 4: Performance Verification & Reporting
*   **Temperature Differential:** Measure the delta T (supply air vs. return air) to ensure the system is transferring heat within design specifications.
*   **Amperage Draw:** Check the amperage of the compressor and motors against the nameplate data (RLA/FLA) to ensure the system is not overworking.
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Verify that the thermostat is correctly calibrated and programmed for the current seasonal requirements.
*   **Documentation:** Complete the digital maintenance log, including all voltage/amperage readings, replaced parts, and recommended future repairs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the "before and after" pressures for cleaning; clients appreciate seeing the performance delta.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an infrared camera to quickly identify hot spots in electrical panels before they become fire hazards.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not use high-pressure power washers on delicate aluminum fins, as this can cause them to collapse, severely restricting airflow.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "small" sounds. A slight rattle is often a sign of a failing bearing that will lead to an emergency shutdown in peak season.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should HVAC preventive maintenance be performed?**
For commercial systems, quarterly maintenance is recommended. For residential systems, a semi-annual check—once in the spring for cooling and once in the fall for heating—is the industry standard.

**2. What is the most common cause of HVAC system failure?**
The most frequent cause is restricted airflow due to dirty filters or obstructed coils, which puts excessive strain on the compressor and fan motors, eventually leading to premature burnout.

**3. Should I change the thermostat settings during maintenance?**
Technicians should verify the thermostat is set to the client's preferred schedule but should ensure the "fan" setting is on "auto" rather than "on" to prevent humidity issues in the summer months.
