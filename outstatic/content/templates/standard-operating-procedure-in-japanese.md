# Standard Operating Procedure: Creating SOPs in Japanese (日本語の標準作業手順書作成)

This document outlines the professional requirements for drafting, reviewing, and maintaining Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) within a Japanese business context. Creating an effective Japanese SOP requires a balance of linguistic precision (using *keigo* where appropriate), cultural adherence to *Kaizen* (continuous improvement), and structural clarity to ensure compliance across all operational levels.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Structuring
*   **Define Scope and Objectives:** Clearly state the purpose of the SOP. Use the format [目的 (Purpose)] followed by the [適用範囲 (Scope)].
*   **Identify Target Audience:** Determine if the document is for internal staff, external vendors, or compliance auditors. Adjust terminology (technical vs. general) accordingly.
*   **Establish Key Terminology:** Create a glossary of technical terms or industry-specific vocabulary to ensure consistency throughout the document.
*   **Determine Hierarchy:** Organize the document using the standard Japanese document structure: Header, Purpose, Scope, Definitions, Procedures, Exceptions, and Revision History.

## Phase 2: Drafting the Content
*   **Use Clear and Concise Language:** Utilize *Desu/Masu* form for general instructions or *Dearu* form for formal, internal manuals. Maintain consistent sentence endings.
*   **Numbering and Bullet Points:** Utilize logical hierarchies (1., 1.1, 1.1.1) to guide the reader through the workflow.
*   **Incorporate Visual Aids:** Include flowcharts (*gyomu nagare-zu*) and screenshots. Japanese manuals rely heavily on visual cues for complex tasks.
*   **Standardize Nouns and Verbs:** Ensure that specific tool names or actions (e.g., "submit" as 提出 vs 送付) are consistent throughout the entire document.

## Phase 3: Review and Approval Workflow
*   **Self-Correction (Check for Logic Gaps):** Read the document aloud to ensure the instructions flow logically without ambiguity.
*   **Peer Review (*Nemawashi*):** Circulate the draft among subject matter experts to gather feedback before official publication.
*   **Obtain Sign-offs:** Ensure the document is approved by the relevant department head, marked with an *Inkan* (stamp) or digital signature.
*   **Final Formatting:** Ensure the document adheres to company branding guidelines, including font (typically MS Mincho or Meiryo) and margin specifications.

## Phase 4: Maintenance and Continuous Improvement
*   **Version Control:** Clearly mark the document with a version number and the date of the last revision (e.g., Ver 1.0, 2023/10/27).
*   **Periodic Audits:** Schedule reviews every 6–12 months to verify the process still reflects current operational reality.
*   **Employee Feedback Loop:** Create a mechanism for employees to report discrepancies or suggest improvements (*Kaizen suggestions*).

# Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Use "Shiji-sho" style:** If the SOP involves high-risk tasks, structure it as a "Work Instruction" (*Sagyo shiji-sho*) which uses imperative forms for absolute clarity.
*   **Pro Tip: Leverage Icons:** Use standard Japanese hazard symbols or UI icons (e.g., ⚠️ for Caution/注意) to break up dense text.
*   **Pitfall: Ambiguity:** Avoid vague terms like "soon" (まもなく) or "carefully" (丁寧に). Instead, use quantitative metrics (e.g., "within 10 minutes" or "verify against Checklist A").
*   **Pitfall: Cultural Context:** Do not over-translate English idioms; ensure the Japanese reflects natural business communication patterns.

# Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I use English terms for software or technical jargon?**
A: Yes, if the software interface is in English, keep the button labels in English within the document (e.g., 「Save」ボタンをクリックします) to avoid confusion during actual system operation.

**Q: How do I handle the "Responsibility" section in a Japanese SOP?**
A: Use the 5W1H method to define who is responsible for each step. Assign specific roles (*yakuwari*) rather than individual names to ensure the SOP remains valid even if personnel change.

**Q: Is it necessary to include an "Exceptions" section?**
A: Absolutely. In Japanese business culture, the *Exception* (*Tokurei*) is often where errors occur. Explicitly stating what to do when a system is down or an unexpected event occurs is critical for operational stability.
