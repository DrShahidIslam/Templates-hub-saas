# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance (PM) Execution

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized framework for executing preventive maintenance (PM) across all operational assets. The primary objective of this procedure is to enhance equipment reliability, extend asset lifecycles, and minimize unplanned downtime by performing systematic inspections, adjustments, and replacements before functional failure occurs. All personnel must adhere to these guidelines to ensure safety compliance and data integrity within the Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS).

## Phase 1: Pre-Maintenance Preparation
*   **Safety Review:** Conduct a Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) assessment and secure all necessary Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
*   **Work Order Verification:** Retrieve the digital or physical work order and verify the asset ID against the database.
*   **Resource Staging:** Gather required tools, lubricants, replacement parts, and cleaning supplies as specified in the asset's technical manual.
*   **Communication:** Notify the department supervisor that the equipment will be taken offline and confirm that the work area is cleared.

## Phase 2: Inspection and Cleaning
*   **Debris Removal:** Clean the external casing, vents, and surrounding area to allow for proper heat dissipation and visual assessment.
*   **Visual Integrity Check:** Inspect all housing, frame, and mounting bolts for cracks, corrosion, or signs of loose fasteners.
*   **Fluid Analysis:** Check levels and visual condition (color/viscosity) of all hydraulic fluids, coolants, and lubricants.
*   **Filter Inspection:** Remove air, fuel, or hydraulic filters to check for contaminants. Replace if debris exceeds the allowable threshold.

## Phase 3: Mechanical and Electrical Verification
*   **Lubrication:** Apply specified grease or oil to all designated points (bearings, gears, linkages) according to the manufacturer’s intervals.
*   **Calibration Check:** Compare current sensor output/readings against baseline factory specifications.
*   **Electrical Continuity:** Inspect wiring harnesses for fraying or heat damage; ensure all terminal connections are tight and free of oxidation.
*   **Drive Components:** Check tension on belts, chains, and pulleys. Adjust or replace components showing signs of dry rot or excessive stretching.

## Phase 4: Operational Testing and Documentation
*   **Functional Run:** Re-energize the machine and observe a full operational cycle under light load.
*   **Noise/Vibration Audit:** Listen for abnormal grinding, squealing, or vibration patterns that indicate misalignments or bearing failure.
*   **Final Documentation:** Close the work order in the CMMS, logging all parts used, total labor hours, and any "As-Found" vs. "As-Left" condition notes.
*   **Safety Clearance:** Remove LOTO tags and return the equipment to production-ready status.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always utilize a "Condition-Based" approach. If a component is showing minimal wear, document it for future monitoring rather than replacing it prematurely, which saves overhead costs.
*   **Pro Tip:** Take photos of the internal components before and after service. This builds a historical visual database that is invaluable for diagnosing future intermittent faults.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-lubrication. Applying too much grease is a common error that can blow out bearing seals and lead to premature failure. Follow the manufacturer's specific volume instructions exactly.
*   **Pitfall:** "Pencil-whipping." Never sign off on a checklist item without physically verifying it. Falsifying maintenance data creates a catastrophic risk to personnel safety.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should the preventive maintenance schedule be reviewed?**
A: PM intervals should be reviewed at least annually or immediately following a significant change in production load, operating environment, or manufacturer update.

**Q: What should I do if I find an issue not covered in the PM checklist?**
A: Document the observation in the "Comments" section of the work order and immediately initiate a separate "Corrective Maintenance" (CM) work order to address the discrepancy.

**Q: Is it better to perform PM based on time or actual usage?**
A: Wherever possible, base PM intervals on actual usage metrics (e.g., run hours, cycle counts, or mileage). Time-based maintenance is only recommended for equipment with low or highly predictable usage cycles.
