# Standard Operating Procedure: Quarterly Preventive Maintenance (QPM)

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for conducting Quarterly Preventive Maintenance (QPM) across all facility assets and equipment. The objective of this procedure is to identify potential failure points, ensure operational safety, optimize equipment performance, and extend the lifespan of critical infrastructure. Adherence to this checklist is mandatory for all maintenance personnel to ensure compliance with safety standards and operational efficiency.

## 1. Documentation & Preparation
*   Verify the current asset inventory and maintenance history logs.
*   Secure necessary Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) including gloves, eye protection, and high-visibility vests.
*   Assemble required testing instruments (multimeters, infrared thermometers, pressure gauges, and vibration sensors).
*   Notify relevant department heads of upcoming downtime to minimize workflow disruption.
*   Review all safety data sheets (SDS) for any chemical agents used during cleaning or lubrication.

## 2. Mechanical & Structural Integrity
*   Inspect all primary fasteners, bolts, and mounting hardware; tighten to manufacturer-specified torque if loose.
*   Examine drive belts and pulleys for signs of fraying, cracking, or misalignment; adjust tension as required.
*   Verify integrity of structural enclosures and protective guards; replace any damaged safety shields.
*   Listen for anomalous noises (grinding, squealing, or knocking) during standard operational cycle testing.
*   Conduct a visual inspection of moving parts for excessive wear or metal fatigue.

## 3. Electrical & Control Systems
*   Inspect electrical cabinets for signs of overheating, discolored wiring, or burnt odors.
*   Verify that all wire terminations are secure and free of corrosion.
*   Clean air intake filters on control panels to prevent dust buildup and thermal throttling.
*   Test emergency stop (E-Stop) buttons and interlocks to ensure immediate system response.
*   Check battery backups/UPS systems for charge levels and error codes.

## 4. Lubrication & Fluid Management
*   Check fluid levels for all reservoirs (hydraulic, coolant, oil); top off using manufacturer-approved fluids.
*   Inspect all hoses, fittings, and seals for visible leaks or signs of seepage.
*   Apply grease to designated lubrication points using the correct grade of lubricant per the maintenance manual.
*   Examine breather caps and filters; replace if saturated or contaminated.

## 5. Calibration & Final Verification
*   Verify that all sensors and gauges are reading within the specified operational tolerances.
*   Perform a "Full Cycle" test under load to ensure equipment performance meets baseline metrics.
*   Update the Central Maintenance Management System (CMMS) with completion dates, findings, and parts replaced.
*   Apply "Maintenance Performed" tags and return the equipment to operational status.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always take "Before and After" photos during the maintenance cycle. These are invaluable for insurance audits and identifying slow-degrading trends in equipment performance.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement a "Clean-as-you-go" policy. Most mechanical failures are first signaled by an accumulation of dust or debris that obscures the problem.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-lubrication. Adding too much grease can damage seals and cause bearings to overheat; always adhere to the specific milliliter or stroke recommendation from the OEM.
*   **Pitfall:** Skipping the E-stop test. Never assume a safety feature is functional just because it was working three months ago.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if I find a critical fault that is not on the checklist?**
A: Cease the preventive maintenance immediately, tag the machine as "Out of Service," and escalate the issue to the Maintenance Supervisor for an emergency repair order.

**Q: Are these checklists applicable to all types of equipment?**
A: This is a foundational template. You must customize specific sections based on individual Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) manuals, as specialized equipment may require proprietary maintenance protocols.

**Q: How long should maintenance records be retained?**
A: Per operational standards, all maintenance logs, repair receipts, and inspection reports should be retained for a minimum of five years or for the duration of the equipment's warranty period, whichever is longer.
