# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Safety Audit

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements for conducting a comprehensive electrical safety audit. The objective is to identify potential hazards, ensure compliance with OSHA and NFPA 70E standards, and mitigate risks associated with electrical fires, arc flashes, and electrocution. This audit must be performed by qualified personnel or certified third-party inspectors on an annual basis or following any significant modification to the electrical infrastructure.

## 1. Documentation and Administrative Review
*   Verify that current, accurate single-line diagrams are posted and updated.
*   Confirm the presence of a documented Electrical Safety Program (ESP).
*   Review training logs to ensure all personnel have up-to-date NFPA 70E and LOTO (Lockout/Tagout) certification.
*   Inspect incident report logs for recurring electrical issues or "near misses."
*   Ensure all electrical equipment has valid inspection tags and documented preventive maintenance schedules.

## 2. Physical Infrastructure and Distribution Equipment
*   **Switchgear and Panels:** Check for visible signs of overheating (discoloration/melting), moisture, or corrosion. 
*   **Clearance Zones:** Verify that a minimum of 36 inches of dedicated clearance is maintained in front of all electrical panels. Ensure no storage items are blocking access.
*   **Labels and Signage:** Inspect all panels for clear, legible arc-flash hazard labels, including voltage levels and boundary distances.
*   **Circuit Breakers/Fuses:** Confirm all breakers are properly labeled, and ensure no open slots exist in panel covers (use blank fillers).
*   **Grounding and Bonding:** Verify that all metallic conduits, enclosures, and non-current-carrying parts are properly grounded.

## 3. Workplace Wiring and Utilization Equipment
*   **Cords and Plugs:** Perform a tactile inspection of all flexible cords for cuts, abrasions, or exposed conductors. Prohibit the use of daisy-chained power strips.
*   **GFCI Protection:** Test all Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCI) in wet locations (kitchens, restrooms, outdoor areas) to ensure they trip within specified timeframes.
*   **Receptacles:** Ensure faceplates are intact and receptacles are securely mounted. Check for "loose" plugs that indicate worn-out contact springs.
*   **Fixed Equipment:** Ensure all machinery is properly bonded to the equipment grounding conductor.

## 4. Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Compliance
*   Verify that LOTO stations are fully stocked with standardized locks, tags, and hasps.
*   Observe a live LOTO procedure to ensure the "Zero Energy State" is verified using a calibrated voltmeter.
*   Check that energy control procedures are specific to the machine and easily accessible to authorized operators.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an infrared thermal imaging camera during peak load times to identify "hot spots" in panels or connections before they fail.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a digital inventory of all electrical assets to track the age and maintenance history of individual units.
*   **Pitfall - The "Storage Trap":** Most electrical fires in commercial buildings are caused by storing combustible materials in electrical rooms. Never allow this.
*   **Pitfall - Misinterpreting Standards:** Do not rely on outdated labels. Always re-calculate Arc Flash boundaries whenever the facility's power distribution configuration changes.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I conduct an electrical safety audit?**
A: A comprehensive formal audit should be conducted at least annually. However, informal "walk-through" inspections should occur monthly.

**Q: What is the most common violation found during these audits?**
A: The most frequent violation is the failure to maintain the 36-inch clearance requirement in front of electrical panels (storing boxes or trash near the gear).

**Q: Do I need a professional electrician to perform this audit?**
A: While a facility manager can perform basic visual audits, any testing involving opening energized panels or verifying grounding integrity must be performed by a qualified, licensed electrician.
