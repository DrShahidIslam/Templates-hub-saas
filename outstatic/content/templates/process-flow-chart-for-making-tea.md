# Standard Operating Procedure: Standardized Tea Preparation Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic methodology for the preparation of high-quality hot tea. By standardizing water temperature, infusion time, and equipment hygiene, this procedure ensures product consistency, minimizes resource waste, and enhances the overall sensory profile of the beverage. This protocol is applicable to all staff members involved in beverage service.

## 1. Preparation and Mise-en-Place
*   Verify the cleanliness of the workstation and sanitize all preparation surfaces.
*   Check water supply; ensure the kettle is free of mineral buildup.
*   Gather necessary components: fresh water, tea (loose-leaf or sachet), a heat-resistant vessel (teapot or mug), a thermometer, and a stirring implement.
*   Ensure the serving vessel is pre-heated by rinsing with hot water to prevent thermal shock and rapid heat loss during the infusion process.

## 2. Water Heating and Temperature Calibration
*   Fill the kettle with fresh, cold, filtered water (avoid re-boiling stagnant water to ensure oxygenation).
*   Heat water to the specific temperature dictated by tea variety:
    *   **White/Green Tea:** 170°F–180°F (75°C–80°C).
    *   **Oolong Tea:** 185°F–205°F (85°C–95°C).
    *   **Black/Herbal Tea:** 208°F–212°F (98°C–100°C).
*   Utilize an infrared or probe thermometer to verify the temperature before pouring.

## 3. Infusion and Steeping Execution
*   Measure the tea accurately: Standard ratio is 2 grams of tea per 8 ounces (240ml) of water.
*   Pour water over the tea leaves or sachet; initiate the timer immediately.
*   Steeping durations based on variety:
    *   **Green Tea:** 2–3 minutes.
    *   **Black Tea:** 3–5 minutes.
    *   **Herbal Tea:** 5–7 minutes.
*   Keep the vessel covered during steeping to maintain temperature and essential oil integrity.

## 4. Extraction and Final Presentation
*   Remove the tea leaves or sachet promptly once the timer sounds to prevent over-extraction and the release of excess tannins (bitterness).
*   Perform a gentle stir to ensure uniform distribution of flavors if additives (milk, sugar, honey) are required.
*   Check the visual clarity and aroma before service.
*   Dispose of used tea materials in the appropriate compost or waste receptacle.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The Tannin Trap:** Never squeeze a tea bag against the side of the mug. This releases concentrated tannins that impart a harsh, astringent flavor. Simply remove it cleanly.
*   **Water Quality:** If your tap water has a high mineral content or chlorine, always use a charcoal-filtered pitcher. Impure water will mask the delicate floral or vegetal notes of the tea.
*   **Temperature Sensitivity:** Using boiling water on delicate green tea leaves will "burn" them, resulting in a bitter, astringent brew. Always adhere to the temperature calibration guidelines.
*   **Equipment Maintenance:** Ensure your kettle is descaled monthly. Mineral deposits (limescale) can negatively influence water chemistry and heat transfer efficiency.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I put the tea bag in before or after the water?**
A: For maximum control, always pour the water into the vessel first, then add the tea. This allows you to monitor the water temperature accurately before the tea enters, preventing premature steeping.

**Q: Can I re-steep my tea leaves?**
A: Yes, many high-quality loose-leaf teas (specifically Oolong and Green teas) are designed for multiple infusions. Adjust the second steeping time by adding 30–60 seconds to the original duration.

**Q: Why does my tea sometimes have an oily film on the surface?**
A: This is usually caused by high calcium content in the water reacting with the tea’s natural polyphenols. It is non-toxic but can be avoided by using filtered or distilled water.
