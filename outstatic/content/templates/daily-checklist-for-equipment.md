# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Equipment Inspection & Maintenance

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory daily inspection requirements for all operational equipment. The primary objective of this protocol is to ensure workplace safety, optimize equipment performance, minimize unplanned downtime, and extend the total lifecycle of company assets. All operators are required to perform these checks at the start of every shift and record the findings in the digital asset management system. Failure to document these inspections or the operation of faulty equipment is a direct violation of safety policy.

## Daily Equipment Inspection Checklist

## Phase 1: Pre-Operational Safety Review
*   **Safety Guards & Shields:** Ensure all protective casings, safety guards, and emergency stop mechanisms are intact and unobstructed.
*   **Warning Indicators:** Verify that all safety decals, warning labels, and high-visibility markers are legible and present.
*   **Clearance Areas:** Confirm the immediate work area is free of debris, liquid spills, or extraneous materials that could cause a slip, trip, or fall.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Verify that the operator is wearing the required PPE specific to the equipment being inspected.

## Phase 2: Mechanical & Electrical Systems
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check all reservoirs (coolant, lubricant, hydraulic fluid) against manufacturer-specified fill lines.
*   **Leak Detection:** Inspect the floor beneath the equipment for signs of active leaking (oil, coolant, or fuel).
*   **Electrical Integrity:** Examine power cords for fraying, exposed wiring, or heat damage. Ensure plugs are fully seated and free of corrosion.
*   **Drive Systems:** Check belts, chains, and gears for proper tension and signs of excessive wear or unusual slack.
*   **Hardware:** Conduct a visual "wiggle test" on accessible bolts, screws, and fasteners to identify vibrations or loose components.

## Phase 3: Operational Functionality
*   **Startup Sequence:** Initiate startup and listen for abnormal noises (grinding, whining, or rattling) that indicate internal mechanical failure.
*   **Control Panel Calibration:** Ensure all toggle switches, digital touchscreens, and joysticks are responsive and correctly calibrated.
*   **Emergency Stop Test:** Cycle the E-stop button (if permitted by safety protocol) to ensure the power-kill sequence engages instantly.
*   **Cycle Completion:** Run one test cycle under no-load conditions to observe that all moving parts track and terminate as programmed.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Use the 'Five-Senses' Method.** Don't just look; listen for odd rhythmic sounds, smell for burning plastic/electronics, and feel for excessive heat or vibration during the warm-up cycle.
*   **Pro Tip: Documentation is Evidence.** If you find a defect, take a high-resolution photo and upload it to the maintenance log immediately. This creates a paper trail for maintenance teams to prioritize repairs.
*   **Pitfall: The 'Normalization of Deviance.'** Do not ignore minor "quirks" (e.g., a sticky switch or a faint rattle) just because the machine "still works." These are almost always early warning signs of a major component failure.
*   **Pitfall: Rushing the Walkaround.** Never perform the inspection while the machine is mid-cycle. Rushing the check is the most common cause of workplace injuries. Dedicate the first five minutes of your shift exclusively to the machine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What should I do if I discover a fault during the daily inspection?**
A: Immediately tag the equipment with an "Out of Service" sign, document the specific issue in the digital log, and notify your direct supervisor. Do not attempt to operate the equipment under any circumstances until it has been cleared by a qualified maintenance technician.

**Q: Am I responsible for performing repairs if I find a loose bolt?**
A: Only if you have been certified as a maintenance technician for that specific asset. For operators, the responsibility is to identify and report. Unauthorized maintenance can void equipment warranties and create significant safety liabilities.

**Q: What if the equipment passes the visual inspection but sounds "off" during operation?**
A: You must stop the operation immediately. Professional judgment is expected; if the equipment’s performance deviates from the established baseline, it is safer to cease operation and report the anomaly than to risk secondary damage to the equipment or injury to yourself.
