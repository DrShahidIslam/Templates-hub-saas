# Standard Operating Procedure: Assembly Operations

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the assembly of components into finished products. The objective of this document is to ensure consistency, maximize operational efficiency, maintain high quality standards, and prioritize personnel safety. Adherence to these procedures is required for all production staff to minimize variance, reduce rework, and ensure that every unit meets defined engineering specifications.

## 1. Pre-Assembly Preparation
*   **Workspace Sanitation:** Ensure the workstation is clear of debris, scrap, and tools from previous shifts. 
*   **Tool Calibration:** Verify that all torque drivers, pneumatic tools, and measuring instruments are calibrated and within their current validity period.
*   **Documentation Review:** Review the specific Assembly Drawing (AD) and Bill of Materials (BOM) assigned to the current batch.
*   **Material Staging:** Confirm all components are present, labeled, and free of physical defects or damage prior to commencing assembly.
*   **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Don required gear, including anti-static wrist straps, safety glasses, and gloves as dictated by the specific product requirements.

## 2. Assembly Execution
*   **Component Verification:** Validate part numbers against the BOM before installation.
*   **Sequential Assembly:** Follow the step-by-step assembly sequence exactly as outlined in the Work Instruction (WI) document. Do not deviate from the sequence to "save time."
*   **Fastener Application:** Utilize specified torque settings. If a torque range is provided, aim for the median value.
*   **In-Process Inspection:** Perform a visual inspection after every three major assembly steps to ensure proper seating, alignment, and orientation of parts.
*   **Handling Sensitive Components:** Use non-marring tools for delicate surfaces and maintain Electrostatic Discharge (ESD) protocols for all electronic sub-assemblies.

## 3. Quality Assurance & Final Check
*   **Functional Testing:** Conduct all required electrical or mechanical "go/no-go" tests as specified in the product testing protocol.
*   **Final Visual Sweep:** Inspect for leftover hardware, loose wires, or cosmetic imperfections (scratches, scuffs, or misalignment).
*   **Data Logging:** Record serial numbers, torque verification logs, and test results in the Production Management System (PMS).
*   **Labeling:** Apply final serial tags, compliance stickers, and branding labels in the exact specified locations.

## 4. Post-Assembly & Housekeeping
*   **Tool Organization:** Return all tools to their designated foam-cutout locations.
*   **Waste Disposal:** Dispose of scrap materials, plastic wrap, and cardboard according to the facility’s recycling and waste management policy.
*   **Shift Handover:** Update the shift log with the current completion status and note any equipment malfunctions or supply shortages for the incoming shift.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use "shadow boards" for tools. If a tool is missing from the board at the end of the shift, it is immediately identifiable.
*   **Pro Tip:** If a part does not fit, do not use force. Stop, verify the revision level of the part, and notify your supervisor.
*   **Pitfall (The "shortcut" trap):** Skipping a single screw or a torque check to meet a quota leads to high field failure rates and costly warranty claims.
*   **Pitfall (Contamination):** Neglecting to clean a workstation frequently results in the accumulation of metallic filings or dust, which can compromise the integrity of delicate internal components.

## FAQ
**Q: What should I do if I discover a defective part mid-assembly?**
A: Immediately quarantine the defective component in the designated "Red Bin," log the defect in the non-conformance report (NCR) system, and pull a replacement part from stock. Do not attempt to repair the part yourself.

**Q: Can I modify the assembly sequence if I find a more efficient method?**
A: No. Any process changes must be submitted through the Engineering Change Request (ECR) process. Unauthorized deviations from the SOP compromise ISO compliance and product safety.

**Q: How often should I check my torque settings?**
A: Torque drivers must be verified with a torque tester at the start of every shift and immediately following any drop or impact to the tool.
