# Standard Operating Procedure: Air Compressor Preventive Maintenance

### Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance protocols for industrial air compressors. Consistent adherence to this schedule is critical to ensuring operational longevity, maximizing energy efficiency, and preventing catastrophic mechanical failure. All maintenance activities must be conducted by authorized personnel following strict Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures. Failure to perform these inspections can result in hazardous pressure releases, equipment overheating, and expensive unplanned downtime.

## Section 1: Daily Operational Checks
*   **Check Oil Levels:** Verify oil levels are within the manufacturer’s recommended range via the sight glass or dipstick.
*   **Monitor System Pressure:** Observe the pressure gauge during operation to ensure it remains within specified operational parameters.
*   **Drain Condensate:** Manually drain moisture from the air receiver tank and refrigerated dryer to prevent corrosion and water carry-over into pneumatic lines.
*   **Check for Unusual Sounds/Vibrations:** Listen for knocking, clicking, or high-pitched squealing that indicates bearing failure or loose internal components.
*   **Visual Leak Inspection:** Scan all air hoses, fittings, and couplings for audible hissing or evidence of air leaks.

## Section 2: Weekly Maintenance Tasks
*   **Clean Intake Vents:** Inspect and clear dust, lint, or debris from intake vents and cooling louvers to prevent overheating.
*   **Check Drive Belt Tension:** Inspect belts for fraying or cracking. Use a tension gauge to ensure they meet manufacturer specifications; adjust as necessary.
*   **Inspect Electrical Connections:** Visually check for loose wires or burnt insulation (ensure power is disconnected).
*   **Air Filter Inspection:** Inspect the air intake filter element; replace if excessive dust or debris is observed.

## Section 3: Monthly & Quarterly Maintenance
*   **Lubrication/Oil Change:** Perform an oil change according to the hour-meter interval. Ensure the correct grade/viscosity oil is used.
*   **Replace Oil Filter:** Always replace the oil filter during an oil change to prevent circulation of contaminants.
*   **Clean/Replace Air Filter:** Regardless of visual condition, replace the intake air filter quarterly to maintain air quality and engine/motor efficiency.
*   **Safety Valve Test:** Pull the ring on the ASME safety relief valve to ensure it pops off and resets correctly. *Caution: Wear hearing protection.*
*   **Clean Aftercooler/Radiator:** Use compressed air to blow out the cooling fins to ensure optimal heat transfer.

## Section 4: Annual Overhaul
*   **Check Motor Bearings:** Inspect for wear and regrease if applicable to the motor type.
*   **Full System Inspection:** Inspect the internal pump valves for carbon buildup or pitting.
*   **Vibration Analysis:** Check the alignment of the motor and pump shaft.
*   **Calibration:** Verify that the pressure switches and gauges are calibrated and accurate.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain an "Equipment Logbook" near the machine. Document every inspection, the date, and the technician’s name. This is vital for warranty claims and predictive analysis.
*   **Pro Tip:** Install a permanent condensate auto-drain system to reduce the risk of tank corrosion and improve downstream air quality.
*   **Pitfall:** Never use generic or automotive-grade oil in an industrial compressor. Always use the manufacturer-approved synthetic lubricant to prevent varnish buildup.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening belts will destroy the motor and pump bearings rapidly. Use a tension gauge—do not "guess" by feel.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring small air leaks. A 1/8-inch hole can cost hundreds of dollars in wasted electricity annually; fix leaks immediately.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should I change my air compressor oil?**
A: This depends on the environment. Generally, every 500 to 1,000 hours for rotary screw compressors, or every 3 to 6 months for reciprocating compressors, whichever comes first. Refer to your specific O&M manual for exact intervals.

**Q: Why is my compressor constantly tripping the thermal overload?**
A: This is usually caused by low voltage, an obstructed air intake, a failing motor bearing, or an ambient temperature that is too high. Check the intake filter first, then verify power supply stability.

**Q: Is it necessary to drain the tank if it has an automatic drain?**
A: Yes. You should perform a manual override test monthly to ensure the automatic drain is not clogged. If the auto-drain fails, moisture will accumulate and rust the tank from the inside out.
