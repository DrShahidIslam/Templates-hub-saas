# Standard Operating Procedure: Preventive Maintenance for CNC Machinery

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance (PM) protocols required to ensure the longevity, precision, and operational safety of CNC machine tools. Regular maintenance is critical to preventing unscheduled downtime, maintaining strict tolerance requirements, and extending the lifespan of precision components such as spindles, ball screws, and guideways. All maintenance activities must be performed by qualified personnel, following original equipment manufacturer (OEM) guidelines and safety lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedures.

## Daily Maintenance Checklist (Operator Level)
*   **Safety Check:** Verify all emergency stop buttons are functional and interlock guards are engaged.
*   **Lubrication:** Check way lube levels; ensure the automatic lubrication system is cycling correctly.
*   **Coolant System:** Inspect coolant concentration levels using a refractometer; remove heavy swarf from the tank screens.
*   **Pneumatics:** Drain water from the air filter/regulator assembly to prevent moisture damage to valves.
*   **General Cleanliness:** Wipe down exposed guideways and remove chips from the machine bed to prevent "chip packing."

## Weekly Maintenance Checklist
*   **Coolant Maintenance:** Skim tramp oil from the coolant surface; check for bacterial growth or foul odors; adjust pH levels as needed.
*   **Air Filters:** Clean or replace the electrical cabinet air intake filters to prevent overheating of the CNC controller and drives.
*   **Way Wipers:** Inspect way covers and wipers for damage; ensure they are effectively sealing out debris.
*   **Tool Changer:** Inspect the Automatic Tool Changer (ATC) arm and cam mechanism; wipe down with a light film of way oil.
*   **Hydraulics:** Check hydraulic pressure levels and look for signs of hose seepage or leaks.

## Monthly & Quarterly Maintenance Checklist
*   **Leveling Check:** Use a precision spirit level to verify machine geometry; adjust leveling bolts if settling has occurred.
*   **Belt Tension:** Inspect drive belts (spindle and axis motors) for signs of wear, fraying, or improper tension.
*   **Ball Screws/Bearings:** Inspect ball screw surfaces for scoring; listen for abnormal noise during rapid traverses.
*   **Electrical Connections:** With power off, perform a visual inspection of terminal blocks; ensure no wires have loosened due to machine vibration.
*   **Backups:** Perform a full backup of the CNC controller parameters and offset files to an external secure drive.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a "Maintenance Logbook" attached to the machine. Documenting every inspection, filter change, and anomaly allows for trend analysis, helping you predict component failure before it stops production.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use high-quality, OEM-recommended lubricants rather than generic alternatives. Viscosity mismatches are the leading cause of premature spindle bearing failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-greasing. Many operators assume "more is better" for bearings. Excessive grease creates internal heat and can blow out bearing seals. Follow OEM grease volume specifications strictly.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "Low Coolant" alarm. Operating with low coolant concentrations leads to accelerated tool wear and poor surface finish, causing downstream quality control issues.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I perform a full geometry check on my CNC?**
A: A full geometry check (squareness, parallelism, and positioning accuracy) should be performed annually or after any significant machine "crash" or physical relocation.

**Q: What is the most common cause of CNC controller failure?**
A: Excessive heat and dust accumulation in the electrical cabinet. Ensuring intake filters are clean is the single most effective way to prevent control board failure.

**Q: Can I use compressed air to clean the machine bed?**
A: Avoid using compressed air to clean off chips. High-pressure air often forces metallic dust and coolant particulates behind way covers and into sensitive seals, causing abrasive wear to the guideways. Use a brush and vacuum instead.
