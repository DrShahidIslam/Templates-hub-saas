# Standard Operating Procedure: Apartment Unit Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting a comprehensive inspection of an apartment unit. Whether performed during move-in, move-out, or routine preventative maintenance, this inspection ensures that the asset is maintained to company standards, safety regulations are met, and liability is minimized. All inspectors must utilize this checklist to document the physical condition of the unit, note necessary repairs, and capture photographic evidence to protect both the property management firm and the resident.

## Pre-Inspection Protocol
*   Ensure you have a mobile device with a camera, a flashlight, a moisture meter, and a digital copy of the unit inspection form.
*   Notify the resident 24–48 hours in advance (if applicable) per local regulations.
*   Always knock and announce your presence before entering, even if the unit is vacant.

## 1. Entryway and Living Areas
*   **Door & Hardware:** Test the deadbolt, knob, and security latch. Check the door frame for signs of forced entry.
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for carpet stains, tears, or buckling. Check hard flooring for scratches, water damage, or gaps.
*   **Walls/Ceiling:** Note any scuffs, holes, water stains, or peeling paint.
*   **Electrical:** Test every outlet with a receptacle tester. Ensure all light switches function.
*   **Windows:** Operate each window; verify locks engage and screens are intact.

## 2. Kitchen Facilities
*   **Appliances:** 
    *   **Refrigerator:** Check temperature, door seals, and interior lighting. 
    *   **Stove/Oven:** Verify all burners ignite and the oven reaches temperature.
    *   **Dishwasher:** Run a quick rinse cycle to check for leaks.
*   **Plumbing:** Open all cabinets under the sink; check for active leaks or signs of past water damage. Ensure the faucet has strong pressure and good drainage.
*   **Cabinetry:** Verify hinges function and drawers glide smoothly without binding.

## 3. Bathrooms
*   **Fixtures:** Inspect toilet flush mechanism and tank fill speed. Check showerhead for mineral buildup and proper spray pattern.
*   **Ventilation:** Hold a piece of tissue to the exhaust fan; it should be pulled taut by suction.
*   **Water Damage:** Inspect vanity, baseboards, and floor tiles around the toilet/tub for "soft spots" indicating dry rot or water intrusion.
*   **Caulking:** Check the perimeter of the tub/shower for cracked or missing caulk.

## 4. Bedrooms and Storage
*   **Closets:** Ensure closet rods are secure and doors track smoothly.
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test all alarms. Verify date of manufacture; replace if older than 10 years.
*   **HVAC:** Check the air filter condition. Change if dirty. Verify the thermostat registers the correct temperature.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Photos are Evidence.** Take wide-angle shots of every room and macro shots of any existing damage. Cloud-sync these immediately to prevent loss of data.
*   **Pro Tip: Smell Test.** If a unit smells musty, follow the scent to its source. It is often a sign of hidden mold behind appliances or under carpeting.
*   **Pitfall: Relying on Memory.** Never "just look" at a unit. Even for routine checks, use the digital form. Memory is subjective and unreliable in the event of a security deposit dispute.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Leaks.** A minor drip under a sink today is a $5,000 floor restoration project next month. Report all leaks, no matter how small.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How do I handle a resident who refuses entry for a routine inspection?**
A: Review your lease agreement regarding "Right of Entry." If the lease grants you access, provide a written notice citing the specific lease clause and schedule a firm time. If they continue to refuse, escalate to your regional manager or legal counsel.

**Q: What should I do if I find evidence of pests?**
A: Do not attempt to treat it yourself. Document the location and type of pest, take photos, and immediately trigger a work order for your professional pest control vendor.

**Q: Should the resident be present during the inspection?**
A: It is generally at the resident’s discretion for routine inspections. However, for move-out inspections, it is highly recommended to have the resident present to sign off on the condition of the unit and mitigate future disputes regarding cleaning or damage charges.
