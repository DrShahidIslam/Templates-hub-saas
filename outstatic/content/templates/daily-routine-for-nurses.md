# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Nursing Routine

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized daily workflow for nursing staff to ensure high-quality patient care, safety, and operational efficiency. By following this structured routine, nurses can maintain consistency, minimize the risk of medication errors, and ensure that all clinical documentation remains compliant with hospital regulations. This protocol is designed to be adaptable to various clinical settings while maintaining the core principles of bedside nursing excellence.

## 1. Shift Start and Handoff
*   **Arrive Early:** Log in to the electronic health record (EHR) system at least 15 minutes prior to the shift start.
*   **Participate in Shift Report:** Engage in a structured "bedside handoff" using the SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) framework.
*   **Verify Supplies:** Ensure the workstation is stocked with personal protective equipment (PPE) and necessary documentation tools.
*   **Patient Rounds (Initial):** Perform a brief "safety check" on all assigned patients to confirm IV patency, monitor placement, and overall patient comfort.

## 2. Morning Assessment and Medication Administration
*   **Vitals Acquisition:** Complete physical assessments and record vital signs for all assigned patients.
*   **Medication Administration:** Adhere strictly to the "Rights of Medication Administration" (Right Patient, Right Drug, Right Dose, Right Route, Right Time).
*   **Scanning Protocols:** Utilize barcode medication administration (BCMA) technology for every patient.
*   **Pain Management:** Assess pain levels and evaluate the effectiveness of PRN (as-needed) medications administered previously.

## 3. Mid-Shift Documentation and Care Coordination
*   **Real-Time Charting:** Complete notes immediately after procedures or assessments to ensure accuracy.
*   **Physician Rounds:** Accompany the attending physician during rounds to provide updates and receive new orders.
*   **Order Transcription:** Promptly review and implement new orders in the EHR.
*   **Interdisciplinary Collaboration:** Communicate with physical therapy, nutrition, and social services regarding patient progress and discharge planning.

## 4. End-of-Shift Closure
*   **Final Rounds:** Perform a concluding safety sweep to ensure IV fluids are sufficient, call lights are within reach, and room environments are tidy.
*   **Documentation Finalization:** Ensure all intake/output logs, nursing notes, and care plans are signed and finalized.
*   **Clean and Restock:** Clear the workstation of unnecessary paperwork and ensure the room is prepped for the oncoming shift.
*   **Handoff Completion:** Provide a concise, objective report to the incoming nurse, highlighting critical updates or pending tasks.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Batch Tasks.** Group your tasks (e.g., med pass and assessment) to minimize interruptions and reduce the frequency of entering and exiting isolation rooms.
*   **Pro Tip: Use a "Brain Sheet."** Maintain a personalized worksheet for notes. This helps track critical "to-do" items and keeps you organized during high-census shifts.
*   **Pitfall: Relying on Memory.** Never rely on memory for medication doses or lab values. Always verify data in the EHR—memory errors are the leading cause of clinical oversight.
*   **Pitfall: Skipping Breaks.** Neglecting hydration and nutrition leads to decision fatigue. Protect your break time to maintain clinical acuity.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How should I handle a discrepancy during the medication handoff?**
A: If a medication count or patient status report differs from your handoff report, immediately verify with the outgoing nurse or the automated pharmacy system. Do not administer medication until the discrepancy is resolved.

**Q: What is the priority if an urgent call light interrupts a medication pass?**
A: Safety is the priority. If it is a life-safety issue (e.g., fall risk), secure your medication tray in a locked cart or designated area before responding to the alarm.

**Q: How often should I update the whiteboards in patient rooms?**
A: Update the whiteboard at the start of every shift and after any significant change in the care plan, such as a change in the primary physician, daily goal, or discharge timeline.
