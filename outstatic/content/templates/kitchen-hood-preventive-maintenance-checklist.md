# Standard Operating Procedure: Kitchen Hood Preventive Maintenance

Maintaining an efficient kitchen ventilation system is critical for fire safety, air quality, and regulatory compliance. This SOP outlines the systematic approach for inspecting, cleaning, and maintaining commercial kitchen hoods to prevent grease accumulation, ensure optimal airflow, and minimize the risk of kitchen fires. Adherence to this protocol is mandatory for all facility management and kitchen operations personnel to maintain health department and fire marshal standards.

## 1. Safety and Preparation
Before beginning any maintenance, ensure the kitchen is shut down or operating at minimal capacity.

*   **De-energize Equipment:** Turn off the hood fans and the kitchen equipment underneath the hood. Place "Lock-out/Tag-out" labels on the fan control switch to prevent accidental activation.
*   **Safety Gear:** Ensure all maintenance staff are wearing appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), including non-slip boots, chemical-resistant gloves, and eye protection.
*   **Workspace Protection:** Cover all cooking surfaces, fryers, and prep stations with heavy-duty plastic sheeting or drop cloths to prevent grease or cleaning agents from contaminating food-grade areas.

## 2. Filter Inspection and Cleaning
Filters are the first line of defense against grease ingress into the ductwork.

*   **Removal:** Carefully remove all baffle filters from the hood housing.
*   **Visual Inspection:** Check for damaged hinges, missing handles, or bent louvers. Replace any compromised filters immediately.
*   **Deep Cleaning:** Soak filters in a high-alkaline degreaser solution. Scrub with a non-abrasive brush to remove baked-on grease from internal channels.
*   **Rinsing:** Rinse thoroughly with hot water and allow to air dry completely before re-installation. Never reinstall dripping wet filters.

## 3. Plenum and Ductwork Inspection
The plenum is the interior space behind the filters where grease collects.

*   **Plenum Wipe-down:** Inspect the internal plenum area. Using a degreasing agent and non-scratch scouring pads, remove all grease buildup from the plenum walls and troughs.
*   **Grease Troughs/Cups:** Empty and clean all grease collection cups and drain troughs. Ensure drainage lines are clear of blockages.
*   **Duct Inspection:** Using a flashlight, inspect the accessible lower ductwork for heavy grease buildup. If accumulation exceeds 1/8 inch, schedule a professional deep-cleaning service immediately.

## 4. Fan and Mechanical Component Check
Proper mechanical function ensures the negative pressure required to vent smoke and heat.

*   **Fan Blades:** Check the exhaust fan blades for grease buildup, which can cause imbalance and motor strain.
*   **Fan Belt Tension:** Inspect the drive belt for cracking, fraying, or slippage. If the belt has more than 1/2 inch of "play," it must be tightened or replaced.
*   **Electrical Connections:** Ensure all wires leading to the motor are free from grease and that conduit connections are tight and corrosion-free.

## 5. Testing and Documentation
Verification ensures the system is functional post-maintenance.

*   **System Startup:** Re-energize the power and activate the exhaust fan. Listen for unusual vibrations, grinding noises, or rattling.
*   **Airflow Test:** Use a piece of tissue paper to verify suction at each section of the hood.
*   **Documentation:** Record the maintenance date, findings, parts replaced, and the signature of the inspector in the official Maintenance Logbook.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** Schedule your deep-clean service during off-peak seasons to ensure you have a "clean slate" before your busiest months of the year.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "Grease Depth Gauge" (a simple metal ruler) to track accumulation rates. This helps in adjusting your maintenance frequency based on actual kitchen volume.
*   **Pitfall:** Never use harsh abrasive steel wool on stainless steel surfaces; this creates micro-scratches that harbor grease and bacteria, leading to faster buildup in the future.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid the "set it and forget it" mentality. Even if a filter looks clean, if the grease cup is full, your system is failing its primary safety function.

## FAQ

**How often should hood filters be cleaned?**
General industry standard is daily for heavy-duty cooking (fryers/grills) and weekly for light-duty operations, though local fire codes may dictate stricter intervals.

**Can I clean the ductwork myself?**
You can clean the accessible lower section of the duct, but NFPA 96 standards generally require that a certified professional cleaning company handle the interior ducting and fan housing annually or bi-annually.

**What happens if I neglect hood maintenance?**
Neglect creates a significant fire hazard due to fuel (grease) buildup. Furthermore, it results in poor indoor air quality, potential equipment damage, and heavy fines during health or fire department inspections.
