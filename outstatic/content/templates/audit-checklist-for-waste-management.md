# Standard Operating Procedure: Waste Management Audit

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting a comprehensive waste management audit. The objective of this audit is to ensure regulatory compliance, identify opportunities for waste reduction and diversion, verify the accuracy of waste disposal data, and minimize environmental impact. By adhering to this audit framework, the organization will maintain rigorous oversight of its waste streams, optimize operational costs, and align with sustainability targets.

## Audit Checklist

## Section 1: Regulatory Compliance & Documentation
* Verify that all waste disposal contracts and service agreements are current and accessible.
* Ensure all Waste Transfer Notes (WTNs) or hazardous waste manifests are signed, dated, and stored for the legally required retention period (e.g., 3 years for non-hazardous, 5+ for hazardous).
* Confirm that the facility holds all necessary environmental permits and licenses for current waste generation levels.
* Review staff training logs to ensure all personnel handling waste have completed mandatory health, safety, and environmental (HSE) training.

## Section 2: On-Site Waste Infrastructure
* Inspect all internal and external waste receptacles for proper labeling, integrity, and cleanliness.
* Validate that segregation at the source is being practiced (e.g., separate streams for paper, plastic, organic, and general waste).
* Assess the physical condition of waste storage areas: ensure surfaces are impermeable, bunded where necessary, and secure from unauthorized access or weather ingress.
* Check spill kits near waste storage areas to ensure contents are complete, accessible, and compliant with current requirements.

## Section 3: Operational Waste Management
* Observe waste collection processes to ensure handlers are using appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
* Conduct a "bin-audit" (sampling) to check for contamination in recycling or compost streams (e.g., food residue in paper bins).
* Audit hazardous waste storage: ensure incompatible chemicals are not stored together and that secondary containment is functioning.
* Verify that waste collection schedules align with actual bin fill-rates to optimize haulage costs and reduce carbon footprint.

## Section 4: Data & Performance Tracking
* Reconcile invoice data from waste contractors against actual waste tonnage reports.
* Calculate the diversion rate: (Total Weight of Recycled + Composted Waste / Total Weight of All Waste) x 100.
* Review "Missed Collection" reports and identify systemic causes for service interruptions.
* Document any discrepancies between reported disposal weights and estimated generation levels.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
* **Pro Tip:** Conduct "unannounced" spot checks. Scheduled audits often lead to temporary "compliance theater" where waste is moved or cleaned up solely for the auditor.
* **Pro Tip:** Engage frontline employees. They are the primary waste generators and often have the best insights into why segregation fails (e.g., "The bin is too far away").
* **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "hidden" waste streams. Don't just focus on the dumpster; look at office paper, e-waste, and redundant equipment that may be sitting in storage areas.
* **Pitfall:** Failing to verify the "End Destination." Sometimes waste is collected as "recycling" but sent to landfill. Audit your service provider’s downstream disposal certificates.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should a formal waste management audit be conducted?**
A: For most facilities, a full-scale audit should be conducted semi-annually. However, if your facility generates hazardous waste, quarterly audits are strongly recommended to ensure continuous compliance with strict safety regulations.

**Q: What is the most common reason for audit failure?**
A: Inconsistent labeling and lack of segregation at the source. If bins are not clearly marked or easily accessible, staff will default to placing all items in the general waste stream, leading to high disposal costs and poor environmental performance.

**Q: Should I hire a third-party auditor?**
A: While internal audits are excellent for daily management, bringing in a third-party consultant once a year provides an objective "fresh set of eyes" and ensures your processes are benchmarked against current industry best practices and emerging legislation.
