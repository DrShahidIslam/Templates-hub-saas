# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance tasks required to preserve the structural integrity, safety, and operational efficiency of a residential property. By adhering to this systematic approach, homeowners can mitigate the risk of catastrophic system failure, reduce long-term repair costs, and maintain property value. This document should be treated as a baseline; environmental factors or specific appliance manufacturer guidelines may necessitate more frequent interventions.

## Exterior & Structural Integrity
*   **Roof Inspection:** Inspect shingles for cracking, curling, or missing segments. Check flashing around chimneys and vents for signs of oxidation or gaps.
*   **Gutters & Downspouts:** Clear debris semi-annually. Ensure downspouts direct water at least six feet away from the foundation to prevent basement seepage.
*   **Foundation & Siding:** Check for spider-web cracking in the foundation or gaps in siding. Seal any penetrations with high-grade exterior caulk to prevent pest ingress.
*   **Landscape Grading:** Ensure soil slopes away from the home’s perimeter. Remove vegetation that is touching the siding or roofline to prevent moisture-related rot.
*   **Deck & Porch:** Check for loose boards, protruding nails, or signs of dry rot. Apply sealant or stain every 2–3 years to prevent UV and water damage.

## HVAC & Climate Control
*   **Air Filters:** Replace HVAC filters every 90 days (or 30–60 days if pets are present).
*   **Condenser Units:** Clear grass, leaves, and debris from around the exterior AC unit. Use a fin comb to straighten bent condenser fins.
*   **Heating System:** Schedule a professional burner/heat exchanger inspection annually before the onset of winter.
*   **Ductwork:** Visually inspect accessible joints for tape degradation or air leaks.
*   **Dryer Vent:** Disconnect and vacuum the exhaust hose once per year to remove lint build-up, a leading cause of residential fires.

## Plumbing & Water Systems
*   **Water Heater:** Drain 1–2 gallons from the tank to flush sediment build-up. Test the Temperature and Pressure (T&P) relief valve.
*   **Supply Lines:** Check washing machine hoses and under-sink lines for bulging, corrosion, or dampness. Replace braided hoses every 5–7 years.
*   **Faucets & Drains:** Clean aerators to remove mineral deposits. Check for slow-draining pipes which indicate partial blockages.
*   **Sump Pump:** Pour a bucket of water into the pit to trigger the float switch and ensure the pump discharges correctly.

## Electrical & Safety Systems
*   **Smoke/CO Detectors:** Test alarms monthly. Replace batteries twice annually (at the start/end of Daylight Savings Time). Replace the entire unit every 10 years.
*   **GFCI Outlets:** Press the "Test" button on all Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter outlets; they should click and cut power. Reset them afterward.
*   **Electrical Panel:** Check for signs of scorching or a persistent "fishy" odor, which may indicate overheating wires.
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Ensure gauges are in the green zone. Shake dry-chemical extinguishers monthly to prevent powder compaction.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (The Logbook):** Maintain a physical or digital "Home Health Log." Record the date of every service and save receipts. This adds significant value during a future home sale.
*   **Pro Tip (Photographic Baseline):** Take high-resolution photos of your attic, crawlspace, and basement annually. These serve as critical evidence for insurance claims if water damage or structural shifts occur.
*   **Pitfall (Over-Tightening):** When performing minor plumbing repairs, never over-tighten plastic connections; they are prone to cracking. "Hand-tight plus a quarter turn" is the industry standard.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring Small Leaks):** A "small" drip under a sink can lead to massive sub-floor rot and mold growth within months. Address moisture at the first sign of discoloration.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I hire a professional rather than doing it myself?**
A: Any task involving pressurized gas lines, high-voltage electrical panels, or roof work above a single story should be performed by a licensed professional to ensure safety and code compliance.

**Q: What is the most critical maintenance task I can do to prevent damage?**
A: Keeping your gutters clean. Neglected gutters are the primary cause of water intrusion into foundations, siding rot, and basement flooding.

**Q: Does my homeowners insurance cover maintenance-related damage?**
A: Generally, no. Insurance covers "sudden and accidental" damage. Damage resulting from a lack of routine maintenance (e.g., a burst pipe that was visibly corroded for years) is often excluded from coverage.
