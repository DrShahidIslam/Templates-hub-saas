# Standard Operating Procedure: Laboratory Protocol and Bioprocessing Compliance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory guidelines for maintaining regulatory compliance, biosafety, and data integrity within a biotechnology facility. Adherence to this document is essential for minimizing experimental variability, ensuring personnel safety according to Biosafety Level (BSL) standards, and maintaining audit readiness for regulatory bodies such as the FDA or EMA. All personnel must review this SOP in conjunction with project-specific documentation before initiating laboratory activities.

## Section 1: Pre-Laboratory Preparation and Safety
*   **Don Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Ensure proper fit of lab coat, nitrile gloves (double-gloving where required), safety goggles, and closed-toe footwear.
*   **Sanitization:** Wipe down all workstations, balances, and pipetting areas with 70% ethanol or approved disinfectant prior to work.
*   **Resource Verification:** Confirm that all reagents are within their expiration date and that media has been stored at the appropriate temperature (e.g., 4°C or -20°C).
*   **Risk Assessment:** Verify that the current laboratory status matches the intended BSL requirements for the specific biological agents or chemical compounds being utilized.

## Section 2: Experimental Execution and Aseptic Technique
*   **Sterility Maintenance:** Utilize laminar flow hoods for all cell culture or sensitive molecular biology work. Ensure the hood blower has been active for at least 15 minutes before work begins.
*   **Labeling:** Clearly label every tube, plate, and flask with the project ID, date, contents, and initials of the researcher.
*   **Documentation:** Maintain an electronic or physical lab notebook concurrently with the experiment. Document lot numbers of all critical reagents.
*   **Technique Consistency:** Utilize calibrated pipettes only. Ensure tip changes between every transfer to prevent cross-contamination.

## Section 3: Waste Management and Post-Experiment Cleanup
*   **Segregation:** Dispose of sharps in puncture-proof containers, biological waste in biohazard-labeled bags, and chemical waste in appropriately tagged liquid containers.
*   **Surface Decontamination:** Clean all bench surfaces and equipment exterior with appropriate disinfectant.
*   **Equipment Log-out:** Power down shared equipment according to specific instrument SOPs and log the usage time for billing and maintenance tracking.
*   **Stock Reconciliation:** Replenish low-inventory consumables and notify the inventory manager of any reagents nearing expiration.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **The "Clean-as-you-go" Rule:** Do not wait until the end of the day to clean. A cluttered workspace is the leading cause of cross-contamination and accidental spills.
*   **Avoid Pipetting "By Eye":** Always use calibrated, certified mechanical or electronic pipettes. Relying on visual estimation is a primary source of data reproducibility failures.
*   **The Documentation Trap:** If it isn’t documented in the official lab log, it didn’t happen. Never rely on memory for incubation times or temperature adjustments.
*   **Environmental Sensitivity:** Be aware of airflow patterns in the lab. Do not place sensitive experiments directly in the path of HVAC vents or near high-traffic entry doors, as these create turbulence and introduce contaminants.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q1: What should I do if a minor biological spill occurs?**
A: Immediately cover the spill with absorbent paper towels, saturate with a 10% bleach solution (or approved disinfectant), and allow it to sit for at least 20 minutes before wiping it up and disposing of it in the biohazard waste stream. Notify your Lab Manager immediately.

**Q2: How often should the laboratory equipment be recalibrated?**
A: All precision instruments (pipettes, scales, incubators, thermal cyclers) must be calibrated at least annually, or immediately following any significant maintenance or transport of the equipment.

**Q3: Can I modify an SOP for a specific experiment?**
A: No. If you identify a need for a process improvement, you must file a "Change Request Form." The modification must be reviewed and approved by the Quality Assurance (QA) department before it can be implemented in the laboratory.
