# Standard Operating Procedure: Venipuncture Blood Sample Collection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the safe, hygienic, and accurate collection of blood specimens via venipuncture. Adherence to these steps is critical to ensure patient safety, minimize the risk of pre-analytical errors, and maintain the integrity of clinical diagnostic results. All personnel must strictly follow Universal Precautions and infection control measures throughout the process.

## 1. Preparation and Patient Identification

*   **Hand Hygiene:** Perform thorough hand hygiene using soap and water or an alcohol-based sanitizer.
*   **Don Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** Wear clean, non-sterile gloves and a lab coat.
*   **Verify Patient Identity:** Ask the patient to state their full name and date of birth. Cross-reference this against the laboratory requisition form and the patient’s ID wristband.
*   **Requisition Review:** Confirm the specific tests required, the order of draw, and whether the patient has followed fasting requirements.
*   **Equipment Assembly:** Gather necessary supplies:
    *   Appropriate vacuum tubes (color-coded by additive).
    *   Needle/holder assembly (or butterfly needle).
    *   Tourniquet.
    *   Alcohol prep pads (70% isopropyl alcohol).
    *   Gauze pads and adhesive bandages.
    *   Sharps container.

## 2. Site Selection and Preparation

*   **Positioning:** Ensure the patient is seated comfortably or lying down to prevent injury in case of syncope (fainting).
*   **Application of Tourniquet:** Apply the tourniquet 3–4 inches above the chosen venipuncture site. Do not leave the tourniquet on for longer than 60 seconds to avoid hemoconcentration.
*   **Vein Selection:** Palpate the antecubital fossa to identify the most prominent vein (typically the median cubital vein).
*   **Disinfection:** Clean the site with an alcohol prep pad in a circular motion, moving from the center outward. Allow the area to air dry completely (do not blow or fan) to prevent stinging and hemolysis.

## 3. Venipuncture Procedure

*   **Anchoring:** Anchor the vein by pulling the skin taut below the insertion site with your non-dominant thumb.
*   **Insertion:** Hold the needle bevel up. Insert the needle into the vein at a 15–30 degree angle.
*   **Tube Engagement:** Once blood flow is established, push the collection tube into the holder.
*   **Order of Draw:** Follow the standardized order of draw to prevent cross-contamination of additives:
    1.  Blood Culture bottles/tubes.
    2.  Coagulation tubes (Light Blue).
    3.  Serum tubes (Red/Gold/Tiger Top).
    4.  Heparin tubes (Green).
    5.  EDTA tubes (Lavender).
    6.  Glycolytic inhibitor tubes (Gray).
*   **Mixing:** Immediately and gently invert tubes containing additives 5–10 times. Do not shake.

## 4. Post-Collection and Specimen Handling

*   **Tourniquet Release:** Release the tourniquet once the final tube begins to fill.
*   **Needle Withdrawal:** Remove the needle smoothly and immediately apply pressure to the site with sterile gauze. Do not have the patient bend their arm; keep it extended to minimize hematoma formation.
*   **Sharps Disposal:** Engage the safety device on the needle immediately and dispose of the entire unit in an approved sharps container.
*   **Labeling:** Label all tubes at the bedside in the presence of the patient, including the date, time of collection, and your initials.
*   **Final Assessment:** Ensure bleeding has stopped and apply an adhesive bandage.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip:** If a vein is "rolling," use your non-dominant hand to apply firmer downward traction on the skin to stabilize the vessel.
*   **Pitfall - Hemolysis:** This is the most common pre-analytical error. It is caused by using a needle that is too small (e.g., 25G), excessive pulling on a syringe, or shaking the tubes rather than inverting them.
*   **Pitfall - Hematoma:** Usually caused by going through the vein upon entry or failing to apply adequate pressure post-withdrawal. If a hematoma begins to form, immediately remove the tourniquet and apply firm pressure.
*   **Pro Tip:** If a patient has a history of fainting, perform the collection while they are lying down in a supine position.

## FAQ

**Q: What should I do if I cannot find a vein after two attempts?**
A: Stop the procedure. Do not attempt a third time; request assistance from a senior phlebotomist or another qualified healthcare professional to prevent patient distress and tissue trauma.

**Q: Why is the order of draw important?**
A: It prevents the transfer of chemical additives from one tube to another. For example, EDTA can interfere with coagulation tests if it enters the light blue tube, leading to critically inaccurate results.

**Q: How long should I leave a tourniquet on?**
A: A maximum of 60 seconds. Prolonged application causes hemoconcentration, where the concentration of blood components (proteins, cells, etc.) increases due to the shifting of fluid out of the vessels, which can skew laboratory results.
