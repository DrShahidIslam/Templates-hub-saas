# Standard Operating Procedure: Civil Works Quality Inspection

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection framework for civil works projects to ensure structural integrity, compliance with engineering specifications, and adherence to safety standards. By following this systematic approach, site managers and quality control engineers can mitigate construction risks, prevent costly rework, and ensure that all milestones meet regulatory and design requirements prior to proceeding to subsequent phases.

## Phase 1: Pre-Pour & Excavation Inspection
Before any concrete is poured or structural load is applied, the foundation and sub-base must be validated for stability.

*   **Excavation Depth & Dimensions:** Verify depths against the foundation design drawings and ensure width accommodates formwork.
*   **Soil Bearing Capacity:** Confirm that site geotechnical reports match actual field conditions (look for signs of soft spots or unexpected water ingress).
*   **Sub-base Compaction:** Ensure the sub-grade is compacted to the specified Proctor density (e.g., 95% Modified Proctor).
*   **Dewatering:** Confirm that the excavation pit is free of standing water, mud, or loose debris.
*   **Survey Verification:** Cross-check the centerlines, corner offsets, and elevations with the master site plan.

## Phase 2: Formwork, Reinforcement & Embeds
This phase ensures that the "skeleton" of the structure is correctly positioned to support the design loads.

*   **Formwork Alignment:** Check that formwork is plumb, square, and sufficiently braced to withstand hydrostatic pressure during concrete placement.
*   **Rebar Grade & Diameter:** Verify that steel reinforcement matches the bar schedule (size, grade, and length).
*   **Clear Cover:** Ensure the correct concrete cover is maintained using approved spacers/chairs to prevent corrosion.
*   **Tie-Wire Security:** Inspect rebar junctions to ensure they are securely tied and that no "floating" bars exist.
*   **Embeds & Conduits:** Verify the exact location, elevation, and secure fixing of mechanical/electrical sleeves and anchor bolts before closing the formwork.
*   **Cleanliness:** Ensure forms are free of sawdust, oil, ties, or construction debris.

## Phase 3: Concrete Placement & Finishing
Real-time monitoring during the pour is critical to prevent structural voids and surface cracking.

*   **Slump Test:** Perform a slump test on the concrete batch to verify workability according to the mix design.
*   **Vibration Protocol:** Confirm that mechanical vibrators are used correctly (avoiding contact with rebar to prevent segregation).
*   **Cold Joint Management:** Ensure the pour rate is sufficient to prevent cold joints between batches.
*   **Leveling & Screeding:** Verify that the surface is finished to the correct elevation and gradient (slope) to ensure proper site drainage.
*   **Curing:** Document the start time of the curing process (using curing compounds, wet burlap, or plastic sheeting).

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "Check-Before-Pour" Hold Point:** Never authorize a concrete pour until a signed "Request for Inspection" (RFI) is completed by both the contractor and the supervising engineer.
*   **Pitfall: Relying on Verbal Approvals:** Always maintain a written or digital log of inspections. If a measurement is taken but not recorded, it does not exist in the eyes of an audit.
*   **Pro Tip: Weather Awareness:** Have a contingency plan for sudden rain or extreme heat during pours. In extreme heat, utilize retarding admixtures or schedule pours for early morning.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Tolerances:** Do not assume "close enough" is acceptable. Cumulative deviations in early stages result in significant misalignment during finishing or structural installation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the most common reason for a failed civil works inspection?**
A: Inadequate reinforcement cover and failure to achieve the required soil compaction density are the two most common causes of inspection failure.

**Q: How long should inspection records be retained?**
A: All inspection checklists and associated photographic evidence should be retained for the life of the structure, or as per the local statutory requirement (typically 7–10 years).

**Q: What should I do if the concrete mix arrives and the slump is significantly outside the design range?**
A: Do not add water to the truck on-site to increase the slump, as this ruins the water-cement ratio and lowers the ultimate compressive strength. Reject the load or return it to the batching plant for adjustment.
