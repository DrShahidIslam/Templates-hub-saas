# Standard Operating Procedure: Office Facility Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for ensuring a safe, professional, and fully functional office environment. Regular maintenance is essential to mitigate operational downtime, extend the lifespan of capital assets, and uphold Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) standards. This document serves as a comprehensive guide for facility managers and administrative staff to conduct routine inspections and coordinate necessary repairs.

## 1. Electrical and Lighting Systems
*   **Fixture Inspection:** Verify all overhead lights are functional; replace flickering bulbs or faulty ballasts immediately.
*   **Emergency Lighting:** Test exit signs and battery-powered emergency lights monthly to ensure compliance with fire safety codes.
*   **Outlets and Wiring:** Inspect wall outlets for scorching, loose fittings, or exposed wiring. Ensure no power strips are daisy-chained.
*   **Server Room/IT Closet:** Confirm cooling units are set to the correct temperature and there are no audible alarms or red-light indicators on UPS devices.

## 2. HVAC and Air Quality
*   **Filter Replacement:** Inspect HVAC air filters quarterly; replace if clogged to ensure air quality and energy efficiency.
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Check that thermostats are programmed correctly and responding to input commands.
*   **Ventilation:** Ensure air registers and returns are unobstructed by office furniture, filing cabinets, or decor.
*   **Humidity Control:** Monitor for signs of dampness or mold near window units or ceiling vents.

## 3. Plumbing and Kitchenette
*   **Leak Detection:** Inspect under-sink plumbing in bathrooms and breakrooms for signs of moisture or water stains.
*   **Appliance Maintenance:** Clean refrigerator coils and ensure microwave/dishwasher seals are airtight and free of debris.
*   **Drain Flow:** Test faucets for proper pressure and ensure drains are clearing without sluggishness.
*   **Water Filtration:** Replace water cooler filters according to the manufacturer’s schedule.

## 4. Furniture and Structural Integrity
*   **Ergonomic Equipment:** Check office chairs for stable wheel function, working height adjustment levers, and secure armrests.
*   **Desks and Tables:** Tighten hardware on standing desks and meeting tables to prevent wobbling or collapse.
*   **Doors and Hardware:** Inspect door closers, hinges, and locks for smooth operation and security compliance.
*   **Flooring:** Check carpet tiles for tripping hazards (curling/loose edges) and ensure floor moldings are intact.

## 5. Safety and Fire Equipment
*   **Extinguishers:** Verify fire extinguishers are charged (gauge in the green zone), accessible, and tagged with a current inspection date.
*   **Smoke/Carbon Monoxide Detectors:** Perform a sound-test monthly to ensure sensors are active.
*   **First Aid Kits:** Audit supplies; replace expired items and ensure the kit is fully stocked according to local regulations.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip (The "See Something, Say Something" Log):** Maintain a digital ticketing system (e.g., Jira or a shared spreadsheet) where employees can report issues immediately. Do not rely on verbal requests.
*   **Pro Tip (Preventative Scheduling):** Perform heavy maintenance (carpet cleaning, HVAC servicing, deep dusting) during off-peak hours or weekends to avoid disrupting employee productivity.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring "Minor" Issues):** A dripping faucet or a loose cabinet hinge often leads to significant, costly damage if left ignored for weeks. Address small repairs immediately to avoid cumulative deterioration.
*   **Pitfall (Documentation Neglect):** Failure to log maintenance activities can lead to liability issues during fire inspections or insurance claims. Always date-stamp and sign off on completion.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should we conduct a full facility inspection?**
A: A comprehensive walkthrough should be performed at least once per month, with more frequent (daily or weekly) "spot checks" for high-traffic areas like bathrooms and kitchens.

**Q: What should I do if a maintenance issue requires a specialized vendor?**
A: If the issue involves specialized systems (e.g., electrical, plumbing, or major HVAC repairs), do not attempt DIY repairs. Use your pre-vetted list of licensed contractors and ensure they provide a certificate of insurance (COI) before commencing work.

**Q: Are there specific requirements for records keeping?**
A: Yes. All maintenance logs, repair receipts, and safety inspection records should be kept on file for a minimum of three years for insurance and compliance auditing purposes.
