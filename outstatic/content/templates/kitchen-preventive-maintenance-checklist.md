# Standard Operating Procedure: Kitchen Preventive Maintenance

This document outlines the mandatory procedures for the routine inspection and maintenance of commercial kitchen equipment. The objective of this SOP is to extend the lifecycle of high-value assets, ensure compliance with health and safety regulations, mitigate fire hazards, and prevent costly operational downtime. All maintenance activities must be logged in the facility maintenance journal upon completion.

## Refrigeration & Cold Storage
*   **Condenser Coils:** Vacuum and brush dust and grease buildup from coils monthly to ensure efficient heat exchange.
*   **Door Gaskets:** Inspect seals for cracks, gaps, or brittleness. Perform the "dollar bill test" to ensure a tight seal.
*   **Drain Lines:** Flush evaporator drain lines with a mild antimicrobial solution to prevent algae growth and blockages.
*   **Temperature Calibration:** Verify internal temperatures using a secondary calibrated thermometer; calibrate the digital thermostat if the variance exceeds +/- 2°F.
*   **Fan Motors:** Check for unusual noise or vibration; ensure fan blades are free of debris and spinning freely.

## Cooking Equipment (Ranges, Ovens, Fryers)
*   **Pilot Lights & Igniters:** Clean buildup around igniters to ensure consistent ignition; verify flame color is steady and blue.
*   **Burner Heads:** Remove and deep-clean burner ports to prevent uneven heating and carbon buildup.
*   **Gas Hoses:** Inspect flexible gas connectors for kinks, cracks, or signs of dry rot. Ensure the safety restraining cable is securely attached.
*   **Fryer Elements:** Check for signs of element pitting or loose wiring connections.
*   **Calibration:** Verify oven thermostat accuracy using a multi-point thermal probe test.

## Ventilation & Exhaust Systems
*   **Hood Filters:** Degrease and clean filters daily; inspect for physical damage (dents or bent mesh) that compromises airflow.
*   **Exhaust Fan Belts:** Inspect drive belts for tension and wear. Replace if cracks or significant "glazing" (shiny, hard surface) are observed.
*   **Grease Trap:** Inspect and clean the primary grease trap assembly. Ensure the drip cups are emptied and clear of obstruction.
*   **Fire Suppression Links:** Verify that fusible links are clean and not coated in grease; ensure the manual pull station is unobstructed.

## Plumbing & Sanitation
*   **Faucets & Sprayers:** Check all connections for leaks. Tighten aerators and descale faucet heads to maintain water pressure.
*   **Floor Drains:** Inspect and clean debris baskets daily. Flush with hot water and an enzymatic cleaner to prevent odors and backups.
*   **Dishwasher Arms:** Inspect spray arms for clogged nozzles; remove and flush with vinegar solution if water flow is restricted.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Create a "Maintenance Calendar" in a shared digital workspace. Assign specific tasks to the opening or closing supervisor to ensure accountability.
*   **Pro Tip:** Keep a "Spare Parts Kit" onsite containing essential items like gaskets, extra fryer basket handles, and universal igniters to reduce downtime.
*   **Pitfall:** Never use harsh chemical cleaners on stainless steel surfaces, as these can cause "pitting" and lead to long-term rust damage. Always use food-grade, non-corrosive stainless steel polish.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening gas connections can cause irreparable damage to threads. Always use a professional-grade leak detection spray to verify connections rather than just tightening by force.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should a professional service technician visit for deep maintenance?**
A: Regardless of your internal routine, we recommend a certified technician perform a full inspection and certification of gas lines, fire suppression systems, and major refrigeration components at least once every six months.

**Q: What should I do if I find a piece of equipment that is "acting up" but still functional?**
A: Do not wait for a complete failure. Log the issue in the maintenance journal, report it to the General Manager immediately, and mark the equipment with an "Under Observation" tag. Early intervention is significantly cheaper than emergency repair.

**Q: Can I use standard water for flushing my ice machine?**
A: No. Always follow the manufacturer’s specific maintenance instructions, which typically require proprietary descaling solutions. Using improper chemicals can damage the nickel-plating of the evaporator, leading to expensive repairs.
