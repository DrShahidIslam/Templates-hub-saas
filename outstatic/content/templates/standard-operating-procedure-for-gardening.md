# Standard Operating Procedure: Professional Horticulture Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements for maintaining a healthy, aesthetic, and productive garden environment. By adhering to these standardized workflows, personnel will ensure optimal plant health, resource efficiency, and site safety. This document is intended for use by both commercial landscaping teams and residential property managers to maintain consistency in horticultural standards.

## 1. Site Assessment and Preparation
*   **Assess Soil Moisture:** Check soil at a depth of 2–3 inches to determine if irrigation is required.
*   **Inspect for Pests/Disease:** Scan foliage and undersides of leaves for signs of aphids, mites, or fungal pathogens.
*   **Tool Sanitization:** Ensure all pruning shears and blades are cleaned with a 70% isopropyl alcohol solution to prevent cross-contamination between plants.
*   **Debris Clearing:** Remove fallen leaves, dead stalks, or litter from plant beds to discourage pest harborage.

## 2. Maintenance and Pruning Protocols
*   **Deadheading:** Remove faded blooms to encourage repeat flowering and redirect plant energy to root and foliage development.
*   **Sanitation Pruning:** Cut away diseased, damaged, or dead (the "3 Ds") wood using clean, sharp bypass pruners.
*   **Formative Pruning:** Remove suckers and crossing branches that impede air circulation or structural integrity.
*   **Cutting Angle:** Make cuts at a 45-degree angle approximately 1/4 inch above a bud, sloping away from the bud to shed water.

## 3. Irrigation and Soil Nutrition
*   **Irrigation Timing:** Perform watering during early morning hours (before 9:00 AM) to minimize evaporative loss and fungal leaf disease.
*   **Water Application:** Direct water to the soil surface (root zone), avoiding wetting the foliage whenever possible.
*   **Fertilization:** Apply slow-release organic fertilizer at the base of plants based on specific species requirements and growth stages.
*   **Mulching:** Maintain a 2–3 inch layer of organic mulch around plants to regulate soil temperature and suppress weed germination (ensure mulch does not touch the plant stem).

## 4. Weed Management and Cleanup
*   **Mechanical Removal:** Pull weeds by the root; ensure the entire taproot is removed to prevent regrowth.
*   **Edge Definition:** Use an edging tool to maintain clear boundaries between turf and garden beds for a professional aesthetic.
*   **Waste Disposal:** Segregate plant waste; compost organic material that is free of disease or seeds.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always sharpen your tools at the start of every shift. A dull blade crushes plant tissue, leading to slow healing and increased susceptibility to infection.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use "The Finger Test" before watering. If the soil feels damp at 2 inches deep, skip the irrigation to prevent root rot.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not "volcano mulch" (piling mulch against the trunk of a tree or shrub). This creates a habitat for rodents and encourages trunk rot.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid over-fertilizing. Too much nitrogen leads to excessive "leafy" growth that attracts aphids and weakens the plant's overall resistance.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I prune my perennials?**
A: Most perennials benefit from a "hard prune" in late winter or early spring. Regular "deadheading" should be performed throughout the active growing season.

**Q: Is it better to water a little every day or deeply once a week?**
A: Deep, infrequent watering is superior. It encourages roots to grow deeper into the soil, making the plant more drought-tolerant. Frequent, shallow watering encourages surface roots that dry out quickly.

**Q: How do I know if my garden is overwatered?**
A: Common signs include yellowing leaves, drooping despite moist soil, and the presence of fungus or mushrooms on the mulch. If the soil remains saturated for more than 48 hours, irrigation schedules must be adjusted.
