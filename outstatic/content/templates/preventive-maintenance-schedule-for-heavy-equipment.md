# Standard Operating Procedure: Heavy Equipment Preventive Maintenance (PM)

## Introduction
The primary objective of this Preventive Maintenance (PM) SOP is to extend the operational lifespan of heavy equipment, minimize unplanned downtime, and ensure compliance with safety and environmental regulations. This procedure outlines the systematic inspection, servicing, and documentation process required to maintain high-value assets—such as excavators, loaders, and haul trucks—in peak mechanical condition. Adherence to this schedule is mandatory for all site foremen, equipment operators, and maintenance technicians to ensure site productivity and operator safety.

## Phase 1: Pre-Operational Inspections (Daily)
*   **Fluid Level Verification:** Inspect engine oil, coolant, hydraulic fluid, and fuel levels. Top off as required per manufacturer specifications.
*   **Visual Walkaround:** Check for visible signs of hydraulic leaks, loose fittings, or frayed hoses.
*   **Ground Engagement Tools (GET):** Inspect bucket teeth, cutting edges, and side cutters for excessive wear or cracks.
*   **Tire/Track Assessment:** Check tire pressure for inflation issues or inspect tracks for tension, missing pads, and debris buildup.
*   **Safety Systems Check:** Verify the functionality of the backup alarm, horn, lights, windshield wipers, and emergency stop buttons.
*   **Cab Cleanliness:** Ensure the operator console is clear of debris to prevent pedal obstruction.

## Phase 2: Scheduled Maintenance (Interval-Based)
*   **250-Hour Service (Minor):**
    *   Change engine oil and replace oil filters.
    *   Inspect air intake systems; replace primary/secondary air filters if restriction indicator is triggered.
    *   Lubricate all grease points (zerk fittings) on pins, bushings, and linkage arms.
    *   Drain water separators in the fuel system.
*   **500-Hour Service (Intermediate):**
    *   Perform all 250-hour tasks.
    *   Replace fuel filters and inspect fuel lines for degradation.
    *   Analyze hydraulic system pressure and test fluid for contaminants.
    *   Inspect cooling system radiator fins for clogs; pressure wash if airflow is obstructed.
*   **1,000+ Hour Service (Major):**
    *   Perform all 500-hour tasks.
    *   Flush and replace hydraulic oil and transmission fluid.
    *   Inspect and adjust valve clearances (if applicable per engine manufacturer).
    *   Check alternator, starter motor, and battery voltage integrity.
    *   Conduct comprehensive structural inspection for welding cracks in the chassis or boom assembly.

## Phase 3: Documentation and Reporting
*   **Logbook Entry:** Every service must be recorded in the Equipment Maintenance Log, detailing hours, parts replaced, and the technician’s ID.
*   **Digital Sync:** Upload daily inspection checklists to the centralized Asset Management Software.
*   **Exception Reporting:** Report any identified defects that require immediate remediation to the maintenance supervisor before the equipment returns to the field.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Utilize "Oil Analysis" (S-O-S). Sending fluid samples to a laboratory during 500-hour intervals can predict internal engine or hydraulic failure long before a breakdown occurs.
*   **Pro Tip:** Standardize grease types. Using the wrong grade of grease can cause chemical incompatibility and premature bearing failure.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "minor" leaks. A small hydraulic weep often indicates a failing seal that will inevitably lead to a catastrophic line burst under load.
*   **Pitfall:** "Over-servicing." Stick to the manufacturer's recommended intervals. Changing oil too early is a waste of budget; changing it too late guarantees reduced component life.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What should I do if a piece of equipment is due for service, but we are in the middle of a critical project deadline?**
A: Never defer major maintenance. The cost of a catastrophic failure during a project is exponentially higher than the 4–6 hours of downtime required for a scheduled service. Reallocate equipment or adjust the project timeline accordingly.

**Q: Why is the daily walkaround so important if we have a scheduled maintenance program?**
A: Scheduled maintenance is proactive, but daily walkarounds are reactive to external variables. Site conditions (mud, rock, debris) can cause damage between service intervals that, if caught early, can be fixed with a simple bolt replacement rather than a major rebuild.

**Q: How do we determine if a part is "worn out" vs. "ready for replacement"?**
A: Always refer to the manufacturer’s "Service Limits" section in the O&M manual. This provides precise measurements (in millimeters or inches) for allowable wear on pins, bushings, and teeth. Never use "gut feeling" to determine the structural integrity of a component.
