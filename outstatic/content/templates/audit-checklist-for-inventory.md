# Standard Operating Procedure: Inventory Audit Protocol

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for conducting a comprehensive inventory audit. An effective audit ensures the accuracy of stock records, mitigates the risk of shrinkage, identifies obsolete or damaged goods, and ensures financial compliance. This procedure applies to all warehouse personnel, inventory managers, and external auditors involved in stock verification. Adherence to these steps is essential for maintaining operational integrity and supporting informed supply chain decision-making.

## Phase 1: Pre-Audit Preparation
*   **Schedule Notification:** Notify relevant department heads and warehouse staff at least 48 hours prior to the audit.
*   **System Freeze:** Initiate a "transaction freeze" in the Warehouse Management System (WMS) to prevent stock movements during the counting process.
*   **Documentation Prep:** Ensure all counting sheets, handheld scanners, and digital logs are calibrated and ready for use.
*   **Housekeeping:** Clear aisles and organize stock to ensure accessibility for counting teams.
*   **Staff Briefing:** Conduct a kick-off meeting to clarify counting zones, safety protocols, and the use of "Blind Count" forms.

## Phase 2: Execution and Counting
*   **Zone Verification:** Assign counting teams to specific, clearly defined physical zones.
*   **Blind Counts:** Utilize a blind count system where teams count stock without prior knowledge of the system-recorded quantities.
*   **SKU Validation:** Verify that the SKU on the physical item matches the SKU on the shelf label and the digital count sheet.
*   **Condition Assessment:** Categorize items into "Sellable," "Damaged/Defective," and "Obsolete/Expired."
*   **Measurement Reconciliation:** Check unit weights or dimensions against system specs if discrepancies are noted in high-value or bulk items.

## Phase 3: Reconciliation and Analysis
*   **Data Entry:** Input physical count data into the central audit software immediately upon completion.
*   **Variance Investigation:** Flag any variance exceeding the pre-set tolerance threshold (e.g., ±0.5%).
*   **Recount Protocol:** Perform a secondary "blind recount" for all flagged variances.
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** Identify if discrepancies are due to administrative error, theft, shipping errors, or system integration failures.
*   **Final Report Generation:** Compile the final inventory variance report, detailing net value differences and recommended adjustments.

## Phase 4: Finalization and Sign-off
*   **System Adjustment:** Apply approved adjustments to the WMS to align stock levels with physical reality.
*   **Process Review:** Meet with the warehouse team to discuss the findings and implement corrective actions for recurring issues.
*   **Audit Sign-off:** Secure signatures from the Inventory Manager and the Finance Controller for audit documentation.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use cycle counting (counting smaller portions of inventory daily) rather than one massive annual audit to minimize operational downtime and maintain constant accuracy.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement QR code or RFID scanning to eliminate manual data entry errors.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "Work in Progress" (WIP) stock; ensure manufacturing supplies and partially finished goods are included in the count.
*   **Pitfall:** Failing to account for goods in transit; ensure a clear cut-off policy is in place for stock that is currently being shipped.
*   **Pitfall:** Overlooking "dead stock" in high-up shelving; use safety equipment to ensure all areas are physically accessed and verified.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should we do if the system count and physical count significantly differ?**
Immediate investigation is required. Check the "last transaction" log for the item, verify if the item was mislabeled, and ensure that recent shipments or receipts were processed in the system before the freeze.

**2. How often should we conduct an inventory audit?**
While annual audits are standard for financial reporting, high-turnover businesses should utilize daily or weekly cycle counting to maintain a "perpetual inventory" status.

**3. What is a "tolerance threshold"?**
This is a predefined percentage of allowed discrepancy for specific items. For instance, high-volume, low-cost items may have a 1% tolerance, while expensive, high-risk items should have a 0% tolerance.
