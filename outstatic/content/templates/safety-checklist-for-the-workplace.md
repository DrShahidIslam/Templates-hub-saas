# Standard Operating Procedure: Workplace Safety Inspection & Compliance

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory safety inspection protocols designed to mitigate workplace hazards, ensure regulatory compliance, and foster a culture of proactive risk management. Regular safety audits are essential to preventing occupational injuries, reducing liability, and maintaining operational continuity. All facility managers, safety officers, and department heads are responsible for executing these inspections consistently to maintain a secure environment for all employees and visitors.

## Workplace Safety Inspection Checklist

## General Facility & Egress
*   **Emergency Exits:** Ensure all exit doors are unlocked, unobstructed, and clearly marked with illuminated signage.
*   **Aisles and Walkways:** Verify that hallways and aisles are free of boxes, equipment, or debris to prevent tripping hazards.
*   **Lighting:** Confirm that all interior lighting and exterior security lights are functional; replace burnt-out bulbs immediately.
*   **Floor Surfaces:** Inspect for spills, loose carpet tiles, or uneven flooring that may cause slips or trips.

## Fire Protection & Life Safety
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Verify that extinguishers are mounted, accessible, charged, and have been inspected within the last 30 days.
*   **Fire Alarms:** Ensure pull stations are visible and not obstructed by shelving or equipment.
*   **Emergency Lighting:** Test battery-operated backup lights to ensure they function during a simulated power outage.
*   **Sprinkler Clearance:** Maintain a minimum 18-inch clearance between stored materials and sprinkler heads.

## Electrical Safety
*   **Extension Cords:** Prohibit the use of extension cords as permanent wiring; ensure all cords are in good condition without fraying.
*   **Power Strips:** Confirm that power strips are plugged directly into wall outlets and not daisy-chained.
*   **Electrical Panels:** Ensure panels are labeled clearly and maintain a 36-inch clear workspace in front of all breaker boxes.
*   **Outlets:** Check for damaged faceplates or charred outlets that may indicate thermal stress.

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) & First Aid
*   **First Aid Kits:** Check inventory against the approved list; remove and replace expired items.
*   **Eye Wash Stations:** Test stations weekly; ensure they are accessible within 10 seconds of hazardous areas and free of debris.
*   **PPE Inventory:** Verify that employees have access to required safety gear (goggles, gloves, vests) and that the gear is in sanitary, usable condition.

## Hazardous Materials & Storage
*   **Labeling:** Confirm all chemical containers are clearly labeled in accordance with GHS/OSHA standards.
*   **SDS Access:** Ensure the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) binder or digital portal is current and easily accessible to all staff.
*   **Storage Practices:** Ensure heavy items are stored on lower shelves and flammable liquids are kept in approved, ventilated cabinets.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Establish a "Safety Champion" Rotation.** Rotate inspection duties among staff members. When employees take ownership of their own workspace safety, compliance rates increase significantly.
*   **Pro Tip: Utilize Digital Logs.** Transition from paper checklists to digital platforms (e.g., mobile apps) to timestamp inspections and automatically alert maintenance when a defect is logged.
*   **Pitfall: The "Normalization of Deviance."** Beware of ignoring minor infractions (e.g., one box blocking an exit) because it has "always been like that." Small deviations often lead to catastrophic failures.
*   **Pitfall: Checking the Box.** Avoid "pencil-whipping" the checklist. If an inspection is performed without genuine scrutiny, it provides a false sense of security that is more dangerous than performing no inspection at all.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should these safety inspections be performed?**
While federal requirements vary by industry, best practice is to conduct a "walkthrough" inspection weekly and a comprehensive audit monthly to ensure consistent compliance.

**2. What should I do if I find a high-risk safety violation during an inspection?**
Immediately isolate the area or equipment (tag-out/lock-out). Report the hazard to your direct supervisor or the EHS (Environment, Health, and Safety) department immediately. Do not wait for the formal report to be filed.

**3. Is it necessary to keep records of these inspections?**
Yes. Documentation is your primary defense during regulatory audits. Maintain inspection logs for at least three years, as these records serve as proof of "due diligence" in the event of an insurance claim or government investigation.
