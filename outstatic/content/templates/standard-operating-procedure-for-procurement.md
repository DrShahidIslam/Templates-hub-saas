# Standard Operating Procedure: Procurement Lifecycle Management

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines the systematic process for procuring goods and services to ensure cost-efficiency, transparency, and operational continuity. This document serves as a mandate for all department heads and procurement officers to maintain fiscal responsibility, mitigate supply chain risks, and ensure that all purchases align with organizational budgetary constraints and compliance standards.

## Phase 1: Requisition and Authorization
*   **Identify Need:** Requesting department identifies a business requirement and verifies that the item/service is not currently in stock or available via internal transfer.
*   **Submit Purchase Requisition (PR):** Complete the formal PR form, including detailed specifications, quantity, and requested delivery date.
*   **Budget Verification:** Financial controller or department lead reviews the PR against the remaining department budget.
*   **Approval Workflow:** Obtain electronic signatures from the authorized budget holder and, if the amount exceeds a specific threshold, the Finance Director.

## Phase 2: Sourcing and Supplier Selection
*   **Market Analysis:** Conduct a brief review of available vendors capable of meeting the technical specifications.
*   **Request for Quotation (RFQ):** Distribute RFQ/RFP documents to at least three qualified vendors to ensure competitive pricing.
*   **Evaluation:** Compare bids based on total cost of ownership (TCO), lead times, vendor reputation, and quality standards.
*   **Due Diligence:** Perform a background check on new vendors, including credit references and compliance with organizational ethics policies.

## Phase 3: Purchase Order (PO) Issuance
*   **PO Generation:** Create a formal Purchase Order referencing the approved PR and the selected vendor’s quote.
*   **PO Review:** Ensure the PO includes precise item descriptions, agreed-upon pricing, payment terms (e.g., Net 30), and shipping terms (Incoterms).
*   **Formal Transmission:** Send the PO to the vendor and obtain a written acknowledgment or signature to create a legally binding agreement.

## Phase 4: Delivery and Quality Assurance
*   **Receipt of Goods:** Warehouse or department personnel inspect the delivery against the packing slip and the PO.
*   **Quality Inspection:** Verify that items meet the technical specifications outlined in the original PR.
*   **Discrepancy Reporting:** If goods are damaged or incorrect, immediately notify the Procurement Officer to initiate a Return Merchandise Authorization (RMA) process.
*   **Goods Receipt Note (GRN):** Formally sign off on the receipt of goods in the inventory management system to trigger the accounts payable process.

## Phase 5: Payment and Closure
*   **Invoice Reconciliation:** Accounts Payable performs a "Three-Way Match" (comparing the PO, the GRN, and the Supplier Invoice).
*   **Payment Execution:** Once verified, release payment according to the agreed-upon credit terms.
*   **Documentation Archiving:** Store all procurement records, including correspondence and receipts, for a minimum of seven years for audit purposes.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always establish "Preferred Vendor" lists for recurring supplies. This reduces administrative overhead and secures volume discounts.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use blanket purchase orders for recurring services (like landscaping or office cleaning) to avoid generating individual POs for every monthly cycle.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "Maverick Spending"—purchasing without an approved PO. This creates financial gaps and prevents proper budget tracking.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not ignore shipping/incoterms. Poorly defined shipping terms often lead to unexpected landed costs, customs fees, or liability issues for damaged goods.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. What should I do if an urgent request arises that bypasses the standard timeline?**
Emergency requests must follow the "Emergency Procurement Protocol," which requires verbal or written approval from the CFO or COO, followed by a retroactive PR submission within 24 hours of the purchase.

**2. How do we handle price fluctuations for raw materials?**
In cases of high volatility, include a "Price Escalation Clause" in the contract with the vendor that outlines specific triggers (e.g., commodity index changes) that allow for a price adjustment.

**3. When is a formal Request for Proposal (RFP) required instead of an RFQ?**
An RFP is required for high-value, complex, or high-risk projects where the solution is not strictly defined, or when the organization requires the vendor to propose creative or technical approaches to a problem.
