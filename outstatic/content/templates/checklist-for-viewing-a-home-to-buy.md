# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Property Inspection for Potential Buyers

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as a professional framework for prospective homebuyers to evaluate a property systematically. When conducting a viewing, emotions often cloud judgment; this guide provides an objective, detail-oriented methodology to identify potential structural issues, verify property maintenance, and assess long-term viability. By utilizing this checklist, you will transition from a casual observer to a strategic buyer, ensuring that every square foot of the property is scrutinized before making an offer.

## 1. Exterior & Structural Integrity
The exterior is the first line of defense against the elements. Do not overlook these areas, as they are often the most expensive to repair.

*   **Foundation:** Look for stair-step cracking in brickwork or large vertical fissures in concrete foundations.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect for missing shingles, excessive granule loss, or sagging rooflines which may indicate truss failure.
*   **Grading/Drainage:** Ensure the ground slopes away from the foundation. Check for signs of water pooling or dampness near the base.
*   **Siding/Paint:** Check for signs of rot in wooden trim, peeling paint, or cracked stucco.
*   **Gutters:** Verify downspouts are directed away from the house and are not clogged or detached.

## 2. Interior Systems & Utilities
Functionality is more critical than aesthetics. A beautiful kitchen cannot compensate for a failing electrical system.

*   **Electrical:** Test light switches and check the age of the breaker panel (look for fuses or outdated brands like Federal Pacific).
*   **Plumbing:** Turn on multiple faucets simultaneously to check water pressure. Check under all sinks for signs of current or past leaks.
*   **HVAC:** Check the manufacture date on the furnace/AC unit. Run the heat and the cooling for a few minutes to ensure they engage properly.
*   **Water Heater:** Note the age and check for corrosion around the tank base or valves.
*   **Windows/Doors:** Open and close every window to check for broken seals (fogging between panes) or sticking frames.

## 3. Interior Aesthetics & Quality of Life
These items impact daily comfort and long-term maintenance costs.

*   **Flooring:** Look for uneven floorboards, soft spots, or significant stains that may require full replacement.
*   **Walls/Ceilings:** Check for water stains (often appearing as yellow or brown circles) which indicate past roof or plumbing leaks.
*   **Attic/Basement:** Look for evidence of pests (droppings, shredded insulation) or mold growth.
*   **Storage/Layout:** Assess if the flow of the floor plan matches your lifestyle and if existing storage is adequate for your needs.

## 4. Neighborhood & External Factors
The home exists within a broader environment that affects its resale value and quality of life.

*   **Noise Pollution:** Listen for proximity to heavy traffic, train tracks, or flight paths.
*   **Neighbor Proximity:** Evaluate privacy levels and the state of repair of adjacent properties.
*   **HOA/Covenants:** Review any Homeowners Association rules regarding parking, exterior colors, or pet restrictions.
*   **Sunlight:** Observe the orientation of the house; south-facing homes receive more natural light but may have higher cooling costs.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

*   **Pro Tip: Bring a Toolkit.** Always carry a flashlight (for basements/attics), a marble (to check floor leveling), and a phone charger (to test every outlet).
*   **Pro Tip: Take Video.** Walk through the house recording a steady video while narrating your observations. Your memory will fade after the third or fourth house.
*   **Pitfall: The "Staging Trap."** Do not be distracted by high-end furniture, pleasant scents, or music. These are designed to create an emotional connection. Focus on the "bones" of the house.
*   **Pitfall: Ignoring Small Repairs.** Never assume that a small fix is inexpensive. A single loose tile can be a symptom of a larger structural movement.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I bring a contractor or professional inspector to the first viewing?**
A: Generally, no. A professional inspection is a contingency for your offer. For the first viewing, use this checklist to decide if the home is worth the investment of a professional inspection later.

**Q: What is the most important "hidden" thing to check?**
A: Always check the smell. A musty odor often indicates mold or dampness trapped behind walls or under flooring that you cannot see visually.

**Q: How many times should I view a home before making an offer?**
A: At minimum, twice. The first viewing is for initial impressions, and the second should be at a different time of day to observe changes in lighting, neighborhood noise, and traffic patterns.
