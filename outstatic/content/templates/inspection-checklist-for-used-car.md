# Standard Operating Procedure: Pre-Purchase Used Vehicle Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection protocol for evaluating a pre-owned vehicle prior to acquisition. The objective is to identify mechanical faults, cosmetic defects, and structural compromises to ensure the asset meets safety standards and value requirements. All inspections should be conducted in daylight, on level ground, with the vehicle engine cold upon arrival.

## 1. Exterior & Structural Integrity
*   **Body Panels:** Inspect for mismatched paint colors, inconsistent gaps between panels (signs of poor accident repair), and uneven texture (orange peel).
*   **Paint Condition:** Check for clear coat peeling, deep scratches, or rust bubbles, particularly around wheel wells and door sills.
*   **Glass:** Inspect all windows and the windshield for cracks, chips, or delamination. Verify that the windshield is original equipment (OEM) if the car is high-value.
*   **Tires:** Check for even tread wear. Uneven wear indicates alignment or suspension issues. Ensure all tires are of the same brand and have sufficient tread depth (minimum 4/32").
*   **Frame:** Kneel and look underneath for signs of frame straightening, weld beads, or excessive corrosion on the chassis rails.

## 2. Under the Hood
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check oil, brake fluid, coolant, and power steering fluid. Dark, gritty oil indicates poor maintenance; milky coolant indicates a blown head gasket.
*   **Leaks:** Inspect the engine block, valve covers, and hoses for wet spots or pooling oil/coolant.
*   **Belts & Hoses:** Squeeze hoses to ensure they aren't brittle. Inspect serpentine belts for cracks or fraying.
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion and check for signs of a recent replacement.
*   **Exhaust:** Look for excessive rust or hanging components. A loud roar upon startup indicates a hole in the exhaust system.

## 3. Interior & Electronics
*   **Controls:** Test every button, switch, and knob. Confirm A/C blows cold and heater blows hot.
*   **Infotainment:** Pair a phone via Bluetooth; test audio quality, GPS, and backup camera functionality.
*   **Dashboard Warnings:** Ensure all warning lights (Check Engine, ABS, Airbag) cycle on with the key and turn off after ignition.
*   **Upholstery:** Check for tears, cigarette burns, or excessive wear on the driver’s seat bolster.
*   **Odors:** Smell for signs of flood damage (musty/moldy) or heavy smoke residue.

## 4. Operational Test Drive
*   **Cold Start:** Start the engine cold to listen for lifter tick, rattles, or exhaust manifold leaks.
*   **Transmission:** Ensure shifts are smooth and without hesitation, clunking, or shuddering.
*   **Braking:** Test for pulling to one side, pulsing in the pedal (warped rotors), or grinding noises.
*   **Suspension:** Drive over speed bumps to check for clunking sounds, which indicate worn struts, bushings, or ball joints.
*   **Alignment:** On a safe, straight road, briefly let go of the wheel to check if the car drifts significantly.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always request a Vehicle History Report (Carfax or AutoCheck). A "clean" title is not enough; check for "Salvage" or "Rebuilt" designations.
*   **Pro Tip:** Bring a magnet. If the magnet does not stick to a section of the body panel, it indicates Bondo (filler) was used to repair a previous dent.
*   **Pitfall:** Never purchase a vehicle at night or in the rain. These conditions effectively mask bodywork defects and paint imperfections.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring the "Check Engine" light or claiming "it’s just a sensor." Assume the worst-case scenario for any warning light illuminated on the dash.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I bring the car to a third-party mechanic?**
A: Yes. Even if you perform this inspection, a professional mechanic with a lift can identify issues (like suspension or transmission internal leaks) that are impossible to see while standing in a parking lot.

**Q: What is a "cold start" and why does it matter?**
A: A cold start means starting the engine after it has been sitting for several hours. This is vital because some engine noises—such as piston slap or worn hydraulic lifters—only occur when the oil is cold and hasn't yet circulated fully.

**Q: If the car has a minor leak, should I walk away?**
A: Not necessarily, but you must estimate the repair cost. Use the cost of the repair as a tool to negotiate the price down. If the seller refuses to adjust the price for known defects, walk away.
