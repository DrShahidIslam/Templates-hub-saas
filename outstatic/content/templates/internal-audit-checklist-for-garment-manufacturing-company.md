# Standard Operating Procedure: Internal Audit for Garment Manufacturing

## Purpose and Scope
This Internal Audit SOP provides a systematic framework for evaluating the operational efficiency, compliance, and quality control standards within a garment manufacturing facility. The scope covers the entire lifecycle from raw material procurement and inventory management to the production floor, quality assurance (QA), and final shipment. This procedure is designed to minimize waste, ensure regulatory compliance, maintain ethical labor standards, and optimize production throughput.

## Section 1: Pre-Production and Raw Materials
*   Verify the accuracy of Bill of Materials (BOM) against purchase orders.
*   Check raw material inspection reports (fabric shrinkage, color fastness, GSM consistency).
*   Assess warehouse storage conditions (humidity control, pest prevention, fire safety).
*   Reconcile inventory records with physical stock counts (FIFO/LIFO adherence).
*   Review vendor certification records (OEKO-TEX, GOTS, etc., where applicable).

## Section 2: Production Floor Operations
*   Evaluate machine maintenance logs (ensure preventive maintenance is current).
*   Audit the cutting room for marker efficiency and fabric utilization (waste reduction metrics).
*   Check sewing line balance and bottleneck management.
*   Verify presence and utilization of standard operating procedures (SOPs) at each workstation.
*   Monitor needle control policy adherence (broken needle logging).

## Section 3: Quality Assurance and Compliance
*   Review AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) inspection reports at each stage (In-line, End-of-line, Final audit).
*   Verify calibration status of all measuring equipment (tape measures, scales, iron temperatures).
*   Check for clear segregation of "Pass," "Fail," and "Rework" garment bins.
*   Ensure compliance with local labor laws (working hours, overtime documentation, minimum wage).
*   Confirm existence of PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) and safety training logs.

## Section 4: Packaging and Logistics
*   Validate packing list accuracy against shipping documents.
*   Check carton labeling and barcode compliance (GSI standards).
*   Inspect storage environment of finished goods prior to dispatch.
*   Review documentation for outbound logistics and third-party freight carriers.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use digital audit software to capture real-time photo evidence. It minimizes bias and provides a clearer trail for corrective actions.
*   **Pro Tip:** Perform "unannounced" spot checks on the factory floor. Scheduled audits often lead to temporary cleanup behaviors that hide recurring issues.
*   **Pitfall - The Paper Trail Trap:** Do not rely solely on signed documents. Documents can be back-dated. Cross-reference paperwork with physical observations and worker interviews.
*   **Pitfall - Focusing Only on Defects:** Don’t just look at what is broken. Evaluate why it broke. If a machine fails repeatedly, the audit should flag the maintenance budget or the operator’s training level, not just the repair log.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should an internal audit be conducted?**
A: For high-volume garment manufacturing, a comprehensive internal audit should be performed at least quarterly, with monthly high-risk spot checks on quality and safety.

**Q: What is the most critical area to audit in a garment factory?**
A: The "In-line" quality inspection process is the most critical. Catching defects during the sewing process is significantly cheaper than rework or returns after the garments are finished and packed.

**Q: What should be the immediate reaction to a failed audit finding?**
A: Implement a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) plan. Identify the root cause, assign a responsible person for remediation, set a deadline, and schedule a follow-up verification audit.
