# Standard Operating Procedure: Compliance Operations in the Textile Industry

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for the Compliance Department within our textile manufacturing operations. As the global textile supply chain faces rigorous scrutiny, this document ensures alignment with international labor standards, environmental regulations (such as REACH, ZDHC, and Higg Index), and localized health and safety mandates. The objective is to maintain ethical sourcing, mitigate legal risk, and uphold brand integrity through continuous monitoring and transparent documentation.

## Phase 1: Social Compliance and Labor Standards
*   **Audit Preparation:** Review all personnel files for age verification documents (prevention of child labor) and ensure voluntary overtime records are maintained.
*   **Wage Verification:** Cross-reference time-clock data with payroll registers to ensure compliance with minimum wage laws and overtime premium mandates.
*   **Worker Interviews:** Conduct monthly anonymous interviews to verify the absence of forced labor, harassment, or disciplinary abuse.
*   **Fire Safety Inspection:** Conduct weekly walkthroughs to ensure all emergency exits are unblocked, fire extinguishers are pressurized, and evacuation routes are clearly illuminated.

## Phase 2: Environmental and Chemical Compliance
*   **Chemical Inventory Management:** Maintain an updated "Hazardous Chemical List" for all dyes and auxiliary agents; ensure every container has a corresponding Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS).
*   **Wastewater Treatment (ETP):** Monitor daily effluent readings for pH, BOD, COD, and TDS levels; ensure discharge falls within local regulatory parameters.
*   **Restricted Substance List (RSL) Testing:** Coordinate quarterly third-party lab testing of fabric swatches to ensure zero presence of restricted azo dyes, heavy metals, or phthalates.
*   **Energy and Resource Tracking:** Log monthly water and electricity consumption to support Sustainability Reporting (e.g., Higg Facility Environmental Module).

## Phase 3: Supply Chain and Sub-contractor Oversight
*   **Supplier Code of Conduct:** Ensure all tier-2 suppliers (yarn mills, embroidery units) have signed the corporate Code of Conduct.
*   **Sub-contractor Audits:** Execute unannounced spot checks on all outsourced facilities to ensure they are not operating under illicit conditions.
*   **Traceability Documentation:** Verify the "Chain of Custody" for raw materials, particularly organic cotton (GOTS) or recycled polyester (GRS), to prevent fraudulent material claims.

## Phase 4: Reporting and Corrective Action
*   **Non-Conformance Tracking:** Log every violation in the central Corrective Action Plan (CAP) database.
*   **Root Cause Analysis (RCA):** Perform a formal RCA within 72 hours of identifying a systemic failure (e.g., repeated overtime violations).
*   **Stakeholder Communication:** Update the Executive Board on compliance health scores at the end of each fiscal quarter.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Digitalization):** Implement a cloud-based compliance dashboard to automate document expiry alerts for certifications like Oeko-Tex or ISO 14001.
*   **Pro Tip (Worker Engagement):** Treat workers as your primary compliance sensors; establishing an effective, non-retaliatory grievance mechanism is the most effective way to catch issues early.
*   **Pitfall (The "Audit Trap"):** Avoid the mistake of "preparing" for audits only days before they occur. Maintain "audit-ready" status every day to avoid the stress and failure rates of superficial compliance.
*   **Pitfall (Opaque Sub-contracting):** Allowing undocumented sub-contracting is the #1 cause of major compliance failures in the textile industry. Ensure your Vendor Management System is strictly enforced.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should we review our Restricted Substance List (RSL)?**
A: Regulations change frequently. Conduct a formal RSL review every six months or whenever there is a major update to global standards (e.g., EU REACH updates).

**Q: What is the best way to handle a supplier who fails a social compliance audit?**
A: Do not terminate immediately unless the violation is egregious (e.g., child labor). Issue a Corrective Action Plan (CAP) with a strict timeline and provide capacity-building support to help them remediate the issue.

**Q: How do we manage compliance during peak season high-pressure periods?**
A: Build "buffer capacity" into your production planning. Compliance usually breaks when management prioritizes output over worker hours; clear communication with sales teams regarding realistic lead times is essential.
