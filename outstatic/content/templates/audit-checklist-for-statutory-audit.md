# Standard Operating Procedure: Statutory Audit Preparation

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the systematic approach required to prepare for a statutory audit. A statutory audit is a legally required review of the accuracy of a company's financial records, statements, and internal controls. The objective of this procedure is to ensure transparency, regulatory compliance, and a seamless audit engagement by maintaining organized documentation and facilitating timely communication between the organization and external auditors.

## 1. Pre-Audit Planning and Documentation
*   **Engagement Letter Review:** Confirm the scope, objectives, and timeline defined in the signed engagement letter.
*   **Audit Team Coordination:** Appoint a primary liaison (typically the Financial Controller or CFO) to serve as the single point of contact.
*   **Trial Balance Finalization:** Ensure the final Trial Balance (TB) is reconciled and locked for the audit period.
*   **Statutory Compliance Check:** Verify that all filings (tax, payroll, local corporate filings) have been submitted and that receipts are available for inspection.
*   **Documentation Repository:** Create a secure, cloud-based folder structure organized by audit area (e.g., Cash, Fixed Assets, Revenue) to facilitate remote access for auditors.

## 2. Balance Sheet and Asset Verification
*   **Cash and Bank Reconciliations:** Prepare bank reconciliation statements for all accounts; provide original bank statements and confirm balances via third-party bank confirmations.
*   **Fixed Assets Register (FAR):** Cross-verify the FAR against the physical existence of assets; ensure depreciation schedules are calculated according to local accounting standards and company policy.
*   **Accounts Receivable:** Prepare an aged debtors list; ensure Provision for Doubtful Debts is supported by documentation and management logic.
*   **Inventory/Stock:** Provide documentation regarding the physical stock-take process, including cut-off procedures and valuation methodology (e.g., FIFO or Weighted Average).

## 3. Profit and Loss (P&L) and Operational Accuracy
*   **Revenue Recognition:** Validate revenue samples against invoices, shipping documents, and proof of service delivery to ensure compliance with revenue recognition standards (e.g., IFRS 15).
*   **Payroll and Benefits:** Reconcile payroll registers to the General Ledger; ensure tax deductions (PAYE/WHT) are correctly recorded and paid.
*   **Operating Expenses:** Provide a sample of invoices for major expense categories, ensuring authorization levels align with the Delegation of Authority (DOA) matrix.
*   **Accruals and Prepayments:** Provide supporting schedules for all material accruals to prove they relate to the correct accounting period.

## 4. Governance and Corporate Records
*   **Board Minutes:** Provide copies of all board and committee meeting minutes held during the financial year.
*   **Legal Correspondence:** Secure written confirmations from external legal counsel regarding ongoing litigation or contingent liabilities.
*   **Contracts:** Compile a file of all material contracts signed during the year, particularly those related to long-term leases, debt financing, or significant vendor agreements.

## 5. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: The "PBC" List:** Start working on the "Provided by Client" (PBC) list as soon as it is received. Auditors appreciate early submissions, which often lead to fewer follow-up queries.
*   **Pro Tip: Narratives:** Document the rationale behind significant management estimates (e.g., impairment tests or bonus accruals) before the auditors ask for them.
*   **Pitfall - The "Drip-Feed" Method:** Providing documents in a fragmented, unorganized manner frustrates auditors and often leads to higher audit fees due to increased time spent on administrative reconciliation.
*   **Pitfall - Changing Numbers:** Never post manual journal entries after the trial balance has been handed over to auditors without notifying them immediately. This undermines the audit trail and compromises internal control integrity.

## 6. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How far in advance should we start preparing for the statutory audit?**
A: Ideally, preparation should be an ongoing year-round process. However, a formal "audit readiness" phase should commence 4–6 weeks prior to the fieldwork date to ensure all reconciliations are current.

**Q: What should we do if we disagree with an auditor’s proposed adjustment?**
A: Maintain a professional, data-driven dialogue. Request the specific accounting standard or regulation they are citing. If a disagreement persists, escalate the matter to the Audit Committee or Board of Directors for a final determination.

**Q: Is it necessary to provide auditors with access to our entire accounting system?**
A: It is generally sufficient to provide "read-only" access to specific modules or to export required reports. Direct system access should be governed by strict data security protocols and revoked immediately following the completion of the audit.
