# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Preventive Maintenance (PM)

## Introduction
The objective of this Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is to standardize the preventive maintenance protocol across the fleet to ensure operational reliability, maximize vehicle lifespan, and uphold the highest safety standards. Adherence to this schedule minimizes unscheduled downtime, reduces long-term repair costs, and ensures compliance with Department of Transportation (DOT) and manufacturer-recommended safety guidelines. This SOP applies to all fleet vehicles and must be executed by certified technicians or designated fleet management personnel.

## Preventive Maintenance Checklist

## Section 1: Fluid & Filter Inspection
*   **Engine Oil:** Check level and viscosity; perform a change based on mileage intervals (e.g., every 5,000–7,500 miles).
*   **Engine Oil Filter:** Replace the filter with every oil change.
*   **Coolant System:** Check coolant level and freeze point. Inspect hoses for cracks, bulges, or leaks.
*   **Transmission Fluid:** Check fluid level and color (should be translucent red; burnt smell or brown color indicates required service).
*   **Brake Fluid:** Check levels and ensure the fluid is not contaminated.
*   **Power Steering Fluid:** Top off if low; inspect hoses for leaks.
*   **Windshield Washer Fluid:** Top off to maximum level.
*   **Air Filters:** Inspect cabin and engine air filters; replace if obstructed by debris.

## Section 2: Chassis & Drivetrain
*   **Tires:** Measure tread depth on all tires; check sidewalls for cuts or bulges.
*   **Tire Pressure:** Adjust all tires to the PSI specified on the driver’s side door placard (do not rely on the number on the tire sidewall).
*   **Brake System:** Inspect brake pads for thickness; check rotors/drums for warping, scoring, or heavy rust.
*   **Suspension:** Inspect shocks and struts for leakage; check bushings for dry rot or play.
*   **Driveshaft/CV Joints:** Check CV boots for tears and the driveshaft for excessive play or vibration.
*   **Exhaust System:** Visually inspect for hangers, rust, or leaks that may cause carbon monoxide ingress.

## Section 3: Electrical & Interior Safety
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion and ensure connections are tight; test voltage and load-holding capacity.
*   **Lights:** Verify full functionality of headlights (high/low), turn signals, brake lights, reverse lights, and hazard flashers.
*   **Instrumentation:** Check the dashboard for active warning lights (Check Engine, ABS, TPMS).
*   **Safety Equipment:** Verify the presence and expiration dates of fire extinguishers and first-aid kits.
*   **Seatbelts:** Ensure all belts retract smoothly and latch securely.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls

### Pro Tips
*   **Digital Logs:** Move away from paper logs. Use Fleet Management Software (FMS) to automate reminders based on real-time telematics data rather than arbitrary dates.
*   **Trend Analysis:** Keep a log of "minor" issues. If a vehicle requires frequent fluid top-offs, it is a leading indicator of a failing seal or gasket before a catastrophic breakdown occurs.
*   **Driver Engagement:** Empower drivers to conduct a "Circle Check" (pre-trip inspection) daily. They are your first line of defense.

### Common Pitfalls
*   **"Neglecting the Tires":** Many managers focus on the engine and ignore tire alignment/rotation, leading to uneven wear and premature replacement costs.
*   **Ignoring Recalls:** Failing to check manufacturer recall databases during scheduled maintenance is a major liability risk.
*   **"If it ain't broke, don't fix it":** This is the single biggest cause of fleet failure. Preventive maintenance is intended to replace parts *before* they fail.

## FAQ

**Q1: What should I do if a vehicle is overdue for maintenance?**
A: Immediately pull the vehicle from the active rotation. Operating an overdue vehicle violates safety protocols and increases the risk of expensive secondary damage and liability.

**Q2: Should I follow the manufacturer’s schedule or a fixed-mileage schedule?**
A: Always follow the manufacturer’s manual as the baseline. However, if your fleet operates under "severe duty" conditions (idling, heavy towing, or extreme temperatures), shorten the service intervals by 20–30%.

**Q3: Who is authorized to sign off on a completed PM check?**
A: Only a designated fleet supervisor or a qualified lead mechanic can sign off on the maintenance log. This ensures accountability for the work performed and verifies that the vehicle is safe to return to service.
