# Standard Operating Procedure: Vehicle Inspection Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory requirements for conducting a comprehensive vehicle inspection. Whether preparing for a long-distance trip, routine maintenance, or a pre-purchase evaluation, following this structured approach ensures mechanical reliability, optimal safety, and the early detection of costly repairs. Technicians and vehicle owners should perform these checks in a well-lit, level environment with the engine cold to ensure accuracy and personal safety.

## 1. Exterior & Structural Integrity
*   **Body Panels:** Inspect for dents, deep scratches, rust, or mismatched paint, which may indicate previous collision repair.
*   **Glass & Mirrors:** Check windshields for cracks or "bullseyes" and ensure all side mirrors are secure and free of obstruction.
*   **Lighting:** Verify operation of low beams, high beams, turn signals (front/rear), brake lights, and reverse lights.
*   **Tires:** Check for uneven tread wear (alignment issues), tread depth (minimum 2/32"), and ensure sidewalls are free of bulges or gashes.

## 2. Under-Hood Mechanicals
*   **Engine Oil:** Remove the dipstick, wipe clean, reinsert, and check oil level and viscosity/color.
*   **Coolant System:** Check reservoir levels; ensure hoses are not brittle, cracked, or leaking. **Note:** Only open when the engine is cool.
*   **Battery:** Inspect terminals for corrosion (white powdery substance) and ensure the mounting bracket is secure.
*   **Belts & Hoses:** Inspect serpentine belt for fraying or missing chunks. Check all accessible rubber hoses for soft spots or leaks.
*   **Fluid Reservoirs:** Verify levels for brake fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer fluid.

## 3. Interior & Electronic Systems
*   **Dashboard Indicators:** Start the engine and ensure all "Check Engine," "ABS," and "Airbag" lights cycle off.
*   **HVAC:** Test the A/C for cold air and the heater for rapid warming; cycle through all fan speed settings.
*   **Controls:** Cycle through all windows, door locks, and seat adjustments.
*   **Safety Equipment:** Ensure seatbelts retract smoothly and lock upon sudden tension. Verify the presence of a spare tire, jack, and lug wrench.

## 4. Under-Car & Suspension
*   **Leaks:** Look for fresh droplets of oil, coolant, or transmission fluid on the pavement.
*   **Exhaust:** Check the exhaust pipe for excessive rust or structural hangers that have disconnected.
*   **Suspension:** Visually inspect shock absorbers and struts for oil "weeping" or dampness, which indicates seal failure.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "White Glove" approach for fluid checks. If the engine oil is milky or frothy, this is a major red flag indicating a blown head gasket.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always carry a digital tire pressure gauge. Relying on the vehicle’s TPMS (Tire Pressure Monitoring System) is insufficient, as these sensors can fail or provide inaccurate readings.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore a burning smell. Even if no fluid is visible, a "sweet" smell usually indicates a coolant leak, while a "burnt rubber" smell indicates a failing belt or accessory pulley.
*   **Pitfall:** Don't skip the "Cold Start." Most mechanical noises (ticking, knocking) are audible only during the first 60 seconds of a cold engine start.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I conduct a full-scale vehicle inspection?**
A: Ideally, a comprehensive inspection should be performed every 5,000 miles or before any long-distance trip (over 200 miles).

**Q: Why is my "Check Engine" light blinking instead of steady?**
A: A blinking light indicates a severe engine misfire. You should pull over safely and turn off the engine immediately, as continuing to drive can cause permanent damage to the catalytic converter.

**Q: What is the most common reason for inspection failure?**
A: Neglected tire maintenance and burned-out lighting elements are the most frequent causes of inspection failures. Both are easily preventable through regular monthly checks.
