# Standard Operating Procedure: LT Panel Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory protocols for performing preventive maintenance on Low Tension (LT) electrical panels. The objective is to ensure equipment longevity, prevent unexpected electrical failures, mitigate fire hazards, and maintain personnel safety. All maintenance tasks must be performed by qualified electrical personnel using appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) following Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures.

## 1. Safety and Preparation (Pre-Maintenance)
*   Ensure all relevant personnel are notified of the scheduled power outage.
*   Verify that the main incomer breaker is switched OFF and locked using a padlock (LOTO).
*   Attach a "Danger: Men Working" warning tag to the panel handle.
*   Verify the absence of voltage using a calibrated multimeter or non-contact voltage tester before touching any internal components.
*   Wear insulated gloves (Class 0), safety goggles, and arc-rated clothing.

## 2. Visual and Physical Inspection
*   Inspect the panel enclosure for signs of corrosion, water ingress, or pest infestation.
*   Check the integrity of the door gaskets to ensure they maintain the panel’s IP rating.
*   Examine the panel exterior for any signs of soot or discoloration indicating previous overheating.
*   Verify the functionality of all panel door hinges, locks, and latches.

## 3. Cleaning and Debris Removal
*   Use a vacuum cleaner with a non-conductive nozzle to remove dust, cobwebs, and debris from the base of the panel.
*   Clean the interior surfaces using a dry, lint-free cloth. Do not use water or wet rags.
*   If heavy dust accumulation exists on busbars, use a dry, soft-bristled brush, ensuring no particles fall into sensitive relay sections.
*   Clean the insulators and support blocks to prevent tracking paths for electrical arcs.

## 4. Mechanical and Electrical Tightening
*   Check all termination points (busbars, cable lugs, and circuit breaker terminals) for tightness using a calibrated torque wrench.
*   Look for signs of "hot spots" such as darkened insulation or pitted metal surfaces.
*   Verify the tightness of control circuit wiring, terminal blocks, and secondary wiring of Current Transformers (CTs).
*   Clean air circuit breaker (ACB) or molded case circuit breaker (MCCB) contact surfaces if recommended by the manufacturer.

## 5. Functional Testing and Verification
*   Operate the breakers manually to check for smooth mechanical movement.
*   Verify that mechanical interlocks between main and DG supply breakers are functioning correctly.
*   Check the calibration and functionality of indicating lamps, voltmeters, and ammeters.
*   Test the Trip/Close functionality of protective relays using a secondary injection test kit if necessary.
*   Ensure the earth link is clean, secure, and properly tightened to the busbar.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use an infrared (IR) thermal camera while the panel is under load (before the shutdown) to identify hidden loose connections that generate heat.
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the torque values applied during tightening to ensure consistent maintenance cycles.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not use compressed air to blow out dust; this often pushes conductive dust deeper into intricate relay components, causing short circuits.
*   **Pitfall:** Never ignore a faint "buzzing" sound. It is a precursor to an arcing fault and requires immediate investigation, regardless of the maintenance schedule.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should LT panel maintenance be performed?**
A: Preventive maintenance should ideally be performed every 6 months for heavy-duty industrial environments and annually for commercial or office buildings.

**Q: Can I perform maintenance while the panel is live?**
A: No. Any invasive maintenance, including cleaning and tightening, must be performed only after the panel is de-energized and verified safe via LOTO procedures.

**Q: What should I do if I find a discolored or melted cable lug?**
A: Do not simply tighten the connection. A discolored lug indicates high-resistance heating. You must cut the damaged section of the cable, re-terminate with a new lug, and inspect the breaker terminal for heat damage.
