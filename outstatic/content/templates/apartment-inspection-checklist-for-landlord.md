# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Apartment Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) serves as the definitive guide for property managers and landlords conducting move-in, routine, or move-out inspections. A thorough, documented inspection is essential for protecting property assets, ensuring tenant safety, maintaining habitability standards, and providing an objective record for security deposit reconciliations. Adherence to this protocol minimizes legal liability and facilitates clear communication between the landlord and the tenant.

## Pre-Inspection Protocols
*   **Documentation:** Ensure you have a copy of the original Move-In Inspection form and a high-resolution camera or smartphone.
*   **Legal Compliance:** Provide the tenant with the legally required notice of entry (typically 24–48 hours) as per local jurisdiction.
*   **Safety Gear:** Carry a flashlight, outlet tester, spare lightbulbs, and basic hand tools.

## Room-by-Room Inspection Checklist

## Kitchen
*   **Appliances:** Test burners on the stove, check the refrigerator/freezer temperature, and cycle the dishwasher.
*   **Plumbing:** Check under the sink for signs of current or past water leaks, mold, or mildew.
*   **Fixtures:** Verify garbage disposal functionality and ensure the faucet aerator is clear of debris.
*   **Cabinetry:** Inspect drawers and hinges for smooth operation and structural integrity.

## Bathroom
*   **Plumbing:** Flush toilets to check for proper seals; turn on all faucets to check for adequate water pressure and drainage speed.
*   **Ventilation:** Confirm exhaust fans are drawing air correctly (test with a single square of toilet paper).
*   **Sanitation:** Inspect grout and caulking around the tub and sink for signs of mold or degradation.
*   **Fixtures:** Examine the showerhead for clogs or mineral buildup.

## Living Areas & Bedrooms
*   **Flooring:** Document all scuffs, stains, scratches, or tears in carpeting or hardwood.
*   **Walls & Ceilings:** Note any cracks, nail holes, paint chips, or water stains indicating potential leaks.
*   **Windows:** Test all locks and ensure sashes slide/open smoothly. Inspect screens for tears.
*   **Electrical:** Use an outlet tester to verify power flow to all receptacles. Test light switches and ceiling fans.

## Systems & Safety
*   **HVAC:** Check the air filter condition and ensure the thermostat is responsive.
*   **Safety:** Verify smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors are operational by pressing the "Test" button.
*   **Entry/Security:** Confirm deadbolts engage fully with the strike plate and door sweeps are intact.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Time-Stamping:** Use a mobile inspection app that embeds date and time stamps on photos. This provides indisputable evidence for potential security deposit disputes.
*   **Pro Tip: The "Tenant Presence" Strategy:** Whenever possible, conduct inspections with the tenant present. It allows for immediate discussion regarding maintenance requests and prevents "he-said-she-said" scenarios.
*   **Pitfall: Normal Wear and Tear:** Distinguish clearly between damage (e.g., a hole in the wall) and normal wear and tear (e.g., slight fading of carpet in high-traffic areas). Charging for wear and tear can lead to legal issues.
*   **Pitfall: Missing the "Hidden" Spots:** Always check behind the refrigerator, inside the oven, and inside the hot water heater closet; these are common areas for hidden maintenance failures.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I conduct routine inspections?**
It is standard practice to conduct routine inspections every 6 to 12 months. Ensure this frequency is clearly stated in your lease agreement to avoid conflict.

**Can I charge the tenant for a cleaning fee after they move out?**
You may only deduct from the security deposit for cleaning if the unit is returned in a condition that requires more work than "standard cleaning" to return it to its original move-in state. Always check local laws, as some states strictly regulate how cleaning costs are handled.

**What should I do if I find significant damage during a routine inspection?**
Document the damage with high-resolution photos, write a summary report, and send a formal "Notice to Cure or Quit" or a maintenance request to the tenant, depending on whether the damage was caused by negligence or natural aging.
