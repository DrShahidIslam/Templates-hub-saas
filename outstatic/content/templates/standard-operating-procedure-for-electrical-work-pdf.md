# Standard Operating Procedure: Electrical Work Safety and Execution

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory safety protocols and operational workflow for conducting electrical work within this facility. Adherence to these procedures is critical to mitigating the risk of electrical shock, arc flash incidents, and equipment damage. All personnel performing electrical tasks must possess the appropriate licensure and ensure that all work complies with the National Electrical Code (NEC) and Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) standards. This document serves as the primary guide for both planning and executing electrical maintenance, installation, and troubleshooting tasks.

## Phase 1: Preparation and Risk Assessment
*   **Permit Acquisition:** Verify that all required work permits are signed and displayed at the job site.
*   **Scope Definition:** Clearly define the work area and specific circuits involved.
*   **Hazard Identification:** Identify potential arc flash hazards and determine the required Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) category based on the facility’s Arc Flash Hazard Analysis label.
*   **Tool Inspection:** Audit all insulated tools for cracks, insulation degradation, or contamination.
*   **Notification:** Notify affected departments or personnel of scheduled power interruptions.

## Phase 2: Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Execution
*   **Verification of Energy Sources:** Locate all primary and secondary power sources (breakers, disconnects, fuses).
*   **Safe Shutdown:** Power down equipment according to manufacturer specifications.
*   **Lockout:** Apply a personal padlock to the energy isolation device.
*   **Tagout:** Attach a standardized tag clearly identifying the worker, the date, and the nature of the work.
*   **Energy Release:** Bleed off stored electrical energy (capacitors) and verify that all mechanical parts have come to a full stop.
*   **Zero-Energy Verification:** Use a calibrated multimeter to test the circuit to confirm the absence of voltage. *Test the meter on a known live source before and after the zero-energy test.*

## Phase 3: Execution of Electrical Work
*   **Work Implementation:** Perform the necessary installation, repairs, or troubleshooting while maintaining proper physical spacing and using non-conductive mats.
*   **Grounding:** Ensure all equipment is correctly bonded and grounded according to facility specifications.
*   **Quality Control:** Visually inspect all connections for tightness, proper torque (if applicable), and correct wiring orientation.
*   **Cleanup:** Remove all debris, tools, and temporary wiring from the work area.

## Phase 4: Re-Energizing and Final Inspection
*   **Clearance:** Ensure all personnel are at a safe distance and all tools have been accounted for.
*   **Lockout Removal:** Remove all locks and tags personally by the individual who installed them.
*   **System Start:** Energize the system and verify proper voltage, amperage, and equipment operation under normal load.
*   **Documentation:** Update the facility electrical logbook, update panel schedules, and close out the work order.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "One-Hand" Rule:** When testing for voltage, keep one hand in your pocket or behind your back whenever possible to prevent a path for current to cross your heart in the event of an accidental discharge.
*   **Don't Trust "Off":** Never assume a switch or breaker is functional or that the circuit is dead just because a machine has stopped. Always perform a physical multimeter test.
*   **Pitfall - Complacency:** The highest accident rates often occur during "routine" tasks. Treat every electrical task as if it were a high-voltage, high-risk procedure.
*   **Pitfall - Incorrect PPE:** Using gloves that are not rated for the specific voltage present is a leading cause of preventable injury. Ensure gloves are tested and stamped by a certified lab periodically.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I perform electrical work if my LOTO device is missing?**
A: Absolutely not. You must never perform work on a system that is not properly locked and tagged. If a lockout device is missing, report it to the Site Safety Officer immediately to establish an alternative safe energy isolation plan.

**Q: How often should my insulated tools be inspected?**
A: Tools should be visually inspected before every single use. Additionally, a formal documented inspection of insulated tools should occur at least every 6 months to check for microscopic cracks or dielectric failures.

**Q: What do I do if I encounter an unlabelled circuit breaker?**
A: You must stop the work process immediately. Do not attempt to guess which breaker controls your work area. Utilize a circuit tracer to correctly identify and label the circuit before proceeding.
