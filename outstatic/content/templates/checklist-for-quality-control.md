# Standard Operating Procedure: Quality Control (QC) Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory quality control requirements for all products and deliverables before final shipment or client release. The purpose of this protocol is to ensure consistent excellence, minimize rework costs, and maintain brand integrity by identifying defects at the earliest possible stage. All production staff and project leads are required to adhere to these steps to ensure compliance with our internal quality standards and customer expectations.

## Phase 1: Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Verify the current version of the "Standard Quality Benchmark" document.
*   Ensure the physical inspection area is clean, well-lit, and equipped with necessary testing tools (e.g., calipers, software debuggers, or checklists).
*   Confirm that all raw materials or components have cleared the initial incoming inspection phase.
*   Ensure the batch/serial numbers match the production order documentation.

## Phase 2: Execution of Quality Checks
*   **Visual Inspection:** Conduct a 360-degree review of the product for aesthetic defects, alignment errors, or surface irregularities.
*   **Functional Testing:** Execute the "Golden Script" or core functionality test to ensure the product performs its primary intended task.
*   **Safety & Compliance Check:** Verify that all safety labels, regulatory stamps, and warning inserts are present and accurate.
*   **Documentation Audit:** Confirm that the user manual, warranty card, and assembly instructions match the specific product version.
*   **Packaging Review:** Inspect the packaging for structural integrity, moisture protection, and correct labeling/barcodes.

## Phase 3: Disposition & Documentation
*   **Pass/Fail Determination:** Clearly mark the item or batch as "Pass," "Fail," or "Rework Required."
*   **Defect Logging:** If an item fails, document the exact nature of the defect in the central Quality Management System (QMS).
*   **Corrective Action Reporting:** If a trend of defects is identified, initiate a formal Corrective Action Request (CAR) to the production team.
*   **Verification:** Sign off on the inspection sheet with name, timestamp, and signature to ensure accountability.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Utilize "Golden Samples." Always keep a perfect, pre-approved physical sample on your desk to compare against production runs.
*   **Pro Tip:** Rotate auditors. Having a fresh set of eyes on a project once a week helps prevent "inspection fatigue," where minor defects are overlooked due to familiarity.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "Batch Assumption." Never assume that because the first ten items in a batch passed, the remainder of the batch will be perfect. Perform random spot checks throughout the entire production run.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not rush the documentation. A QC check that is performed but not documented is legally and operationally void.

## FAQ
**Q: What should I do if I find a defect that is not covered in the checklist?**
A: Immediately quarantine the affected units and escalate the issue to the Operations Manager. Update the QC checklist following a root-cause analysis to ensure the new defect is covered in future inspections.

**Q: How many units should be inspected in a large batch?**
A: Use the AQL (Acceptable Quality Limit) standard (typically ISO 2859-1). For most high-volume production, a random sampling of 5-10% is standard, though this should increase if the initial failure rate exceeds 1%.

**Q: How do we handle "Minor" defects that don't affect functionality?**
A: Minor aesthetic defects should be logged as "Non-Conforming" and reviewed by the Product Manager. They may be sold as "B-Stock" at a discount rather than being discarded or reworked, provided they do not pose a safety risk.
