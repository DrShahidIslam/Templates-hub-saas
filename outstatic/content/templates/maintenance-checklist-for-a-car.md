# Standard Operating Procedure: Routine Vehicle Maintenance

Effective vehicle maintenance is the cornerstone of operational longevity, safety, and reliability. This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the essential inspection and servicing protocols required to maintain a vehicle in peak mechanical condition. By adhering to this systematic approach, operators can mitigate the risk of catastrophic failure, optimize fuel efficiency, and ensure compliance with safety standards. This guide serves as a foundational checklist for routine maintenance cycles, typically performed every 5,000 to 7,500 miles or semi-annually, depending on the manufacturer’s service manual.

## Phase 1: Fluid & Filtration Systems
*   **Engine Oil:** Check the dipstick level and clarity. Replace oil and filter according to viscosity specifications provided in the owner’s manual.
*   **Engine Coolant:** Inspect reservoir levels when the engine is cold. Ensure the mixture is free of debris and exhibits the correct color (e.g., green, orange, or pink as specified).
*   **Brake Fluid:** Check the master cylinder reservoir. Ensure fluid color is translucent; dark or opaque fluid indicates contamination and requires flushing.
*   **Windshield Washer Fluid:** Top off with a seasonal cleaning solution to maintain visibility.
*   **Air Filters:** Inspect the engine air filter and cabin air filter. Replace if dust, debris, or dark discoloration is present.

## Phase 2: Tire & Chassis Integrity
*   **Tire Pressure:** Measure PSI on all four tires and the spare. Adjust to the manufacturer’s recommended pressure found on the driver-side door jamb placard.
*   **Tire Tread Depth:** Use a depth gauge to ensure tread is above 4/32”. Check for uneven wear patterns, which may indicate alignment or suspension issues.
*   **Brake Components:** Visually inspect brake pad thickness and check rotors/drums for scoring, warping, or excessive rust.
*   **Suspension/Steering:** Check for leaking struts or shocks, and inspect rubber bushings for cracking or dry rot.

## Phase 3: Electrical & Visibility Systems
*   **Battery Terminals:** Inspect for corrosion (white, powdery buildup). Clean with a wire brush and ensure terminal connections are tightened.
*   **Lighting Check:** Perform a full sweep: headlights (low/high beam), turn signals, brake lights, reverse lights, and hazard flashers.
*   **Wiper Blades:** Inspect for rubber cracking or streaking. Replace if they fail to clear the windshield effectively in a single pass.
*   **Dashboard Indicators:** Ensure no persistent "Check Engine," "ABS," or "TPMS" warning lights are active.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip (Documentation):** Maintain a digital or physical service log. Recording the date, mileage, and specific parts used increases the resale value and provides a trail for warranty claims.
*   **Pro Tip (The Cold Engine Rule):** Never check coolant levels or remove the radiator cap while the engine is hot; the system is pressurized and can cause severe thermal burns.
*   **Pitfall (Ignoring "Minor" Sounds):** Operators often ignore faint squeaks or rattles. In vehicle maintenance, audible cues are usually the first warning sign of a part nearing end-of-life. Address them immediately.
*   **Pitfall (Over-tightening):** When replacing oil drain plugs or lug nuts, avoid using impact guns. Over-torquing can strip threads or warp metal components, leading to expensive repairs.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I follow the dealership's "every 3,000 miles" oil change recommendation?**
A: Modern synthetic oils and engine tolerances have rendered the 3,000-mile rule largely obsolete. Always default to the interval specified in your owner’s manual, which often ranges from 5,000 to 10,000 miles depending on driving conditions.

**Q: Why does my TPMS light stay on even after I inflate the tires?**
A: Some vehicles require a "re-learn" process after pressure adjustment, which can be done via the vehicle's dashboard menu or by driving at a consistent speed for several miles. Check your manual for the specific reset procedure.

**Q: How do I know if I need a professional mechanic versus a DIY fix?**
A: DIY tasks should be limited to "consumables" like bulbs, filters, and fluids. Any task involving the braking system, drivetrain, or complex electrical diagnostics should be performed by a certified technician to ensure safety and prevent voiding warranties.
