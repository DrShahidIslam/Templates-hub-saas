# Standard Operating Procedure: Water Chiller Preventive Maintenance

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory preventive maintenance requirements for industrial water chillers. Consistent execution of these procedures is critical to maximizing equipment lifespan, maintaining cooling efficiency, reducing energy consumption, and preventing catastrophic compressor failure. Maintenance should be performed by qualified HVAC technicians in accordance with the manufacturer’s technical manual and site safety protocols.

## Section 1: Pre-Maintenance Safety and Preparation
*   Verify the chiller is powered down and locked out/tagged out (LOTO) as per OSHA standards.
*   Ensure the area around the chiller is clear of debris and accessible.
*   Check that all necessary Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) is available (gloves, safety glasses, chemical-resistant gear).
*   Review the previous maintenance log to identify recurring issues or specific component concerns.

## Section 2: Water Loop and Fluid Quality
*   **Water Quality Analysis:** Test water pH, conductivity, and corrosion inhibitor levels. Adjust chemical treatment dosages as required.
*   **Strainer Inspection:** Isolate the flow and clean the Y-strainer. Check for scale buildup or metallic debris.
*   **Pump Inspection:** Listen for abnormal bearing noise and check pump seals for weeping or leaks.
*   **Expansion Tank:** Verify the tank is not waterlogged and maintain the correct bladder pressure.
*   **Flow Switches:** Test the flow switch operation to ensure the chiller shuts down immediately upon loss of flow.

## Section 3: Condenser and Evaporator Maintenance
*   **Condenser Coils (Air-Cooled):** Use a fin comb to straighten bent fins and clean debris/dust using a coil-safe cleaner or low-pressure compressed air (blowing from inside out).
*   **Condenser Tubes (Water-Cooled):** Open the heads and brush-clean the tubes to remove scale, algae, and mineral deposits.
*   **Fan Inspection:** Check fan blades for fatigue, balancing issues, and cleanliness. Verify tightness of motor mounts.
*   **Evaporator:** Inspect insulation for signs of condensation or damage; ensure no water leaks are present on the evaporator shell.

## Section 4: Electrical and Control Systems
*   **Tighten Connections:** Inspect all electrical terminals, contactors, and relays. Use a calibrated torque driver to tighten loose connections to prevent arcing and overheating.
*   **Contactors:** Inspect for pitted or burned points. Replace if damage exceeds manufacturer specifications.
*   **Control Sensors:** Calibrate or verify the accuracy of the chilled water supply/return temperature sensors and refrigerant pressure transducers.
*   **Software Log:** Download the chiller controller’s alarm history and review for high-pressure cutouts, low-flow alarms, or frequent cycling.

## Section 5: Refrigeration Circuit
*   **Leak Detection:** Conduct a visual inspection of all brazed joints and flare connections for traces of refrigerant oil (a common sign of a leak). Use an electronic leak detector.
*   **Sight Glass:** Check the refrigerant sight glass for bubbles (indicating low charge or moisture) or cloudy color (indicating desiccant breakdown).
*   **Compressor:** Check oil level (if applicable) and monitor for vibration. Verify the crankcase heater is operational.
*   **Insulation:** Ensure all suction line insulation is intact to prevent sweating and loss of cooling efficiency.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the "Suction" and "Discharge" pressures while the chiller is running under normal load; trending these values is the best way to predict a failing compressor before it dies.
*   **Pitfall - Over-Cleaning:** Do not use high-pressure washers on aluminum condenser fins, as the force can easily collapse the fins and restrict airflow.
*   **Pitfall - Neglecting Water Treatment:** The most common cause of chiller failure is internal scaling. Treat the water loop as the "circulatory system" of the machine; if it fails, the chiller fails.
*   **Pro Tip:** Replace the filter-drier every two years at minimum, or immediately after any maintenance that involves opening the refrigeration circuit.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**1. How often should preventive maintenance be performed?**
For most industrial applications, a full inspection should be conducted quarterly. If the environment is dirty (e.g., near manufacturing dust), condenser coil cleaning may be required monthly.

**2. What should I look for in the sight glass?**
A clear sight glass is ideal. Bubbles indicate a low refrigerant charge or a restricted liquid line filter-drier. A yellow or pink tint indicates that moisture has entered the system and the desiccant needs immediate replacement.

**3. Why is LOTO (Lockout/Tagout) so important for a chiller?**
Chillers contain high-voltage electrical components and pressurized refrigerant/water lines. LOTO prevents the accidental startup of fans or pumps while a technician is inside the unit, preventing severe injury.
