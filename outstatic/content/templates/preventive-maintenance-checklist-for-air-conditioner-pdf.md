# Standard Operating Procedure: HVAC Preventive Maintenance Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory technical requirements for performing a comprehensive preventive maintenance check on commercial and residential air conditioning systems. The objective of this protocol is to maximize equipment efficiency, extend the operational lifecycle of the HVAC unit, improve indoor air quality, and mitigate the risk of catastrophic system failure. Adherence to this checklist ensures compliance with industry safety standards and manufacturer warranty requirements.

## Phase 1: Electrical and Safety Inspection
*   **De-energize the Unit:** Confirm the main power disconnect is switched to the "OFF" position. Utilize a lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedure if working in a multi-user facility.
*   **Visual Wiring Audit:** Inspect all electrical terminals, contactors, and wiring for signs of overheating, charring, or loose connections. Tighten terminals as per torque specifications.
*   **Capacitor Testing:** Utilize a multimeter to test the microfarad (µF) rating of the start and run capacitors. Replace any capacitor measuring +/- 10% outside of the manufacturer’s nominal rating.
*   **Voltage Verification:** Once powered on, measure and record voltage and amperage draw across the compressor and fan motor to ensure they remain within the "Rated Load Amps" (RLA) specified on the data plate.

## Phase 2: Condenser Unit Maintenance
*   **Debris Clearance:** Remove all obstructions, foliage, and debris from the external cabinet.
*   **Coil Cleaning:** Utilize an approved foaming coil cleaner and a low-pressure water source to clear the condenser fins. Do not use high-pressure washers, as this may bend the aluminum fins and restrict airflow.
*   **Fan Motor Inspection:** Check the fan blade for balance and signs of pitting or cracks. Ensure the motor bearings are quiet; if the motor has grease ports, apply the manufacturer-recommended lubricant.
*   **Refrigerant Line Check:** Inspect insulation on the suction line. Ensure refrigerant piping is secured and free from vibration-induced rubbing.

## Phase 3: Evaporator and Internal System Maintenance
*   **Air Filter Replacement:** Remove and replace air filters. Standard pleated filters should be evaluated for MERV rating compatibility with the system’s static pressure capacity.
*   **Drain Pan and Line:** Flush the condensate drain line with an algaecide solution to prevent microbial buildup. Ensure the drain pan is free of standing water and clear of sediment.
*   **Blower Assembly:** Inspect the blower wheel for dust accumulation. A dirty wheel creates imbalances that lead to premature bearing failure.
*   **Coil Integrity:** Inspect the evaporator coil for signs of oxidation or icing. If the coil is heavily fouled, perform a chemical deep clean to restore heat exchange efficiency.

## Phase 4: Performance Verification
*   **Temperature Differential ($\Delta T$):** Measure the dry-bulb temperature of the return air versus the supply air. A properly functioning system should typically yield a $\Delta T$ between 15°F and 22°F.
*   **Static Pressure Check:** Measure the system's external static pressure (ESP) to ensure it does not exceed the manufacturer’s limit, which can cause motor strain.
*   **Thermostat Calibration:** Verify the control board’s response to set-point changes and ensure the thermostat is correctly programmed for the season.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always document the "Before" and "After" amperage readings. This historical data is the best predictor of a compressor motor failing in the near future.
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a soft bristle brush rather than a wire brush when cleaning aluminum fins to avoid creating galvanic corrosion points.
*   **Pitfall:** Never "top off" refrigerant without a formal leak detection test. Low refrigerant is almost always a symptom of a leak; adding gas without fixing the leak is a violation of environmental regulations (EPA Section 608).
*   **Pitfall:** Over-lubricating fan motors is a common error. Excess oil can gum up the motor windings and cause short-circuits.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How often should preventive maintenance be performed?**
For residential systems, semi-annual maintenance (once in spring for cooling, once in fall for heating) is recommended. Commercial systems should be inspected quarterly due to higher run times and debris loads.

**2. Can I use a pressure washer to clean my outdoor unit?**
Generally, no. Most pressure washers exceed the PSI threshold that the delicate aluminum fins can handle. High pressure will fold the fins, creating an airflow blockage that significantly degrades performance.

**3. Why is it important to check the condensate drain line?**
A clogged condensate line is the #1 cause of interior water damage. Algae and biological growth thrive in humid lines; neglecting to clear them regularly can lead to overflow, which triggers safety float switches and shuts down the unit, or causes ceiling/wall leaks.
