# Standard Operating Procedure: Playground Safety Inspection

Maintaining a safe environment for recreational play is a critical operational responsibility. This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory safety inspection protocols for public and facility-managed playgrounds. The objective is to identify potential hazards—such as mechanical wear, environmental debris, or structural compromises—before they pose a risk to users. All inspections must be documented, and any identified hazards must be mitigated immediately by restricting access to the affected equipment until repairs are completed.

## 1. General Site and Perimeter Inspection
*   **Signage:** Ensure all age-appropriate usage signs and emergency contact information are legible, firmly attached, and free from graffiti.
*   **Fencing and Barriers:** Check that perimeter fencing is secure, latching mechanisms function correctly, and there are no gaps large enough for a child to crawl through.
*   **Vegetation:** Inspect for overgrown branches, poisonous plants, or sharp shrubs within the play zone.
*   **Lighting:** Confirm that all pathway and area lighting are functional, particularly for facilities operating during evening hours.

## 2. Surfacing and Ground Integrity
*   **Loose-Fill Materials:** Check wood chips, rubber mulch, or sand for adequate depth (minimum 9–12 inches depending on equipment height) and ensure coverage extends the required fall zone radius.
*   **Unitary Surfaces:** Inspect rubber tiles or poured-in-place surfaces for cracks, holes, buckling, or loose seams.
*   **Drainage:** Confirm that the site is free of standing water, which can degrade surfacing materials and create slip hazards.
*   **Foreign Debris:** Scan the entire area for broken glass, needles, animal waste, or discarded metal.

## 3. Structural Integrity and Equipment
*   **Hardware:** Tighten any loose bolts, screws, or S-hooks. Ensure that all hardware is recessed or covered with protective caps to prevent snagging.
*   **Corrosion and Splinters:** Inspect metal components for rust and wood components for rotting, splinters, or peeling paint (lead-free paint compliance).
*   **Moving Parts:** Test swing chains, merry-go-rounds, and spring rockers for smooth movement. Check for "pinch points" or "crush points" where fingers could be trapped.
*   **Stability:** Physically shake stationary equipment to ensure foundations remain firm and are not wobbling or shifting in the ground.

## 4. Fall Zones and Clearance
*   **Equipment Spacing:** Confirm that no new obstacles (benches, trash cans, or trees) have been placed within the designated use zones.
*   **Entrapment Hazards:** Check for openings between 3.5 inches and 9 inches, which may pose head or torso entrapment risks to children.
*   **Protrusions:** Ensure there are no exposed bolt ends or hooks that could catch clothing or skin.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "Safety Inspection Log" template. Documentation is your primary defense in the event of an incident. Keep records for at least three years.
*   **Pro Tip:** Conduct inspections during "peak use" hours occasionally; observing how children actually use the equipment often reveals hazards that are invisible during static inspections.
*   **Pitfall:** Over-tightening hardware. While loose bolts are dangerous, over-tightening can cause metal fatigue or strip threads. Use the manufacturer’s torque specifications if available.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "hidden" wear. Always check the underside of platforms and the interior of tubes, as these areas often hide corrosion or wasp nests that go unnoticed from a standing position.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should these inspections be performed?**
A: A visual "walk-through" inspection should be performed daily. A comprehensive, documented technical inspection—checking all hardware and structural fasteners—should be performed at least monthly, or quarterly if usage is low.

**Q: What should I do if I find a piece of equipment that is unsafe?**
A: Immediately cordon off the area using caution tape or physical barriers. Remove the equipment from service entirely. Do not attempt "field repairs" unless you have the appropriate parts and training; contact a certified playground inspector or the manufacturer for maintenance.

**Q: Who is authorized to perform these inspections?**
A: While daily walk-throughs can be done by custodial or site staff, formal safety inspections should ideally be conducted by an individual with a Certified Playground Safety Inspector (CPSI) credential.
