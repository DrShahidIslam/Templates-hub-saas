# Standard Operating Procedure: Workshop Safety and Operational Inspection

## Introduction
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory inspection protocols for workshop facilities to ensure a safe, efficient, and compliant working environment. Regular inspections are critical to identifying hazards, preventing equipment failure, and maintaining organizational standards. All supervisors and assigned safety officers are required to conduct these inspections at the designated frequency to mitigate operational risks and uphold the integrity of workshop assets. Failure to adhere to these procedures can lead to unsafe working conditions and potential regulatory non-compliance.

## Inspection Checklist

## 1. Facility and Infrastructure
*   **Flooring:** Check for spills (oil, coolant), loose debris, tripping hazards, or structural cracks.
*   **Lighting:** Ensure all overhead lights are functional; replace flickering or burnt-out bulbs immediately.
*   **Ventilation:** Verify that exhaust fans and air filtration systems are operating at full capacity.
*   **Exits:** Confirm all emergency exits are clearly marked, illuminated, and entirely free of obstructions.
*   **Signage:** Ensure all mandatory safety, hazard, and informational signage is visible and in good condition.

## 2. Tools and Equipment
*   **Hand Tools:** Inspect for mushroomed heads, frayed handles, or bent components.
*   **Power Tools:** Verify that power cords are intact, plugs are undamaged, and guards are securely attached.
*   **Stationary Machinery:** Check that emergency stop buttons are accessible and functional; ensure all safety interlocks are operational.
*   **Calibration:** Verify that precision instruments (calipers, torque wrenches) have valid calibration stickers.
*   **Storage:** Ensure tools are returned to their designated shadow boards or cabinets at the end of the shift.

## 3. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
*   **Availability:** Confirm that appropriate PPE (safety glasses, gloves, ear protection) is stocked and accessible.
*   **Integrity:** Inspect communal PPE (face shields, welding masks) for scratches, cracks, or structural weakness.
*   **Compliance:** Check that all personnel currently on the floor are wearing required PPE for their specific work area.

## 4. Fire and Emergency Systems
*   **Fire Extinguishers:** Verify the pressure gauge is in the green zone and the inspection tag is up to date.
*   **First Aid Kits:** Confirm the kit is fully stocked, items are within expiration dates, and the seal is intact.
*   **Spill Kits:** Ensure kits are stocked with absorbent materials and located in high-risk areas.

## 5. Waste and Chemical Management
*   **Hazardous Waste:** Ensure all chemicals are stored in secondary containment and correctly labeled per GHS standards.
*   **Disposal:** Verify that oily rags are stored in fire-rated, self-closing metal containers.
*   **Cleanliness:** Confirm that the workshop is clear of scrap metal, wood chips, and unnecessary combustible clutter.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Utilize a digital inspection app or tablet-based form. This allows you to attach photos of identified hazards directly to the report, creating an indisputable audit trail for corrective actions.
*   **Pro Tip:** Implement the "5S" methodology (Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain) as part of your inspection routine to boost long-term workshop efficiency.
*   **Pitfall:** "Check-the-box" mentality. Avoid performing inspections by rote. If you find yourself marking "Pass" without physically testing the item, you are compromising the safety of your team.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "Minor" issues. A small oil leak or a fraying cord today is a major liability or catastrophic failure tomorrow. Address small defects immediately.

## FAQ
**Q: How often should a comprehensive workshop inspection be performed?**
A: A visual "walk-through" should be performed daily by a supervisor. A detailed, documented formal inspection should be conducted at least monthly, or following any major equipment maintenance.

**Q: Who is authorized to conduct these inspections?**
A: Inspections should be led by the Workshop Manager or a designated Safety Lead. However, all employees are empowered—and encouraged—to flag hazards immediately.

**Q: What is the correct procedure if a piece of equipment fails inspection?**
A: Immediately initiate a "Lock-Out/Tag-Out" (LOTO) procedure. Do not allow the equipment to be powered on until a qualified technician has repaired the fault and the supervisor has signed off on the re-inspection.
