# Standard Operating Procedure: Commercial Building Preventive Maintenance

## Purpose and Scope
This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes a systematic framework for the preventive maintenance (PM) of commercial building assets. The objective is to extend the service life of equipment, minimize unplanned downtime, ensure regulatory compliance, and optimize operational efficiency. This procedure applies to all facility management personnel and external contractors responsible for the mechanical, electrical, and structural integrity of the facility. Adherence to this schedule is mandatory to maintain building safety and protect capital investments.

## Preventive Maintenance Checklist

## HVAC and Climate Control
*   **Air Handling Units (AHUs):** Inspect belts for tension/cracking, clean drain pans, and lubricate fan bearings.
*   **Filters:** Replace or clean filters monthly or per manufacturer specifications to maintain Indoor Air Quality (IAQ).
*   **Condenser Coils:** Pressure wash coils to remove debris and ensure optimal heat transfer.
*   **Thermostats/BMS:** Verify calibration of sensors and confirm software integration with Building Management Systems.
*   **Ductwork:** Visually inspect for leaks, mold, or obstructions every six months.

## Electrical Systems
*   **Distribution Panels:** Perform infrared thermography annually to identify "hot spots" indicative of loose connections.
*   **Lighting:** Audit LED drivers and ballast functionality; replace lamps in bulk to minimize labor costs.
*   **Emergency Power:** Test Uninterruptible Power Supplies (UPS) and initiate load bank testing for backup generators monthly.
*   **Exit/Emergency Lights:** Verify battery backup and illumination levels; log results for fire marshal compliance.

## Plumbing and Water Systems
*   **Backflow Preventers:** Schedule annual certified testing and report submission to local water authorities.
*   **Pumps/Booster Systems:** Check for seal leaks, vibration, and noise levels.
*   **Drains/Traps:** Inspect floor drains and roof scuppers for debris to prevent water ingress during storm events.
*   **Water Heaters:** Flush sediment from tanks and test Pressure Relief Valves (PRV).

## Structural and Life Safety
*   **Fire Suppression:** Inspect sprinkler heads for clearance and schedule quarterly professional fire alarm testing.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect for membrane punctures, ponding water, and flashings twice annually (post-winter and pre-winter).
*   **Exterior/Façade:** Check sealants, masonry joints, and window gaskets for degradation.
*   **Fire Doors:** Ensure self-closing mechanisms and latches function correctly.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip: Implement Predictive Maintenance.** Use IoT vibration sensors on large motors to detect bearing failure before it occurs, moving beyond calendar-based maintenance.
*   **Pro Tip: Digital Logbooks.** Transition from paper logs to a Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS) to track historical data and technician accountability.
*   **Pitfall: Deferred Maintenance.** Do not delay critical repairs; small, unresolved issues (such as a leaking roof flashing) often escalate into capital-intensive structural failures within 12–18 months.
*   **Pitfall: Documentation Neglect.** If a maintenance task is not logged, it effectively did not happen in the eyes of insurance adjusters or regulatory auditors. Always attach photos of completed work.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should I review my preventive maintenance schedule?**
A: Review your PM schedule annually. As equipment ages, the frequency of inspection and the intensity of the service required may need to increase to prevent failure.

**Q: Should I outsource or perform maintenance in-house?**
A: Perform basic tasks (filter changes, visual inspections) in-house to save costs. Outsource specialized, code-mandated, or high-risk tasks (elevators, fire alarms, boiler certifications) to licensed, bonded professionals.

**Q: What is the most common reason for a failed preventive maintenance program?**
A: "Scope Creep" or inconsistency. Often, programs fail because teams prioritize reactive emergency work over scheduled maintenance. To succeed, treat PM tasks as "non-negotiable" appointments in your operational calendar.
