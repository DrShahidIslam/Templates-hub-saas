# Standard Operating Procedure: Property Rental Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the rigorous process for conducting rental property inspections. The objective is to maintain property asset value, ensure tenant compliance with lease agreements, and document the physical condition of the premises for liability protection. This protocol applies to move-in, routine periodic, and move-out inspections, ensuring consistency, transparency, and data-driven record-keeping for both property managers and tenants.

## 1. Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   **Notify Tenant:** Provide written notice at least 24–48 hours in advance (or as per local jurisdictional laws).
*   **Gather Documentation:** Carry a copy of the original move-in inspection report, lease agreement, and a digital camera or tablet with inspection software.
*   **Equipment Check:** Ensure your device is fully charged, bring a pair of disposable gloves, a flashlight, and a moisture meter (if mold/leaks are suspected).
*   **Review History:** Note any outstanding maintenance requests or recurring issues reported by the tenant.

## 2. Exterior and Structural Assessment
*   **Roof and Gutters:** Inspect for loose shingles, debris, or sagging gutters (if ground-level visibility allows).
*   **Siding and Paint:** Note any peeling paint, cracks, or damage to the building envelope.
*   **Landscaping:** Check for overgrown vegetation touching the structure, trip hazards, or unauthorized modifications.
*   **Security:** Ensure all gate latches function and exterior lighting is operational.

## 3. Interior – General Condition
*   **Flooring:** Inspect for stains, rips, burn marks, or loose tiles/boards.
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Check for water stains (signs of leaks), excessive scuffs, holes, or unauthorized paint colors.
*   **Doors and Windows:** Ensure all locks operate smoothly, screens are intact, and windows seal properly.
*   **Smoke and CO Detectors:** Test every unit and record the expiration dates.
*   **Pest Control:** Scan for signs of droppings, webbing, or structural damage caused by vermin.

## 4. Kitchen and Bathrooms
*   **Plumbing:** Run taps for 60 seconds; check under sinks for active leaks or signs of past water damage.
*   **Appliances:** Verify that the refrigerator cools, the oven heats, and the dishwasher cycles.
*   **Ventilation:** Confirm exhaust fans are pulling air and are not excessively clogged with dust.
*   **Fixtures:** Check for broken tiles, cracked porcelain, or loose towel bars and faucets.

## 5. Move-Out Specifics (Final Handover)
*   **Deep Clean Verification:** Inspect behind appliances, inside drawers, and inside the oven/microwave.
*   **Trash Removal:** Ensure no personal property or debris remains inside or in exterior storage areas.
*   **Key Return:** Confirm all keys, garage remotes, and access fobs are accounted for.
*   **Final Utility Check:** Verify utilities are scheduled for transfer back to the landlord/owner.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always take wide-angle photos of the room first, followed by close-ups of any identified damage. This provides context for the location of the issue.
*   **Pro Tip:** If a tenant is present, keep the inspection professional but conversational. Use the time to build rapport, which often leads to better tenant care of the property.
*   **Pitfall:** Avoid "quick-scanning." Missing a small damp spot under a sink today can result in thousands of dollars in mold remediation costs tomorrow.
*   **Pitfall:** Never rely on memory. If it is not documented in the report with a corresponding photo, legally, it does not exist.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How often should periodic inspections be performed?**
A: Standard practice is every 6 months. However, property managers may opt for quarterly inspections for high-risk tenancies or if the property has a history of maintenance neglect.

**Q: Can I use the tenant's move-in inspection report to finalize the move-out?**
A: Yes, the move-in report serves as your baseline "control" document. Comparing current conditions to the original baseline is essential for determining what constitutes "normal wear and tear" versus "tenant damage."

**Q: What is the best way to handle a dispute over damage during move-out?**
A: Always refer back to the photographic evidence from the move-in inspection. If the damage was documented at move-in, the tenant is not liable. If it is new, provide the itemized repair invoice as justification for the security deposit deduction.
