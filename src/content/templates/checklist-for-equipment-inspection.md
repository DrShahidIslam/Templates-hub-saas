# Standard Operating Procedure: Equipment Inspection Protocol

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory requirements for conducting routine equipment inspections to ensure operational safety, minimize unplanned downtime, and extend the lifecycle of organizational assets. Consistent inspections are the first line of defense in predictive maintenance; they allow technicians to identify mechanical wear, electrical irregularities, or safety hazards before they escalate into critical failures. All personnel authorized to operate or maintain site equipment are required to adhere to this protocol during every inspection cycle.

## Pre-Inspection Preparation
*   Verify the equipment is powered down and locked out/tagged out (LOTO) where applicable.
*   Gather all required personal protective equipment (PPE) as dictated by the specific asset safety data sheet.
*   Ensure the inspection checklist and a logging device (or paper form) are readily available.
*   Clean the area surrounding the equipment to ensure visibility of all components, including base plates and floor mounts.

## Exterior and Structural Integrity
*   **Chassis and Frame:** Inspect for cracks, structural deformation, rust, or signs of metal fatigue.
*   **Fasteners and Hardware:** Verify that all bolts, nuts, and screws are tightened to manufacturer-specified torque settings; look for missing hardware.
*   **Guards and Shields:** Confirm all safety guards are securely mounted and in place; ensure no sharp edges or pinch points are exposed.
*   **Labels and Signage:** Check that safety warnings, operational instructions, and serial number plates are legible and securely attached.

## Mechanical and Fluid Systems
*   **Fluid Levels:** Check oil, coolant, and hydraulic reservoirs against specified dipstick or sight-glass markers.
*   **Leaks:** Inspect hoses, seals, and gaskets for evidence of drips, weeping, or pooling fluids.
*   **Lubrication:** Verify that grease points are serviced and that lubrication lines are not crimped or damaged.
*   **Moving Parts:** Inspect belts, chains, and drive shafts for proper tension, alignment, and absence of excessive fraying or wear.

## Electrical and Control Systems
*   **Wiring/Cabling:** Check for frayed insulation, exposed conductors, or signs of heat damage (discoloration/melting).
*   **Connections:** Ensure terminal blocks and plugs are securely seated and free from corrosion or oxidation.
*   **Control Panel:** Verify that emergency stop buttons function, indicators illuminate correctly, and tactile buttons are not sticky or jammed.
*   **Sensors:** Clean all photoelectric, limit, or proximity sensors to remove dust or debris that could impede accurate data collection.

## Operational Testing
*   **Initialization:** Perform a cold start to observe for unusual noises (grinding, squealing, or high-pitched vibrations).
*   **Functionality:** Cycle the equipment through its full range of motion or operational sequence to ensure smooth performance.
*   **Calibration:** Verify that the output (pressure, temperature, or speed) matches the settings on the control interface.
*   **Shutdown:** Execute an orderly shutdown to ensure the machine powers down in accordance with standard safety protocols.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Use a "clocking" method for reporting issues. Instead of saying "there is a loose bolt," specify "loose bolt at the 3 o'clock position on the primary drive assembly."
*   **Pro Tip:** Take photos of any identified anomalies. A digital time-stamped photo is far more useful to a maintenance team than a vague text description.
*   **Pitfall - The "Normalization of Deviance":** Do not ignore minor vibrations or noises simply because "it has always sounded like that." Habitual minor issues often precede catastrophic failures.
*   **Pitfall - Paper-Whipping:** Avoid checking off boxes without actually physically inspecting the component. A "pencil-whipped" checklist creates a false sense of security and creates liability.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How often should these inspections occur?**
Inspection frequency is determined by the manufacturer's maintenance manual and the intensity of usage. Generally, high-cycle equipment requires daily checks, while low-frequency equipment may be inspected weekly or monthly.

**2. What should I do if I find a fault that is not on the checklist?**
If a fault is identified that could compromise safety or operational efficiency, do not proceed with usage. Tag the machine as "Out of Service," notify your supervisor immediately, and document the discovery in the 'Notes' section of the inspection report.

**3. Who is authorized to perform these inspections?**
Inspections should be performed by personnel trained on the specific equipment. If an inspection involves removing electrical covers or accessing internal hydraulic systems, it must be performed by a certified technician.
