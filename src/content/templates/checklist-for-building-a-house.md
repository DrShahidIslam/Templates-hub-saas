# Standard Operating Procedure: Residential Construction Management

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional workflow for overseeing the construction of a residential property. Building a house is a complex orchestration of logistics, regulatory compliance, and craftsmanship. To ensure the project remains on schedule and within budget, this guide provides a structured framework for managing the transition from architectural design to final handover. By following this systematic approach, stakeholders can mitigate risk, maintain quality standards, and ensure all structural and legal requirements are met throughout the construction lifecycle.

## Phase 1: Pre-Construction & Permitting
*   Finalize and approve architectural blueprints and structural engineering plans.
*   Obtain necessary building permits and municipal zoning approvals.
*   Secure comprehensive builder’s risk insurance and liability coverage.
*   Conduct a site survey and soil test (geotechnical report).
*   Establish a project budget, including a 15% contingency fund.
*   Finalize contracts with the General Contractor (GC) and primary subcontractors.

## Phase 2: Site Preparation & Foundation
*   Clear the building site of vegetation, debris, and obstructions.
*   Stake out the house footprint based on the site plan.
*   Complete excavation for the foundation and utility trenches.
*   Install temporary site power and water connections.
*   Pour footings and foundation walls; ensure proper drainage (weeping tile).
*   Pass foundation inspection before backfilling.

## Phase 3: Framing & Exterior Envelope
*   Erect floor, wall, and roof framing systems.
*   Install sheathing and moisture barriers (house wrap).
*   Install windows and exterior doors (the "dried-in" phase).
*   Complete roof shingling or metal roofing installation.
*   Install exterior siding, trim, and soffits.

## Phase 4: Rough-Ins (Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing)
*   Install HVAC ductwork and units.
*   Perform rough plumbing (drainage, venting, and water lines).
*   Conduct electrical rough-in (wiring, outlet boxes, and service panel).
*   Install low-voltage wiring (data, security, and smart home systems).
*   **Mandatory:** Pass all municipal mechanical, electrical, and plumbing inspections.

## Phase 5: Interior Finishes & Interior Build-Out
*   Install wall and ceiling insulation.
*   Complete drywall installation (hanging, taping, and sanding).
*   Install interior doors, baseboards, and window casings.
*   Apply primer and interior paint.
*   Install cabinetry, countertops, and flooring (tile, hardwood, carpet).
*   Install plumbing fixtures (faucets, toilets) and electrical fixtures (lights, switches).

## Phase 6: Final Handover & Closeout
*   Perform a final walkthrough to generate the "punch list" of deficiencies.
*   Complete all punch list items.
*   Perform a final professional deep clean of the interior.
*   Obtain the Certificate of Occupancy (CO) from the local building authority.
*   Conduct a final walkthrough with the owner to hand over keys, manuals, and warranties.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "Rainy Day" Contingency:** Always build your timeline around potential weather delays. Never schedule trades back-to-back without a buffer day.
*   **Communication Log:** Document every change order in writing. Verbal agreements are the leading cause of budget overruns and disputes.
*   **Insulation Integrity:** Prior to drywall, personally inspect the insulation gaps. Poorly insulated corners or around windows are common pitfalls that lead to high energy costs.
*   **Pitfall: Scope Creep:** Changing finishes or layout designs after construction has begun is significantly more expensive than planning them upfront. Lock in your choices early.

## FAQ
**Q: How do I handle unexpected cost increases during the build?**
A: Use your 15% contingency fund immediately. If the cost exceeds that, re-evaluate "must-have" vs. "nice-to-have" features and consider downgrading interior finishes to stay within the total budget.

**Q: Why are building inspections so critical?**
A: Inspections protect your investment by ensuring work meets safety codes. Skipping inspections can lead to massive liabilities when you attempt to sell the home, or worse, structural failure.

**Q: What is a punch list and why does it matter?**
A: A punch list is a document created at the end of a project detailing minor repairs or unfinished tasks (e.g., a scratched cabinet door or an outlet that doesn't work). Ensuring this is completed before final payment is your primary leverage to ensure quality workmanship.
