# Standard Operating Procedure: Tax Return Preparation

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the professional requirements and systematic workflow for the preparation of tax returns. The objective is to ensure 100% data accuracy, full compliance with tax legislation, and the optimization of tax liabilities for clients. Adherence to this checklist minimizes risk, prevents audit triggers, and ensures that all filing deadlines are met with maximum efficiency.

## Phase 1: Client Onboarding and Information Gathering
*   [ ] Send engagement letter and receive signed copy from the client.
*   [ ] Request tax organizer (digital or paper-based) via secure portal.
*   [ ] Confirm receipt of all core documentation: W-2s, 1099s (INT, DIV, MISC, NEC, B), and K-1s.
*   [ ] Verify personal identification details (legal names, SSNs/ITINs, and current address).
*   [ ] Review previous year’s tax return for recurring carryovers (capital losses, NOLs, charitable contributions).

## Phase 2: Income and Deduction Verification
*   [ ] Reconcile all reported 1099/W-2 income against client-provided summaries.
*   [ ] Verify business income and reconcile bank statements for S-Corps, Partnerships, and Sole Proprietors.
*   [ ] Audit itemized deductions: Check for substantiation (receipts, mileage logs) for medical expenses, taxes paid, mortgage interest, and charitable donations.
*   [ ] Assess self-employed health insurance premiums and retirement plan contributions.
*   [ ] Verify eligibility for tax credits (e.g., Child Tax Credit, EITC, Education Credits).

## Phase 3: Technical Review and Calculation
*   [ ] Perform preliminary data entry into tax software.
*   [ ] Conduct a "flux analysis": Compare current year figures against the prior year to identify significant variances.
*   [ ] Execute internal review of depreciation schedules for fixed assets.
*   [ ] Run the tax software’s automated diagnostic and error-checking tools.
*   [ ] Calculate estimated tax payments made throughout the year and apply credits to the current balance.

## Phase 4: Finalization and Filing
*   [ ] Generate the draft tax return and Client Review Summary.
*   [ ] Perform a final sanity check (does the tax liability make sense relative to the income profile?).
*   [ ] Secure client signature on Form 8879 (e-file authorization) or equivalent.
*   [ ] Submit return via electronic filing system (e-file).
*   [ ] Confirm receipt of the electronic acknowledgment from the tax authority (IRS/State).
*   [ ] Deliver final copy to client via encrypted file transfer.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always set an "internal deadline" at least 72 hours before the actual filing deadline to allow for unexpected delays or missing document requests.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a centralized "Permanent File" for each client that includes basis tracking for assets and carryover schedules to save time during future preparation.
*   **Pitfall:** Do not accept unsorted spreadsheets or loose receipts. Require clients to submit data in a standardized format to prevent costly "garbage-in, garbage-out" errors.
*   **Pitfall:** Overlooking state-specific filing requirements (e.g., local city taxes or nexus requirements) is a common cause of penalty notices. Always double-check state apportionments.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: What should I do if the client is missing a 1099 form?**
A: Do not guess or estimate income. Request that the client contact the issuing institution to obtain a copy. If it is unavailable, check the tax authority’s transcript records to verify the reported amount before finalizing the return.

**Q: How do I handle a significant flux variance from the previous year?**
A: Document the reason for the variance (e.g., business closure, change in employment, large one-time asset sale) in the workpapers. This serves as a defensive record in the event of a future audit.

**Q: When is it necessary to file an extension?**
A: Extensions should be filed immediately if missing information prevents the completion of an accurate return by the deadline. Note that an extension is only for filing, not for the payment of tax; always estimate and pay tax liabilities by the deadline to avoid interest and underpayment penalties.
