# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Diesel Generator Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the mandatory daily inspection routine for diesel generator sets. Consistent monitoring is the most effective defense against mechanical failure, ensuring that standby or prime power assets remain ready for immediate deployment during grid instability or emergency events. All observations must be recorded in the generator logbook, and any deviations from specified parameters must be reported to the maintenance supervisor immediately.

## 1. Visual Inspection and Fluid Levels
*   **Fuel System:** Check for visible leaks around fuel lines, filters, and the tank. Ensure fuel levels are at 90% capacity or higher to prevent moisture condensation and ensure runtime readiness.
*   **Lubrication System:** Check oil level using the dipstick. Ensure the oil is within the "Operating Range" markers. Inspect the engine block and base frame for signs of oil seepage.
*   **Cooling System:** Inspect the radiator coolant level. Ensure the cooling fins are free of debris or obstructions. Check coolant hoses for signs of bulging, cracking, or perishing.
*   **Air Intake:** Check the air intake pre-cleaner and filter housing for blockages (e.g., leaves, dust, or nests).
*   **Exhaust System:** Visually inspect the exhaust manifold and piping for signs of soot staining, which indicates leaks.

## 2. Battery and Electrical Systems
*   **Battery Condition:** Inspect battery terminals for corrosion or loose connections. Tighten and clean as necessary.
*   **Charger Status:** Verify the battery charger output meter is displaying a normal "float" charge.
*   **Control Panel:** Ensure the generator control panel is in "AUTO" mode. Verify there are no "Fault" or "Warning" LEDs illuminated on the display.
*   **Wiring:** Check all accessible control wiring for signs of rodent damage or thermal discoloration.

## 3. Operational Environment
*   **Housekeeping:** Clear the immediate area of flammable materials, rags, or tools. Ensure the generator room is adequately ventilated.
*   **Engine Surroundings:** Ensure the area under the unit is clean and free of fluid puddles.
*   **Access:** Confirm that the fire extinguisher is accessible and the inspection tag is current.

## 4. Operational Checklist (If Weekly Run is Performed)
*   **Start-up:** Confirm the engine starts smoothly without excessive cranking.
*   **Oil Pressure:** Verify oil pressure reaches the manufacturer’s specified nominal range within 15 seconds of startup.
*   **Coolant Temperature:** Monitor the temperature gauge to ensure the engine warms up to the standard operating range.
*   **Frequency/Voltage:** Confirm output frequency (Hz) and voltage (V) are steady and within project tolerances.
*   **Shutdown:** Listen for unusual vibrations or mechanical "knocking" sounds during the shutdown sequence.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** Always carry a white rag during inspections. Wiping down components not only cleans them but makes fresh leaks (oil, fuel, or coolant) significantly easier to spot.
*   **Pro Tip:** If your generator is located in a high-humidity area, ensure your block heater is functioning correctly to prevent internal condensation.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Small Leaks:** Never dismiss a "minor" drip. Diesel engine vibrations often turn small leaks into catastrophic failures under full load.
*   **Pitfall - Battery Complacency:** Batteries are the #1 cause of generator starting failure. Even if the generator seems fine, replace batteries every 2–3 years regardless of appearance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What should I do if the generator is in "Manual" mode when I arrive?**
A: Immediately determine why it was switched. If it was for maintenance, ensure the task is complete, notify the facility manager, and return the switch to "AUTO" mode to restore standby readiness.

**Q: How often should the fuel tank be sampled for water?**
A: While daily inspections look for external leaks, you should drain the water separator/fuel filter bowl monthly, or immediately if the "Water in Fuel" alarm triggers.

**Q: Should I touch the engine while it is running?**
A: Never touch moving parts or hot exhaust components while the engine is running. Always use non-contact infrared thermometers for temperature checks if you suspect an overheating issue.
