# Standard Operating Procedure: Daily Electrical Panel Inspection

## Introduction
The daily inspection of electrical distribution panels is a critical preventative maintenance activity designed to identify early warning signs of electrical failure, such as overheating, moisture ingress, or component degradation. By adhering to this daily SOP, facility personnel can mitigate the risks of unplanned downtime, fire hazards, and equipment damage. This procedure emphasizes a non-invasive, visual, and sensory inspection approach. Note: Any observation of abnormal conditions requiring internal access must be deferred to a licensed electrician utilizing proper Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) and Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) procedures.

## Daily Inspection Checklist

## Phase 1: Environmental and External Integrity
*   **Clearance Verification:** Ensure the 36-foot-wide by 6.5-foot-high (or as per local NEC/OSHA code) clearance zone in front of the panel is free of debris, storage boxes, or equipment.
*   **Enclosure Condition:** Inspect the exterior of the panel cabinet for signs of physical impact, rust, or dents that could compromise the integrity of the housing.
*   **Moisture Check:** Scan the area for signs of water leakage from overhead pipes, condensation on the cabinet surface, or pooling water at the base of the panel.
*   **Door and Lock Security:** Ensure the panel door closes securely and that the locking mechanism is functional to prevent unauthorized access.

## Phase 2: Sensory Inspection
*   **Thermal Detection:** Use the back of your hand (held at a safe distance) to feel for radiating heat emanating from the panel cover.
*   **Auditory Assessment:** Listen for abnormal sounds, specifically buzzing, humming, or crackling, which may indicate loose connections or arcing.
*   **Olfactory Assessment:** Smell for the distinct odor of burning plastic or ozone, which are primary indicators of overheating or insulation breakdown.

## Phase 3: Documentation and Reporting
*   **Labeling Integrity:** Verify that all circuit directories and warning labels are legible and firmly attached.
*   **Status Logging:** Record the inspection in the digital maintenance management system (CMMS) or the physical logbook.
*   **Discrepancy Reporting:** If any abnormal conditions are noted, immediately tag the unit with a "Caution" sign and notify the Facility Engineering department.

## Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **Pro Tip:** If your facility allows, use a handheld infrared (IR) thermometer or thermal imaging camera during your daily rounds. This allows for the identification of "hot spots" before they become catastrophic failures.
*   **Pro Tip:** Maintain a dedicated logbook or digital app to track historical data. If a specific breaker trips consistently, the data will help determine if the issue is a faulty breaker or a growing load imbalance.
*   **Pitfall:** Never attempt to open the dead-front cover of the panel unless you are a qualified electrician. Most electrical injuries occur during the removal of covers when personnel accidentally bridge live bus bars.
*   **Pitfall:** Ignoring "nuisance" odors. A faint smell of ozone or plastic is rarely a coincidence; it is almost always a sign of a high-resistance connection that needs immediate tightening by an expert.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I perform these checks while wearing conductive jewelry?**
A: No. It is strict industry standard to remove all metallic jewelry, including watches, rings, and necklaces, before performing any electrical inspections to prevent accidental short-circuits.

**Q: What should I do if I see a "tripped" breaker during my daily check?**
A: Do not reset the breaker immediately. A tripped breaker is a safety device acting as intended. Identify the equipment connected to that circuit, investigate for potential overloads, and contact a lead technician to perform an insulation resistance test before re-energizing.

**Q: How often should a deep-dive, invasive maintenance check be performed?**
A: While this SOP covers daily visual inspections, a deep-dive preventative maintenance (PM) cycle—which includes tightening lugs to manufacturer torque specifications and vacuuming out dust—should be performed at least annually, or more frequently depending on the environment (e.g., high humidity or dusty manufacturing plants).
