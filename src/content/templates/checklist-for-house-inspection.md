# Standard Operating Procedure: Professional Property Inspection

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) provides a comprehensive framework for conducting a thorough house inspection. Whether you are a potential homebuyer, a property manager, or an investor, this checklist is designed to standardize the evaluation process, mitigate risk, and identify critical maintenance issues before they escalate. By following this systematic approach, you ensure that no structural, mechanical, or cosmetic defect is overlooked, allowing for data-driven decision-making regarding the asset.

## 1. Exterior and Structural Assessment
*   **Foundation:** Examine for major vertical or horizontal cracks (greater than 1/8 inch), bowing walls, or signs of shifting.
*   **Roofing:** Inspect for missing, curled, or damaged shingles; check flashing around chimneys and vents for rust or gaps.
*   **Siding and Trim:** Verify there is no rotting wood, damaged stucco, or loose panels; check the integrity of caulking around windows.
*   **Grading and Drainage:** Ensure the ground slopes away from the foundation to prevent water pooling; check gutters and downspouts for blockages.
*   **Driveway and Walkways:** Look for significant cracking or trip hazards caused by heaving concrete.

## 2. Interior Systems and Infrastructure
*   **Electrical Panel:** Check the age of the panel; look for signs of overheating or scorch marks; ensure all circuits are clearly labeled.
*   **HVAC System:** Note the age and service record of the furnace/AC; test for airflow strength and listen for unusual grinding or rattling noises.
*   **Plumbing:** Test water pressure in all sinks and showers; check under cabinets for signs of active leaks or water stains (moisture damage).
*   **Water Heater:** Inspect for corrosion at the base or signs of leaks; verify the date of manufacture.
*   **Attic and Insulation:** Check for signs of roof leaks (dark staining), pest intrusion, and the depth/consistency of insulation.

## 3. Interior Living Spaces
*   **Windows and Doors:** Ensure all windows operate smoothly and locks engage; test doors for sticking or improper latching (an indicator of frame shifting).
*   **Flooring:** Walk the perimeter to check for "soft spots" or unevenness, which could indicate subfloor damage.
*   **Walls and Ceilings:** Look for hairline cracks or discoloration, which are often indicative of past water infiltration.
*   **Ventilation:** Confirm that kitchen and bathroom exhaust fans vent to the exterior rather than into the attic or ceiling void.

## 4. Pro Tips & Pitfalls
*   **The "Smell Test":** Upon entering the house, pay close attention to musty or damp odors. These are often the first sign of hidden mold or persistent water intrusion.
*   **Check the Age of the "Big Three":** Always prioritize the age and condition of the Roof, HVAC, and Water Heater. These represent the highest capital expenditure items for any homeowner.
*   **Don't Ignore the Crawl Space:** If the house is not on a slab, accessing the crawl space is non-negotiable. Inspect for standing water, vapor barrier degradation, or signs of termite activity.
*   **Pitfall - Rushing the Process:** Never attempt to inspect a property during a social visit. Schedule a dedicated time, bring a high-intensity flashlight, and take photos of every room.
*   **Pitfall - Ignoring Exterior Grading:** Most basement leaks are caused by poor landscaping. If the dirt is touching the siding, you are at a high risk for wood rot and foundation moisture.

## 5. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Should I bring a professional home inspector even if I use this checklist?**
A: Yes. While this checklist helps identify obvious issues and red flags, a licensed home inspector uses specialized tools like infrared thermal cameras and moisture meters to detect hidden problems that are invisible to the naked eye.

**Q: What is the most common deal-breaker discovered during an inspection?**
A: Structural issues—specifically major foundation cracks or failing load-bearing supports—are generally the most significant deal-breakers due to the extreme cost and complexity of the repairs required.

**Q: How long should a thorough property inspection take?**
A: For a standard single-family home, a professional-grade inspection typically takes between 2 to 4 hours, depending on the size of the property and the complexity of the mechanical systems.
